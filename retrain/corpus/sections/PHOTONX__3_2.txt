3.2 Capacity of participants and consortium as a whole #@CON-SOR-CS@# #@PRJ-MGT-PM@#

Consortium description and complementarity: The PhotonX consortium consists of 15 partners from 7 European countries (5 Member States: Spain, Germany  Ireland, France, Netherlands, and 2 Associated Countries: UK and Switzerland), combining excellent science and frontier research (ULM and HS), deep-tech SMEs (ALPES, NV, JLM, IRIS), large industrial (VW, CELSA) and SME (MyB) end-users (USAAR for fermentation systems), and expert organisations in standardisation (UNE), exploitation (TEMA), IPR (ICP), co-creation (BRITREST) and the leading industry association that promotes the sustainable development of organizations working in the field of photonics in Europe (EPIC).
The partners cover the full value chain required to achieve the project’s objectives: development of advanced photonics technologies (Mid-IR, SERS, integrated photonics), complementary sensing modalities (electronic noses), multimodal integration and AI data fusion, validation in three high impact industrial use cases, and pathways to standardisation and market exploitation. The consortium has been structured to integrate disciplinary and interdisciplinary expertise, linking sensing, engineering, data science, industrial process expertise, business exploitation, and social sciences/humanities perspectives in co-creation and upskilling (for digital adoption).
Industrial/commercial involvement: Industrial partners (MyBiotech, Van Werven, CELSA) act as early adopters, providing real-world complex industrial scenarios for validating the performance and reliability of the multimodal sensing platform, and for demonstrating its ability to respond to important market needs, anchoring the exploitation strategy. Technology SMEs in the consortium (IRIS, ALPES, NV, JLM) provide the pathway to scalable products and services, ensuring results are market ready. They are supported by UNE (standards), ICP (securing IP assets), BRITEST (alignment with industrial processes, operator requirements, and upskilling for adoption), TEMA framing the business case scenarios to generate market pull, and EPIC representing over 800 companies working in the field of photonics in Europe and fostering a vibrant photonics ecosystem by maintaining a strong network and acting as a catalyst and facilitator for technological and commercial advancement. This ensures the project balances scientific innovation with industrial viability and market exploitation.
Gender and open science aspects: The consortium is committed to gender balance and inclusiveness in project governance, stakeholder engagement, and industrial trials, and will apply a gendered innovation approach in requirements gathering, co-creation, and dissemination. Open science practices will be implemented through FAIR data management, open access to publications, and contributions to standardisation, always in tandem with robust IP Management for securing IP assets to ensure we protect European novelty for reinforcing competitive advantage.
Strong project management, led by a leading SME in the photonics integration space: IRIS brings longstanding experience in managing complex EU Framework Programme, as well as commercial industry projects, to ensure efficient project management, governance, transparent communication, and risk management across the consortium. Its dual role as a technology provider and integrator positions it to align technical progress with industrial needs, keeping the project focused on delivery, exploitation, and impact.

O1. To scale up advanced photonics sensing platforms to TRL6: Mid-Infrared Spectroscopy with Interband & Quantum Cascade Laser (ICL/QCL) technology (O1a), Surface-Enhanced Raman Spectroscopy (O1b), and InGaAs single-photon avalanche diodes (SPAD) camera module with on-chip processing (O1c)
O1a. To develop mid‑infrared spectroscopy (based on FTIR (=> broadband spectra for wide analyte coverage) with the alternative option of QCL/ICL (=> narrowband spectra for target analyte coverage) coupled to iHWGs). The objective is to design and optimise integrated hollow waveguides (iHWGs) that act as miniaturised gas cells and photon conduits. These iHWGs will form the core of a modular mid-infrared spectroscopy system in which they are coupled with appropriate sources and detectors. FTIR broadband sources will enable simultaneous detection and discovery of multiple analytes, while QCL/ICL narrowband emitters will provide targeted coverage for specific low-concentration compounds. By tailoring the geometry, optical path length, and fabrication materials of the iHWGs, compact, robust, and versatile sensing modules will be created that operate reliably even in harsh industrial environments. The development will follow an iterative optimisation strategy, ensuring that different iHWG structures are adapted to path-length requirements, environmental conditions, and use-case targets, thereby delivering a complete, application-oriented MIR sensing system.
KPIs: Development of a tailored iHWG structures for harsh environments (corrosive gases, high humidity, high temperatures). Demonstration of broadband FTIR-iHWG systems for multi-analyte detection and narrowband QCL/ICL-iHWG systems for single-analyte detection for low concentrated target molecule. Stable operation of both systems. Validation in PhotonX use cases.  ■Significantly lower energy consumption (30% reduction), much faster acquisition and lower latency (20–100× improvement in response time), higher sensitivity (20–30% lower LoD), and a footprint reduced by more than half compared to current devices. ■Compact head for 4-5× volume reduction with improved uptime and reduced calibration needs.
O1b. To demonstrate a fully integrated indium gallium arsenide (InGaAs) single-photon avalanche diode (SPAD) sensor with on-chip photon count accumulation, enabling real-time data processing directly at the pixel level and reducing reliance on external hardware. This approach will deliver unprecedented system-level performance in terms of data throughput, frame rate, and compactness, paving the way for an efficient and fast short-wave infrared (SWIR) imaging in, e.g. industrial machine vision solutions. A second critical objective is to establish a new state-of-the-art in sensor format by successfully fabricating a 192×128 InGaAs SPAD array, the largest format available to date. KPIs: Sensor/Hardware: ■Array size: 192×128; ■Integration yield: >80% of hybridized sensor-readout circuit (ROIC) assemblies are functional; ■Frame rate: Demonstrate up to 8000 fps. System-Level: ■On-chip processing power: Reduce external data transfer by ≥60% compared to baseline readout; Innovation/Exploitation: Scalability: ■Roadmap to ≥150k pixels in future projects; ■IP creation: ≥ 1 patent filed.
O1c. To develop and demonstrate a robust, scalable, and intelligent SERS sensing platform for real-time industrial monitoring, through a double approach, integrated with AI-driven analytics: The key results will be: 1) Next-generation substrates: reproducible, durable, high-uniformity SERS substrates (plasmonic–dielectric metasurfaces or SHINERS nanoparticles). 2) Advanced Raman optical modules: dual-wavelength excitation, background suppression, ruggedized spectrometers with auto-calibration.  KPIs: ■Fabricate SERS substrates with EF ≥ 10⁶, stable over 1000 h under industrial conditions; ■ Raman instrumentation module with system SNR ≥ 20 and automatic fluorescence suppression delivering >10× lower LOD than current Raman; Edge-AI algorithms for robust, automated quantification and predictive process control.

O3. To design, develop, and validate the PhotonX configurable platform, including Hardware Configurator, Data Layer (AI-driven fusion), and Decision Layer.
IRIS will scale up and extend its multirange spectroscopy testbed to into a robust multimodal integration platform by embedding fusion at three levels: physics (co-optimised sensing elements such as SERS-enhanced Raman), signal (shared sampling and sample conditioning frames for Raman, NIR, etc.), and data (hybrid-ensemble modelling combining diverse modalities for robust prediction). The project will extend the platform with MIR ICL/QCL, SPAD imaging, intelligent SERS, and non-optical chemical sensor arrays. Shared hardware design where feasible (e.g. common gas sampler for both MIR and chemosensors), will reduce footprint, redundancy, and cost, while low-latency (<100 ms) hybrid-ensemble AI pipelines with drift compensation will enable real-time, explainable, and reliable monitoring in industrial environments. A dedicated Decision Engine will be developed to translate fused data streams into actionable process insights, anomaly alerts, and operator guidance, ensuring practical usability and trust in industrial settings. The scaled-up platform will evolve from a laboratory testbed to an interoperable industrial configurator, underpinning compact, cost-effective, and European-manufactured multimodal sensing devices. KPIs: Interoperable multimodal configurator platform ready for integrating multimodal solutions in the 3 industrial Use Cases; ■ Modular hardware configurator functional & tested in ≥3 configurations; ■ Data & AI layers validated and interoperable with ≥2 industrial IT systems (and also FIWARE + GAIA-X) and developed in compliance with AI Act.
Alignment with call topic: ■ ‘Integration of sensors into multimodal systems, supported by AI-driven data analytics...’ ■ ‘Improved system efficiency, reliability, adaptability, and scalability while maintaining EU sovereignty’.

Reusability of data/research outputs: Where possible, we will make the data produced in the project usable by third parties including after the end of the project. We will provide the documentation needed to validate data analysis and facilitate data re-use (e.g., readme files with information on methodology, codebooks, data cleaning, analyses, variable definitions, units of measurement, etc.). Where possible, data will be made freely available in the public domain to permit the widest re-use possible. The aim will be for data to be licensed using standard reuse licenses (e.g., Creative Commons, Open Data Commons). The provenance of the data will be thoroughly documented using the appropriate standards, and all relevant data quality assurance processes will be described.
Curation and storage/preservation costs: Research data/output management will be managed through Subtask 7.2.2 Data Management and FAIR compliance- these costs are for the most part personnel costs for the experts involved. A core team with prior expertise in Data Management (and data quality assurance) will be mobilised under the leadership of the Project Coordinator. A reasonable budget has been planned under ‘Other Costs, Works and Services’ for making data FAIR and for long term preservation.
Data Security: The data will be safely stored in trusted repositories for long term preservation and curation. Provisions will be made for data recovery as well as secure storage and transfer of sensitive data.
Regulatory and Ethical aspects: e.g., compliance with GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation) for any personal data gathered during the project.

Description of work:  
Task 1.1 Compact ICL/QCL MIR [Lead: UULM; Participants: HS, ALPES  M1–M24]
Broadly tuneable hybrid ICL/QCLs external cavity source: Within this task, ALPES will work on the specifications set by ULM on the choice of the emission spectrum, and also on the specification set by the system integrator. For the development of the new lasers, a selection of the epitaxial material available in ALPES will be made followed by the definition of the experimental plan for the design of the lasers. The focus will be on the development of the fabrication process for the waveguide design and gain material. ALPES will design both strain-balanced and lattice matched heterogeneous quantum cascade laser structures with a flat gain profile for broadband emission. Active region designs will be adapted to the PhotonX needs, average optical power, spectral gain coverage, power dissipation. The proprietary tools developed in ALPES will be used to perform a self-consistent simulation of the active region and the optical cavity. The developed broadgain devices will be integrated into a single ALPES’ GLIDER. An optical assembly will be used to overcome polarisation difference between ICLs and QCLs. A set of 1 ICL and 3 QCLs will be integrated in the GLIDER, in order to produce an ultra-wide spectral tuning device, capable of a tuning range of over 1200 cm-1 in total. Further to the development of the laser assembly, ALPES will develop the custom-designed driving electronics for its devices, designed to work with the MIR sensing tool.
Within this task, UULM will establish a laboratory setup to evaluate the performance of the newly developed ICL/QCL sources under labratory conditions. This configuration will enable benchmarking of wavelength tuning range, output power, coupling efficiency into substrate-integrated hollow waveguides (iHWGs), and spectral resolution. Subsequently, the sources will be interfaced with tailored iHWGs acting as miniaturised gas cells to characterise spectroscopic parameters such as signal-to-noise ratio (SNR), detection limits and response time. A dedicated gas-mixing system with certified gases will be used to generate reproducible scenarios with defined concentrations, allowing systematic performance assessment. Comparative measurements with FTIR spectrometers will be conducted to establish quantitative reference data and ensure calibration fidelity. The results from these evaluations will guide the iterative optimisation of iHWG geometries, coupling optics, and detector integration, thereby delivering a validated MIR sensing subsystem prepared for subsequent platform integration within PhotonX.
T1.2 Fully integrated InGaAs SPAD sensor with on-chip photon count accumulation [Lead: NV; M1-24]
This task covers the complete development cycle of the InGaAs SPAD camera module, from pixel-level design through to full system characterisation. Work will begin with the design and optimisation of individual SPAD pixels and their integration into a 192×128 array, validated first through simulations and then through the fabrication and testing of single-pixel structures. In parallel, the supporting camera electronics will be designed, including the PCB, ROIC control firmware, and a basic user interface, enabling early functional validation. The ROIC will be prepared through dicing, mounting, and bonding and will be tested with the emulated SPAD inputs prior to hybridisation. A packaging will be developed, and it will ensure thermo-electric cooling and hermetic sealing, with prototypes being tested for thermal and vacuum validation. A dedicated 3D integration process will be established, optimising bump deposition and flip-chip bonding on dummy structures before applying the process to hybridise the SPAD arrays with the ROIC. The fully integrated devices will then be packaged and characterised under controlled conditions, capturing images and benchmarking key performance metrics such as the dark count rate, photon detection efficiency across wavelength and temperature, and overall image quality. By performing this work in an iterative process, supported by simulation-driven design, modular testing, and fallback options, it will be ensured that risks (see Table 3.1e) such as non-uniformities, excess noise, bonding failures, or below-spec performance are identified early for their mitigation.
Task 1.3 Intelligent SERS sensing platform for real-time industrial monitoring [Lead: IRIS; Participants: Engagenment with endusers through Task 3.1) M1–M24]
In this task, IRIS will develop an industrial-grade, reproducible, and quantifiable SERS platform through a dual innovation strategy that advances both substrate technology and Raman detection, supported by AI-driven analytics. On the substrate side, IRIS will engineer next-generation of SERS substrates including SHINERS designs with polymer-silica shells and optional graphene or other interlayers to improve both chemical and mechanical stability. In addition, IRIS will evaluate hybrid plasmonic–dielectric metasurfaces (HPD-MS) for achieving uniform enhancement while reducing photo-thermal effects and spectral artefacts. On the Raman instrumentation side, the task will implement dual-wavelength excitation (532/785 nm), high-performance filters, and deep-cooled or time-gated detectors among other opto-mechanical approaches to suppress fluorescence, optimise signal-to-noise, and withstand rugged industrial conditions. The optical setup will be co-designed to align with the multimodal integration framework in WP2, ensuring shared interfaces and compatibility with the Industrial Multimodal Integration Platform. AI-driven spectral analytics will be developed providing required baseline correction and drift compensation, as well as predicting modelling. These capabilities will be designed for seamless integration with the WP2 hybrid-ensemble data fusion layer, enabling SERS to contribute robustly to multimodal sensing configurations.
Task 1.4 Non-optical chemical sensing array [Lead: JLM; M1–M24]
In this task JLM will develop a set of chemical sensor modules that can directly be integrated into the PhotonX platform. These modules will share a common form factor and digital interface allowing to select the most suitable module for the targeted application. After selection of the most promising sensor technologies suitable for the requirements defined in Task 3.1 and the modules will be developed according to the design architecture defined in Task 2.1 and the PhotonX multimodal configurator stack (T2.2).
The developments will be based on existing modules and building blocks available at JLM (e.g. MOX sensor modules, electrochemical sensor modules, PID modules, based on commercially available sensors components as well as new gas sensors, that are currently under development through collaborations of JLM (e.g. EU project AMUSENS). Selection criteria for suitable sensors are detectable analytes, limits of detection, cross sensitivities, reproducibility, long term stability, speed of response, dimensions, sample volume requirements, cost and power requirements. At least three different modules will be developed covering a wide range of applications. Each module is equipped with a micro controller, which performs synchronised data acquisition and data pre-processing and provides calibration information. Using a standardised communication protocol (e.g. based on CAN, UART or I2C) these modules will allow for plug and play configuration, making servicing / replacement of modules as well as adaptation of a system to new requirements easy and straight forward. Further flexibility is achieved by installing more than one module within a PhotonX system.
Drift-compensation strategies will be designed in parallel, supported by temperature and humidity correction mechanisms provided by the MIR platform (T1.1). Sensor characterization will cover reproducibility, response time, recovery, and baseline stability under controlled laboratory conditions with certified gas mixtures, with results feeding directly into WP2 algorithm development.
Finally, optimized chemical sensors arrays will be transferred to WP2 for testing with the multimodal integration platform, prior to integration into multimodal demonstrators in WP3, as part of the multimodal solutions in which the chemical sensing modules will provide real-time, low-level screening of gas headspaces in a fusion with the photonic modalities to enhance detection performance, classification accuracy, and reliability under fluctuating industrial conditions. | Description of work:

After selection of the most promising sensor technologies suitable for the requirements defined in Task 3.1 and the modules will be developed according to the design architecture defined in Task 2.1 and the PhotonX multimodal configurator stack (T2.2).
The developments will be based on existing modules and building blocks available at JLM (e.g. MOX sensor modules, electrochemical sensor modules, PID modules, based on commercially available sensors components as well as new gas sensors, that are currently under development through collaborations of JLM (e.g. EU project AMUSENS). Selection criteria for suitable sensors are detectable analytes, limits of detection, cross sensitivities, reproducibility, long term stability, speed of response, dimensions, sample volume requirements, cost and power requirements. At least three different modules will be developed covering a wide range of applications. Each module is equipped with a micro controller, which performs synchronised data acquisition and data pre-processing and provides calibration information. Using a standardised communication protocol (e.g. based on CAN, UART or I2C) these modules will allow for plug and play configuration, making servicing / replacement of modules as well as adaptation of a system to new requirements easy and straight forward. Further flexibility is achieved by installing more than one module within a PhotonX system.
Drift-compensation strategies will be designed in parallel, supported by temperature and humidity correction mechanisms provided by the MIR platform (T1.1). Sensor characterization will cover reproducibility, response time, recovery, and baseline stability under controlled laboratory conditions with certified gas mixtures, with results feeding directly into WP2 algorithm development.
Finally, optimized chemical sensors arrays will be transferred to WP2 for testing with the multimodal integration platform, prior to integration into multimodal demonstrators in WP3, as part of the multimodal solutions in which the chemical sensing modules will provide real-time, low-level screening of gas headspaces in a fusion with the photonic modalities to enhance detection performance, classification accuracy, and reliability under fluctuating industrial conditions.

Description of work:  
Task 2.1 Architecture design of the industry-grade PhotonX integration platform [Lead: IRIS; Participants: IRIS will engage with all sensor developers (linking with WP1) and the UC owners via T3.1); M1–M9]: This task will design the architecture for the PhotonX multimodal integration platform (using IRIS Multirange Spectroscopy Fusion architecture- figure 6, as the starting point). We will define how the photonic modules (MIR ICL/QCL, SERS, SPAD) and the non-optical modules (chemical sensor arrays) will be physically and digitally combined into a unified framework. The design will specify hardware interfaces, data pathways, synchronisation strategies, and software components required for hybrid fusion across the physics, hardware, and data layers. A strong focus will be placed on modularity and up-and-down scalability to ensure that the platform can accommodate both current demonstrators and future sensor extensions (and already as was discussed the Multirange Spectroscopy Fusion testbed architecture incorporates VIS; NIR; SWIR; MWIR; Raman; LIBS; Fluorescence; and MV). The configurator will allow side-by-side testing of alternative fusion strategies, modular swapping of probes, and alignment of heterogeneous data streams. The task also comprehends implementing the capability to integrate of prior knowledge, encapsulated in algorithms and databases provided by the hardware providers based on domain-wise expertise to allow the execution of non-agnostic (non-black-box) data processing approaches.   
Hardware and software integration will be validated through iterative prototyping, ensuring compatibility across all modules. The configurator will serve both as a research platform for developing fusion strategies and as the foundation for the design of industrial-grade multimodal analysers.
Task 2.3 AI-driven hybrid ensemble modelling and data fusion [Lead: IRIS; Participants: ULM, NV, JLM; M6–M15] This task will focus on implementing hybrid ensemble AI algorithms to fuse multimodal data streams, combining complementary strengths of physics-level integration, hardware synchronisation, and advanced analytics. The algorithms will be designed for real-time performance, optimised for noise reduction, drift compensation, and cross-modality correlation. Data pipelines will be developed to ensure interoperability with industrial systems, including SCADA, MES, and digital twin frameworks. Likewise, data pipelines will include, whenever either possible or required, domain-wise prior knowledge in order to avoid “black-box” approaches, when feasible, particularly, using available empirical or theoretical (first-principle-based) rules for data conditioning and processing for each sensor type. Models will be benchmarked against current state-of-the-art approaches to demonstrate improvements in accuracy, detection limits & decision support.
Task 2.4 Consolidation of the multimodal integration prototype [Lead: IRIS; Participants: HS, ALPES, NV, JLM; M15–M18]. This task will consolidate the developed modules (photonic and non-optical sensors), configurator stack, and hybrid ensemble algorithms into a stable multimodal integration prototype. The prototype will integrate where feasible shared hardware components such as embedded computing, probe interfaces, and communication modules to ensure compactness, modularity, and robustness. The focus will be on verifying interoperability across modules, ensuring real-time data fusion performance, and performing stress tests under controlled lab conditions representative of the targeted industrial environments. The validated integration prototype will be ready for configuring and integrating the case-specific multimodal demonstrators according to the requirements of the UCs in WP3.
Task 2.5 Development of the AI-enabled Decision Engine [Lead: IRIS; Participants: UULM, NV, JLM; M12–M24]: This task will build the decision layer that transforms fused multimodal sensor outputs into actionable insights for industrial operation. The engine will include rule-based logic, explainable AI modules, and predictive models to provide recommendations for process control, anomaly detection, and quality assurance. Interfaces will be designed to ensure compatibility with industrial IT/OT systems (e.g. SCADA, MES, digital twins), enabling real-time decision support and automated feedback loops. The decision engine will also provide transparency by linking back predictions to modality contributions, ensuring operator trust and supporting regulatory compliance. Task 4.3 IPR Management and Technology watch (Lead: ICP. Partners: All; M1-M36)
Subtask 4.3.1 IP Protection. ICP (M1-M36). This subtask handles the filing of 3-5 commercially strategic patents from PhotonX. As such, the outputs/results generated by the project will be analysed as they emerge. Up-to-date reviews of the SOTA and patents will be performed periodically to continually confirm FTO and the novelty of the KERs. This ST includes: a) providing basic, intermediate and advanced IP and patent training sessions across the project’s duration to enhance the partners understanding of IP and patenting fundamentals and processes; b) analysing project workstreams and assisting partners in 1:1 sessions to identify and refine innovations from their work suitable for IP applications (Patenting, Trademark, Copyright, Trade Secrets, etc); c) ensuring that European Patent Office fundamentals are met in partners’ innovations including novelty, inventive step, technicity, industrial applicability and non-obviousness; d) prior art assessments to identify pre-existing IP and relevant prior patent art; e) scoring patents to determine suitability for formal filings using ICP’s commercial IP assessment framework; f) defining filing strategies for national and regional patent offices; g) selecting outside counsel in line with EC PP processes and engaging patent attorney support for patent application legal drafting; h) formal filing at regional patent office; i) patent office engagement for office actions, amendments and prosecution; j) guiding partners on paths to achieving EU-wide unitary patent protection which has the potential to significantly raise the commercial, asset and reputational value from their IP investment. IRIS (M1-M36): A ‘Technology Watch’ repository will be populated and actively updated with new literature, articles, publications, scientific journals, conference papers and media articles relevant to the research areas of the KERS. Relevant results regarding ongoing research and industry activity (identified using parameters defined with the consortium) will be summarised in periodic updates to keep partners informed on the evolving SOTA as the project progresses. These subtasks will output a consolidated report (D4.4- a first version at M18, and a final version at M36 on IP protection measures and patent activities, documenting implementation of IP Tools (Trademark, Copyright, Trade Secrets, etc.), patent landscape activity, external attorney tendering process, IP assessment framework and selection methodology, filing strategies undertaken, as well as reporting on the technology, KER, periodic updates and dedicated repository.

Task 6.1 Project and Consortium Management [Duration: M1-36. Leader: IRIS. Participants: All]
IRIS will act as Project Coordinators of PhotonX. From their experienced PMO, they will implement effective procedures for the day-to-day administrative and strategic coordination of the project. A highly experienced and organised Project Coordinator (PC)- Dr. Sara Sanchez from IRIS (PhD in Applied Spectroscopy and chemometrics, experienced Project & Innovation manager & specialist in photonic sensing, spectroscopy (notably SERS), and sensor data analytics)- will lead the consortium in project monitoring and control (ensuring work is delivered on time, to budget and to expected scope/quality). The PC will manage internal communication, consortium meetings, and decision-making processes, ensuring timely delivery of deliverables and milestones. Centralised meetings of all partners (Project Steering Committee-PSC) will be held every 6 months and chaired by the PC. WP Meetings involving the implementation teams of all active WPs will be held monthly (and chaired by the corresponding WP Leader). Agendas, minutes and action items will be documented and available to all partners. Progress monitoring will be aligned with clearly defined KPIs per WP. The PC (with the assistance of the PMO support team in IRIS) will take responsibility for ensuring all progress and financial reporting requirements are fulfilled, as well as coordinating input for periodic reviews. The PC will act as the primary interface with the European Commission and will ensure that all requirements of the Grant Agreement and Consortium Agreement are adhered to. Collaborative software tool, with a secure document repository and project tracking features, will be set up by the PC and its active use promoted for supporting cooperation and transparency among the project partners. 
Task 6.2 Risk Management and Quality Monitoring [Duration: M1-36. Leader: IRIS. Participants: All]
This task establishes and continuously updates the risk register (monitoring and updating the risks included in Table 3.1e), including scientific, technical, operational and any conflict-related risks. It will ensure that mitigation plans are implemented and reviewed at each consortium meeting. A quality assurance framework will guide internal peer review of project deliverables to ensure that each meet consistently high standards.
Task 6.3 Data Management and Open Science [Duration: M1-36. Leader: IRIS. Participants: All]
Subtask 6.3.1 Data Management [Leader: IRIS]: The Data Manager (Dr. D. Nettleton from IRIS) will lead the consortium in sound data management in compliance with FAIR practices and GDPR. A Data Management Plan (D6.3) will be drawn up, maintained and implemented by M6 & updated biannually. DMP will define and maintain the status of the following: data that will be generated during research; metadata, standards and quality assurance measures; plans for sharing data; copyright and intellectual property rights of data; data storage and back-up measures; data management roles and responsibilities. 
Subtask 6.3.2 Open Science [Leader: UULM]: In close collaboration with the dissemination activities in Task 5.2, as well as informed by the IP strategy in Task 4.3), this subtask will manage the project’s 'open science' practices to ensure: 1) early and open sharing of research; 2) promote open access to research data & other related research outputs; 3) effective research output management; 4) implementation of measures to ensure reproducibility of research outputs; 5) meaningful involvement of relevant stakeholders in the cocreation of content. This work will also link with data management (T6.3.1), whereby open Science practices will be applied wherever feasible, including the release of representative datasets under FAIR principles.
Task 6.4 Ethical and Inclusive Research [Duration: M1-36. Leader: IRIS. Participants: All]
IRIS with consortium support will oversee the project’s ethical responsibilities, incl. identification and mitigation of potential ethical risks related to data use, AI deployment, dual-use concerns, and sustainability, implementing responsible research practices across the consortium, and ensuring gender and inclusion measures are integrated into project activities. Cross-cutting issues related to gendered innovation will also be effectively managed, by considering whether sex, gender, and intersectional analysis could reveal new perspectives, particularly in relation to usability, design of verification tools, or application-specific needs. | Description of work (Lead UULM. Description of risk (Likelihood/
Severity- Low/Medium/High (L/M/H) | WP | Proposed risk-mitigation measures
Inefficient coupling of the ICL/QCL into the iHWGs. (M/M) | 1 | Iterative optimisation of the iHWG design (incoupling geometries, alignment) and use of optical lenses or mirrors.
Insufficient sensitivity to address the desired LODs using iHWGs in combination with ICL/QCL sources. (M/H) | 1 | Optimisation of the iHWG optical path length (different geometries and lengths) to increase the effective absorption path.
Instability of operation in harsh environments (high temperature, corrosive atmospheres). (M/H) | 1 | Careful substrate material selection and conditioning/bypassing of gas streams prior to analysis in the iHWG.
SPAD device performance below specifications (excessive dark count rate, crosstalk, or poor detection efficiency). (M/M) | 1 | Extensive simulation-driven design, iterative pixel/array optimisation, and early testing on single-pixel and dummy structures will identify and correct non-uniformities. Post-fabrication isolation techniques and calibration firmware will further stabilise performance.
SERS substrates degradation (M/H) | 1 | Application of protective coatings and/or conditioning (i.e. thermal management).
SERS device do not meet industrial robustness (M/M) | 1 | Early testing in industrial conditions, allowing iterative development.
Fluorescence background in complex samples (M, H) | 1 | Implement dual-wavelength excitation (532/785 nm) with automatic switching. Use baseline correction algorithms. Time-gated raman development.
Failures in 3D integration or packaging (bonding defects, thermal management issues, or leaks in hermetic sealing). (M/M) | 1 | Process development will be carried out first with dummy structures to tune bump deposition and bonding parameters. Multiple runs with spare dies and fallback to proven foundry processes will ensure yield. Package designs will be validated via thermal & vacuum tests before full assembly.
Integration of heterogeneous sensors leads to data desynchronisation or latency in fusion pipelines. (M/H) | 2 | Implement time-stamping and buffering strategies, modular data pipelines, and iterative stress testing of synchronisation.
AI-driven hybrid ensemble models underperform in real-time due to computational overhead. (M/H) | 2 | Optimise models for embedded computing, apply pruning/quantisation strategies, and maintain fallback decision rules with single-modality baselines.
Industrial environments introduce unforeseen interference (dust, high temperature, multiphase flows) that compromise sensor stability. (M/M) | 3 | Perform environmental stress tests in WP2, ruggedise probes, and design bypass/conditioning options where needed.
Multimodal demonstrators fail to meet target KPIs (accuracy, speed, robustness) in real-world validation. (M/H) | 3 | Use iterative two-loop validation (TRL4 lab → TRL5 industrial), refine models with on-site calibration data, and maintain reconfigurability of the demonstrators to swap underperforming modalities.
Lack of consensus among partners on IPR ownership and licensing models slows exploitation.  (M/M) | 3 | ICP is an expert in leading consortia IP strategies and management. HE IPR rules understood at proposal stage, and will be formalised in the Consortium Agreement from the outset. Use pre-defined background/foreground IP matrices, and leverage (early) conflict resolution mechanisms in CA.
Exploitation and business restrictions. Conflicts on IPR, governance of the project or project plan executions. (M/M) | 4 | Contingency plan to activate the decision-making mechanism through the EAB and IC. Proposal of exploitation routes alternative to those defined in the Exploitation and Business plan.
Missing business opportunities (M/H) | 4 | Dissemination plan (WP6) and active “problem solving” strategy in WP6 will allow us to be aware of emerging business opportunities and its ability to catch them.
Limited stakeholder engagement or low visibility of dissemination activities reduces project impact. (L/H) | 5 | EPIC’s leadership makes this a low-level risk. Leverage EPIC’s & consortium networks (Photonics21, Plastics Recyclers Europe, Steel associations, Pharma consortia), ensure targeted outreach via events, multilingual communication materials & alignment with industrial associations’ agendas.
Competitive implementation timeline & Misalignment between partners on technical integration and demonstration timelines leads to delays or resource bottlenecks. (M/M) | 6 | Strong governance structure with regular WP leader coordination meetings, milestone-based tracking, risk monitoring via T6.2, and contingency reallocation of resources to critical paths. Good cross WP engagement assisted by Technical Manager. Industrial pace.
