Emerging technologies for conversion of the organic content of Municipal Solid Waste

Fixed EC Keywords

Abstract 7 
 
Page 3 of 54 
PERCAL will exploit Municipal Solid Waste (MSW) as feedstock to develop intermediate chemical products at high 
yield and low impurity level with huge industrial interest. These will be complementary to the bioethanol (current 
PERSEO technology), to achieve a cascade valorisation of the MSW components, i.e.: 
• Lactic acid (LA) to produce: 1) Eco-friendly ethyl lactate solvents by reactive distillation from lactic acid 
&bio-ethanol to be used in cleaning products and inks and 2) hot-melt adhesives for cardboard and other non-food 
applications in combination with maleic anhydride by reactive extrusion. 
• Succinic acid (SA) as an intermediate building blocks to production of polyols for the polyurethane industry.  
• Biosurfactants by chemical and/or microbiological modification of protein and lipid fraction from remaining 
fractionof MSW fermentation. 
IIn order to minimize the MSW heterogeneous composition (due to seasonal and origin variability driven by local 
economic, social and climate conditions) limitations as a fermentation feedstock, four main innovations will be 
proposed: i) New enzymatic cocktails to maximize hydrolysis of fermentable organic matter with low inhibitors 
production, ii) the use of high yield, specific and robust strains for each selected acid, iii ) the extraction of 
fermentation by-products acting as inhibitors to succinic acid production via novel membrane electrolysis 
employing an integrated continuous fermentation coupled with simultaneous organic acid removal in comparison 
with SA sequential fermentation followed by its separation using an electrodialysis-based and iv) optimize 
simultaneous saccharification and fermentation for lactic acid production followed by a downstream separation 
process based on membrane electrodialysis. To maximize the yield and purity of target organic acids, continuous 
and single step fermentation process will be prioritized in order to allow their integration in the PERSEO plant.

1.3.3. WT3 Work package descriptions 
Work package number 9 WP1 Lead beneficiary 10 1 - IMECAL 
Work package title 
Characterization, handling and pre-processing of MSW from different localizations and 
seasons. Final products requirements definition 
Start month 1 End month 18 
Objectives 
- Definition of intermediates requirements and related standard to effectively produce the targeted final products.  
- Characterization of representative organic MSW samples to be used as substrates in the project.  
- Improvement of enzymes cocktails to maximize organic hydrolysis for MSW compositions. Target to produce up 
to85% of potential fermentable sugars contained in the organic MSW. 
Description of work and role of partners 
WP1 - Characterization, handling and pre-processing of MSW from different localizations and seasons. Final 
products requirements definition [Months: 1-18] 
IMECAL, AIMPLAS, ATB, AUA, UGent, CENER, TBWR, VISUM, HAYAT, COVESTRO, EXERGY  
Task 1.1. Final products and intermediates requirements Task Le ader: HAYAT; Other partners: YPR, COVESTRO, 
IMECAL, EXERGY and AIMPLAS. 
Based on a review of the current petrol based or renewable selected products and intermediates properties, 
environmental requirements and especially economic limitations, a list of specific requirements will be defined for each 
one. In a further evaluation  step these requirements will be summarised to specific requirement -lists that serve as a 
basis for future development. The aim here is to group requirements so that more general target areas can be defined 
that allow the use of material developments in mo re than one application. The report will also include the specific 
standards required for each case-study selected. 
Task 1.2. Study of variability of organic MSW. Task Leader: IMECAL Other partners: all.  
The main challenge to use the organic fraction of MSW as feedstock for bio-based industries lies in its high complexity 
and variability in composition. This heterogeneous composition depends on three main parameters:  
• The waste collection system. The organic fraction of MSW contains less amount of inserts in  function of the 
MSWcollection method employed (door-to-door, municipal bring points…) 
• Geographical location. Differences in climate conditions and nutrition culture of EU -countries affects the 
specificcontents of food and therefore its chemical compositio n. Bio-waste coming from rural or urban areas also 
exhibits different compositional characteristics. 
• Seasonality. Climate variations during the year have also shown an important impact in the food content and bio -
waste composition. 
In this task inputs rega rding MSW composition variation from all the partners will be shared and bibliographic data 
will be analysed in order to better know the bio-waste composition and variability across Europe, PERCAL has partners 
and support companies of 12 different countrie s, including Mediterranean, Atlantic, continental and warm and cold 
countries. This analysis will serve as a basis to select ten MSW samples that will be characterized in task 1.3.  
Task 1.3. Characterization of MSW samples. Task Leader: IMECAL Other partne rs: ATB & AUA. 
In this task the selected substrates of task 1.2 will be fully analysed following the NREL methodology for lignocellulosic 
materials in order to know their potential for the intermediates production. 
• The glucan and hemicellulose content, whi ch are the polysaccharides that contain the fermentable sugars, will 
bedetermined by acid hydrolysis and subsequent HPLC analysis. 
• Humidity degree will be measured by dying in oven at 105ºC and the ash content by kiln calcination at 575ºC.  
• Fats and waxes will be analysed by means of Soxhlet extraction with chloroform. 
• Soluble extractives will be analysed by water incubation. 
• Pectin and Lignin content will be determined by extraction with EDTA or with acetic acid and NaClO2.  
• Protein content will be determine d by specific staining and spectrophotometry against bovine serum 
albumin(bicinchoninic acid assay) as well as by Total Nitrogen analysis (Kjeldahl method).  
This analysis will serve as a basis to select three representative MSW samples that will be used as feedstocks in the 
project.

Task 1.4. Pre-treatment and enzymatic hydrolysis improvement from PERSEO bioethanol® process. Task Leader:  
IMECAL 
The selected samples will be pre -treated by means of thermo -chemical processes in order to liberate glucan and 
hemicellulose content for subsequent enzymatic hydrolysis. Both treatments will be based on state-of-the art processes 
performed currently at PER SEO semi-industrial plant. Current saccharification processes are able to liberate around 
65% of all potential fermentable sugars present in the organic fraction of MSW. New enzymes cocktails supplied by 
NOVO will be tested with the aim of producing more t han 85% of all the potential fermentable sugars present, while 
minimising the generation of inhibitors. 
The dosage of the enzymatic cocktail will be calculated based on the present protein content. Five dosages will be 
evaluated for each cocktail, in dupli cate. Fermentable sugars will be analysed at the beginning of the enzymatic 
hydrolysis (t=0), at 24 and 48 h. 
In order to diminish the inhibitory effects of some compounds (weak acids, furfural, hydroxymethyl furfural) towards 
the fermentative microorganisms in the pre-treatment strategy might be modulated depending on the intrinsic resistance 
of microbial strains to these hydrolysates. The amount inhibitors produced will be determined by liquid 
chromatography. 
Task 1.5. Validation of fermentation processes. Task Leader: IMECAL; Other partners: AUA & ATB. 
The hydrolysates generated in Task 1.3 with the three representative samples will be validated in terms of toxicity and 
potential carbon sources for the ethanol, LA and SA production. Tolerance tests will b e performed with the yeast 
responsible of ethanol fermentation, and the different bacteria employed for the production of LA and SA. The tolerance 
of the selected strains will be evaluated in 96 -well growth experiment assays in ten different substrate conc entrations. 
Microbial growth will be measured spectrophotometrically. Flask scale fermentation reactions will be performed taking 
into account the tolerance of the different strains. Production of the target molecules will be tested following the 
indications of the different partners involved in fermentation. Production of the target compounds, together with other 
metabolites, will be followed by HPLC (RID detector). Interaction with task 1.3 will be required if inhibition issues 
arise. Detoxification or feed strategies will be proposed if required to cope with tolerance or inhibition problems.

Work package number 9 WP2 Lead beneficiary 10  3 - ATB 
Work package title Microbial conversions of MSW to lactic acid.  
Start month 2 End month  32 
Objectives 
This WP2 aims at bioconversion of the hydrolysed carbohydrates from organic municipal solid waste to lactic acid 
(LA). The utilisation and upgrading of organic municipal solid waste streams and the purification of lactic acid as a 
monomer for green solvent s will be addressed. Biotechnological tools for the development of non -food industrial 
products and services and/or biogenic sources as feedstock are applied. 
Description of work and role of partners 
WP2 - Microbial conversions of MSW to lactic acid. [Months: 2-32] 
ATB, IMECAL, AUA, TBWR, HAYAT 
Lactic acid (LA) from organic municipal solid waste streams (MSW) feedstocks will be produced by ATB at laboratory 
and pilot-scale using sequential saccharification and fermentation approaches. In another valorization path, suitable raw 
material will be applied for lactic acid production via simultaneous saccharification and fermentation approaches using 
newly isolated strains. These complementary processes will be evaluated in parallel and assessed regarding op timum 
LA production that is suitable for subsequent processing. 
Task 2.1. Screening and selection of new/alternative bacterial strains. Task Leader: ATB.  
Screening and selection of new/alternative bacterial strains with respect to high product yields, suff icient substrate 
uptake, tolerance against eventually remaining impurities/inhibitors and product quality. One of the first targets will be 
the selection of the most efficient and robust LA producing microorganism among the ones mentioned in the state of 
the art or beyond. ATB will employ several screening algorithms to identify microorganisms that satisfy the 
performance criteria (e.g. high specific growth rate, high productivity, maximum sugar consumption, optical purity, 
high process stability etc.) with regard to industrial application. We will screen the most promising microorganisms in 
first simple fermentations in tubes, bottles, shake flasks and lab-scale bioreactors. 
Task 2.2. Fermentation processes for converting biogenic fraction MSW into lactic a cid. Task Leader: ATB Other 
partners: AUA. 
Develop efficient fermentation processes for converting bio waste into lactic acid (L(+) isomer). Mixtures and/or single 
fractions of the (C6/C5) sugar solution from pre -treated fractions will be converted to lact ic acid in order to provide 
compounds for further processing steps. 
Depending on the steps before (WP1/sub -tasks for pre -treatment and/or hydrolysis) and the available analytical 
information the different materials, hydrolysates, sugars, mixtures etc. have  to be characterized in view of their main 
components and possible impurities. If necessary additional enzymatic treatment could be adapted for a better release 
of sugars and/or nutrients. Finally a fermentation broth has to be designed which represents an  optimal mixture of all 
necessary carbohydrates, macro- and micro-nutrients for the bacteria. 
ATB will develop an efficient LA fermentation process through the optimization and elucidation of various parameters. 
Optimization of fermentation conditions will  focus on initial sugar concentration, addition of commercial (e.g. yeast 
extract) or crude nutrient supplements (e.g. oilseed meal hydrolysates, grass juice), and addition of pH correcting agents 
(e.g. ammonia, caustic etc.). Further studies will focus on the evaluation of kinetic parameters including the inhibitory 
effect of by-products and/or impurities to microbial growth and product formation. Fermentation runs will be carried 
out in batch, fed -batch and repeated batch fermentation mode in order to com pare productivity, yield, final LA 
concentration, cost aspects and environmental criteria. The adapted strain(s) may be able to sustain inhibitory conditions 
during continuous fermentation (optional). 
Task 2.3. Downstream processing (DSP) for recovery of lactic acid Task Leader: ATB; Other partners: TBWR 
ATB will adapt and/or develop LA purification considering process scalability, purity/quality, robustness, separation 
yield and cost-competitiveness. One criterion should be the comparison with already exis ting figures related to other 
fermentation feedstocks, whereas the down -stream procedure for eventually unknown MSW -related components and 
impurities needs to be adapted due to missing experiences. In the case that there are still residual sugars in the br oth, a 
nanofiltration step could be integrated in order to recover unconsumed substrate to enhance the overall process 
productivity. All the necessary components (filtration, electrodialysis, ion exchange, evaporation etc.) are available on 
bench (20-50 litres) as well as pilot scale (800-1000 litres).

Task 2.4. Up-scaling of the entire process Task Leader: ATB; Other partners: IMECAL 
Up-scaling of the entire process chain including down stream processing for the provision of product samples in high 
quality. The optimised process in lab-scale (1-3 L bioreactors) will be scaled up in pilot-scale at 50 L bioreactor system. 
Process operation will be mainly dependent on the fermentation mode t hat will be selected in previous tasks 2.2 and 
2.3. The pilot facility for the production of lactic acid at ATB and the corresponding pre -treatment and down -stream 
unit operations will be adapted to the waste materials under investigation to compare the pr ocess performance with 
previous feedstocks. General parameters such as utilisation of carbon sources, possible limitations by lacking nutrients, 
response/stability of microorganism and bioreactor and downstream separation performance at higher scales will be 
elucidated. This task will also provide several product samples in certain qualities for further processing and useful data 
for economic and environmental assessment, respectively. From the evaluation view not only the quantitative 
estimation but also t he qualitative (impurities, optical purity) validation of the “waste -based lactic acid” will be an 
important part of the project. 
The overall bioprocess design for lactic acid production at the pilot scale will be proposed, including up - and down-
stream pr ocesses. Overall process testing will include all accompanying steps, such as hydrolysis, filtration, 
fermentation, full downstream processing based on membrane processes (i.e. electrodialysis) achieving a highly pure 
concentrated lactic acid, and will rel y on previous results generated within WP2. Moreover, the wastewater from the 
pilot scale down streaming is collected to investigate its reutilization in the preceding processes.

Task 3.1. Evaluation of MSW as fermentation feedstock. Task Leader: AUA Other partners: CENER.  
Subtask 3.1.1 – Selection of bacterial strain and optimisation of fermentation media: The primary target will be the 
selection of the most efficient and robust succinic acid producing bacterial strain when the different MSW hydrolysates 
produced in WP1 are used. AUA will screen Basfia succiniciproducens, Actinobacillus succinogenes and Escherichia 
coli AFP184 that have been previously ide ntified by AUA as the most efficient bacterial strains depending on the 
feedstock employed. AUA will screen the bacterial strains in single or two -stage (only in the case of E. coli AFP184) 
fermentations in Duran bottles and bench-scale bioreactors with constant supply of CO2 in the succinic acid production 
stage. 
The optimal bacterial strain will be employed in the optimisation of the fermentation medium. The hydrolysis of 
polysaccharides, proteins (the supply of crude N sources will reduce the supply of yeast extract) and phytic acid (source 
of phosphorus) contained in MSW will lead to the production of a nutrient -rich MSW hydrolysate. AUA will employ 
ICP-MS analysis in order to identify the content of minerals and thus minimise the supply of commercial sa lts. 
Preliminary fermentations in Duran bottles will be carried out in order to assess the inhibitory effect of the MSW 
hydrolysates (produced in WP1) on bacterial growth and succinic acid production as compared to control fermentations 
carried out with commercial sugars (using the same mixture of sugars present in MSW hydrolysates), yeast extract and 
corn steep liquor. In the case that lower efficiency is observed when the MSW hydrolysates are used, AUA will study 
the effect of potential inhibitors present  in MSW hydrolysates (e.g. furfural, 5 -hydroxy-methyl-furfural, acetic acid, 
phenolics etc) and the effect of commercial nutrient supplementation (e.g. yeast extract, corn steep liquor) using a 96 -
well microplate reader followed by design of experiments (D oE) carried out in Duran bottles. The DoE will focus on 
the optimisation of nutrient composition. AUA will optimise the fermentation medium regarding nutrient content 
(addition of yeast extract, salts, MgCO3 and corn steep liquor) and will propose pre-treatment strategies for the removal 
of potential inhibitors. 
Subtask 3.1.2 – Optimisation of lab -scale fed -batch and continuous cultures: AUA will optimise fed -batch and 
continuous cultures using the selected bacterial strain and fermentation media identified in Task 3.1.1. Optimisation of 
fed-batch cultures will focus on initial sugar concentration, addition of MgCO3 or Na2CO3 and feeding strategies. 
Continuous cultures will be optimised regarding initial sugar concentration, dilution rate and bacterial cell 
immobilisation. Fermentation modes of operation will be compared regarding productivity, yield, final sucinic acid 
concentration, capital/operating cost requirements and environmental criteria (e.g. non -renewable energy utilisation, 
waste generation).

Subtask 3.2.1 – Study of electrolysis membrane extraction using a synthetic system: A number of commercially 
available anion exchange membranes will be assessed according to electrochemical parameters such as applied current, 
resistance and potential across the electrochemical cell, and subsequent flux of succinic acid and critical co-products 
(e.g. acetic acid, lactic acid). After an appropriate membrane is chosen, the ideal operational parameters can then be 
assessed according to other process parameters, including extractant concentration, pH and conductivity. This will 
be critical towards minimising the input power per  extracted kg of succinic acid, and equivalent masses of OH -

Subtask 3.2.2 – Optimisation of the integrated system at lab-scale: AUA will use the data from subtask 3.2.1 for the 
development of the integrated system, wh ich will consist of 7 L bioreactor coupled to a high -rate electrochemical

through the extraction cell. This novel system will be developed at A UA in lab -scale in close collaboration with 
visiting researchers from UGENT. AUA and UGENT will study the effect of organic acid extraction on productivity 
and yield during fermentation. The effect of the applied current will be elucidated along with the e ffect of the

membrane togethe r with organic acids will be identified in order to assess the potential effect on fermentation

Task 3.3. Succinic acid purification. Task Leader: AUA Other partners: UGENT 
Conventional purification of succinate salts from fermentation broths requires cell removal via filtration, colour and 
protein removal via activated carbon treatment, acidification of organic acids using resins, concentration of succinic 
acid and removal of acetic and formic acids via evaporation, crystalisation of succinic acid followed by drying. AUA 
has optimised the above process to yield succinic acid crystals with purity higher than 99.5% and recovery yield 
higher around 80% when fermentations were carried out in spent sulphite liquor based media. This downstream 
process will be used as base -case scenario. AUA will optimise the purification of succinic acid produced by the 
electrolysis extraction system. The optimal concentration of succinic acid in the extracted aqueous solution will be 
identified in order to minimise en ergy consumption during evaporation which is required in order to remove the 
acetic and formic acids that also pass through the extraction electrolysis cell. The composition of the aqueous 
solution after the extraction will be identified in order to develop the appropriate separation scheme. Any sufficiently 
small anion will cross the membrane (e.g. chloride, sulfate, phosphate, formate, and more), and effective recovery 
and recycle of these compounds will be investigated. 
Task 3.4. Process scale-up Task Leader: UGENT; Other partners: AUA 
UGENT will use the data from tasks 3.1 -3.3 regarding the lab -scale operation of the fermentation and in situ 
membrane electrolysis extraction in order to move the operation over to a pilot scale unit of > 50 L. The aim will be 
to control the succinic acid production via fermentation exclusively with the applied current (i.e. the electrolysis of 
water to hydroxide), and develop a system to continuously extract the succinic acid crystals from  the acidic extract, 
such as a cooling and settling unit. The process can be investigated under conditions for maximum production rate, 
versus maximum yield, versus minimal power input to develop a set of parameters to compare against existing 
processes in succinic acid production. Other operational parameters more unique to membrane electrolysis, (e.g. 
membrane resistance, electrode potential, electrode fouling, voltage losses) will be investigated in detail, to compare 
the membrane electrolysis lifetime a nd operation against the other existing technologies. The process scale up will 
mimic the fermentation, extraction and purification of succinic acid, with the goal of minimising product output 
according to added chemicals as a first goal (i.e. completely r eplacing chemical caustic and acid control with 
electrochemistry), and followed by minimising the power investment per kg of succinic acid product. At an 
anticipated production rate of 20 g/L/d, here we aim to produce 1 kg/d in continuous, steady state ope ration, which 
should allow sufficient product for assessment and benchmarking against existing processes.  
Task 3.5. Optimisation of bioprocess based on electrodialysis downstream separation system. Task Leader: ATB 
Other partners: AUA 
ATB and AUA will optimise the production and purification of succinic acid in 20 – 50 L scale experiments using 
the unit operations (bioreactor, ultra/nanofiltration, softening, bipolar membrane electrodialysis (BMED), 
cation/anion exchange resins, activated carbon, vacuum evaporation) available at ATB. Succinic acid separation will 
commence with cell removal using hollow fiber membranes. Softening is then carried out by a series of anion and 
cation exchange resins to remove various impurities and metals th at could cause membrane fouling, pipe jam or 
pump erosion. BMED will be employed to convert the succinate (and other organic acid by -products) into the 
respective acid form. In the BMED configuration, which is composed of three compartments, both the anions of the 
organic acids and the sodium cations are transferred across the anion and cation exchange membranes, and then they 
are combined with H+ and OHrespectively, which are produced in the bipolar membrane intermediate layer, to form 
the organic acids and NaOH in the acid compartment and base compartment, respectively. The process parameters 
 
Page 23 of 54 
to be evaluated and optimized are the initial organic acid feed, the voltage of BMED process as well as the conductivity 
and the pH. Another important factor is to reduce the water transport per molarity succinate treated. The most important 
hurdle in this process is to prevent membrane fouling. In order to do so, the adequate pretreatment methods should be 
carried out in conjunction to the following measures: a) rever sing the polarity of electrodes, b) decrease operating 
current, c) improve the hydraulic conditions in stack compartments, d) add chemicals to prevent the formation of 
precipitates or microbial growth, e) extract products before saturation. After electrodi alysis, the product stream is 
processed via cationic/anionic resin or activated carbon in order to remove colour impurities, residual carbon or/and 
nitrogen sources. The final purification steps involve vacuum evaporation, crystalisation and drying. This p rocess will 
be carried at ATB in collaboration with researchers from AUA. The results will be compared with the results achieved 
in previous tasks by electrolysis membrane extraction.

Task 4.1 Characterization of the fermentation residue of the ethanol/LA and SA processes Task Leader: CENER Other 
partners: IMECAL 
Fermentation residues from the fermentatio n of ethanol, SA and LA processes will be analyzed employing the 
characterization techniques described in Task 1.2. Those residues with the highest content in protein and lipids will be 
selected for the revalorization of these fractions within the following tasks. Depending on the composition the strategy 
for the lipid and protein fraction will be evaluated. Processes for the fractionation of lipids and proteins rich fractions 
are well established in the literature, but adaptations and optimizations to the raw material are needed. 
Task 4.2 Hydrolysis of protein fraction Task Leader: CENER Other partners: IMECAL  
Hydrolysis of proteins into amino acids is the first step of converting proteins into surfactants. It is necessary to work 
with concentrated biomass, therefore if its water content is very high concentration by centrifugation or filtration will 
be applied. In order to increase the yield of the enzymatic hydrolysis some pretreatment processes will be studied: 
extraction with ethanol to remove color, previous lipid extraction, milling, heating, additional enzymes. Test conditions 
will be selected from literature in term of solid loads and other variables. The enzymatic processes will be evaluated on 
these pre-treated samples. Enzymes working at neutral or basic pH will be preferred such as papain (pH 7.0), pancreatin 
(pH7.5), Alcalase® 2.5L (pH 8) and Flavourzyme®1000L (pH 7). Working temperature will be around 50 -60ºC, and 
3-4 hours of incubation. Optimal dosage described in literature will be tested. Finally, free aminoacids will be quantified 
by a colorimetric test based on nihidrin reaction and its aminogram will be analyzed by HPLC from samples treated 
with phenylthio isocyanate 
Chemical hydrolysis methods (based on acid or alkali) will be revised from . Acid hydrolysis completely destroys 
tryptophan and partially damages tyrosine, serine and threonine. Alkaly hydrolysis can modify arginine, lysine, cystein, 
serine and threonine. Therefore, pure chemical hydrolysis will be avoided. The enzymatic approach will be the preferred 
one, although, combination with mild acid hydrolysis will be evaluated in order to maximize yield and minimize 
processing time. The protein hydrolyzate will be employed as a Nitrogen source for fermentation procedures and as 
synthons for biosurfactant production. 
Task 4.3 Extraction and hydrolysis of the lipid fraction Task Leader: CENER Other partners: IMECAL  
There are two main approaches for lipid valorisation: the first one is a first step of extraction, followed by the subsequent 
esterification or saponification reactions; the second approach is the direct treatment without separation. In the second 
case ext reme chemical agents are required, and it can be harmful for the protein fraction, therefore, within the 
framework of this project the lipids will be extracted as the first step in revalorization process. Lipid extraction will be 
performed on the dried fermentation residue because water can affect the subsequent saponification reactions. It can be 
performed with a single solvent or with solvent mixtures. Extraction tests will be performed in a soxhlet system with 
organic solvents (dichloromethane, ethyl ace tate: ethanol, or mainly ethyl lactate: ethanol) for 18 hours. After the 
extraction, solvent will be removed and the degree of extraction will be quantified for each case. The lipid fraction will 
be analyzed in order to know the content in acylglycerols (b y GC-FID). Analysis of fatty acid composition will be 
performed by GC-FID or GC-MS of methanolized samples. 
Task 4.4. Synthesis of the amino-lipid biosurfactant Task Leader: CENER Other partners: IMECAL 
By the combination of chemical, enzymatic or a combin ation of both strategies, surfactants will be obtained by the 
condensation of amino acid and lipids. The N -acylation of the amino group by condensation with fatty acids from the 
saponified lipid fraction or directly from the acylglycerides, will be prepare d by either chemical methods or by lipases 
(from Candida antarctica & Rhizomucor meihei). Aqueous or solvent reaction systems will be evaluated. For the 
chemical approach the activation of the acyl carbon of the fatty acids will be performed by the formati on of acyl 
chlorides in strongly alkaline aqueous medium containing the aminoacids. Protection of Nitrogen -amino group will be 
 
Page 26 of 54 
taken into account for some amino acids. A mixture of different molecules is expected, so therefore state of the art 
isolation and characterization techniques will be applied. Surface active properties of the surfactants will be tested such 
as surface tension at the critical micelle concentration, critical micelle concentration, maximum surface excess 
concentration at the air/aqueous solution interface and minimum area per surfactant molecule at the air/aqueous solution 
interphase. 
Task 4.5. Validation of the biosurfactants in relevant industrial applications Task Leader: HAYAT.

Subtask 5.2.1 Production and purificaction of lactide from lacti acid. The procedure of lactide obtention from lactic 
acid consists basically of heating up lactic acid in a glass vessel under vacuum whilst steering and distilling off water 
in order to promote a polycondesation reaction to produce a lactic acid polycondesate. Once the polyester is formed an 
addition of a catalyst will lead us to transform the polycondensate into lactide. The dimer (lactide) will be purified from 
the crude by distillation and  by solution crystallization. For the production of up several kilos, a 15L reactor of 
AIMPLAS’s kilolab facilities will be employed. 
Subtask 5.2.2 Production of Low molecular PLA and PLA-copolymers by reactive extrusion. Lactides will be used for 
their Ring Opening polymerization using a large L/D twin screw extruder as a continuous reactor. In order to optimize 
the reaction conditions in terms of reaction time, initiatior, ratio of copolymer, catalyst and temperature a pre -
optimization employing a Haake minilab extruder will be carried out. 
Once the different polymers and copolymers have been obtained by reactive extrusion and by solution procedures, those 
compounds will be characterized employing several techniques as Differential scanning calorimetry (DS C), 
thermogravimetry (TGA), Gel permeation chromatography (GPC) and rheological tests which are currently available 
in AIMPLAS. In addition the molecular weight of the polyester will be analyzed employing a new NMR method based 
in the correlation of diffusion coefficients acquired by proton NMR spectroscopy and the MW. The method lead us to 
explore and analyze with just some milligrams of sample properties as MW & polidispersity index  
Once the best conditions has been optimized several tests will be done to  adjust the reactive extrusion conditions to 
obtain a PLA grade with a low molecular weight suitable to be used in hot melt adhesives. Special attention will be 
done in the selection of type and amount of the catalyzer. The reaction will be designed to be done in the residence time 
of conventional extrusion process. The PLA will be characterized. 
Subtask 5.2.3 HMA by PLA grafting. Initial tests to PLA graft will be done at lab scale in a discontinuous 1 L reactor 
using some reactive substances (glycolic acid, maleic acid, methacrylate or glycidyl acrylate) and suitable peroxides to 
create a chemical graft bonds between the matrix and selected monomers. The objective is to achieve a percentage of 
graft superior to 1% w/w. The percentage of co -monomer will be determinate by Fourier -transform infrared 
spectroscopy (FT -IR) and their thermal stability by differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) and thermogravimetric 
analysis (TGA).The best results obtained in solution will be transferred to a reactive extrusion pro cess employing a 
Haake minilab extruder.Once the reaction conditions have been optimized the process will be scale up using a twin 
esxtrusion processing parameters (screw design, residence time, feeding of each component, etc..) will be optimized. 
The best PLA grafted grade obtained will be stabilized and processing improved by adding by melt compounding the 
suitable additives to be used as a HMA (e.g., antioxidants, UV stabilizers, plasticizers, tackifiers, waxes, etc…).  
Finally, in order to have a cost competitive product, we the possibility to produce the HMA in one compounding steps, 
using lactides and reactive co -monomers will be studied, i.e., lactide polymerization, reactive commoner grafting and 
adding and mix the rest additives for final HMA formulation.

Description of work. 
Process parameters of PERSEO Bioethanol® semi-industrial plant are currently controlled with a SCADA technology. 
New monitoring technologies developed by VISUM will improve the performance of the plant control by obtaining 
relevant parameter information through advanced sensors and adding an intelligent plant control platform with decision 
support models. The objectives of this WP consist of studying the adaptability of PERSEO Bioethanol® plant to 
produce lactic acid and succinic acid as well as the design and testing of new sensors and control platform for the 
monitoring of the process parameters of interest in a laboratory scale and the study of the scalability of the processes 
and control system at full plant level. 
Task 6.1. Study of parameters of the pr e-treatment and fermentation process for the production of bioethanol, lactic & 
succinic acid and the by-products stream. Task Leader: IMECAL Other partners: VISUM. 
In order to determine the main parameters in the fermentative processes, current control pa rameters measured on line 
in PERSEO pilot plant during the simultaneous saccharification and fermentation processes for the production of 
bioethanol are pH, temperature, pressure and material flow. Additional parameters that would be useful to monitor in 
real time for the better control of the process progress are the viscosity and homogeneity of the feedstock, quantification 
of liberated sugars and quantification of generated ethanol, which are currently measured off line.  
Moreover, taking into account the optimization for the microbial conversion of MSW to lactic and succinic acid (WP 2 
and WP3) the adaptation of PERSEO plant will be studied for the production of both intermediates. In particular, the 
required conditions of the fermentation unit (temperatu re, retention time, pH control, addition of nutrients…) for the 
optimum production of the organic acids at semi-industrial scale will be evaluated, studying the main parameters to be 
controlled for the respective fermentation processes. Control of the subs trate viscosity progress and quantification of 
liberated sugars will be common to the three different type of fermentations. Quantification in real time of lactic and 
succinic acid produced will be also evaluated. 
Task 6.2. Study of parameters of the separ ation/purification processes for the production of bioethanol, lactic acid and 
succinic acid. Task Leader: IMECAL Other partners: VISUM 
To determine the main parameters in the separation/fermentation processes. Each of the target intermediates require 
different separation and purification techniques. Bioethanol is purified by means of distillation. The range of 
temperature, pressure, flows and amount of ethanol at the top of distillation columns are all important parameters to be 
studied. In case of lactic acid, the integration of all the necessary chemical purification systems (filtration, 
electrodialysis, ion exchange, evaporation units etc.) for the achievement of the required quality will be evaluated and 
the relevant parameters to control will be identi fied. Similarly, the adaptation of the electrochemical separation 
described in WP3 at laboratory scale for the purification of SA will be also studied to be implemented in PERSEO 
plant. 
Task 6.3. Monitoring of the processes parameters. Task Leader: VISUM O ther partners: IMECAL. 
Based on the parameters studied by IMECAL in Tasks 6.1 and 6.2, VISUM will build at least 1 monitoring system with 
adjusted sensors, possibly a sampling system, and a data acquisition and treatment platform to be able to track the 
Critical Control Parameters (CCPs) and the Critical Quality Attributes (CQAs). Potential sensors to be used include but 
are not limited to NIR or other optical technologies. This system will be applied at laboratory scale where the different 
processes for the production of bioethanol, lactic acid and succinic acid will be studied. These tests will allow to study 
the feasibility of monitoring the parameters listed as critical to quality of the processing of OMSW and will provide 
deep understanding of the processes in real time. VISUM will also develop the associated calibration models to analyse

Task 7.1 Evaluation of synergies with other projects involved in MSW valorisation. Task Leader: AIMPLAS, Other 
partners: IMECAL, EXERGY. 
There are several projects aiming to make biofuels and/or bio-based products from MSW (FP6, FP7 and H2020). Some 
of them feed the waste to microorganism; some others first go via a hydrothermal liquefaction process or a gasification. 
In the second case, it is important to check that no chemical by -products will affect the downstream microorganism 
process. Synergies with cu rrent or past fuel related projects will be evaluated using the CORDIS database, personal 
contacts and information from the different partners will be employed. A stakeholder board, or an advisory board will 
be proposed in the first months of the project t hat include mainly companies and research centres from the biofuel 
sector (such as Lanzatech, Enerkem, French institute of Petroleum and New Energies, BP, Shell or Total). In terms of 
Board members, we plan to involve several related European associations. 
Task 7.2. Modelling and simulation of the overall process. Mass and energy balances. Leader EXERGY, with the 
support of all partners 
In order to evaluate the environmental impact of the project in terms of energy and mass streams, including the actual 
CO2 balance which aims to be positive, virtual simulations of the entire PERCAL process at the process scale and/or 
larger scale (as a case study) will be run in order to evaluate the different balances. The results will be complemented 
with the LCA outcomes in order to evaluate the real environmental impact of the project. 
Additionally, simulations will be used for the optimisation of the process and the identification of potential hotspots. 
Results will be reported along with potential enhancements of the ov erall PERCAL process and will be used for the 
evaluation of the exergy analysis. The capacity for the up -scaled simulation will be decided during the project’s 
execution. 
Task7.3. Product Life-Cycle-Assessment. Leader AUA, with the support of all partners. 
Life Cycle Assessment considers the entire life cycle of a product to assess the environmental impacts associated with 
all the stages of the product's life from -cradle-to-grave (i.e. from raw material extraction through material processing, 
manufacture, distribution, use, repair and maintenance, and disposal or recycling). 
To ensure the consistency of the LCA studies performed in PERCAL, Task 7.3 will build upon existing and ongoing 
LCA activities on bio -based products and processes, and will take into acc ount the guidelines of the ILCD handbook 
project , which has developed guidelines to make LCA studies more coherent and comparable. Furthermore, the LCA 
approach will be used to compare bio -based and non -biobased products within the same LCA framework, thi s 
comparative approach generates results which are more robust than a reliance on absolute values and will help in the 
marketing claims of the advantage of the new solutions vs. conventional synthetic counterparts.  
The LCAs will be performed using dedicate d software (e.g. Umberto NXT software) and the Ecoinvent database for a 
selected set of raw material/production process/product, taking into account use and disposal or re -use, i.e. ‘from 
cradleto-grave’. The LCA will follow the ILCD guidelines and ISO 140 40 methodologies. This will include the 
definition of the following aspects: goal, scope and system boundaries; life cycle inventory; life cycle impact assessment 
and interpretation of the results. 
A dynamic approach with feedback to the process development activities will be implemented, using the outputs of the 
LCA analysis. The pilot tests and demonstrators will likewise provide feedback to the Life Cycle Inventory, with up -
to-date field data. 
 
Page 35 of 54 
The LCA will serve both as a characterisation tool for the performance of the developed processes, and as a means of 
identifying areas where process optimisation can make the largest environmental savings. This information will be fed 
back to the RTD development and scale-up activities so that effective process optimisations may be realised. 
Task 7.4 Economic analysis. Lead partner: EXERGY, Other partners: All partners  
An economic study is required to validate the economic feasibility of the improved materi als and technologies 
developed in the project and take further decisions. Parallel to the environmental analysis, an economic analysis of the 
materials will be conducted using life cycle costing (LCC) and will be complemented with techno-economic assessment 
(TEA). LCC is a methodology for the systematic evaluation of the economic costs associated with the entire life cycle 
of a product or process, including not only direct costs but also external relevant costs and benefits. LCC also follows 
a life cycle approach, but it assesses the economic costs of the product or process instead of the environmental impacts 
as in LCA. The procedure used for the economic analysis will be similar to that used for the environmental analysis. 
The economic costs of current sce narios will be assessed and compared with the costs of alternative scenarios and 
current solutions. EXERGY will be responsible for conducting the LCC studies, which will take into account not only 
investment and operating costs but also other external relevant costs, such as environmental costs. 
Task 7.5 Social analysis. Lead partner: EXERGY, Other partners: All partners  
Social impacts must also be taken into account when developing new technologies. A social analysis is necessary to 
determine whether the products coming from MSW can be accepted by customers. 
A social analysis of the materials and technologies will be conducted using social life cycle assessment (S-LCA), which 
will be performed in parallel to the environmental (LCA) and economic analyses (LC C). S-LCA is a methodology to 
assess the social and socio-economic aspects of materials or processes and their potential positive and negative impacts 
along their life cycle. These aspects assessed in S -LCA are those that may directly or indirectly affect stakeholders, 
including workers, local community, consumers, value chain actors and society. S -LCA also follows a life cycle 
approach, but the impacts in this case are related to socio -economic processes and comprise human rights, working 
conditions, health and safety, socio-economic repercussion, and so forth. 
As in previous analyses, the procedure used for the social analysis will involve the assessment and comparison of social 
impacts between current scenarios and alternative scenarios, these latter bein g based on the new developments from 
this project. 
EXERGY will be responsible for conducting the S -LCA studies and the data required for the development of the S -
LCA studies will be provided by other project partners and by organise some discussions along the project with 
engaging researchers, industry, policymakers, NGOs and Trade Unions, as well as desk research on identifying potential 
issues. 
Task 7.6: Process inefficiencies Analysis Leader: EXERGY. 
This task will study process life cycles from a thermodynamic perspective in order to analyse inefficiencies at different 
stages in process and propose potential improvements. An exergy assessment methodology to determine specific exergy 
contents to all mass and energy in and outflows will be developed. Where  necessary, computerized simulation tools 
(i.e. COMSOL or TRNSYS) will be utilized. 
The potential for improvements will be assessed by the overall exergy efficiency, which is the real exergy over the 
exergy used in the process. This study will compare: (1) the efficiency of the processes before and after the implantation 
of PERCAL innovations and (2) the efficiency with regards to other similar biological processes.  
Aside from the determination of the efficiency for the different heat transfers along the pr ocess, exergy analysis will 
establish the potential causes for the detected inefficiency and will decrease cost associated to operation and 
maintenance of the system by means of a suitable assignment of energy optimisation measures at different levels of the 
process.

and for the use of the fina l products on the envisaged applications and fulfil EC Directives and specific national laws

performed before the end of the project in order to enable any processing/product specification changes. Input from 
final users is required. A regulatory compliance report will be written to make sure that the products and technologies 
also fulfil all standards and legislation in force with regards to safety criteria.

Task 8.4 Business Models and Plans for KER. Task leader: EXERGY with the support of all partners.  
Development of business models: EXERGY will develop business models to support the continued exploitation and 
eventual commercialisation of each key project result (please see Impact section for more details). This task will be 
led in coordination with the  dissemination/communication (Tasks 8.1/8.2) activities to ensure the coherence of the 
project’s messages towards the outside world. 
The task will involve outlining, refining and defining the business models through two actions:  
Action 1. Business Model Development. A two-step process will be used to build an initial plan. Step 1) Understand, 
refine and define the innovations value proposition through the analysis of, and discussion with consortium partners 
and through literature review considering 1) Poli cy/Environmental/Social/Technological (PEST) issues, 2) 
competitive intensity analysis, 3) the need for alliances and collaboration and finally a 
Strengths/Weaknesses/Opportunities/Threats (SWOT) analysis. Step 2) Build economic models describing the cost 
and revenue projections for the innovations, this allows profit margins to be calculated and business sensitivities to 
be identified. This action will draw on information produced in Task8.2. Estimating revenue potential draws on the 
knowledge gathered in the development of the value proposition. The economic model will allow understanding of 
future cash flows and highlight the need for additional funding or external investment to reach the first sales.  
Action 2. Business Model Validation. A two -step process will be used to validate the business model. Step 1) To 
ensure valuable feedback, careful stakeholder identification and recruitment is required. In collaboration with project 
partners, a targeted list based on stakeholder profiles will be drawn -up. The target list will ensure appropriate value 
chain coverage with an emphasis on the potential to become early innovation adopters. Stakeholders will be recruited 
through an initial contact, introduction of the value proposition and request for initial feedbac k, recruitment will 
draw on the stakeholder database developed for dissemination activities. Step 2) Feedback on the business model 
will be captured through interviews, questionnaires and, if relevant, visits to the experimental demonstration sites, 
depending on the targets’ profiles. The feedback analysis will lead to a review and update of the value proposition 
and revenue/cost models within the business models. 
Business Plan: The developed business models will be detailed in a business plan that will be the central part of the 
exploitation plan. Business Plan will be developed as a sustainability strategy outlining what should happen to the 
project outputs at the end of the project, and to explore how they can be sustained. The business plan will include 
client definition, a strategy for marketing, sales and operations financial models and go -to market strategies. 
New business opportunities will be assesed during the drafting of the different business models and the business 
plan. The new value chains (three at least) will be analysed from a business approach along with the real opportunities 
of penetration in the market from MWS as raw material. The impact on the actual industrial sectors where PERCAL 
is envisaged to be introduced (See Section 2.1) will be determined and compared to current industries practises. This 
activity, in alignment with the exploitation activities, will set the most approapiate route for the results: licensing, 
marketing/direct sales or vending. The analysis of the new business oppo rtunities will be performed taking into 
consideration these options for the consortium members as well as other biobased stakeholders which might be 
involved in the new value chains.

Task 8.5 Intellectual Property Management. Task leader: EXERGY with the support of all partners. 
This task will undertake a regular review and assessment of the consortiums ‘Freedom to Operate’ in the areas of 
exploitable outputs. Reviewing ‘Freedom to Operate’ throughout the project will ensure that the consortium is safe to 
produce a product or use a production technology without infringing or violating third party intellectual property rights 
or highlight the need for mitigating actions by the consortium (eg. acquisition of license, which would impact the 
exploitation). 
Furthermore, IPR management will include the mapping of existing patents and potentially overlapping IPR; and when 
the developing partner identifies possible novelty in a KER being developed, an assessment of the new knowledge 
generated during the project (outputs and other knowledge such as data); a proposition of optimal IPR protection options 
(e.g. patent, copyright, trademark, confidentiality), in line with the selected business model options (Task 8.4) and 
taking into account possible co -ownership; the prop er implementation of the IPR protection measures during the 
project. The findings and recommendations for IPR protections will be included in the final exploitation plan (D8.3). 
EXERGY will lead the IPR management by providing guidance to the partners for their FTO and patentability search 
but each partner will be responsible of the right management of his result. When necessary, after the initial identification 
of a promising (patentable) idea, external professional support from a patent agent will be soug ht.

chemistry, engineering, bioprocesses, biorefinery, etc.). The two workshops will be organised in M24 and M36, in two

through the arrangement of ‘short stay’ training courses designed for scientists/Technicians to take place at partner 
companies’ facilities; c) Technical Guidance Document: A ‘Guide to the production and use of green chemical 
intermediates using MSW as a main raw material’ will be prepared and published after completion of the project, in 
order to contribute to the technology transfer, dissemination and training. According to best industrial and 
environmental practices, all partners will co-operate in the elaboration of this Guide, which will contribute to an optimal 
profit of the new chemical products advantages. This guide will be developed by all Partners (D8.8) and will include 
the safety, regulatory, economic and environmental issues  identified in WP8, and will be made public in the second 
Project Workshop (M36); d) Publishing scientific articles in journals and conferences (subject to the consortium’s IP 
protection publication policy) and detailed in table 2.2.c. (section 2.2); e) Development of policy briefs and case studies 
targeting policy makers responsible for drafting EU or national legislation, regulations and guidelines affecting MSW

ANNEX I- PERSEO® BIOETHANOL Technology  
Bioethanol production. Following the pre-treatment, the PERSEO Bioethanol® Technology developed by IMECAL 
and CIEMAT for producing bioethanol will be demonstrated on a real-life environment with different organic fraction 
of municipal solid waste (OFMSW). The demonstration plant is equipped with all the reactors, fermenters, and 
separation and distillation units needed for transforming the OFMSW to bioethanol. The typical ethanol yields 
obtained vary from 100 – 130 liters of bioethanol per t onne of organic waste in dry bases . The bioethanol 
production involves several steps:   
A. The raw material is subjected to a pre-treatment which aims to eliminate the inserts contained in the organic 
matter (stones, plastics, metals, glass, etc.).

B. A thermochemical dilute acid hydrolysis of the raw material will be done in order to produce a solubilizing 
of the cellulose and hemicellulose contained in the MSW.   
C. After the feedstock is hydrolysed, a simultaneous saccharification and fermentation process is done. The 
cellulose saccharification and fermentation of glucose to ethanol is produced in the same fermenter, adding 
the enzymes, yeast and nutrients suitable for efficient conversion of sugars to ethanol.   
D. The fermentation sludge obtained is subjected to a distillation process in order to concentrate the ethanol 
produced in the fermentation of the organic fraction of MSW.   
E. Dehydration process is carried out of the mixture by passing through a molecular sieve to achieve 
dehydration required for ethanol (> 99.5%) as a final product.

In thermal process, bioethanol can be produced via gasification of the lignocellulosic feedstock at a high pressure and 
in absence of inert gases and further conversion of the syngas into bioet hanol. In the biochemical pathways, the 
lignocellulosic feedstock is firstly pre ‐treated in order to make the cellulosic component more accessible to the 
cellulases for the subsequent enzymatic saccharification step. During the pre ‐treatment a significant portion of 
hemicelluloses is converted into monosaccharides, mostly xylose (a C5 sugar). C6 sugars – mainly glucose is released 
after the enzymatic hydrolysis step and is then fermented with or without the C5 sugars depending on the design 
options.  Several technology options are under development including separate hydrolysis and fermentation (SHF) 
or simultaneous saccharification and co‐fermentation (SSCF) of C5 and C6 sugars with or without onsite production 
of the cellulases.  
The main challenges of the  biochemical route concern the integration of the system which requires a coordinated 
effort in improving each step to achieve an overall higher efficiency and a decreased production cost of bioethanol. 
In that sense, a particular attention should be paid to the feedstock cost. One of the strong assumptions in developing 
second generation bioethanol is the possibility to process low ‐cost feedstock. The concept of the process for 
enzymatic hydrolysis of cellulose and simultaneous fermentation, so -called Simu ltaneous Saccharification and 
Fermentation (SSF) is a promising technology. It was introduced by Gulf Oil Company, US and the University of  
Arkansas (Gauss et al. 1976; Huff et al. 1976) and various reports on bioethanol production have mentioned the 
superiority of ethanol yield and productivity using the SSF process 1,2.The sequence of steps for the SSF is virtually 
the same as for the separate process, except that saccharification and fermentation steps are combined in one vessel, 
however, the presence of  yeast or bacteria along with enzymes minimizes the sugar accumulation in the vessel, 
consequently higher rates, yields and concentrations of ethanol are possible using SSF at lower enzyme loading. 
Additionally, the presence of ethanol makes the mixture le ss vulnerable to contamination by unwanted 
microorganisms3,45.

With regards to the production of bioethanol from MSW, there are some examples of industrial and pilot plants but 
none of these projects has validated a whole process able to transform the Stabilized Organic Fraction (SOF) available 
in MSW into not only biofuels (bioethanol and biomethane) but also other value -added co -products such as 
biofertilisers. There are a few industrial plants for conversion of MSW to bioethanol. The first international waste to 
bioethanol demonstration plant was built by IMECAL – CIEMAT in 2007, based in the biotechnological process 
PERSEO® Bioethanol. The PERSEO project in Valencia (Spain) started to obtain bioethanol  from the organic 
fraction of the municipal solid waste. Another demonstration plant was inaugurated by Abengoa in 2013. Located 
in Spain, the organic matter is pre-treated to produce organic fibre, which is subsequently converted into bioethanol 
by using a fermentation and enzymatic hydrolysis of SOF from MSW. However, their strategy is not focused on the 
study of the most appropriate pre - treatment. Since pre -treatment has been viewed as one of the most expensive 
processing steps in cellulosic biomass-to-fermentable sugars conversion.   
In Sweden, St1 Biofuels Oy has completed a waste -to-ethanol project (Etanolix) which recycles feedstock such 
as biowaste and process residue from local bakeries and bread from shops that is past its sell-by date into ethanol for 
transport fuel. In Finland, there is another plant (Bionolix) that produces ethanol from biowaste from shops and 
households.   
Outside Europe, the first industrial-scale municipal solid waste to biofuel facility was opened in Canada in 2014 by 
Enerkem. Despite being the first industrial scale waste-to-biofuels facility of its kind to turn household garbage into 
biofuels and biochemical, their technology is based on gasification and subsequent catalytic synthesis, differing 
from the novel configuration of BIOWASTE where bio-ethanol will be produced more efficiently through alcoholic 
fermentation. Another example is Coskata a semi -Commercial Facility in Illinois (USA) which produces ethanol 
from both biomass feedstock and natural gas. In July 2014, ANDRI TZ received an order to supply services for 
Fiberight’s cellulosic ethanol plant in Iowa, USA. ANDRITZ Group´s biorefinery is focused on the transformation 
of municipal solid waste (MSW) and other organic materials into cellulosic ethanol as the primary product. Linked
