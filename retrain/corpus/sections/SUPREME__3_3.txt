Objectives  
The objective of WP1 is to improve the performance of the minerals processing industry, with a focus on atomisation and 
powder metallurgy. This will be achieved by improving the robustness of the process against constantly varying ore feed, 
optimizing plant throughput and energy consumption, valuable mineral recovery, and minimizing waste streams. 
Description of work

will be implemented Task 1.1. The objective of the task is to improve the performance of the grinding process and to introduce 
new mineral analysis measurement from the hydrocyclone overflow, which would also be beneficial for the subsequent flotation 
process in Task 1.2.

Mineral analysis research will be conducted with both slurries and dry material, in an existing OUTOTEC Courier 8 SL device. 
Analyser is currently used to measure elemental contents, but high resolution spectral data is not yet utilized to its full extent.

Task 1.2: Improved mineral flotation equipment with advanced control Leader: OUTOTEC/ Start: M12 / End: M24 
Task 1.2 focuses on the flotation step improvment. Mechanical improvements will be introduced in order to improve flotation

- new type of air injection mechanism at the bottom of the cell for improving air flux quality 
- rpm control for the flotation cells to dynamically control the flotation efficiency vs. material quantity 
An inovative and closed loop control system will be devloppeed and tested in SUPREME project: a new set of variables will be 
introduced for control, based on current and new sensors inputs (elemental assays, flow measurements, level measurements, 
froth characteristic measurements..). An algorithm using SoA “multivariable control techniques” will be implemented and 
demonstrated before M24.  
Since a very important parameter in flotation process control is the reliable slurry assays of various streams, it is obvious ly 
needed to get accurate information on them. Task 1.2 will also develop a new type of sampler that will comply with the strictest 
sampling standards, and will study new methods for accurate sampling error calculations. A sampler providing a representative 
cut from the large process pipe will be demonstrated in M30, with a target of fluid consumption reduction by at least 15%.

Task 2.2: Reduce energy and water consumption in water-atomization process. Leader:  GKN – Start: M1 / End: M36 
Water is considered as the most economic atomization process: compared to gas atomization, water is carrying more 
momentum in the process of breaking up the molten metal stream. Moreover, water is much more easily recycled then gas 
and currently, water recovery per kg of produced powder is more than 90% (remaining 10% are due to evaporation losses). 
Drawback of water atomizing is the higher quench rates which lead to more irregularly shaped particles that are not viewed as 
favourable for additive manufacturing processes.  
Objectives of Task 2.2 will mainly focus on energy/yield improvement of the water atomization process. GKN will develop water 
atomizing techniques to produce more spherical and better flowing powders by using lower water to metal ratios with atomizing 
jet design to improve the momentum transfer to the molten metal stream. Suitable quantification of flowability will be qualified 
on both 316L and 17 -4PH stainless ste els (e.g. shear force, dynamic angle of repose) and measurements will then be 
correlated to the processability in the SLM machine (Task 3.5). This will allow the finer powder sizes required by the additi ve 
manufacturing systems to be produced and also lead  to a more favourable yield of raw materials. By avoiding high gas 
consuming atomizing processes (utilizing water atomization) the environmental impact of the powder production process is 
significantly reduced: both energy consumption and raw material waste could decrease by 30%.  
Task 2.3: Increase yield of gas and water atomization processes. Leader:  ASL/ Participant:  GKN, CEA, IPC, RHP, 
RENISHAW, TWI – Start: M12 / End: M36 
It is known that for atomization, powder shape and sizes can be altered by util izing different atomizing parameters such as 
gas/water pressures, melt temperature, nozzle diameter, melt chemistry, etc. Conventional powder metallurgy processes 
(normal press and sintering process) uses more than 80% of the powder distribution (PSD).  However, additive manufacturing 
processes, and particularly SLM, utilize less than 30% of the normal distribution. By working in SUPREME with RENISHAW, 
IPC, CEA, RHP and TWI (partners involved in additive manufacturing), atomizing parameters and particle sizes will be varied 
to obtain the lowest possible cost powder to achieve the desired properties of the final product as well as still maintain enough 
build speed in the process.  
For the Task 2.3, GKN will produce (M12) 5 batches (minimum quantity 50 kg) of 316L and 17-4PH with different optimization 
parameters. ASL will produce (M12) also 5 batches (same quantity) of Inconel 625 material. These powders batches will be 
characterized (granulometry, composition, flowability) and tested in SLM by respectively IP C, CEA and Renishaw. Powder 
production yield towards manufacturing process will then be assessed, with a target of 10-15% raw material saving.  
Task 2.4: Optimized ball milling production efficiency. Leader:  MBN / Participant:  IRIS, TECNALIA – Start: M1 / End: 
M24 
Ball milling is an efficient way to produce powders, and it has a very low fluids consumption. However, optimization of the 
process is possible since it is currently difficult in ball millers to control dynamically the particle size output and p hases 
formation, and often, an excess of mixing energy is wasted. MBN will monitor the efficiency of the current process route to 
produce FeCuSn powder (suitable for MIM) which include synthesis, classification and comminution of coarser powder. The 
powder quality will be characterized and benchmarked against PSD, morphology, flowability, chemical composition and batch 
to batch homogeneity. IRIS will optimize the processing route to maximize the powder yield in the 5 -38µm by investigating 
process parameters (energy, time, temperature) and classifying conditions. 
MBN will develop an automated transporting system to improve the downstream and upstream handling of powders during 
processing by high energy ball milling, based on pneumatic approach which allows a fast discharge of the milling reactor. Such 
automated and continuous powder handling system will connect the milling plant with the classification device and include a 
micronization system that is disaggregating the coarser powder fraction (above 38 µm) for fully recovering all the powder and 
maximize the yield. Powder will be then conveyed automatically in a proper mixer to homogenise the powder and improve the 
batch to batch consistency.  
Both improvement developed in SUPREME project will allow increase process efficiency in the PSD by 30% to target approx. 
100% of powder usage rate. Material homogeneity (apparent density) will be improved as well by 10%, since it is a crucial 
parameter for the quality of the feedstock used in the MIM process (Task 4.2). 
TECNALIA will also build 10 to 20 sintered specimens of FeCuSn + diamond powder for mechanical characterisations 
(hardness, toughness, and modulus) and microstructure investigations in order to tune optimally the powder particle size 
distribution and homogeneity for best performances (M18).

Objectives  
WP3 objective is to increase the yield and the productivity of 3D manufacturing processes (Laser Metal Deposition, Plasma 
Metal Deposition, and Selective Laser Melting). Task 3.1 is targeting to increase SLM process efficiency by 50% by increasing

powder reuse, with a 30% raw material reduction objective. Same approach will be investigated for LMD and PMD processes, 
where in this case, 30-50% of the powder is wasted after printing and could be recollected.

significant cross-linked gains. As state d in WP2, water atomization process is less energy consuming than gas atomization

Task 3.2: LMD and PMD process yield optimisation. Leader:  RHP/ Participant:  TWI – Start: M1 / End: M36 
LMD and PMD processes have a powder efficiency in range 60-85%. This value depends on geometry, nozzle type, material 
and of course powder flow rate in the nozzle. For large components with a low volume ratio (i.e. casings), these processes are 
more attractive than SLM, and increasing productivity is needed (without degrading surface quality). Task 3.2 will focus on 
improving LMD and PMD deposition sp eed from 0.2 kg/hour to 1 kg/hour while maintaining the obvious advantages of near 
net shape (only finishing required), good surface finish (10-15 microns Ra) and low heat input (to minimise in process distortion). 
The demonstration of Task 3.2 will be carried out on nickel-based alloys and is divided in 3 sub-tasks: 
- Task 3.2.1: Process parameter optimisation for Hastelloy C22 (from ASL). A stable parameter set to allow a 5 -time 
productivity increase will be set for LMD and PMD. Investigation of the effects of laser power, scan speed, power-gas 
beam focus, different nozzles and different laser sources (CO 2, disc) will be made on 15 geometric samples and 30 
mechanical coupons. A design of experiments model will be built incorporating significant known parameters affecting 
the processes. A particular care will be taken on large parts to avoid distortion (>0,5 meter). 
- Task 3.2.2: Material Testing. Microstructure and geometrical accuracy of test samples will be investigated in order to 
validate the parameter sets  developed in task 3.2.1. In particular, crack formation/crack length, porosity, grain size 
and orientation, and binding defects will be investigated and evaluated. This will be supported by non -destructive 
evaluation of the deposited materials. Additional ly, mechanical testing will also be carried out to provide sufficient 
data for relevancy of application and benchmarked against end user requirements. This could include tensile and 
compression tests. 
- Task 3.2.3: Minimize gas consumption. An optimised gas guiding system during processing and reduction of 
overspray in PMD and LMD process will be demonstrated, by an optimisation of the plasma torch (RHP) and on the 
laser torch (TWI), to reduce by at least 30% the gas consumption.

Task 3.4: Energy saving by replacing gas -atomized by water -atomized powder for SLM process. Leader: IPC/ 
Participant: GKN, ASL - Start: M1 / End: M24 
Up to now, most of powders used in SLM process are manufactured by gas atomization, since this process de liver particles 
with a very spherical shape. However, as explained in Task 2.2, gas atomization is using huge energy (to heat and compress 
the gas), which is directly translated into the high price of the powder (100 €/kg for ferrous based material up to 2 50 €/kg for 
nickel based material). Moreover, gas atomization consumes high quantities of gas (N2 or Ar) which are not recovered. Water 
atomization process is up to 5 times cheaper and 10 times lower in energy consumption, but the powder with lower density 
and flowability due to non-spherical powder morphology are not yet validated for SLM process. The ambitious objective of Task 
3.4 is to realize different powder blends from water and gas atomized powders and to investigate the materials properties.  
- Batch 1 (50 kg): 100% gas atomized maraging steel (= reference) from ALS 
- Batch 2 (50 kg): 75% gas atomized maraging steel from ALS + 25% water atomized powder from GKN 
- Batch 3 (50 kg): 50% gas atomized maraging steel from ALS + 50% water atomized powder from GKN 
- Batch 4 (50 kg): 25% gas atomized maraging steel from ALS + 75% water atomized powder from GKN 
- Batch 5 (50 kg): 100% water atomized powder from GKN 
Parameters optimization will be done for theses 5 batches and for each batch 20 test samples (before and after post treatment) 
will be done by IPC. Benchmark parts (to define the build ability of the powder / SLM process parameters combination) will be 
done (minimum vertical wall thickness, minimum diameter / length ration for a cylinder, dimensional accuracy, maximum 
overhang without support, roughness depending on the surface part angle and maximum channel diameter for circular, angled 
and flat channels shapes). The output of Task 3.4 will allow to define the optimum ratio of water and gas atomized powder and 
thus, the level of energy savings (from 0% to 80%). 
Task 3.5: Optimized powder yield granulometr y. Leader: IPC/ Participant:  GKN, ATOMIZING SYSTEMS, CEA, RHP, 
TWI – Start: M12 / End: M36 
The powder specification for additive manufacturing is generally giving as 10-45µm for SLM, 40-100µm for LMD and 50-150 
µm for PMD, although there is no formal stan dard regarding this. Many works in the literature show that size distribution has 
strong influence on the material properties, although no specific rule is given yet. Strong productivity gains could be achieved 
by such an approach since it may indirectly i ncrease the yield by 10 to 30%, as related to Task 3.2. It is also known that a 
slight variation (moving mean PSD “left” or “right” side) in mean particle size distribution (PSD) value could disturb the optimum 
SLM process parameters, having an impact on the structural integrity of the manufactured components. A strong focus in this 
Task 3.4 will be given to the deep understanding of PSD (Particle Size Distribution) and additive manufacturing process outputs 
(parts quality, defects, mechanical properties) in order to be able to quantify the potential savings (M36). The task is divided in 
2 sub-tasks 
- Task 3.4.1: Optimum granulometry for SLM process. A comparative study between gas atomized powder and water 
atomized powder will be realized to quantify the diff erence in terms of powder properties (granulometry, grain 
morphology, flowability, chemical composition) and their effect on optimal SLM process parameters (laser power, 
scanning speed, hatching distance, layer thickness, stripes width and overlap). The demonstration will be carried out 
by IPC on maraging steel and by CEA on 316L (both atomized by GKN). For each material, 4 batches of 50kg with 
different PSD (directly from atomization or by sieving) will be treated in SLM machine, and 10 mechanical coupons 
per batch of powder with be build (meaning 80 coupons in total). 
- Task 3.432: Optimum granulometry for LMD and PMD processes. As for SLM, it is obvious that PSD will affect the 
LMD and PMD deposition quality, and for thin walled, flowability is an important  parameter (skew of fine/coarse 
particles within a distribution is critical). Same methodology will apply here, with Inconel 625 for LMD and Hastelloy 
C22 for PMD process. 
 
It is important to remark that once built, most additive manufacturing parts may require a degree of post -processing if is tis

and closed internal pores, residual stresses and anisotropic grain structures may occur and negatively influence the mechanical 
properties, especially the fatigue behavior. These issues will be addressed by suitable thermal post -processing techniques, 
such as stress relief annealing, HIP and/or heat treatment, performed by TWI on a dow n-selected samples (up to 10 in total)

WP6, led by IRIS, will address process control, a common need in previous WPs, which will allow optimizing all processes 
towards appropriate key parameters. Within this work package, control models will be developed for all cases and the control 
platform will be implemented. The sensor systems will be integrated in each case for process monitoring. Thereby, getting

Description of work  
Task 6.1: Process control system design. Leader: IRIS / Participant: OUTOTEC, RENISHAW, ASL, PRISMA – Start: M1 
/ End: M8 
Within this task, the design of the process control system will be made for each of the 4 case-study installations. The process 
control system will be composed of a set of sensors and a process control platform which will integrate the inputs from all 
process relevant information and produce recommendations or even closed loop control for improving the yield and critical 
process parameters. The following subtasks are planned:  
- Control model development and the sensor distribution design 
- Selection of optimal sensors for all case studies 
- Design of a common control platform for all case studies 
- Integration strategy planning 
An initial round of industrial requirements from the case-studies will be performed based on information from the previous Work 
Packages bu t also on direct interaction with the industry representatives. This will allow obtaining a thorough system 
specification leading to a solid design of the final solution.  
Task 6.2 Sensor system development. Leader: IRIS / Participant: OUTOTEC, RENISHAW, ASL, PRISMA – Start: M8 / 
End: M15 
Under this task the needed set of sensors will be sourced or developed depending on the needs of each case study. In as 
much as possible the components used will be taken from off -the-shelf solutions to ensure market repea tability and price 
competitiveness. Parameters to be monitored include: power/energy consumption (within all processes), gas usage (in gas 
atomization, and part (additive) manufacturing), material use rate (part (additive) manufacturing).  
Therefore, sensor systems will be developed for each parameter at each case as the following:  
- Monitoring Sensor for voltage and current supplied (power consumption monitoring) 
- Gas gauges and gas sensors for gas consumption 
- Optical sensors (level meters, material flow meters) for material usage. The specifications of the sensors depend on 
the monitor parameter desired for each case

metallurgy and in the above activities is key to EPMA’s success year on year. 
 
The EPMA has a long tradition of establishing networks, the oldest one is now over 25 years old. Th e EPMA is currently 
coordinating 5 networking groups, which gather research and industry partners in different areas of the Powder Metallurgy: 
Ferrous Structural Parts, Hard Materials, Metal Injection Moulding, Hot Isostatic Pressing, Metal Based Additive Manufacturing. 
The coordination includes organising closed and open meetings and following up all initiatives that are decided within the 
networks. 
 
Furthermore, the EPMA would establish a strong link between the project and the circa 200 corporate members in order to foster 
innovation and best practice exchange in all the Powder Metallurgy fields. Every year the EPMA organises the largest European 
PM Conference “EURO PM” (ca. 800 participants) along with a World PM Congress in Europe (ca. 2000 participants) every six 
years, EPMA also holds a series of annual Summer Schools and training courses designed for engineers and graduates who are 
working in the PM sector. Congresses and training events of this sort help foster academic and industry collaborations, as it helps 
to bring together horizontal and vertical industrial sectors to meet and discuss future projects and share best practises.
