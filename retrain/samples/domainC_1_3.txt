=== Domain C: hyperspectral imaging for automated plastic waste sorting in circular economy facilities ===
=== Section 1_3: Methodology and approach ===

1.3.1 Objectives

The overall objective of this research is to develop a novel hyperspectral imaging system for the automated sorting of post-consumer plastic waste from non-plastic waste in circular economy facilities. This system will integrate with existing waste sorting infrastructure to enhance the efficiency and accuracy of the sorting process.

To achieve this, we set the following specific objectives:

- To develop a hyperspectral imaging system for real-time acquisition of spectral data from post-consumer plastic waste.
- To design and develop algorithms for the accurate identification and classification of different types of plastics based on hyperspectral data.
- To integrate the hyperspectral imaging system with existing mechanical sorting mechanisms for automated sorting.
- To validate the performance of the developed system in a real circular economy facility.

1.3.2 Research questions

The research questions guiding this study are:

- How can hyperspectral imaging be optimized for the identification of post-consumer plastic waste in circular economy facilities?
- What algorithms are most effective for the classification of different types of plastics using hyperspectral data?
- How can the developed hyperspectral imaging system be seamlessly integrated with existing waste sorting infrastructure?
- What is the impact of the automated sorting system on the overall efficiency and accuracy of the waste sorting process in circular economy facilities?

1.3.3 State-of-the-art

The current state-of-the-art in waste sorting primarily relies on manual sorting and conventional optical sorting technologies, such as near-infrared (NIR) spectroscopy and X-ray fluorescence (XRF) spectroscopy. These technologies offer some level of automation but are limited in their ability to accurately identify and sort complex waste streams, particularly in post-consumer plastic waste.

Recent advancements in hyperspectral imaging technology have shown promise in addressing these limitations. Hyperspectral imaging captures spectral information across a wide range of wavelengths, providing detailed information about the material composition of objects. This technology has been successfully applied in various fields, including agriculture, food processing, and environmental monitoring, for material identification and classification.

In the context of waste sorting, hyperspectral imaging has started to gain attention for its potential to improve the accuracy and efficiency of sorting processes. Studies have demonstrated the capability of hyperspectral imaging to differentiate between various types of plastics, including those that are challenging to identify using conventional techniques. However, there is a lack of integrated systems that combine hyperspectral imaging with automated sorting mechanisms in circular economy facilities.

Our research aims to bridge this gap by developing a comprehensive hyperspectral imaging system specifically designed for the automated sorting of post-consumer plastic waste in circular economy facilities. By leveraging the strengths of hyperspectral imaging and integrating it with existing sorting infrastructure, we aim to enhance the efficiency and accuracy of the waste sorting process, thereby contributing to the advancement of circular economy practices.

1.3.4 Methodology and approach

Work conducted in this section will encompass:

- **Task 1.1:** Definition of user requirements and system specifications.
- **Task 1.2:** Development of a hyperspectral imaging system.
- **Task 1.3:** Design and development of classification algorithms for plastic waste.
- **Task 1.4:** Integration of the imaging system with mechanical sorting mechanisms.
- **Task 1.5:** Validation of the system in a real circular economy facility.

**Task 1.1: Definition of user requirements and system specifications**

Objective: To gather and analyze user requirements for the hyperspectral imaging system and define the system specifications accordingly.

Approach: We will conduct interviews and workshops with stakeholders, including facility operators, waste management experts, and technology providers, to understand the specific needs and constraints of circular economy facilities. Based on this information, we will define the technical specifications for the hyperspectral imaging system, including spectral range, resolution, acquisition speed, and integration requirements.

**Task 1.2: Development of a hyperspectral imaging system**

Objective: To design and develop a hyperspectral imaging system tailored for the identification of post-consumer plastic waste.

Approach: We will select appropriate hyperspectral sensors and optics based on the defined specifications. The system will be designed to capture spectral data in the relevant wavelength range for plastic identification. We will develop a data acquisition setup that ensures real-time imaging capabilities and is compatible with the operational environment of circular economy facilities.

**Task 1.3: Design and development of classification algorithms for plastic waste**

Objective: To develop algorithms capable of accurately identifying and classifying different types of plastics based on hyperspectral data.

Approach: We will collect a comprehensive dataset of hyperspectral images of various plastic materials. Using this dataset, we will design and train machine learning algorithms, such as support vector machines (SVM) and convolutional neural networks (CNN), to classify plastics based on their spectral signatures. The algorithms will be optimized for real-time processing and accuracy.
