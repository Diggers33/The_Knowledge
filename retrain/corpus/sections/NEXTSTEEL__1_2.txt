1.2 Methodology

Overall methodology. Underpinning concepts, Models & Assumptions
The NEXTSTEEL project aims to decarbonize the EU steel sector by combining three technological approaches that will be investigated over 5 strategically linked and integrated research and innovation blocks:
BLOCK 1. Produce high quality, biobased reducing agents for Low-CO2 iron and steelmaking: The research activity will focus on transforming biowaste streams into valuable products, maximizing the valorisation of carbon to produce biogenic reducing agents usable for the decarbonization of the steel sector. As inorganic nutrients like P, K, etc. are harmful for the steelmaking industry, the focus will be on hydrometallurgical processes that also allow the full recovery of the inorganic nutrients to make them usable in agriculture. The quality and usability of biogenic reducing agents will be tested in lab and pilot steelmaking furnaces
BLOCK 2. Improve circularity in steelmaking by reusing residual streams for new green steel processes: NEXTSTEEL will approach different circularity solutions aimed at using steelmaking residues as feedstock for the production of new biobased reducing agents. Particularly, slag obtained by Si-Mn-Fe furnaces (ERA, SINTEF) will be tested as a catalyst in the methane cracking unit (REC) with a view to obtaining a combined effect: produce hydrogen for DRI and obtain a C-deposition on the slag that can be used as a reducing agent in the Si-Mn production process. Moreover, slag from Scrap EAF (CELSA) will be sent to REC and TEC to be compacted in mixtures with bioanthracite to produce bioanthracite granules and briquettes with low dust and high density, adequate pH, and optimised for the use in EAF.
BLOCK 3. Increase the energy efficiency of steelmaking by maximizing the waste heat recovery and increasing the rate of bioenergy use: A specific energy assessment and modelling study will be performed. Particularly, the activity will focus on a future biobased steelmaking, where pyrolysis and hydrothermal treatment processes (HTT, SCWG) will be installed within iron and steelmaking sites, whereby the thermal energy required could be obtained by reusing the waste heat of DRI, EAF, Sintering and BFs. With this solution, the excess energy from pyrolysis and the process gases could be used directly for steelmaking, as well as for producing renewable bioelectricity. This research will be carried out under the leadership of POLI, taking energy data from both pilot tests performed (CEA, REC, AMMR), and the existing steelmaking processes run by steel industries involved in the consortium (CELSA, AMMR, ERA). All the data will be elaborated by modelling tools for identifying the different integrated energy systems, for determining the plants integration strategy, emission savings’ potential, & economic benefits of the proposed solution.
BLOCK 4. Digitalisation and Intelligent Process Control (HoloSteel): A dedicated digital intelligence layer (HoloSteel) will support the integration and stability of the bio-based steelmaking pathways. HoloSteel will provide real-time monitoring, process simulation, and decision support across multiple steelmaking routes. By combining Process Analytical Technologies (PAT), Quality by Design (QbD), and a modular agent-based digital twin architecture, HoloSteel will deliver system-wide predictability and adaptive control, especially under variable feedstock conditions. All digital assets will be FAIR-compliant, enabling structured data management, interoperability with existing plant systems, and integration with DIGITAL-61 actions. This digital layer will be critical for increasing the reliability, transparency, and scalability of low-CO₂ steelmaking operations.
BLOCK 5. Industry 5.0, Skills, and Business Case: To ensure deployment readiness and long-term sustainability, NEXTSTEEL will integrate Industry 5.0 principles into the technology scale-up process. This includes the co-creation of new operational roles and training materials to address the emerging skills gap in digital and green steelmaking environments (towards Operator 5.0). It will also build out a quantified business case and exploitation roadmap, including techno-economic analysis and lifecycle costing for the biobased reductants, biohydrogen, and circular integration strategies. This block will also include coordinate with European Social Fund Plus (ESF+) initiatives to support future upskilling programmes. It will also ensure alignment with European industrial and policy roadmaps (e.g. Clean Steel Partnership, Net-Zero Industry Act), ensuring that the innovations are positioned for investment-readiness and real-world adoption.
: the NEXTSTEEL process focuses on using biowaste streams (sludge. organic fraction of municipal waste, agri-food industry residues) to produce biobased reducing agents. These materials are abundant biogenic sources available and, as opposed to solid biomass, they have currently a very low or negative cost/value. However, the char obtained from these biowastes contains high amounts of inorganics, such as P, K, Mg etc, harmful for the steel sector. At the same time, these elements are of high value for agriculture. The main goal of this block is to demonstrate the technical feasibility of an integrated process capable of transforming the biogenic carbon contained in biowastes, producing bio-anthracite and H2-rich gas, ensuring the extraction and recovery of inorganic elements, such as phosphorus, magnesium, iron. The NEXTSTEEL solution consists of an integrated process (figure 1) as follows: a fast hydrothermal treatment (Fast HTT), at high heating rate and low residence time, to replace the drying process and transform biowaste into two materials: a volatile carbon-rich solid called hydrochar (55-60% of initial carbon), and a liquid phase (30-35% of the initial carbon in a soluble form). A chemical leaching step is performed on the hydrochar to extract >99% of the Ca, Mg and P, thereby reducing ash content in the solid. After leaching, the obtained low-ash hydrochar is upgraded by slow pyrolysis to obtain a low-volatile, carbon -rich high value bioanthracite. In the event of still high concentration of inorganics, such as Al, Fe, Si, slow pyrolysis will be performed with the use of alkali additives. The use of alkali additives solubilises the inorganic compounds present in the solid after pyrolysis which can be removed by a bio-anthracite washing step and recovered to produce chemicals (flocculating agents and P-rich fertiliser). On the liquid phases, SWCG and AD are performed to turn all the organic carbon contained in the aqueous phase into a H2 rich gas (called SCWG gas). Both these products- the gas and the bioanthracite- are usable as biogenic reducing agents in the DRI and EAF process routes, decarbonizing the steel sector. The extracted inorganics are recovered by precipitation and reused for other industrial purposes in a circular model. In addition to its role as a reducing agent in DRI and EAF processes, the H2 rich gas can serve as a clean alternative to natural gas as fuel in burners of reheating furnaces, thus contributing to a reduction in fossil fuel consumption. Finally, a novel green-hydrogen generation process is proposed, tested and developed by REC and POLI in the H2STEEL project. The process is based on using the bioanthracite as a catalyst in a new methane catalytic pyrolyser that transforms methane & biomethane from the AD process into green hydrogen. High quality C-enriched bioanthracite is obtained as a bio-product suitable for use in EAF and other steelmaking processes.
Methodology and approach in producing biogenic reducing agents at TRL 4. The process will be first optimized in laboratory at TRL4 in a research-industry partnership: the industry partners will provide the research teams with relevant information on the quality of biogenic reducing agents to be achieved, and the market requirements. This activity will be based, first on tests in small scale units of 300-2000 ml capacity for HTT and SCWG tests and on small scale pyrolysis and chemical leaching batch reactors (2 kg capacity). The tests will enable identification of the most promising feedstocks to be used at industrial scale and will also define the optimized process conditions to ensure quality, sustainability and the economic feasibility of the process. The proposed process will be tested on a variety of biowaste residues presenting different properties. This approach will contribute to achieving high quality of the final products, and to optimize the whole process in terms of economic and environmental sustainability (assisted by the HoloSteel modular digital intelligence layer). The development and optimization of the processes to produce biogenic reducing agents will be performed using pilot units available at REC & CEA that will enable 5 biowaste types to be processed under different process conditions for validating the processes and the quality of the products. These pilot units will enable the production of reliable quantities of green gas and bio-anthracite, which will then be processed and tested by TEC and SIN as reducing agents in steel and ironmaking processes.
Methodology and approach in producing biogenic reducing agents at TRL 4-6: One of the main obstacles for the validation of coal alternatives in steelmaking is the large quantities needed by steel industries in order to perform pilot tests in their furnaces; the pilot scale furnaces that will be used by AMMR, CELSA, ERA in NEXTSTEEL will require >100kg of bioanthracite for  not only demonstrating the quality at TRL6, but also for identifying any barrier with regard to handling, injection, and emission aspects. Methodology and approach testing Low-CO2 solutions for steelmaking: This core activity (in WP3) focuses on identifying optimal, sustainable solutions to decarbonise steelmaking by both recycling steel industry waste and testing biogenic reducing agents in DRI, EAF, and BF processes, as well as in alloys used for stainless steel production. Tests will begin at small scale in batch furnaces, then move to demo-scale at industrial partner sites. TEC will conduct trials at its 400m² CIRMETAL facility, equipped to support decarbonisation and metal circularity under various atmospheric conditions. Test equipment will include lab scale vacuum induction furnace and muffle furnaces, evaluating the performance of bioanthracite by replicating steelmaking and ironmaking processes. Testing and characterization equipment will also be employed, including Wavelength Dispersive X-ray Fluorescence (WDXRF) and optical emission spectrometer (OES) among others..
Products validation at TRL 6: The main products obtained through the integrated NEXTSTEEL process (bioanthracite and green gas rich in H2 and CH4) hold tremendous potential for decarbonising different processes in the metallurgy industry, which will be studied through four different target applications, as briefly explained below.
Application 1. Direct use of process gas and bioanthracite in Demo-electric and blast furnaces in the steel sector: The combined use of the produced gas (rich in H2 and CH4) and the bioanthracite as reducing agents in Electric and blast furnaces will be assessed. Industrial viability of gas injection and biochar use in Blast furnaces has already been proven to contribute to fossil coal consumption reduction. Bioanthracite tests at pilot scale will be performed by AMMR. For each test in lab-scale BF route will use 20 kg of bio-anthracite , including the use in sintering and BF. For the EAF tests, 800 kg of bioanthracite should be injected per test. The tests will enable the characterization of the process and the validation of the quality of the produced bioanthracite.
Application 2. Use of Supercritical water gasification (SCWG) gas as a biogenic reducing agent in DRI furnaces and bioanthracite as a carburizer in Electric furnaces: The most common DRI technology currently uses a mixture of CO and H2 produced from steam reforming of natural gas or from coal gasification. The NEXTSTEEL solution uses bio-syngas produced in the SCWG process. The H2-rich gas produced by SCWG will be tested to assess its potential use as a biogenic reducing agent in DRI furnaces, to produce Fe. Furthermore, the bioanthracite will also be studied as a carburizer and/or foaming agent, in electric furnaces where Fe is melted, and hot metal is produced. The solution could contribute to a fully defossilised hot metal production process, avoiding the use of fossil fuels. Pilot-scale metallurgical furnace with capacity of 100kg of molten metal with flexibility to carry out small scale trials of different materials and test model gas is available at TEC. A prototype combustion chamber is also available to examine the feasibility of using H2-rich gases as an alternative to natural gas in a pilot plant that replicates reheating furnace conditions, focusing on the impact of different combustion atmospheres on temperature distribution, exhaust gas composition, energy consumption and steel oxidation.
Application 3. Use of bioanthracite as reducing agent in submerged arc furnaces for SiMn production: Biogenic reducing agents are not only required for the steel sector, but also for all metallurgical processes in which mineral ores are reduced to metals to produce different alloys. Methodology for BLOCK 4- Digitalisation and Intelligent Process Control (HoloSteel): The transition to carbon-neutral steelmaking depends not only on the development of new production routes such as DRI, EAF, smelting reduction or electrolysis, but critically on the ability to manage the increased variability of input materials (scrap, low-grade ores, bio-based reductants) while still delivering high-quality steel at an industrial scale. The complexity of the metallurgy and the interdependency of upstream and downstream parameters require a new level of real-time control and system-level insight. The NextSteel digital team propose to address this need for precision, adaptability and reliability in future steelmaking systems by embedding QbD principles into the core of process development, supported by PAT technologies for real-time monitoring and control, and integrated with an AI-based decision support through a modular, holonic multi-agent system (MAS) Digital Twin that models the plant as a decentralised, intelligent system. The integrated digital system (HoloSteel) will deliver real-time adaptability, scenario-driven decision-making, and operational reliability in low-CO₂ steelmaking. Looking at each element of the system in turn:
■ QbD Framework: Building on the outputs of Blocks 1–3, we systematically map how variability in feedstock (e.g., biogenic reductants, scrap blends, low-grade ores) and process parameters (e.g., temperature, residence time, slag composition) affect key product quality attributes. This enables data-informed control strategies that enhance tolerance to alternative materials and ensure consistent mechanical and microstructural performance.
■ PAT-Driven Monitoring: The PAT layer will serve to detect key variations in raw material properties and intermediate products—composition, contaminants, morphology—and link these to metallurgical outcomes via multiscale data analysis. This will allow early prediction of deviations, quality risks, and process inefficiencies, creating a feedback loop for correction. To this end, advanced inline/online optical and spectroscopic tools provide real-time data on impurities, morphology, thermal behaviour, and compositional variation. These data streams—generated at key process nodes (reduction, slag formation, melting, off-gas)—are fed into HoloSteel for process fingerprinting, early deviation detection, and dynamic quality assurance. This layer also supports predictive KPIs for energy use, emissions, and residue valorisation potential (feeding into WP4 and LCA).
■ Holonic AI-based Multi Agent System Digital Twin Architecture: In this setup, the steelmaking process is structured using a holonic reference architecture, in with each major subsystem (e.g. feedstock intake, reduction, melting, slag handling, off-gas management, auxiliary units) is modelled as a functional holon with its own data profile, process logic, and interaction rules, forming a modular, interoperable unit within the broader system. This holonic approach mirrors the complexity of real operations and enables and enables local autonomy while supporting system-wide coordination. To activate and manage this holonic structure, a Multi-Agent System (MAS) layer is embedded within the digital twin. Each holon is paired with a dedicated AI-based agent responsible for interpreting real-time data, simulating scenarios under varying feedstock compositions and operating conditions, and supporting operators in making process adjustments that ensure both stability and quality. Through inter-agent communication, these agents enable dynamic negotiation, adaptation, and coordination across the system, ensuring both local optimisation (e.g. slag chemistry) and global performance (e.g. energy balance, emissions, quality consistency). It also supports the broader sustainability assessments in Block 3 by providing real-time material and energy flows, predictive performance data, and operational fingerprints for LCA/LCC simulations. Rather than relying on a monolithic simulation model, HoloSteel operates as a modular, decentralised digital ecosystem of functional holons. The agent-based layer empowers continuous scenario testing, predictive control, and intelligent adaptation under real-world constraints, enabling the adoption of alternative reducing agents and variable input streams across different production routes (BF-BOF, EAF, DRI). The architecture will adhere to FAIR principles  and will be designed for interoperability.
■ Decision Support Integration: Tailored dashboards and DSS tools support both operators and managers in strategic and operational decision-making, from adjusting feedstock blends to optimising energy efficiency and emissions profiles. These tools translate data into action and enable safe experimentation within the pilot plants. HoloSteel’s development is tightly coupled with the physical pilots, using TRL6 data to calibrate, validate, and continuously improve the models. Its modular design ensures scalability and reusability, laying the foundation for broader adoption across the steel sector and alignment with the DIGITAL-61 initiative and Industry 5.0 principles.
We will ensure that the AI developments comply with EU Artificial Intelligence Act, upholding the principles of transparency, accountability, and human oversight. Our AI systems will be: Lawful, in full compliance with applicable EU legislation; Ethical, following the Ethics Guidelines for Trustworthy AI, with attention to data privacy, transparency, fairness, and bias mitigation; Robust and reliable, through rigorous scenario testing, monitoring for unexpected behaviours, and incorporation of hybrid human-AI oversight where needed; Explainable, using interpretable models and providing context, limitations, and access to relevant training data to support user understanding and trust.
Methodology for BLOCK 5- Industry 5.0, Skills, and Business Case:
Industry 5.0 dimension of NextSteel for driving the low-carbon transformation of steelmaking:  This dimension of our methodology ensures that human, organisational, and market needs are fully embedded in the steelmaking decarbonisation pathway. Alongside technological innovation, the project addresses organisational, social, and business-level aspects need to be addressed in the innovation process. An expert team from SSH, process industry and business will work with steel industry decision-makers and their workforce to co-create operational and organisational strategies for the low-carbon transition, including optimised plant design and risk mitigation for using  heterogeneous, low-carbon feedstocks. Environmental, social, and governance (ESG) principles will be integrated into business governance to align innovations with corporate responsibility and sustainable industrial policy frameworks. Skills and capacity-building activities will prepare workers for new roles in digitally enabled, low-carbon operations. Scalable training materials will be developed and tested, potentially extended via the European Social Fund Plus. Decision-support tools will be created for both technical operators and business leaders to manage process variability, digital workflows, and investment planning.
Business Case Development and Exploitation Strategy Methodology: NextSteel explores 3 low-CO₂ steel production routes: (i) Electric Arc Furnace (EAF) and Blast Furnace (BF) with bioanthracite; (ii) hydrogen-based Direct Reduced Iron (DRI) with EAF; and (iii) silicomanganese (SiMn) production using C–SiO₂-rich bioanthracite. In parallel with the technology development, we will design a high-level exploitation strategy centred on the integrated biowaste valorisation chain, which combines Fast Hydrothermal Treatment (HTT), Supercritical Water Gasification (SCWG), and Methane Catalytic Pyrolysis (MCP) to produce high-quality bioanthracite and green hydrogen. These products will feed the 3 decarbonised steelmaking routes. Based on an adjudication and ranking of the low-carbon routes, we will prioritise the candidates for full Business Case development (but the goal is that all validated and viable routes are included). For example, we start with the DRI+EAF route, which offers strong energy integration potential, and alignment with ongoing investments in hydrogen-based steelmaking across Europe, such as HYBRIT, H2 Green Steel, and the IPCEI H2 value chains that signal a pan-European shift toward hydrogen DRI technologies. It enables the dual use of biogenic inputs (bioanthracite and green hydrogen), offers modular retrofitting potential within existing infrastructure, and represents a scalable and cost-effective decarbonisation pathway. Its relevance to both new and existing steelmaking sites makes it the most strategic starting point for demonstrating market viability. The additional process routes (BF/EAF, SiMn) could then be part of subsequent business cases, to support diversified market uptake, de-risk deployment, and respond to varying plant contexts across Europe.
The business case (presented in  Section 2.1 in a preliminary version) will be fully developed in Task 6.5 (Business Case and Exploitation Strategy development) to position the market and technological advantage of the prioritised DRI+EAF route, which will be supported by industrial validation and demonstration work with the steelmakers (WP3), LCA/TEA analysis (WP4) & alignment with current investment trends in green steel.  ICP, a proven expert in IP and R&D exploitation will lead this work following a two-tiered exploitation strategy: (i)  System-level, focusing on the business cases for the validated low-carbon steelmaking routes (as described above); and (ii) Component-level, targeting commercialisation of individual innovations and prioritised Key Exploitable Results (KERs), e.g. digital tools, processes, supporting that route (see Sec. 2.2). At both levels, the strategy ensures alignment with the needs of steelmakers, SMEs, and policymakers, identifying fit-for-purpose exploitation routes. ICP will apply its validated 4-phase commercialisation framework (Figure 2), adapted from Lean Start-up principles, to guide planning through iterative market validation. At system level, adoption pathways for low-carbon production routes will be mapped through detailed analysis of market dynamics and industry needs. At component level, KERs will be consolidated into strong commercial offerings for the steel sector. Subtask 6.5.1 will deliver an in-depth market analysis to support informed, partner-driven decisions and maximise post-project uptake.

How the methodology complies with the ‘do no significant harm’ principle: NextSteel directly supports the Green Steel transition by enabling the substitution of fossil-based reductants in iron & steelmaking. It fully complies with the ‘Do No Significant Harm’ (DNSH) principle under Taxonomy Regulation (EU) 2020/852 and contributes to climate change mitigation, pollution prevention & circular economy objectives. The project valorises underused biowaste streams (eg. sewage sludge, industrial digestate, food waste) to produce bioanthracite & biogenic syngas for BF, EAF & DRI routes, reducing CO₂ emissions without impacting biodiversity or land use, as no woody biomass or food crops are used. Its circular model extends the resource lifecycle of organic waste and avoids landfill or incineration, with carbon recovery of up to 70% via efficient HTC-based conversion. Through HoloSteel, the project embed PAT, QbD, and holonic digital twin technologies for real-time monitoring of emissions, material flows, and energy use. DNSH compliance is supported by ongoing LCA, carbon balance analysis, and exploration of carbon credit pathways, ensuring that all innovations contribute to net-zero goals without unintended environmental harm.

Gender dimension: Once seen as a ‘dirty industry’, steel is becoming increasingly digital and sustainable, and the transition to Clean Steel is an opportunity to increase female participation, in an industry whereby female representation is low, ranging from 3% to 29% (S&P Global, 2019) and 17% on average at board level. In NextSteel, we will adopt a 'gendered innovation’ approach (Task 7.2) applying sex, gender, and intersectional analysis to enhance innovation and discovery. Our communication and Dissemination, Exploitation & Communication outputs will incorporate any gender-relevant findings in the project’s developments and also ensure gender inclusive language and visuals. Our consortium team mobilises strong female leadership and diverse participation, and we will leverage the opportunity to showcasing the experiences of women in the project and contribute to Thought Leadership on gender in Steel and STEM to inspire wider inclusion in the sector. We will also highlight the potential impacts at the macro level: according to McKinsey if companies were to double the share of women in the tech workforce to ⁓45%, or an estimated 3.9 million additional women by 2027, GDP could get a boost of as much as €260 billion to €600 billion.
Open Science Practices: Open science practices (i.e., an approach based on open cooperative work and systematic sharing of knowledge and tools as early and widely as possible in the process) will be implemented as an integral part of our proposed methodology and for increasing our chances of delivering our objectives, as follows:
■Early and open sharing of research: All publications will be made in open access (OA) journals, with early sharing of publications (pre-prints) to satisfy OA requirements and fast knowledge sharing via deposit in repositories.
■OA to research data and other related research outputs: following the 'as open as possible, as closed as necessary' principle, and balanced with a robust strategy for ensuring that commercial exploitation, protection of personal data or confidentiality/security are not jeopardised.
■Research output management: use of lab books and effective (and digitized where possible) documentation procedures and effective packaging of research results.
■Measures to ensure reproducibility of research outputs: Mandatory technical standards will ensure that scientific information, publications, data and other outputs from our project, as well as the metadata about them, is available for re-use in the long term (see below), involving the use of persistent and unique identifiers, certified repositories compliant to European Open Science Cloud standards and FAIR principles.
■Involving all relevant knowledge actors including citizens, civil society and end users: we will raise awareness of the strategic importance of European steel production and the role of innovation, technology and the Circular BioEconomy in enabling a ‘Clean Steel’ transition, in an industry that is vital to manufacturing, construction, decarbonisation, and the creation of local jobs and sustainable growth. We will also create inspiring public content highlighting the role of women in steel, STEM and business, and showcasing female leadership in NextSteel.
