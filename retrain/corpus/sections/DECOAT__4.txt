1.1.  The project summary
Project Number 1 814505 Project Acronym 2 DECOAT
One form per project
General information
Project title 3 Recycling of coated and painted textile and plastic materials
Starting date 4 The first day of the month after the signature by the Agency
Duration in months 5 55
Call (part) identifier 6 H2020-NMBP-ST-IND-2018
Topic CE-NMBP-26-2018
Smart plastic materials with intrinsic recycling properties by design (RIA)
Fixed EC Keywords Materials engineering
Free keywords coated plastics, coated textiles, recycling, debonding-on-demand, automotive, household
electronics, outdoor gear
Abstract 7
The main goal of DECOAT is to enable circular use of textiles and plastic parts with (multilayer) ‘coatings’, which
are typically not recyclable yet. These ‘coatings’ comprise functional and performance coatings and paints as well
as adhesion layers. Therefore, novel triggerable smart polymer material systems and the corresponding recycling
processes will be developed. The triggerable solutions will be based on smart additives (like microcapsules or
microwave triggered additives) for the ‘coating’ formulations that will be activated by a specific trigger (heat,
humidity, microwave, chemical).
A continuous recycling pilot plant will demonstrate the novel DECOAT principle that allows upgrading existing
mechanical recycling by adding tools for sorting by and activation of the trigger. The optimal use of the Creasolv®
process for recycling of coated parts will be assessed. The focus is on recycling of the bulk material, but re-use of the
coatings materials themselves will also be tackled. Using these recycling processes, circular use of demo cases for
outdoor gear, household electronics and automotive parts will be validated.
The novel triggerable DECOAT technologies will create new markets for additives, coatings, paints and adhesives
fulfilling the recycling need. The concepts will support designers and product developers for making ‘recyclable-by-
design’ products. This will create direct business opportunities for the DECOAT partners and serve as examples for
promoting DECOAT solutions to the wider stakeholder community.
The targeted products (parts) are coated plastic from cars, electrical and electronic equipment and coated textiles
which produce annually almost 3.5 million tons waste. DECOAT will lead to a decrease in landfilling of ca. 75% and a
reduction in the carbon footprint by at least 30% for these products. By enabling their recycling, DECOAT is expected
to generate on medium term a novel market of over 150 million in Europe (or ca 500 jobs).

1.3.3. WT3 Work package descriptions
Work package number 9 WP1 Lead beneficiary 10 5 - NTUA
Work package title Triggerable materials for coatings
Start month 1 End month 46
Objectives
The main objective in this work package is to develop triggerable materials for debonding on demand.
• Specification and requirements of end user cases on materials
• Synthesis of microcapsules, nanoparticles and smart hybrid materials, with triggerable response to heat, steam and
irradiation, for weakening the adhesive strength and facilitating layer separation
• Thermo-responsive core-shell nanostructured co-polymers
• Synthesis of SAP based microcapsules (water or solvent swelling). Synthesis of novel multi-structured SAPs
microcapsules with magnetic nanoparticles to react under MW irradiation.
• Development of microwave assisted materials (hybrid CNT structures with SiO2 and/or metal nanoparticles)
• Characterization of developed triggerable materials
Description of work and role of partners
WP1 - Triggerable materials for coatings [Months: 1-46]
NTUA, CENTEXBEL, CTAG, VAUDE, Panasonic ES, MBT, MAIER, RESCOLL, C-TECH, DEVAN, IRES
Within this WP, all triggerable nano- and micro-materials will be developed and characterised, in order to work upon
specific stimulus (heat, steam, mechanical, microwave, chemical).
Task 1.1 – Specifications and requirements for end-use cases - Definition of testing procedures - CTB, NTUA, CTAG,
VAUDE, PANASONIC, MAIER, DAIMLER (M1-M5) - 15%.
Within WP1, we will work towards materials that provide a generic solution for de-coating, ie solutions generally valid
for substrate with a coating of for example PVC, acrylate or TPU. All polymer matrices used within the project will
be defined. However, to focus the project efforts, we will concentrate on the challenges posed by the use cases that are
selected by the end-users, see section 2.1.2. Here, we list these parts as well as their basic functional requirements. The
goal is to further specify these requirements within this task so that a clear picture emerges of the core needs (must haves),
of the options (nice to have) and of the boundary conditions. Moreover, the requirements during operational conditions
will be considered, in order to choose the appropriate materials, which will be developed in the following tasks.
For example, MAIER started to determine the technical requirements of the final demonstrator parts to be developed in
the project, see table below. At the onset of DECOAT, precise specifications will be defined for the interior and exterior
part, taking as a reference the standards of automotive OEMs (PSA, Toyota, etc). The main characterizations defined
by MAIER will be directed to the fulfilment of the specifications especially related to high gloss aesthetic, abrasion and
scratch resistances of its products determined by the principal OEMs, which are the final users of the products.
Task 1.2 – Synthesis of microcapsules and nanoparticles with triggerable response to heat and steam for weakening the
adhesive strength and facilitating layer separation - NTUA, CTB, DEVAN, RESCOLL, CTECH (M4-M25) - 40%.
In this task, different nano and microstructured materials are proposed for multiple functionalities to coatings. One
important point that will be considered from the beginning is the compatibility between the nanomaterials and coating
formulations, that will be tested in WP2. The goal is to make during the first phase of the project, adequate quantities
of the materials (ca 100g/each), that will be made available to WP2 (coating formulations development & testing) and
to WP3 for detection.
Subtask 1.2.1 – Thermo-responsive core-shell nanostructured co-polymers
Microstructure particles with a melamine formaldehyde, polyurea or acrylate-based shell comprising triggerable
response active core ingredients will be developed within this sub-task by DEVAN. The active ingredients will be chosen
on the basis that upon heat treatment, they will expand and break the polymeric microcapsules walls and will be released
into the coatings, weakening the adhesive strength and facilitate layer separation. In this WP, DEVAN will make use
of its know-how (see also section 1.4) to enhance the compatibility of nanomaterials with the coating formulations. At
this point, both the chemical linkage between the nanomaterials and coating formulations will be addressed, but also the
particle size distribution of the particles in the coating formulations: homogeneous particles have a less negative impact
in the coating characteristics, such as gloss loss and smoothness, which will be tested within WP2.
Page 11 of 52
In this sub-task, different hybrid core-shell nanostructured co-polymers will be also synthesized by NTUA. Organic
polymers with glass temperature (Tg) in the range of 20oC to 40oC such as poly(propyl methacrylate) and poly(butyl
methacrylate) will form the core, in order to act as softening points in the primers of RESCOLL which will be
incorporated. To avoid this function during everyday operation, a protective shell will be applied around the core. The
shell will consist of copolymers such as poly(4-vinylpyridine-co-methacrylic acid) due to the fact that these polymers
demonstrate higher Tg (151oC and 220oC, respectively) than the processing and operational temperature of the products,
as well as the debonding temperature that will be studied in WP2. Furthermore, the presence of methacrylic acid
structure units in the copolymer shell will impart hydrophilic properties to the nanostructured co-polymers rendering
them dispersible in water-based solutions (waterborne formulations). Moreover, the polymers that constitute the shell
will be cross-linked using an appropriate compound such as ethylene-glycol-bismethacrylate. Furthermore, different
dyes which will be investigated and selected, will be used as the initiator for the aforementioned radical polymerizations
in order the final product to demonstrate absorption under visible irradiation (in-situ polymerisation with the selected
dye, in order to enable smart optical sorting in WP3). Finally, iron oxides will be incorporated into the shell that will
act as magnetic beads into the produced core-shell nanostructured co-polymers, which will be under 100 nm. The aim
of proposed synthesis is the fabrication of multifunctional materials with smart intrinsic sorting properties that will be
able to increase their functionality in the presence of a possible magnetic field, due to the presence of inductive heat,
improving its debonding properties.
Subtask 1.2.2 –Super Absorbent Polymers (SAP)
One of the strategies to obtain coatings with debonding properties is to incorporate Super Absorbent Polymers (SAP).
Cross-linked cores will be fabricated via radical polymerization. Furthermore, organic dyes will be also used in the
structure of the SAPs, to enable the optical sorting in WP3. Different kinds of agents can be also incorporated within the
SAPs for additional functionalities, to form hybrid materials that will react onto stimuli such as microwave irradiation.
End-of-life separation can be achieved by exposure to heat sources (e.g. IR radiation), which leads to the melting of
the microcapsule shell, along with activation of the inner superabsorbent core by steam (moisture), in the case of water
swelling. The SAPs will also be used in combination with the Creasolv technique (WP2) where they will swell under
selective solvents to enhance the debonding functionality.
Subtask 1.2.3 – Microwave & Rf assisted materials
NTUA will investigate the synthesis of hybrid microwave and Rf assisted materials, based on metal nanoparticles,
silicon carbides and multiwalled carbon nanotubes (MWCNTs). Emphasis will be given to the MWCNTs, the growth of
which will be achieved through the Catalytic Thermal Chemical Vapor Deposition (CT-CVD) method. The MWCNTs
will be functionalised with metal nanoparticles or will be grown on SiO2 nanoparticles. They will be incorporated in
the coating in a concentration lower than 1% wt. The aim will be to provide selective heating of the primer layer and
remove the coating, or to melt the coating itself and remove the coating layer. Heating effect could be tuned by changing
% of additive or to add MW transparent materials such as silica or alumina to disperse the particles and moderate the
heating effect.
In addition, in order to enhance the selective heating and melting properties of the adhesive, enhancement of the
efficiency of induction to microwave will be examined adding nano-sized metal particles with high dielectric constant,
e.g. zinc oxide. Zinc oxide has high dielectric constant that could shorten the microwave treatment time needed for
debonding because of the selective heating of the adhesive in a shorter time by enhancing the heating efficiency of the
adhesive by the irradiation of microwave.
Task 1.3 – Triggerable primers for interfacial debonding – RESCOLL (M4-M25) - 10%.
RESCOLL will develop debonding primers as described in patent EP1611217. These primers will be based on the
combination of degradation agents (blowing agents) and binders, selected to fit with mechanical properties and thermal
constraints of the different use cases. Thermal triggering of the primers (in the range of 100 to 200°C) will allow to
cleanly separate the parts at the location of the primers. The nanoparticles and microcapsules developed in Task 1.2
will be incorporated in the primer formulations. The formulated primers developed in WP1 will be used as a baseline
for further adjustments in WP2.
Task 1.4 – Selection and up-scaling of the most promising process - DEVAN, NTUA (M13-M46) - 25%.
The most promising core/shell microcapsules that will be developed in the Task 1.2 will be selected for further up-
scaled production. DEVAN will undertake the up-scaling, to provide enough materials for the demonstration activities
to the rest WPs. The goal is to provide the amounts required for further testing in WP2, WP3 and especially WP4 and
WP5. Therefore, DEVAN will upscale the most promising prototypes in its pilot plant micro-encapsulation reactors
with sizes up to five kilograms. DEVAN will check and prepare guidelines to scale up the production of the successful
microcapsules prototypes to ton scale.
Task 1.5 – Characterization of developed triggerable materials - NTUA, (M7-M46) - 10%.

The aforementioned produced materials (Task 1.2-1.4) will be characterized with several methods in terms of their
morphology, structure and composition. The average nanocontainer size and their morphology will be determined
by Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) and Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM). The coated and hollow
nanocontainers will be characterised by FT-Infrared Spectroscopy (FT-IR), Thermogravimetric Analysis (TGA),
Differential Thermal Analysis (DTA), energy dispersive x-ray analysis (EDX), dynamic light scattering (DLS) and X-
Ray Diffraction (XRD). The magnetic particles to be used in thermoplastic matrix will be characterized in this task by
means of advanced microscopic techniques.

WP2 - Development of coating debonding systems [Months: 1-46]
CENTEXBEL, AIMPLAS, Fraunhofer, CTAG, NTUA, VAUDE, Panasonic ES, MBT, MAIER, RESCOLL, C-

Description of work
Task 2.1: Coating formulations, basic coating tests and characterisation - NTUA, CTB, AIMPLAS, CTAG, IVV,
RESCOLL, DEVAN (M1-M13) - 10%.
The triggerable materials developed in WP1 will be added to polymeric coating formulations used in textiles (TPU,
PVC, acrylates) and in plastics (TPU, PVC, epoxy). The goal is to first obtain coating formulations with a relatively high
loading of the triggerable additives and to maintain the required properties of the coating formulation, typically viscosity,
wettability, foaming properties. Next, also combinations of different triggers into a single coating will be tested.
The main challenge will be to obtain coating formulations with a sufficient loading of the additives, that are stable in
time, i.e. the occurrence of agglomeration or sedimentation should be monitored and prevented.
Next in this task, simple flat A4-samples will be produced on PES textiles and on PC and ABS plastic plates. The coating
process will be investigated, e.g. the influence of the triggerable additives on drying time and wettability.
At this stage, a single layer of coating will be produced in one thickness. These coated A4-samples will be used for
physico-chemical and mechanical characterisation. This includes chemical and elemental composition will be studied
by FT-Infrared Spectroscopy (FT-IR) and Energy Dispersive X-Ray Analysis (EDX), respectively. The morphology of
coatings will be studied with optical microscopy and SEM and AFM measurements (to complement the coating surface
characterization at nanoscale). The dispersion of the nanoparticles and microcapsules in the final solid state coatings
will be evaluated with microCT measurements. Also other relevant (nano)mechanical properties will be evaluated, e.g.
(nano-)scratch tests, microhardness, nanoindentation, roughness.
The produced coated materials will also be used for defining the protocol for measuring the debonding effect, see next
task.
Task 2.2: Development of DECOAT protocol for assessment of debonding - CTB, RESCOLL, IVV, CTAG, AIMPLAS,
NTUA, MAIER, MBT (M4-M25) - 25%.
The goal of this task is to define a protocol to determine the debonding or separation capacity of the coatings with
triggerable additives. Although it might seem fairly easy, this is a crucial step for the project. It will help to assess the
efficiency of the different options, how they can be compared and optimised.
This task will start as soon as first results of 2.1 are available, because first triggerable coated substrates are needed
to debond and to evaluate. We will start from the existing standardised test ASTM D3330 for 180° detachment (peel
strength test method of adhesive). Goal is to have an objective measurement method to be able to determine if the
separation is sufficient or not. We will define parameters like the residual bond strength, the maximum amount of
remaining coating allowed for each material/ end use case in order to be able to recycle the substrate material in a high-
tech application (circular use). We will also develop basic evaluation for measuring the quality of the substrate material
after the debonding process and for the ability for rebonding/recoating of substrates.
The outcome of this task will be the ‘DECOAT protocol’ for measurement of the debonding.
Task 2.3: Optimisation of the coating debonding system and assessment of the separation capacity – CTB, AIMPLAS,
IVV, NTUA, CTECH, RESCOLL, MAIER (M8-M31) - 30%.
Here, we will build on the experience gained in Task 2.1 on the actual coating process. Indeed, we will further tune,
still using A4-scale flat substrates, the concentration of trigger additive, in order to determine the lowest concentration
Page 16 of 52
needed for an effective separation. The additives will be tested via addition into a primer layer (with a trigger-less top
layer) and added in a single layer coating. The influence of the application method for these layers will be studied,
e.g. via direct coating or transfer coating in case of knife coating on textiles) as this results in a different degree of
penetration into the substrate. The latter is expected to have a large influence on debonding effect. So, the difference
between these coating techniques will be assessed.
To structure these activities, one can in principle opt for grouping via the triggerable additives or via the external trigger
applied. Here, we opt to describe as close as possible to how the practical work will be organised, which will be a
combination of both:
• To understand the debonding mechanism of the thermally triggered microcapsules their behaviour will be investigated
by looking at the volume expansion of thermally expansive microcapsules by the heating trigger. The shape and size of
the particle before and after the heating will be observed using SEM.
• For the coatings triggered by MW, the main activity will be to develop MW processes to ensure sufficient uniformity
within the cavity. Therefore, several process parameters will have to be tuned: power, pulse duration, cavity uniformity
of process,… The main challenge will be to determine the energy input needed for good debonding while ensuring none
of the materials suffers deterioration, e.g. because of charring. MW will be applied to standard susceptors, the novel
carbon-based susceptors and SAPs functionalised for MW interaction.
• For the blowing agent based adhesives and primers (INDAR technology) we will prepare bonded/coated samples for
comparative assessment of initial mechanical performance vs reference material configurations (i.e. not debondable)
and residual mechanical performance after triggering (in the debonded state). Mechanical testing will be based on
international standards, depending on substrates and applications (peel, lap shear, pull out, scratch test for paints).
Thermal ageing resistance of the debonding formulations will be tested to ensure that no anticipated loss of mechanical
strength or debonding capability occurs. The blowing agents will also be combined with MW triggering and with
microcapsule addition to check reinforcing / synergistic effects.
• The chemical triggering (the Creasolv process) is based on a difference in dissolution of the coating and substrate
materials. Different types of coating-substrate combinations will be shredded and fed to the process. This will be done
for standard coatings and for coatings with SAPs functionalised to interact with specific chemical solvents. Further, we
will investigate the influence of dense versus foamed coatings and the type of coating-substrate interface (from very
flat towards high roughness). For each material the specific process conditions need to be defined like type of solvents,
duration of treatment and temperature. Furthermore, specificity of the chosen solvent and dissolution conditions will be
double-checked with respect to substrate polymers and other plastics types that may be present in real waste streams.
After solvent based debonding the purity of the obtained fractions will be evaluated by means of e.g. microscopy, IR
and DSC.
The separation capacity of the coating debonding systems based on above mentioned various combinations of triggerable
additives and external triggers will be assessed according to the ‘DECOAT protocol’ (see Task 2.2). Depending on
the additive, the suitable debonding triggers will be evaluated: thermal (oven, IR, steam), MW and chemical. For each
additive it needs to be checked whether the suited debonding process actually leads to a separation of the coating layer
and the substrate. It is expected that this testing will lead to a further refinement of our testing protocol, so this task
will run in strong interaction with Task 2.2.
The measurement protocol will also reveal, after the debonding step, how much residual coating will still be present on
the textile or plastic. The ‘cleanliness’ of the substrate will be evaluated via microscopy or IR. If a considerable amount
of impurities is still present, an additional step in the Creasolv process can be considered to dissolve these impurities,
see also Task 4.3.
Task 2.4: Broadening the application field of the coating debonding system - RESCOLL, DEVAN, CTB, CTAG,
MAIER, MBT, PANASONIC, VAUDE (M19-M41) - 25%.
Up to here all research will be performed on flat substrates of specific chemistry (PES, PC, ABS). Here we extend: in real
life coated items are not flat, but 3D objects (especially for plastics). The coating technique will need to be adapted to
be able to coat 3D objects. Further, the triggerable coatings will also be applied onto standardly used substrate materials
like PA for textile and PUR, PE and PP for plastics. The geometry and chemistry will have an influence on the separation
capacity, so the most promising combinations of triggerable material and external trigger will be here assessed again.
In real life coatings are having a functional performance (see T1.1), which cannot be compromised by the addition of the
triggers. The developed coating will therefore be assessed for these properties like waterproofness, abrasion resistance,
weldability (typical for textile substrates) and scratch resistance, gloss and anti-corrosion (typical for plastic substrates).
The outcome of this task will be a portfolio of coating debonding solutions. A solution in the portfolio will be specified
by the triggerable material, the external trigger, the coating matrix, the substrate material and geometry, the need for a
primer layer or not, and an evaluation of separation capacity (energy and time needed, cleanliness of substrate).

Task 2.5: Tailoring of most promised debonding systems for further processing in WP3, WP4 and WP5 - DEVAN,
AIMPLAS, CTB, RESCOLL, NTUA, IVV (M19-M46) - 10%.

other WP2 tasks are finished.
Within tasks 2.2, 2.3 and 2.4, a whole range of materials (combinations) will be developed. Key element will thus be the
selection and development of solutions ideally suited for the subsequent steps of sorting, (large scale) debonding, actual
recycling and re-use of the materials. A selection of the most interesting solutions will be made based on mechanical
properties, processability and performance in the final coated parts (tests will be done in the framework of WP5). In
addition, focus will be given to the tailoring of the coating properties facilitating their further use for the specific end-
user parts. A close interaction with WP3, WP4 and WP5 will be established to allow constant feedback.

- To monitor the processing related to the trigger: synthesis of triggerable materials, coating and debonding step,

- To develop the monitoring approach for the recycling pilot line
- To design an optical sorting and control system to detect and single out the triggerable parts from a waste stream
Description of work and role of partners
WP3 - Optical monitoring of debondable materials, processing, sorting and recycling [Months: 5-46]
IRIS, Fraunhofer, NTUA, RESCOLL, C-TECH, OSM
Description of work
Task 3.1 Monitoring of the microcapsule production and coating and debonding processes - IVV, IRIS, NTUA,
RESCOLL (M5-M22) - 35%.
Within this task, we will develop the optical methods that will help to control the crucial processes of DECOAT:
microcapsules production (T3.1.1), coating process (T3.1.2) and the debonding process (T3.1.3).
Throughout this task, samples will be provided by WP1 and WP2, together with reference measurements results, to
calibrate the planned photonic techniques.
Subtask 3.1.1. Microcapsules monitoring via selected dyes
In line with WP1, IRIS and IVV will work with NTUA to define a range of dyes to be used in the core or shell of the
microcapsules. Those will be selected in terms of the wavelength they emit, the detection threshold, the cost and the
compatibility with the coating matrix and substrate material.
Those dyes will be added during the synthesis process. NIR and measurements based on fluorescence dyes (referred to as
POLO technique) will be tested to monitor the thickness of the shell (via the ratio with the core and size measurement),
but also of the crosslinking ratio (as allowed by specific dyes selected for POLO system), where applicable. Indeed both
parameters are determining the microcapsule response to the respective stimuli for debonding.
Subtask 3.1.2. Primer and nano/micro-composite coating monitoring
A method based on hyperspectral imaging (HSI) will be developed by IRIS to monitor the thickness and distribution
of primer on the substrate where it is applied in case of INDAR modified materials to ensure that the proper debonding
can then take place.
In terms of coatings containing either microcapsules or metallic nanoparticles, a dispersion monitoring system will be
set up based on respectively fluorescence spectroscopy imaging or diffractometry.
Subtask 3.1.3. Debonding process monitoring
The key step for DECOAT is the actual debonding. Within Task 1.1 we will define the requirements for ‘purity’, in Task
2.2.2 we will work out a protocol for determining, off-line, the debonding capacity. Here, we will develop the optical
method to monitor the debonding process online.
Samples with various degrees of removed coatings will be provided to assess the capability to quantify residual coating
material. This is needed as such contaminations caused by residual coating material can affect the further reprocessing.
Hence, the optical monitoring can provide the necessary quality control. This will be based on POLO for materials
containing the fluorescent dyes or on HSI.
Task 3.2 Selection of the identification techniques for triggerable materials - IRIS, IVV, RESCOLL, DEVAN, NTUA
(M10-M35) - 20%.
This task deals with the identification in a waste flow of the parts that include the triggered materials developed in the
project. Test will begin with single-point measurement before shifting to imaging solutions that can deal with macro-
scale objects. It is relevant to automatized waste sorting facilities but also as the triggered materials may only be locally
discernible in section views (e.g. in case of primer/adhesives as opposed to coatings, especially for bonded parts, level
1 disassembly) or in case a spatial distribution of thickness/residual coating is needed. Tests will initially start with
Page 21 of 52
monomaterials of triggered materials and then continue with coated substrates (case that will be validated in WP4-5)
and bonded materials.
For the microcapsules dispersed in a coating or adhesive, we will rely on detecting the dyes that were selected in the
previous task independently of the matrix polymer to which they are added. Related inputs from IVV obtained during
task 3.1 will be provided as a basis. Fluorescence spectroscopy coupled with an HSI camera will be used as most
versatile technique but alternative techniques could be considered in case of insufficient resolution or for post project
exploitation in more specific cases to lower the investment such as: (i) Fluorescence spectroscopy single point in tandem
with a conventional (unfiltered) machine vision camera; (ii) Filters and grey-scale camera with a set of narrow band-
pass filters in tandem with a conventional (unfiltered) machine vision camera; (iii) RGB camera (only if dyes emit in
the Visible range and can be distinguished).
For the INDAR primer or MW triggerable materials nanocomposites, we will respectively use NIR based technique
and fluorescence spectroscopy (optionally X ray or simple fluorescence in case also fluorescence dyes are grafted to
the particles).
The techniques will be calibrated with a relevant set of samples from WP2. For each case where several options are
possible, the most suitable one will be selected depending on cost, detection level, versatility (e.g. to detect several of
our triggers), etc.
Task 3.3 Sorting prototype concept and prototyping - IRIS, IVV (M20-M46) - 35%.
Subtask 3.3.1. Design and building of the hardware systems
Based on the results of the offline trials performed in the previous task and in interaction with the design of the pilot line
from WP4 (Task 4.2), the architecture of the system hardware will be conceptualized. The needs (and linked suitable
positioning) for the inspection systems will be defined. In principle we need the initial detection and post coating removal
verification. That will be the basic hardware system. Two variations will also be considered: the option without the
monitoring of the removal and the option to identify triggerable parts before the assembly/grinding.
The optical systems, as well as the enclosure and other optomechanical parts will be designed using mechanical CAD
tools (e.g. SolidWorks) with inputs from ray-tracing software (e.g. ZEMAX) or Maxwell solvers when appropriate. The
components (light source, beam shaping and imaging optics, detector and data acquisition hardware) will be selected
from commercially available options whenever possible and be customised when necessary.
Subtask 3.3.2 Software development
The software is needed for driving the instruments, processing the data by means of predictive models and delivering
the results through the user interface and towards the pilot line operators. Hardware drivers and the raw data acquisition
layer will be set up for the core device used in the previous subtask. The right modelling package will be embedded. The
initial software version, which will include the required mathematical treatment of the images/spectra, will be delivered
for the lab test rig for each of the triggered materials type.
The final version of the software, adapted for semi-continuous sorting, will be built upon the initial version while
incorporating additional functions required by the recycling pilot line (WP4) operator. In particular, the software will
integrate in a single front end the software modules required for each of the core devices. A simple user friendly
Graphical User Interface will be set up by IRIS with guidance from AIMPLAS and the recyclers in the Advisory and
Support Group.
Subtask 3.3.3 Hard- and software validation
Using the hardware and software from T3.3.1 and T3.3.2 respectively, the imaging capabilities will be validated before
transferring and integrating the system into the pilot line. The hardware, software and calibration models will be adjusted
as needed. A user manual including operational guidelines will be delivered. Factory Acceptance Test (FAT) procedure
will be implemented following ISO 9001:2008 processes.
Task 3.4 Guidelines for use of optical techniques for monitoring and quality control in coatings and textiles and plastics
processing – OSM, IRIS, IVV (M29-M46) - 10%.
Within tasks T3.1, T3.2 and T3.3 several techniques will be tested and applied to plastics and textiles processing. Indeed,
we will develop solutions for the process control of the microcapsules production, of coating thickness and uniformity,
of the debonding. We will also determine the level of contamination (from the left-over coating) on plastics and textiles
as well as the presence of specific markers within waste streams.
These results will be used to provide guidelines for required process monitoring systems to be implemented in line
during the post project upscaling phase to support constant production quality. However, these results will be also be
useful beyond the applications foreseen within DECOAT. Therefore, we will also compose guidelines for use next to
DECOAT.

WP4 - Continuous recycling pilot line [Months: 19-49]

Task 4.1 Set-up and implementation of the recycling pilot line - AIMPLAS, CTECH, IVV, IRES, IRIS (M19-M37) -
35%.
Although in practice, the chemical and mechanical part can be combined, the so-called mechanical recycling line will
be located at AIMPLAS and the so-called chemical recycling line at partner IVV. This is due to practical reasons: the
chemical part cannot easily be transported and requires a non-explosive environment.
Based on the results obtained in Tasks 2.3 and 2.4 and in WP3, this task will be focused on designing, implementing,
controlling and optimizing the components of the recycling pilot plant.
Subtask 4.1.1 Mechanical line.
The following devices will be installed and adapted to set up the mechanical pilot recycling line:
1) Optical sorting: to remove the non-suitable parts in the waste stream and to show the identification of the triggers
2) Grinding: reduction of the size of the plastic parts for improving the efficiency of the activation system and for more
easy feeding the waste into the centrifuge and the recycled plastics into the extruder,
3) Activation: this is the unit where the triggers (IR, heat, MW) can be applied to loosen the (primer layer of the) coating
to ease separation. The key activity here is the building of dedicated MW equipment: cavity design, selection of energy
range, feeding system.
4) Separation: actual physical separation of coatings and plastic parts. Two options: (i) ‘spinning’ the material in a
high speed centrifuge – this is expected to be the most general technique; (ii) density separation for some material
combinations like coatings on a TPU foam (for example the MBT use case).
5) Melt pelletising: final extrusion step to obtain recycled pellets free of coatings to be reused for new applications. The
melt extrusion process will be assisted by Sc-CO2 cleaning technology for removal of VOCs and odors.
In addition, IRIS will install the monitoring systems delivered in WP3 in the selected points of the recycling process
for sorting and coating removal control. They will train AIMPLAS staff and attend the initial trials to verify the correct
monitored results.
Subtask 4.1.2 Chemical line.
The plant (size of 10kg batches) will include a grinding unit, dissolution unit, a separation unit, that allows separating
dissolved coating from undissolved substrates, as well as drying unit for both undissolved and dissolved materials and a
final extruder incorporating degassing, which will produce recycled granules. If degasification problems occur, grinded
materials from the separation units will be introduced in the AIMPLAS melt compounding unit with Sc-CO2 cleaning
technology.
Task 4.2 Activation system dependent process optimization (Creasolv, MW and IR) at laboratory level - IVV, AIMPLAS,
CTAG, CTECH, NTUA (M19-M40) - 35%.
For each of the coated waste streams that we consider, a selection needs to be made to identify which of the proposed
debonding systems, developed in WP2, is the most suitable activation technology. This selection will be based on an
analysis with respect to their effectiveness in activating the triggers in the distinct applications among different materials.
This needs to be done for the triggered mechanical and chemical type of recycling. This work builds further on the
activities from WP2 (especially Tasks 2.2 and 2.3) and will be implemented in interaction with Task 2.4. In other words:
the lab-scale findings of WP2 will be upscaled and translated towards industrial equipment. The outcome of task 4.2
will be established processing parameters for the different activation systems.
Subtask 4.2.1 Mechanical line.
Page 26 of 52
Using tests specimens with coatings with various percentages of MW susceptors (prepared in WP2) and discontinuous
laboratory MW and standard ovens, the capacity of the melting of the primer layer will be evaluated. With the help
of a DoE methodology, the materials with susceptors will be subjected to different MW and heating doses (taking
into account as main parameters of the equipment: frequency, voltage power and time of exposure). The increase of
temperature of the specimen during the test will be monitored by an infrared camera. The objective is to reduce the
time inside the oven to less than 5 minutes without matrix degradation. The goal is that the process does not modify
the final properties of the matrix. The properties of removed coating material will be measured using DSC and/or TGA
tests. The amount of remaining coating on the plastic part will be evaluated according to protocol defined in Task 2.2.2.
The activation technologies will be characterised using parameters such as: not affecting the properties of the substrate
material, residence time in the oven and the energy and materials required for the coating removal.
Subtask 4.2.2 Chemical line.
The Creasolv® Process will be used for a selective dissolution of coatings and for initial sample production. This may
be done a) by dissolution of the coating itself, or b) in case of presence of a primer layer by dissolving that layer only.
Whereas a) would allow a solid/liquid separation of dissolved coating from undissolved substrate directly, b) implies
a subsequent separation of coating films from substrate matter. It is expected that coating material and substrate will
exhibit differences in density, thus separation of both after removing the primer layer will be performed by density
separation in liquids exhibiting a density between the densities of both materials. This in order to obtain a stream with
components that can be separated by density or other separation technologies. Dissolved coatings will be separated
from the solvent and the solvent will be completely re-used in the process. The coating polymer will be recovered and
evaluated for a suitable second life application in Task 4.4.
Task 4.3 Validation of the recycling pilot line performance using well-defined waste streams made with flat test-
specimens - AIMPLAS, IVV, MAIER, MBT, PANASONIC (M31-M46) - 15%.
The goal is to validate the two recycling pilot lines. For this purpose, specific test samples will be produced. The test
samples will be a combination of coating material, triggerable additive and substrate. The task will validate the recycling
pilot plants (mechanical at AIMPLAS and Creasolv at IVV) for at least two types of substrates (plastic object and textile
fabric).
Each of the defined process parameters will be optimized for each waste stream to minimize energy/material
consumption per kilogram of treated material and maximize the final properties of the final recycled products.
Task 4.4 Maximising the application potential of the recycled materials - CTAG, AIMPLAS, PANASONIC, MAIER,
MBT, IVV, CTB, NTUA (M31-M49) -15%.
Here, we will characterise the recycled materials coming from Task 4.3. First, we need to determine the purity
of the material, see also Task 2.2.2, to check if we get in the scaled up line the same levels as on smaller scale.
Second, we need the classical characterisation to determine the quality of the recycled materials and to determine the
recycling yield, using the most suitable techniques (for example Electronic microscopy, DSC, DMTA, rheological tests,
thermal behaviour, ageing behaviour). NTUA can support this task with: SEM/EDX, TGA, rheology measurements,
hardness measurements, microCT analysis. The purity of the substrate material will be investigated by specific analysis
(microscopy, FTIR, DSC, REM). CTAG can test the recycled material following end-user standards: climate ageing,
UV and IR resistance, resistance to different chemicals, fatigue, impact, tensile strength. The quality of the recycled
materials will be determined following the suitable EN/ISO standards to assess if we are above or below the maximum
level of damage that the coating can withstand and considering the different types of materials.
On top of that, we will study the selection of suitable additives to upgrade the recycled polymer materials, eg by
protecting from degradation during recycling with the goal to widen their lifespan (e.g. thermal protection, antioxidants,
compatibilizers, chain extenders, UV stability, etc.). For this, we will use the state-of-the-art at that moment as this is a
field in full progress. The goal is to optimise the substrate material during recycling for maximising the circular use.
Based on the characterisation and ‘upgrading’ of the recycled material, an analysis of the potential uses of the recycled
materials will be done. This will depend on the purity of the materials we can get. This is crucial as the re-use of the
recycled materials might not be necessarily for the same applications as the original one, but might be more suited for
other applications. We will investigate that, making sure that these other applications go for the highest added value
as possible.

Task 5.1: Eco-design of use case products in view of project technological developments - MBT, MAIER, CTAG,
PANASONIC, ECOMAT, VAUDE (M34-M47) - 15%.
Activities of this task aim at selecting the most appropriate materials and processing, among those developed in previous
WPs, to be used for the manufacturing of the final prototypes. The primary selection criterion will be the level of
compliance to the requirements of each end use case, as specified in WP1, both in terms of materials properties and in
terms of production parameters. Interim results of the LCA, LCC and safety analysis (WP6) will also be considered as
well as the updated intelligence on exploitation (WP7).
Where needed a redesign of the (coating of the) part will be performed to make the recycling easier. This could mean
for example the addition of a tie layer to introduce the required debonding adhesives or using a different type of coating
material.
The redesign will also have to take into account that materials need to be used that can go through several cycles of use.
We will consider the full cycle of processes the parts / products need to go through. This means that based on
the developments made in WP1 to WP4, the full value chain for making the product circular cycle will be made.
This includes the use of the debonding technology developed. That way, we will make a realistic assessment of the
disassembly, the triggered mechanical recycling and/or the Creasolv recycling steps that will be needed to obtain the
substrate material, and potentially also the coated material, again as a secondary raw material. If the initial requirements,
defined in Task 1.1 can be met, the materials could be fully used for the same application again. In the case not all
requirements can be fulfilled, the most likely scenario, alternative solutions will have to be sought, eg addition of virgin
material, use of the materials for alternative but also high value applications, perhaps adding an extra cleaning/recycling
step/…
The outcome of this task will be a report describing for at least 8 material combinations (which will be based on the use
case of the end users) the value chain and technologies to reach circularity. These 8 cases will act as examples and the
goal is to make this public, to inspire and stimulate external partners.

Task 6.1: Risk assessment & Safe-by-Design principles - IRES, NTUA & ALL PARTNERS (M1-M46) - 20%.
A Safe-by-Design approach will be applied in the early stages of DECOAT project and will emphasize on the decision
making process regarding design, materials used and methods in order to enhance the safety of final products and control
the related health risks. Safe-by-Design aims at addressing safety issues already during the R&D and design phases of
the proposed technologies. Safe by Design approaches will be employed to minimise health hazards, environmental and
economic impacts. One of the key elements for establishing a safety management system and implementing Safe by
Design principles into the DECOAT project, is to ensure that risks associated with hazards are evaluated and managed
within acceptable safety requirements, via risk assessment techniques and methods. DECOAT project aims to set
out specific duties applying to employers and manufacturers focusing on risks associated with the use of hazardous
substances. Special attention will be given to the description of properties, health hazards, precautions for use (e.g.
REACH) and safe handling of hazardous substances, providing vital information not only to the employers but also to
end-users. Finally, the aim of this task is to supervise safety procedures at the early stage and update them periodically
while toxicological information received from Task 6.4 (from nanomaterials synthesis to the final disposal) becomes
available.
Task 6.2: Life Cycle Assessment - ECOMAT, IRES & ALL PARTNERS (M13-M53) - 25%.
Life cycle assessment studies will be implemented in order to evaluate the environmental impacts of the product systems
incorporating the developed coatings and the respective recycling processes, as well as determine the environmental
performance improvements of the suggested processes compared to the current end-of-life scenarios (landfilling or
energy recovery). Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) is a structured, comprehensive and internationally standardised method
for environmental impacts of product systems. LCA takes into consideration products’ full life cycle, beginning from
the extraction of resources, continuing with the production, use, recycling and ending with the disposal of remaining
waste. It is distinguished from other environmental assessment methods by two unique features: the analysis from
‘cradle-to-grave’ and the ‘functional unit’. Specific guidelines and standards, such as ISO 14040 and 14044, provide
the indispensable framework for Life Cycle Assessment (LCA). DECOAT project will take advantage of this decision
support tool, using commercial software, in order to develop competitive and sustainable innovative products.
By M13 the four most prominent applications (coated material + coating) will be chosen for serving as a base for the
assessment within the project as case study parts (1 coated textile, 1 coated electronics part, 2 automotive parts). The
assessment of environmental impacts of the product systems will be based on the functional units of a service provided
by a respective part to the main end user over a period of one year.
The assessments will run from M13 on and in the beginning (estimated TRL3-TRL4) would include screening LCA
studies with identification of the hotspots for the materials and production choices at the moment of assessments on
the laboratory or experimental scale, as well as the assessment of the base level product systems scenarios. These
Page 34 of 52
will make a significant contribution to the development of the technology, incorporating eco-design strategies to the
product development. Once the technology development reaches the point of a reasonable certainty about the production
processes (TRL5) and allows to decide about the type of coating material and product recycling technology for each
of the trial parts, a full LCA will be implemented. As a result, four full LCA studies will be reported covering each of
the study case part with the chosen coating material and recycling technology. Sensitivity analysis is to be implemented
for the final full LCA studies in order to evaluate the performance from the perspectives of different scenarios of
the products’ future, and to validate the improvements independently on the analyses methodological choices. Special
attention will be paid to evaluation of the impact of methodological choices regarding the product systems’ end-of-life,
including landfilling, incineration, bulk material recycling, and coating material recycling.
Data collection will be conducted continuously throughout the project. It will include regularly primary data collection
for the processes under investigation with involvement of technical experts in workshops and questionnaires and
supported by the secondary data collection with use of internationally recognised databases, including specialized on
the process data in coating industry.
Task 6.3 Life Cycle Costing - IRES, NTUA, ECOMAT & ALL PARTNERS (M19-M53) - 25%.
Life Cycle Costing (LCC) is an assessment of all costs related to a product or service, over the entire life cycle,
from production over use until disposal. IRES will combine LCC with LCA in order to provide a holistic approach
regarding Life Cycle Cost. This combination will lead to the estimation and the same time minimisation of the economic
impact arising from the applied processes and the material used. As a general pillar of this WP economic analysis
will include material costs, manufacturing costs, operation costs and maintenance costs of developed multifunctional
systems. A comparison among all these costs, regarding DECOAT’s innovative materials and traditional ones, will be
made. Main focus of this task will be the economic sustainability and the competitiveness of both the final materials and
the developed processes. In this framework, LCC will be applied in order to ensure that the evolution of the produced
materials will be evaluated on time to match with the commercial life of recycling products. In addition, issues related to
the evolution that technology and the market can be submitted, giving a competitive advantage that will boost the EU’s
circular economy. Thus, the economic analysis which will take place into DECOAT project refers not only to the cost of
materials and manufacturing but also includes the wider implications of technology selection on project’s performance.
Task 6.4 Toxicity, Release and Exposure assessment - NTUA, IRES, CTB, AIMPLAS, CTAG, CTB, IVV (M7-M46)
- 10%.
Due to that fact that safe by design always includes a compromise between functionality and safety, residual health risks
cannot be excluded in DECOAT proposal, where a variety of nanomaterials is synthesized in WP1. In this task, this
residual health risk will be assessed by performing in vitro toxicity tests initially on the developed hybrid nanomaterials,
which have not been extensively studied. Moreover, release tests will be executed to the final formulations and
representative specimens, to ensure that the nanomaterials are embedded in the adhesives, without any risk of mitigation.
Finally, exposure assessment will be conducted to identify, characterise and quantify releases in the recycling processing
activities (WP4) that may present an exposure risk, through open source software. The activities undertaken will
allow the development of best practice risk management, to minimise and control the health risks for researchers,
manufacturers and end-users involved with the project.
Task 6.5 Eco-design tool - ECOMAT, IRES, OSM (M13-M53) - 20%.
To make use of the results of the assessments of the WP6, and to facilitate dissemination of DECOAT technological
solutions, Task 6.5 will develop an eco-design tool for benefits and strategies identification. The tool aims to support the
industrial sectors (both Project partners and external parties) to identify eco-design strategies for new products based
on the DECOAT solutions (qualitative module), as well as to quantify the benefits of using the DECOAT solutions
in their businesses (quantitative module). To increase accessibility of the tool, it will be developed in the form of a
web tool available for use by the external parties at the website of the DECOAT project. The scope will focus on one
generic combination of a coated material and a coating solution, and thus would be applicable to multiple products
within different sectors. The exact materials and coatings will be identified and selected in M13 based on the preliminary
assessment results on the most prominent and elaborated combination.
The tool will be developed in three main stages. At the first stage (M13-M19) internal and external research will be
implemented to identify the functionality of the tool and the scope of the required output information. At the end of the
stage, the conceptual design of the tool will be developed, including structure, features and functionality. The second
stage (M20-M40) will focus on translating the conceptual design into a qualitative and quantitative assessment tool
with the main purpose of communicating the tangibility of DECOAT results. As this stage the basic architecture of the
tool will be developed. The tool will exploit first the preliminary screening LCA results, and will be digitalised as a
web-based tool that went through technical and functional testing. The qualitative outputs will identify the main user’s
opportunities to benefit from including DECOAT solutions into own product design or value chain. At the final stage

(till M55) the tool will be adjusted to the quantitative higher certainty results of full LCA and LCC studies, incorporating
the qualitative assessment of the potential benefits to exploit the DECOAT solutions.

Task 8.4: Data Management - IRES, OSM, ALL partners (M1-M55) -20%.
Throughout DECOAT, large amounts of Data are expected to be generated. IRES will be responsible for the Design and
Implementation of a solid Plan for the collection, storage, evaluation and exploitation of the data that will be produced
during and after DECOAT. Openness, accessibility to beneficiaries and reusability of the produced Data will also be
ensured by the Data Management Plan, in order to open the road for the Open Innovation Environment (OIE). The Data
Management Plan includes all the necessary steps for efficient collection, safe storage, deep exploitation and evaluation
of the data. These steps involve the Data Repository for sharing data and the Design and Implementation of a process,
which will integrate data coming from different sources (Data Mapping) and securely store them. IRES will also take
action for Metadata Handling and Sensitive Data Handling. Furthermore, Data produced throughout DECOAT will be
shared among open European Research Databases. The project will comply with the newly imposed rules concerning
data quality, sharing and security (also GDPR principles will be applied).
A Data Management Plan will be produced by M13 and updated at the project midterm and end. We will ensure that:
research data will be easily discoverable, accessible, assessable and intelligible, usable beyond the original purpose for
which they were collected, interoperable to specific quality standards, whenever relevant. The database will be used as
a first basis for exploitation of results and further exchange of knowledge and expertise.
