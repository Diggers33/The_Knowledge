Steel Sector Overview  
The steel industry remains crucial for manufacturing, construction, automotive, and many other sectors. Global steel producti on 
has stabilized at around 1.9 billion metric tons annually. Asia, particularly China, dominates production, accounting for over 50 % 
of global output.1 The EU steel sector is a significant player in the global market, producing around  152 million tonnes of steel 
annually and providing approximately 2.5 million jobs.2 Despite being the second-largest producer after China, the EU's steel 
demand has faced a downturn due to various factors, including economic disruptions and high energy costs . In 2022, the EU's 
apparent steel consumption fell to one of its lowest levels, reflecting poor demand conditions.2 
The steel industry has long operated in a VUCA (Volatile, Uncertain, Complex, Ambiguous) environment, where fluctuations in 
energy prices, raw material availability, and construction demand created significant challenges. However, today's world is 
evolving beyond VUCA into a BANI  (Brittle, Anxious, Nonlinear, Incomprehensible) framework, introducing even greater 
unpredictability. In 2022, the EU steel sector faced a significant crisis marked by a sharp decline in apparent steel consumption, 
reaching one of its lowest levels in recent history. This downturn was driven by several factors, including economic disruptions 
from the COVID -19 pandemic and geopolitical tensions, which led to reduced industrial activity and lower demand for steel. 
Additionally, unprecedented rises in energy prices , particularly gas, significantly increased production costs, making 
steel manufacturing more expensive and less competitive.  Supply chain disruptions, such as port congestion and raw 
material shortages, further exacerbated th e situation. Key steel-consuming sectors, including construction and automotive, 
also struggled, contributing to decreased industrial output.  
The combined impact of these challenges resulted in economic instability, job losses, and weakened strategic positioning  
in the global steel market, highlighting the urgent need for the EU to invest in new technologies, improve energy efficiency,  and 
enhance supply chain resilience to stabilize and grow its steel industry.  
Leveraging AI for steel industry transformation 
In response to these challenges, the integration of advanced technologies, particularly Artificial Intelligence ( AI), has become 
essential for the steel industry. The European AI landscape is at a pivotal moment, with technological capabilities rapidly 
transforming business operations. Companies are transitioning from often still experimental AI adoption to integrating AI with other 
advanced technologies, to solve complex business challenges. The critical focus is shifting from simply having AI capabilities  to 
strategically deploying them to drive innovation, increase efficiency, and build competitive advantage, all while carefully managing 
AI-related implementation risks. AI investment across Europe has surged recently , with total AI spending in 2024 estimated to 
have reached EUR 46.4 billion, with software occupying largest share (57.0%), followed by services (23.7%) and hardware 
(19.3%).3  
Spending on AI is expected to continue to grow rapidly over the next few years, nearly tripling over four years to EUR 133.6 billion 
in 2028.3 This highlights the importance of AI algorithms and applications in driving innovation and digital transformation. 
Among AI segments generative AI is a standout category, expected to account for one-third of the market by 2028. This 
impressive growth, however, points to a huge shift in how businesses operate, with a likely influx of new products and services 
powered by Generative AI (GenAI), from more human-like chatbots to AI-created content to generative app or website interfaces. 
Despite the potential of generative AI, companies are likely to use a mix of AI technologies to solve different business problems. 
While there is a strong belief in the transformative potential of GenAI across various industries, traditional AI technologies will 
continue to see substantial growth, highlighting the ongoing importance of comprehensive and diverse AI solutions in the 
European market . Beyond financial projections, the AI  landscape is expected to experience significant technological 
advancements in the coming years. Improvements in model accuracy, efficiency, and explainability are anticipated, driven by 
innovations in algorithms, data processing, and hardware. In the longer term, emerging technologies like quantum computing and 
neuromorphic computing may also prove transformative.

Given the rapid advancements in AI and its transformative potential as well as the transition from VUCA to BANI, the steel industry 
must leverage these technologies to navigate the increasingly complex and unpredictable market environment. This is where the 
RIVER project will make a difference.

for energy, scrap, and lime ; rapid market swings ; extreme price fluctuations ; and escalating pressure to increase 
sustainability and decarbonise. Traditional supply-demand models are failing in the face of energy transition policies, emerging

The RIVER project directly addresses the first RFCS objective:  Cross cutting issues: digitalization, skills and social

RIVER overarching goal is to boost the global  competitiveness of European steelmaking through a transformative AI -
powered shift in production planning -firmly anchored in digital transformation,  sustainability and workforce 
empowerment. By embedding sustainability KPIs into operational decision-making, RIVER targets an increase of up to 2.2% in 
EBITDA. To track this, Life Cycle Assessment work from the outset will correlate economic performance (including EBITDA gains) 
with sustainability indica tors using ke y metrics such as  CO₂ reduction and the circularity index, based on ISO 59020.  This 
approach is closely aligned with the principles of the Draghi Report and the Clean Industrial Deal, both of which emphasize the 
importance of linking sustainable practices with long-term industrial competitiveness.

RIVER O1 ➔ Demonstration of an Integrated Modular Digital Strategy for Adaptive and Sustainable Steelmaking  
O1 is dedicated to the full pilot demonstration and replication of the integrated  RIVER solution in live industrial settings. The 
technical, economic, environmental, and social benefits will be validated across two complementary use cases, in the 
construction (BCN) and automotive (GSW) sectors, representing Make-to-Stock and Make-to-Order production models, 
respectively (see Table 1). At the core of RIVER’s innovation is the enhancement of adaptive capacity of both production 
process and workforce. By increasing the access and management capacity of real-time and forecasting data from both market 
and production systems, the solution enables significantly more agile and informed planning and responsiveness.  
The pilots will assess performance KPIs (e.g., yield, emissions, spread, delivery reliability) as well as  organizational 
change. It will evaluate cross -functional collaboration across Planning, Logistics, Production, Finance, and Commercial 
departments, monitor GenAI agents’ adoption, and track workforce engagement and co-creation outcomes. To ensure alignment 
with EU Taxonomy and Green Deal targets, RIVER will adapt and embed standardized LCA tools and open -source frameworks 
for benchmarking CO₂ reduction and energy efficiency being developed in a sister project, PRISMA, for BCN’s use case.  
The RIVER solution will operate within a robust, cybersecure architecture ensuring data integrity, system resilience, and ethical 
management of sensitive industrial information, in full compliance with EU regulations. 
Critically, RIVER's ambition goes beyond validation. The project is designed from the outset to support scaling, replication, and 
future commercialization of the tools developed. A Scalability and Replicability Playbook will consolidate best practices, technical 
configurations, and change management strategies, paving the way for EU-wide deployment. 
In addition, a dedicated exploitation and sustainability plan will outline the go -to-market (GTM) strategy, ensuring the long -term 
viability, adoption, and market penetration of the RIVER tools beyond the project’s lifetime.

Exploitation strategy: The RIVER project’s exploitation strategy aims to deliver a scalable and market -ready suite of AI -
powered digital tools designed to enhance adaptability, efficiency, and sustainability in steelmaking. The exploitation pathw ay 
revolves around six Key Exploitable Results (KERs): KER1 (AI-powered Control Tower, an integrated decision support platform 
combining a digital twin, forecasting engine, simulator and interactive dashboard), KER2 (Production and Scheduling 
Optimisation Tool), KER3 (Forecasting Engine for demand, energy and scrap), KER4 (Optimised Scrap Procurement Module), 
KER5 (Logistics Optimisation Tool), and KER6 (GenAI-enabled Learning System for workforce empowerment and co-creation). 
Each KER will follow a differentiated exploitation route according to its nature, maturity, and potential market. KER1 and KER3 
will be exploited primarily as a unified SaaS or PaaS solution for large steel producers, with flexible deployment models ranging 
from cloud-based subscriptions to on -premises licensing. KER2 and KER5  will be offered as modular optimisation plugins 
compatible with existing MES/MPS platforms, facilitating gradual adoption. KER4 is intended for transfer via consultancy 
service or spin -off, targeting procurement departments. KER6 will be exploited through  institutional licensing for vocational 
training centres or as a corporate learning solution. The overall strategy includes internal deployment and reference validation 
within CELSA, followed by progressive commercialisation and scaling across the EU steel sector. The exploitation process will 
be supported by a tailored Go-To-Market strategy, customer segmentation, and channel partners, and by an Exploitation and 
Sustainability Plan outlining business models, IP management, and legal agreements. Each KER wi ll have a designated 
exploitation lead, responsible for technical readiness, business development, and protection of IP assets. These measures 
ensure a robust transition from pilot validation to EU-wide deployment, securing long-term impact and competitiveness.

RIVER O2 ➔ Unlocking Prescriptive Planning through an Integrated AI-Powered Control Tower  
O2 aims to set up an AI-powered control tower to create a unified, real-time decision-making hub. This adaptive decision 
engine will integrate real-time and forecasting data from both production and the market , combining advanced demand 
and procurement forecasting with GenAI-based scenario simulation. By unifying these data streams, the RIVER control tower 
will establish a  holistic, data-driven planning ecosystem. In doing so, the control tower empowers decision-makers to 
evaluate alternative strategies that optimize production, procurement, and logistics in real time , thereby driving enhanced 
economic performance and significant reductions in environmental impact (see Table 2). This prescriptive decision -making 
capability will enable steelmakers to proactively manage resource and supply chain volatility, reduce costs and emissions, an d 
strengthen competitiveness in line with RFCS goals and the European Green Deal. 
• Integrated Planning and Execution : The control tower integrates demand and procurement forecasts with real -time 
production data , ensuring seamless coordination across production, procurement, and logistics activities . This enables 
dynamic, real-time adjustments based on changing market conditions and supply chain inputs. 
• Generative AI -Based Scenario Simulation underpinned by a Digital Twin of the supply chain: RIVER will layer a 
generative-AI scenario engine atop its real -time digital twin of the steel value chain. RIVER’s forecasting tools/models will 
become data pipelines fed into the digital twin. The Control Tower will then use the twin’s outputs to generate multiple 
prescriptive “what-if” scenarios based on variables like demand volatility, scrap and energy pricing, and logistical constraints. 
These scenarios are grounded in real-time data, providing decision-makers with prescriptive insights to evaluate the benefits 
and risks of different strategies, balancing cost efficiency, production performance, and sustainability goals (e.g., improve d 
speed, reduced CO₂ emissions). 
• Interactive dashboard interface: A user-friendly dashboard will allow planners and decision -makers to interact with the 
digital twin of the value chain, modifying and visualising multiple scenarios based on different drivers such as working capital 
desired level, delivery date fulfilment or EBITDA and CO optimisation.  
• Continuous Learning and Feedback Loop : The system captures user feedback and system performance to  iteratively 
improve AI models and scenario accuracy, driving better decision support over time.

RIVER O3 – Developing digital methodologies and standalone tools for monitoring, control and forecasting  
O3 focuses on developing a set of modular digital tools that serve as the backbone for the holistic operational view , bridging 
market intelligence with production and scheduling. Enhanced insights and agility will enable steelmakers  to better align and 
monitor production, optimize procurement decisions, and adjust to rapidly changing market conditions , improving 
both operational efficiency and sustainability (see Table 3).  
By integrating historical data, real-time market signals, macroeconomic indicators, RIVER will provide:  
• Demand Forecasting: AI/ML models will generate multi -level forecasts for construction and automotive sectors, based on 
sales data, customer trends, and economic indicators.  
• Purchasing Forecasting : Since scrap and energy are the biggest purchasing costs in the secondary route, RIVER will 
develop a predictive model for scrap price, availability, and location, and adapt an existing energy price forecast model, 
benchmarking results from the FLEX4FACT project in which a similar tool has been developed for CELSA.  They will enable 
proactive and cost-effective procurement strategies.  
• A logistics optimization tool to align material flows with transport constraints, enhancing logistics efficiency and supporting 
sustainability goals. 
To ensure optimal process performance & system-wide data availability, RIVER will deploy:  
• Integrated traceability system , combining smart inventory management, advanced charge mix determination, billet 
weighting system for smart cutting, bar to bar tracking and billet and product labelling, supporting greater resource efficiency 
and process visibility.  
• Integrated Predictive models for key equipment such as the Electric Arc Furnace (EAF), Ladle Furnace (LF), and reheating 
furnace. The EAF and LF models will build upon those validated for BCN  in the ALCHIMIA project, which will  be further 
enhanced through improved charge mix determination.  
• Quality forecasting tool to reduce reprocessing and improve product consistency while optimising sequencing. 
• Upgraded and integrated MES and MPS systems, enabled by real-time and forecasted data flows, which they will increase 
production process agility and optimise stock cycle efficiency.  The system will optimize hot charging (HC) by delivering the 
best HC strategy without compromising top priorities in steel production, such as electricity costs and delivery deadline. Since 
HC rate optimization is crucial for energy efficiency, cost savings, and sustainability, the HC algorithm will be further ref ined 
as market forecasting capabilities improve (O4).

Ambition 1 – Enhance competitiveness of the European steelmaking sector by strengthening adaptive capacity  
State-of-the-art: Integrated Business Planning (IBP) extends traditional Sales & Operations Planning (S&OP) by combining 
commercial, operational, and financial data into a unified decision -making process. In theory, IBP enables companies to align 
supply chain execution with corporate strategy, maximizing responsiveness, effici ency, and profitability.  However, in the steel 
sector, IBP adoption remains limited and fragmented. Current planning systems are siloed, updates are slow and manual, and 
decisions are often made using simplified, generic cost-opportunity trade-offs. The complexity of balancing competing priorities—
like minimizing energy cost, reducing emissions, maximizing customer service, and optimizing inventory —makes real -time, 
integrated planning n early impossible with current tools. As a result, strategic, tactical, and operational decisions are often 
disconnected, and real -time reactivity is limited. Market -specific commercial strategies (e.g., pricing vs. volume) and real -time 
constraints (e.g., freight bottlenecks , production delays, volatile energy prices) are rarely integrated into a single platform, 
hindering overall performance. 
Bottlenecks:  
• Disjointed Decision-Making Tools: Planning systems in steelmaking often remain siloed13 across business functions, 
forcing users to rely on disconnected spreadsheets and legacy tools. This fragmentation hinders comprehensive trade -
offs analysis between costs, service, emissions and inventory. 
• Lack of Strategic Scenario Planning : Today’s tools do not support dynamic generation of alternative scenarios for 
different optimization objectives (e.g., lowest cost vs. best on -time delivery). Planners lack support to weigh strategic 
options in real time. 
• Data Overload without Intelligence: Planners are overwhelmed by vast amounts of data—on scrap and energy prices, 
customer orders, plant performance, and transport availability —but lack intelligent tools to synthesize this information 
into actionable insights. 
• Rigid Response to Real -Time Changes: The inability to simulate or reconfigure plans in real time prevents timely 
adaptation to events such as sudden energy price spikes, unplanned outages, or shifts in order priorities14. 
• No Learning Loop Between Users and AI: Manual adjustments by human planners are not captured and fed back into 
forecasting models or decision systems, limiting the improvement of AI suggestions over time. 
RIVER progress beyond SOA: 
An AI-Powered Supply Chain Control Tower enabling dynamic adjustments of all features within one decision-making system. 
At the heart of RIVER system is a GenAI-powered scenario engine  capable of generating multiple optimized production and 
supply chain plans. Each scenario will reflect different strategic priorities, such as minimizing scrap and energy costs, maximizing 
EBITDA, improving service levels, or achieving CO₂ reduction targets. Rather than replacing human decision-making, the control 
tower will present planners with tailored, data -driven options, enabling them to choos e the most appropriate strategy based on 
current business goals. 
This scenario intelligence will be underpinned by a real-time digital twin of secondary steelmaking route supply chain , 
continuously simulating system behaviour and forecasting the impacts of various “what-if” decisions. For instance, by integrating 
electricity price forecasts with digital models of EAF operations, the system can recommend batch sequencing strategies that 
reduce energy consumption by scheduling high-intensity operations during off-peak hours.

Ambition 2 – Leveraging on cutting edge AI to reinforce market forecasting capacity. 
State-of-the-art: In a BANI environment, the steel industry faces extreme demand volatility. Traditional forecasting methods —
relying on historical data, market reports, and informal sales insights —are static, fragmented, and lack the agility needed for 
proactive planning. Commercial capabilities rely mostly on the relationships of the sales teams with very limited use of digital and 
analytics solutions as a result, steelmakers often set broad annual sal es targets and adjust them reactively. Furthermore, public 
steel demand forecasts need to be updated frequently as new data and market development occur, revealing the limitations of 
statistic forecast models and the importance of dynamic, scenario-based approaches.  
Forecasting practices differ by sector:  in construction, apparent demand (distributor purchases) and real demand (project 
pipelines) are difficult to reconcile ; in automotive, producers depend on client -supplied forecasts and macroeconomic trends —
often without structured tools to synthesize them. Additionally, commercial strategies alternate between volume and price-driven 
priorities, segmented across regions, product lines, and customer profiles. Yet pricing decisions remain largely intuitive, with weak 
governance and poor traceability of deal terms. Volume allocation strategies are static and siloed, limiting agility and undermining 
margin potential. Ultimately, the lack of integrated, data -driven forecasting and pricing capabilities inhibits the shift from reactive 
to proactive commercial planning. 
Beyond demand, critical variables such as scrap availability and prices , energy prices, and logistics capacity suffer from 
limited forecasting horizons, leading to reactive procurement and transport planning that increases costs and emissions. 
Bottlenecks:  
To address these inefficiencies, steelmakers must overcome the following structural bottlenecks: 
• Reactive vs. Prescriptive Forecasting: Forecasts are static and backward-looking. There is a need to shift toward dynamic, 
AI-enhanced tools that suggest actions and simulate outcomes. 
• Fragmented Data and Siloed Systems: Demand, energy, scrap, and logistics data are not integrated, limiting the ability to 
perform cross-functional planning or optimize trade-offs. 
• Limited Forecast Horizons for Inputs: Scrap and energy market visibility is too short-term to support proactive procurement 
or production strategies15. This results in higher input costs and missed decarbonization opportunities. 
• Inefficient commercial decision -making stems from intuitive pricing and static, siloed volume allocation, which together 
weaken governance, reduce traceability, misalign priorities, and limit flexibility —undermining competitiveness and margin 
potential. 
• Unstructured Sales Validation Process : Demand forecasts are not consistently validated or iteratively refined with sales 
teams, reducing confidence in the data and its operational utility. 
• Inefficient Logistics Planning: Short-term visibility results in costly, reactive bookings rather than optimized, consolidated, 
and lower-emission transport16. 
RIVER progress beyond SOA:  
Move from reactive "predictions" to proactive "prescriptive" systems that align forecasting with operational execution.  
RIVER will address these bottlenecks through a unified, AI -enabled forecasting and planning platform that integrates demand, 
supply, and logistics intelligence into a cohesive system. At its core, advanced machine learning algorithms will combine historical 
trends, real-time market signals –including unstructured data sources such as news articles and industry reports processed via 
natural language processing (NLP) –, macroeconomic ind icators, and structured commercial inputs.  This approach enables 
dynamic, scenario-based forecasts  rather than statistic single -value predictions, allowing steelmakers to plan and make 
decisions more realistically under uncertainty. The system will support segmentation by product, customer, and region, enabling 
flexible commercial target setting. This approach not only determines overall demand but also provides granular forecasts tha t 
drive volume allocation and pricing strategies across different product segments and regions. 
To extend visibility beyond demand , sophisticated scrap and energy forecasting  tools will be developed. These tools will 
enhance understanding of scrap availability, pricing, and geographic location, facilitating optimised scrap procurement strategies 
and inventory management. Energy price forecasting will be improved though benchmarking existing numerical weather prediction 
model-based solutions with innovative solutions such as those developed in the FLEX4FACT project. 
Integrated logistics optimisation will complement production forecasts by enabling earlier and smarter transport bookings. This 
will reduce premium transport charges and improve load consolidation . By co -optimising logistics and production, RIVER will 
reduce emissions, avoid bottlenecks, and enhanced delivery performance, creating a more sustainable and efficient supply chain.

1.2.1 RIVER concept  
The steel supply chain today is facing several challenges 20: Decarbonization pressures are significant, as industry needs to 
reduce its substantial carbon footprint, representing 7-9 % of global emissions ; Global overcapacity, particularly from China, 
continues to depress global prices and create market instability ; Rising and volatile energy costs are a major concern, as the 
steel industry is one of the most energy -intensive manufacturing sectors; Raw material price volatility, especially for essential 
inputs like iron ore, coking coal, and scrap metal, directly impacts margins and competitiveness ; Trade tensions and 
protectionism have fra gmented the global steel market, creating complex compliance requirements and market distortions ; 
Supply chain disruptions, such as port congestion and shipping container shortages, affect both raw material procurement and 
finished product distribution; Digital transformation imperative: to stay competitive, steel companies need to quickly embrace 
Industry 5.0 technologies, which include advanced analytics, artificial intelligence, automation, and the human dimension. This 
aligns with the urgent needs of th e EU to increase its digital competitiveness, as highlighted in the Competitiveness Compass 
Report. The integration of technologies such as AI and Generative AI (GenAI) in steel manufacturing is pivotal for addressing 
efficiency and decarbonisation challenges as also mentioned in recent EU report s and Action plan such as EU Competitiveness 
Compass Report21 and the AI Continent Action Plan22. This digital transformation demands substantial investment in new systems, 
as well as changes in organizational culture  and the development of new skill sets, which traditional steel producers often find 
challenging to cultivate internally ; Workforce challenges , including an aging workforce and difficulty attracting young talent, 
create critical skills gaps and knowledge transfer problems. 
To address these challenges, RIVER will develop and demonstrate a resilient, adaptive and low -carbon steel supply chain by 
integrating real -time data, AI -driven forecasting, and collaborative decision -making across the value chain. Its ambition is to 
reshape planning and operations through four interdependent building blocks, each contributing to smarter, more sustainable 
production. 
Block 1: AI for Execution and Scheduling 
This block focuses on optimizing production and scheduling by enhancing integration between Manufacturing Execution Systems 
(MES) and Master Production Scheduling (MPS) using real -time and forecasted data on critical material and energy flows. 
Upgrades to MES/MPS—including their unification across Melt Shop and Rolling Mill —will improve traceability, enable 
consumption forecasting, and support agile, data -driven decision -making. AI -enhanced MPS will calculate stock cycles and 
optimise hot charging rate up to a two-month horizon. Internal logistics constraints will also be considered and optimised to ensure 
products can be evacuated. These enriched data flows will feed the Available-to-Promise (ATP) and Demand Management (DM) 
tools, enabling smarter logistics planning through improved inventory grouping and return trip optimization. All tools will follow a 
user-centric design, co-developed with operators to align with Industry 5.0 principles of sustainability and inclusivity. 
RIVER will further differentiate itself from standard OEM solutions and specialized scheduling software by embedding 
reinforcement learning (RL) techniques into the scheduling logic. RL agents will autonomously optimize dispatching and 
production sequencing based on live MES/MPS data and multi-objective reward functions (e.g., throughput maximization, energy 
efficiency, delay minimization), trained within a digital twin environment. This adaptive, learning-based approach enables dynamic 
rescheduling in response to real-time disturbances (e.g., delays, machine failures), going beyond the static, rule-based heuristics 
or deterministic optimizers typically offered today. Additionally, RIVER’s integration of deep forecasting models 
(LSTM/Transformer) for demand and process prediction will allow predictive adjustments across the entire planning -execution 
continuum, a capability rarely available in current steel sector scheduling tools. 
Block 2: AI for Market Forecasting and Planning 
This block introduces AI/ML-based forecasting tools that move beyond historical analysis to deliver forward-looking, multi-horizon 
insights. With up to a 12-month forecasting range, the tools will cover market demand and procurement variables such as scrap  
and energy prices, as well as external logistics optimisation. Forecasts will be integrated into a user-friendly dashboard alongside 
key production data, enabling all stakeholders to make faster, data -driven decisions. This approach ensures that productio n 
planning is more responsive to market volatility and operational constraints.  
Unlike traditional forecasting systems which rely primarily on time-series extrapolation, RIVER will implement hybrid deep learning 
models (e.g., LSTM -Prophet ensembles and Temporal Fusion Transformers) capable of learning non -linear dependencies and 
integrating exogenous variables like macroeconomic indicators, regulatory changes, or commodity futu res prices. This provides 
a richer, context-aware predictive capability. In addition, RIVER will embed explainable AI (XAI) techniques to ensure transparency 
and trustworthiness of forecasts, enabling users to understand the drivers behind each prediction —a clear differentiation 
compared to existing "black-box" solutions.

Sub-challenge 1.1: Shedding Light on the Production Black Spot to Enhance Traceability, Optimization, and Forecasting 
Traceability in scrap sourcing is essential for EAF -based steel production, ensuring material quality, regulatory compliance, and 
support for responsible, sustainable sourcing. It enhances efficiency and sustainability by improving knowledge of scrap 
composition. Achieving the optimal scrap mix is a major challenge for the sector, as it impacts energy use, by-product generation, 
steel quality, and production economics. In the ALCHIMIA project, CELSA developed a smart inventory system and EAF model 
to optimise MS process. However, a key challenge remains: improving the accuracy of scrap information at the scrap bay, which 
is critical for determining the charge mix and ultimately the melting parameters and the quality of the cast billets.  
Solution: RIVER proposes the following solution: enhancing scrap inventory management and charge mix determination by 
integrating new computer vision data and use it to upgrade the EAF model, which will be integrated with the rest of furnace 
optimisation models boosting impact in resource efficiency. Better input control will reduce variability, cut energy use, optimize 
alloys, lower costs, and strengthen CELSA’s environmental and market position.

radar/vision fusion and whose Z (height) is verified by the crane grab encoder. This yields near -real-time volume and weight

Sub-challenge 1.2: Advancing Predictive Quality Forecasting in Steel Production 
Quality forecasting in steelmaking is crucial not only for meeting customer specifications, reducing defects, and optimizing 
production processes, but also for improving production planning by minimizing disruptions caused by quality issues. However,  
accurate quality prediction is challenging due to the inherent variability of input materials like scrap, the complexity of the melting 
process, and the numerous interacting factors influencing final steel properties. 
Quality forecasting in EAF steelmaking currently combines statistical methods and machine learning techniques. Traditional tools 
like Statistical Process Control (SPC) monitor key parameters but face limitations with complex, high -dimensional data. 
Regression models predict steel properties based on process variables, while advanced machine learning methods such as neural 
networks, support vector machines, and random forests improve accuracy by capturing complex relationships and adapting to 
changing conditions. Additionally, real-time sensor data and analytics enable online quality prediction and control. Despite these 
advances, challenges remain around data quality, model interpretability, and robustness, with ongoing research focused on 
creating more expl ainable AI models, incorporating uncertainty quantification, and enhancing adaptability to varying conditions 
and steel grades. 
Solution: RIVER will develop an AI -based quality forecasting system for steel production at BCN, inspired by the approach 
explored in the ProcTwin project but adapted to the specific needs and complexity of the site. The solution will integrate advanced 
numerical modelling, real -time sensor data acquisition, distributed machine learning, and data mi ning techniques to monitor, 
predict, and control product quality throughout the steelmaking process.

Sub-challenge 1.3: Forecasting in a BANI Framework 
In the BANI context, traditional linear and static forecasting approaches struggle to capture rapid changes and interdependencies. 
The brittle nature of market conditions means small shocks can cause disproportionate impacts, while anxious market sentiment 
leads to sudden shifts in demand and supply. Nonlinearity arises from complex feedback loops between steel demand, energy 
costs, and raw material availability. The incomprehensibility of these intertwined factors requires advanced modelling techniques 
that incorporate real -time data, scenario analysis, and adaptive learning to improve resilience and responsiveness. Most 
steelmakers still depend on outdated approaches that struggle in today's volatile BANI environment. Static regression models fail 
to handle sudden market disruptions, while rule -based heuristics cannot capture critical nonlinear relationships like how energy

shocks through ensemble forecasting and adjusts  to sudden demand changes via reinforcement learning that updates 
procurement and production in real time. Multi -agent digital twins simulate thousands of scenarios to optimize cost, quality, and 
efficiency. Automated retraining ensures the system stays effective as new patterns emerge.

Sub challenge 1.4 Multi-variable integration models for the development of the AI-powered Adaptive control-tower 
To fully leverage the developed monitoring, control, and forecasting tools, they must be integrated. It is a significant scie ntific 
challenge due to the need to combine multiple forecasting models that predict many different but interconnected aspects of t he 
steel supply chain. Variables such as demand forecasts, scrap prices, energy costs, CO₂ emissions, and inventory levels interact 
in complex, often nonlinear and conflicting ways, making it difficult to prioritize or model their dynamic relationships. For instance, 
increased scrap availability might lower procurement costs but raise CO₂ emissions due to transportation, while shifts in demand 
forecasts can prompt faster production that increases energy use. These variables evolve over time and influence each other i n 
ways that traditional forecasting struggles to capture. The challenge is to d evelop resilient, adaptive multivariate models that 
integrate diverse datasets, account for nonlinear dependencies, and adjust dynamically without fixed variable prioritization.  
Advanced machine learning methods like ensemble models, deep learning, and Bayesian optimization offer promising approaches 
by learning from historical data and maintaining temporal consistency. Yet, creating a unified framework that balances competing 
objectives and uncertainties remains an open research problem requiring sophistic ated scenario analysis, real -time data 
integration, and multi -criteria optimization. Successfully addressing this challenge is essential for steelmakers to make agile, 
informed decisions amid the tightly coupled and rapidly changing dynamics of demand, raw materials, and energy costs. 
Solution: RIVER will develop a unified, adaptive multivariate modelling framework that integrates diverse forecasting tools across 
the steel supply chain, capturing interdependencies among demand, scrap prices, energy costs, emissions, and inventory. Instead 
of isolating  variables, the system will leverage multi -objective optimization algorithms, dynamic Bayesian networks, and 
reinforcement learning to model real -time interactions and feedback loops between these factors. Central to this architecture is 
the AI-powered Control Tower, which consolidates demand, procurement, energy, and emissions forecasts into a cohesive 
structure. Advanced techniques such as generative AI, Pareto-front optimization, and uncertainty quantification models will enable 
the generation of multiple planning scenarios, explicitly balancing competing objectives such as cost, CO ₂ emissions, lead time, 
and service level. Rather than passively predicting outcomes, the system will simulate counterfactuals and propose prescripti ve 
strategies that reconcile conflicting KPIs under uncertainty. Continuous online learning modules will recalibrate models using live 
data streams, ensuring that the scenario engine dynamically adapts to evolving market and operational conditions. This approach 
empowers decision -makers with a resilient, mathematically grounded platform for agile, optimal plann ing in a volatile, 
interconnected steelmaking environment.

2.1.1 Outcome 1 – CO2 reduction achieved in the steel sector or target group  
Unique Contribution: RIVER aims to deliver a step-change in EAF steel decarbonization by embedding AI across the melt shop, 
rolling mill, and supply-chain planning. Currently, EAF-based steelmaking emits around 0.35-0.9 t CO₂ per tonne of steel, with 
approximately 25% coming from anthracite and natural gas consumption, ~25% from electricity consumption and another 50% 
from lime and scrap use, logistics, and other indirect sources. RIVER’s integrated AI-driven approach targets a data-backed and 
replicable 2.7% CO₂ reduction (around 13.6 kg CO₂/t, considering an average emission of 500 kg CO ₂/t) - a significant gain 
at scale - delivering both environmental and cost benefits, while laying the foundation for more ambitious future reductions as the 
AI tools are enhanced over time. Importantly these reductions can be achieved without major infrastructure changes, 
demonstrating how targeted, intelligent optimisation can deliver real environmental impact at scale and serve as a foun dation for 
continuous decarbonisation. Three primary levers drive this impact. 
1. Increasing energy and material efficiency through process-level optimization and strategic scheduling combines 
advanced scrap mix determination and energy -flow models for EAF, LF, and reheating furnaces together with dynamic 
hot-charging schedules while minimising idle reheating periods. Simulations show that this can cut specific energy 
consumption up to 2 % and together with quality forecasting tool and improved traceability, increase metal yield by 1%. 
This ~3% reduction in scope 2 and ~2% reduction in scope 1 emissions translates into approximately 6.2 kg CO₂/t saved 
(considering an average 250 kg CO ₂/t for scope 1 & 2 emissions in EAF steelmaking). This effect is easier to seize at 
the construction-sector pilot (BCN site), enjoying larger batch flexibility and availability of several rolling mills, while the 
automotive pilot (GSW site), despite tighter quality requirements, still achieves around a 3.5 % improvement. 
2. Maximising the share of renewables by improving coordination with the electricity market uses AI to align furnace 
operations with renewable energy windows. By shifting 4 % of heats into lower-carbon tariff periods, a further 3 % CO₂ 
saving in scope 2 (assuming 4  kWh/heat, 0.10 kg CO₂/kWh grid intensity in Spain and 0.02  kg CO₂/kWh in low‐carbon 
hours), equivalent to around 3.7 kg CO₂/t. The same lever - the timing of energy use - applies equally in both the MTS 
(BCN) and MTO (GSW) frameworks, so both sites can achieve this target despite different order‐fulfilment logics.  
3. Logistics optimization  reduces emissions from inbound scrap logistics (~15kgCO2/t) and outbound billet transport 
(~74kgCO2/t). The increased rate of local scrap purchasing can reduce by 6% inbound transport emissions while 
integrating load consolidation, routing optimization, and backhauls RIVER reduces transport-related emissions by up to 
4%. Given that these logistics represent around 30% of scope 3 emissions (average scope 3 = 250 kg CO₂/t), this adds 
another ~ 3.7 kgCO₂/t reduction. While a 4% cut in outbound transport emissions proved achievable under the MTS 
system, it was far more challenging in the MTO framework because stricter delivery ‐date requirements leave less 
flexibility for load consolidation and routing optimization. 
Together, these three levers contribute the 2.7% average reduction in CO ₂/t, achieved without major CAPEX, supporting both 
near-term ROI and long-term decarbonisation. 
Scale and significance & target groups: When combined, these measures are expected to deliver a total CO₂ reduction of 11.3 
kgCO₂/t in BCN and 13.5 kgCO₂/t in GSW, validated through dual-layer Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) methodologies (ISO 14040–
44), under multiple operational scenarios. At industrial scale (1.5 Mt/y in BCN and 0.7Mt/y GSW), this translates into approximately 
17 000 t CO ₂ saved annually at the construction -sector pilot site, and around 9 500 t CO ₂ at the automotive -sector pilot site, 
depending on operational flexibility. At full dep loyment across CELSA sites (average production of 3 .5 Mt/y), this translates into 
>47 000 tonnes of CO ₂ saved annually  due to energy -efficient operations, optimised material use and improved 
coordination with market.

Building upon T5.2, AQUI in T5.3 will extend the Control Tower’s scenario engine by adding 
two new decision -support modules: one for scrap procurement and one for energy -cost

requirements, predicted scrap-market prices, and supplier performance data. It then runs an 
optimization routine that determines, for each production plan, the best combination of sc rap 
grades, supplier allocations and distance, purchase volumes, and total cost. The user interface 
will display , for each time window or heat , the candidate scrap types, the recommended 
supplier, the tonnage to order, distance and the estimated cost. In the energy-cost optimization 
module, the system combines the same production forecasts with electricity price projections. 
It calculates the trade-off between running more tons through the EAF/LF at different hours or 
shifts and the corresponding energy  cost. Results are presented at daily, monthly, and per -
shift levels. The HC algorithm (T3.4) will be upgraded leveraging on the enhanced forecasting 
capacity of critical parameters for HC planning (e.g. electricity vs gas hourly prices). Planners 
can select the production schedule , hot charging rate and scrap purchasing mix that best 
balances cost savings against output targets.  As in T5.2, CLO will validate the integration by 
comparing the model outputs with historical data and current operational metrics.

4.1 Ethics  
Ethics  
Describe ethics issues that may arise during the project implementation and the measures you intend to take to solve/avoid them. 
Describe how you will ensure gender mainstreaming and children’s rights in the project activities. 
AI Ethics and Governance 
To ensure the ethical development and deployment of AI within the project, we will adopt a framework fully aligned with the 
principles outlined by the European Commission and UNESCO, focusing on human agency, transparency, fairness, and 
accountability. All AI models (ranging from demand forecasting to scheduling algorithms) will be subject to a robust ethics -
by-design approach. This includes pre -deployment risk assessments, documentation of model decision pathways, and 
importantly, integration of explainable AI (XAI) methods to ensure decisions are traceable and interpretable by both technical 
and non-technical stakeholders. Human oversight will be a mandatory component in all high -stakes decisions, especially 
those influencing labor allocation, pricing, or access to resources. 
Furthermore, data governance throughout the project will adhere strictly to GDPR and relevant national data protection 
standards, ensuring the use of anonymized and ethically sourced datasets. During model development, particular attention 
will be given to  identifying and correcting bias through design -stage interventions, including the careful selection of 
representative training data and the implementation of machine learning techniques. This is especially crucial for all models  
within the RIVER project as these have a direct influence on decision -making process. In parallel, all system outputs will be 
continuously monitored for consistency with ethical guidelines, with clear documentation of assumptions and limitations to 
support responsible use . As mentioned thoroughly in the proposal, the system always includes a human -in-the-loop for 
decision traceability to safeguard user autonomy and to reduce the risk of any possible algorithmic harm.
