Introduction: Aligned with the scope of HORIZON-CL4-INDUSTRY-2025-01-TWIN-TRANSITION-37 Solving issues in carbon-neutral iron and steel making processes with diverse input materials of varying quality, the overall goal of NextSteel is to validate innovative low-CO₂ iron and steelmaking solutions by enabling the use of diverse, lower-quality biogenic residues in core steelmaking production routes—namely Blast Furnace–Basic Oxygen Furnace (BF-BOF), Electric Arc Furnace (EAF), and Direct Reduced Iron (DRI). The project will develop industrially scalable processes for the production of bioanthracite and green hydrogen from biowastes and validate their controlled use as alternative reductants in BF-BOF, EAF and DRI, addressing their impact on process stability, product quality, and emissions reduction.
NextSteel proposes an advanced integrated system, based on innovative thermochemical processes, to convert biowastes into high-performance/quality biobased reductants, reducing production costs and ensuring high product sustainability by maximizing the energy efficiency, and combining the recovery of biogenic carbon with the extraction of critical and strategic minerals, while also addressing the impact of replacing fossil-based reducing agents with biobased alternatives on input materials, processes, and the quality of the final iron and steel output. A range of heterogeneous feedstocks will be targeted including: (1) sewage and industrial sludge; the organic fraction of municipal solid waste (fresh, digested; digestate from manure and agricultural residues; contaminated urban biowaste; and various agri-food wastes, such as tomato waste, vinasse, and rice husk. Figure 1 provides a high-level schematic of the NEXTSTEEL approach, whereby the technological routes involve Fast Hydrothermal treatment (HTT) to pretreat the biowaste, separating the biogenic carbon and elements in two main streams: a carbon-rich solid (hydrochar) and a liquid phase with high organic carbon content. In addition, two refinery lines will be investigated and scaled up for maximising the valorisation of the biogenic carbon. A bioanthracite line aims to transform the solid (hydrochar) into a low-ash, high quality bioanthracite, involving the following processes: 1) Chemical leaching of raw hydrochar to extract over 99% of calcium (Ca) phosphorus (P), reducing ash content; 2) Slow oxidative pyrolysis at temperatures from 600°C to 900°C to turn hydrochar into a stable, bioanthracite lkali-aided pyrolysis followed by hot washing to maximize the ash extraction  inorganic content of feedstock with high Al and Si. A green gas line aims to transform the liquid phase into green hydrogen via three processes: 1) Supercritical Water Gasification (SCWG) of the HTT liquid to generate hydrogen-rich gas, with optional catalytic enhancement; 2) Anaerobic digestion (AD) to turn the biogenic organic carbon from the HTT liquid into biomethane, coupled with 3) Methane pyrolysis using bioanthracite as catalyst, to produce green hydrogen and to obtain C-enriched bioanthracite as a spent catalyst.
The resulting bioanthracite and green hydrogen will be integrated into three Low-CO2 production routes:
Coupling H2-based direct iron reduction with EAF using biobased gas & biogenic anthracite to produce pig iron
Replacing coal in scrap metal EAF and BF by biogenic anthracite (bioanthracite)
Replacing coke and SiO2 in silico-manganese alloy production by producing a C-SiO2 rich bioanthracite from sewage sludge.
Research will address the system-level challenges in these routes, optimising and validating the process adaptations required for stable operation, emissions reduction, and consistent product quality under variable feedstock conditions. The integration of the three routes in steelmaking sites will be also investigated, in order to maximize the energy efficiency of the new Low-CO2 production processes. This will be supported by a modular digital intelligence layer (HoloSteel) combining Process Analytical Technologies (PAT), Quality by Design (QbD), and a holonic agent-based digital twin enabling advanced monitoring, characterisation, and control to ensure system-level reliability, product consistency, and operational resilience. This introduces a strong Industry 4.0 dimension, whereby HoloSteel will enable real-time monitoring, simulation, and predictive control to manage feedstock variability and support optimal decision-making at plant level, while enhancing transparency, responsiveness, and system resilience across steelmaking operations transitioning to low-CO₂ pathways.
An Industry 5.0 approach complements this by embedding human-centric innovation, skills development, and strategic capacity-building. This includes the co-creation of new operational roles and targeted training programmes with input from plant operators, industrial partners, and social stakeholders, ensuring alignment with real-world needs. Both technical operators and business decision-makers will be equipped with the knowledge and tools required to manage process variability, digital interfaces, and alternative feedstock strategies. Business decision-makers will benefit from interpretable data visualisation and scenario-based modelling provided by HoloSteel, supporting investment planning and strategic oversight. A robust business case will underpin the proposed low-CO₂ process routes evaluating techno-economic performance, operational risk, and market adoption scenarios. Environmental performance will be systematically assessed. This holistic approach (combining technology, workforce evolution and strategic foresight) ensures steelmaking decarbonisation is not only technically feasible, but also economically viable and socially sustainable. Combined, these innovations represent a viable pathway to reduce dependence on fossil-based reductants, with the potential to cut greenhouse gas emissions by up to 24 Mt CO₂ annually by 2050.

State of the art of chemical leaching of solid products for ash reduction and inorganics recovery: The recovery of phosphorus (P), as well as other inorganics, from hydrochar or biochar has already been assessed in different studies,, with successful results in P extraction and degree of demineralisation. The relevant ash reduction achieved by chemical leaching has 2 main impacts on the process: strong reduction of sludge solid mass with reduced energy consumption, and far superior quality of the obtained hydrochar. Experiments have shown that >9of P can be extracted by hydrochar acid leaching. In the case of chemical leaching of hydrochars containing high amounts of SiO2 and Al2O3, these cannot be effectively demineralised. In fact, acid leaching has been demonstrated to have no effects on the silicates present in the char  and, thus, a biocoal with high ash concentration is produced16.
Going beyond the state of the art: The process proposes an alternative solution to the literature to go beyond the state of the art and optimise the process. The hydrochar leaching will be performed by selecting acid mixtures with low oxidizing properties, so as to minimize the carbon leaching in the obtained liquid. Moreover, the hydrochar will be processed by pyrolysis using alkali reagents such as Na2CO3 and K2CO3. In fact, alkali reagents they react with silica and other inorganic compounds to produce soluble elements, such as K and Na-silicates. As such, the char obtained after pyrolysis will be washed to extract the solubilized inorganic compounds and obtain a biocoal with a limited concentration of ash and an increased amount of carbon.
State of the art in the treatment of the aqueous effluents resulting from the HTC process: According to both the literature and recent tests performed by the NEXTSTEEL partners, between 20 and 30% of the carbon content contained in the feedstock end up in the HTC aqueous phase. The aqueous effluent resulting from the HTC process poses a big concern, as it contains a high concentration of organic compounds that cannot be discarded directly in the environment. The complex mixtures of organic molecules are difficult to degrade by conventional methods (e.g., biological wastewater treatment).
In order to fully recover the biogenic carbon present in the biowaste, in NextSteel the aqueous phase generated from the HTT process will be treated through the SCWG process for two main goals: i) to produce a high calorific biogenic gas (rich in CH4 and H2) that will be used for heating the metallurgical furnaces; and, ii) to valorise the carbon content of the effluent, as a previous wastewater treatment process for improving the process sustainability. Two main routes will be investigated with and without a catalyst. The results obtained during the lab scale work will be compared with those obtained in the continuous SCWG pilot plant.
