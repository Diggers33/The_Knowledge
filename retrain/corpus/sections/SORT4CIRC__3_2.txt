1.1.1 Main objective / overall purpose  
The EU generates 12.6 million tonnes of textile waste per year. Currently, only 22% of Post-Consumer Textile (PCT) 
waste is collected separately for reuse or recycling, while the remainder is often incinerated or landfilled1. However, 
with the updated legislative directives on separate textile collection across the EU by 1 January 20252, the amount of 
textile waste collected will increase. To ensure the sustainable recovery of this material, upscaling sorting and 
recycling capacity and efficiency must go hand in hand. Sorting for reuse and recycling is the critical step of the value 
chain that decides the next best use and enables textile resources to cycle repeatedly through the supply chain (SC). 
Textile reuse clearly offers greater environmental and economic benefits than recycling, but its sorting is carried out 
manually and is associated with high labour and energy costs in the EU. Several brands and manufacturers are 
already incorporating recycled content in their collections; however, most are not using PCTs. To date, recycled fibre 
and fabrics made from PCTs are priced higher, intimately related to the high costs required to process them. 
SORT4CIRC’s overall ambition is to improve value creation and cost efficiency through automated sorting 
and innovative recycling. This will generate new revenue streams for sorters and recyclers by enhancing 
sorting for reuse and turning non -re-wearable textiles into valuable feedstock for high -value recycling. 
The project will strive to unlock the current technological gaps for textile identification and value- 
chain traceability while providing an overall sound business case driving circularity within the Textile 
Value Chain (TVC). 
The use of modern digital technology in the sorting process will make it easier to achieve comprehensive 
information on the materials and fabrication process step. This information can then be integrated into automated 
Pre-Sorting and facilitating Fine Sorting (PSFS) and Pre-Sorting and Sorting for Recycling (PSSR) processes. The 
different complementary technologies are interlinked with interoperable architecture to achieve complete traceability. 
The results generated are cross-checked to ensure reliability and consistency, enabling informed decisions about the 
optimal channel for reuse or recycling besides ensuring high quality input to reuse and recycling. 
In response to this challenge, SORT4CIRC will develop a systemic, transformative solution by pioneering, 
scalable solutions for PSFS & PSSR, coupled with traceability and ‘Design for Disassembly’ (DfD). An 
innovative solution that can be developed and deployed across the industry actors, maximising the impact 
on waste reduction and resource conservation. Fostering a more resilient and sustainable textile sector, 
reducing the industry's reliance on natural resources and decreasing emissions, aligning with the objectives 
of the EU Green Deal Industrial Plan3. The overall scope is described in Figure 1.

1.1.3 Main innovations and ambitions / progress beyond the state-of-the-art (SOTA)  
Ambition 1 – Automated PSFS sorting, recognition monitoring, measurement and classification technologies 
SOTA. Textile PSFS systems still rely heavily on manual decision-making processes. While some innovations to 
automate these processes have been announced and are in development, they have not yet been fully developed or 
launched4. Moreover, the PSFS needs to ensure effective mechanisms for high quality of cloth wear condition 
aligned with second-hand market and reuse market pipelines. The SOTA shows the gap for effective SKU based 
cloth categorization and high accuracy wear condition for reuse market5. 
Bottlenecks. Current manual PSFS processes make sorting subjective, focusing primarily on product type and 
quality. This limits resale options to traditional second -hand shops and export markets. To unlock new resale 
channels and prevent re -wearable items from being r ecycled, especially as separate textile collection in all EU 
member states, legally mandatory from 2025 on, will increase collection volumes by more than 80%, improved 
sorting quality and detection of more relevant product attributes are needed. Innovative computer vision solutions, 
including those from REFA, are being developed alongside free-fall detection approaches. 
To reliably assess the quality of a garment, more than 95% of its surface must be visible, which requires the 
garment to be fully unfolded. There are two distinct approaches to robotic handling systems: free-fall systems with 
separation and flat presentation systems. Both have significant limitations. 
1. Free-fall systems require robotic separation, where individual garments are extracted from a pile and 
placed onto a conveyor in random orientations. While this step alone already takes more than two seconds 
per garment, it does not solve the issue of unfolding. In a free -fall state, garments are not sufficiently 
flattened, making it impossible to derive detailed product attributes or assess the garment’s quality with 
the required precision. This leads  to unreliable detection and necessitates additional manu al sorting, 
reducing the overall utility and cost-effectiveness of the system. 
2. Flat presentation systems, on the other hand, ensure garments are fully unfolded and laid flat on a 
conveyor belt. This approach enables accurate quality assessment, as the entire surface of the garment is 
visible. However, achieving this flat presentation with robotic systems requires significant processing 
time. Current state -of-the-art robotic solutions need upwards of 30 seconds, often approaching a full 
minute, to unfold and flatten a single garment. These long cycle times are far slower than manual handling 
and make such systems economically unfeasible for industrial-scale sorting operations. 
While free-fall detection eliminates the need for unfolding, it struggles with image resolution for garments larger 
than accessories or kidswear. Tests by REFA revealed incomplete unfolding, leading to unpredictable flight paths 
incompatible with the limited depth of field of high-resolution imaging systems. High-resolution images are crucial 
for detecting defects and avoiding costly false positives, where reusable items are mistakenly recycled, impacting 
sorting plant economics and undermining waste hierarchy principles. Furthermore, due to the limitations in 
detection capabilities of free-fall systems, an additional fine-sorting step will still be required. In comparison, the 
fine sorting process can occur automatically when a garment is unfolded on a conveyor belt, making the free-fall 
solution economically less attractive. 
In summary, free-fall systems suffer from inadequate quality detection due to incomplete visibility of garments, 
while flat presentation systems face prohibitive processing times and costs. A semi -automated solution, where 
garments are manually unfolded and placed on a conveyor belt for automated quality assessment, offers a practical 
compromise. This approach is 30% more efficient than purely manual sorting and combines human dexterity with 
the precision of digital technologies. While robotic systems may improve in the future, their current state is 
insufficient to meet the demands of large-scale garment sorting. Any advancements in robotic unfolding could still 
be integrated into existing workflows, but for now, semi-automation remains the most reliable and cost-effective 
option.

leading to increased volumes for second-hand markets instead of recycling. 
Ambition 2 – Automated PSSR sorting, recognition monitoring, measurement and classification technologies 
SOTA. The existing processes for disassembling textiles are either manual or mechanical, but both involve a 
high percentage of losses ranging from 20-60% and are economically not viable6. PSSR processes have been done 
manually. In the recent years, several NIR spectroscopy solutions have been launched for a more accurate material 
composition detection due to new requirements from innovative recyclers7. 
Bottlenecks. Today, the pre-sorting stage is fully manual and labour-intensive. Manual PSSR chemical recycling 
requires precise identification and strict specifications. To supply these markets, it is necessary to develop 
automated or semi-automated sorting of the non -re-wearable fraction by fibre type and colour. Some integrators 
have started to use the hyperspectral technique for recycling. However, it still needs to be improved to determine 
the composition of garments with two or more mixed materials. It needs to be combined with other optical/photonic 
technologies to provide additional recognition functions like identifying black-dyed clothes and garments with low 
content (<3%) of elastane. The existing NIR technology faces limitations in accurately sorting textiles due to issues 
like the difficulties of penetration of multilayer textiles and the presence of carbon -based pigments. Integrating 
innovative techniques as a pre-recycling process and scalability to the available recycling technologies is currently 
lacking. Mechanical disassembly does not offer a solution to multi-material/multi-layer products and reduces 
the fibre quality and recyclability rates. 
Innovation. New multimodal automatic PSSR monitoring: SORT4CIRC will provide an innovative and cost - 
effective textile analysis system for automated PSSR (for recycling using a co -integration of multimodal 
imaging/photonics technologies (Laser Induced Breakdown Spectroscopy (LIBS), hyperspectral imaging, Vis - 
NIR and short/mid-wave infrared (SWIR/MWIR))).While automated PSSR systems will record image data of 
garments to detect the presence of finishing chemicals, coatings or other undesirable components on the surface 
of functional textiles, enabling the separation of u nidentified components and accessories. It will detect and 
identify the characteristics of multi-layer materials in a textile article. Automatic sorting will classify garments and 
fabric structures and recognise textile materials. It will identify single or multi -component materials with the 
capacity to detect < 3% of other types of materials in the textile, even in the presence of black dyes or wear 
conditions or soiled textiles. Algorithmic strategies will be developed to closely match the actual composition of 
the garment as measured in the laboratory or to give better compositional estimates in the presence of low-content 
materials. The monitoring system will use advanced AI algorithms and deep ML to constantly increase its 
capacity for improving consequently the current technology will be the robustness and accuracy of the 
classification algorithm for complex compositions or new unknown compositions. A feedstock with a well- 
defined composition ready for recycling (mechanical, chemical, enzymatic fibre -to-fibre recycling) will be 
sorted and sent to recyclers for evaluation.

1.2.1 Overall methodology  
Due to the lack of the SOTA automated textile sorting technologies available for PSFS and the limitations of current 
NIR technologies used for PSSR such as identification of multilayers, material composition below 3%, black textiles 
based on carbon pigments, coatings and finishing chemicals, nylon6 and nylon66 identification, cotton and linen 
differentiation and fabric structure recognition, sorting systems must be upgraded to guarantee a reliable, affordable 
and accurate feedstock for second -hand markets and feedstock for recycling. With the learnings of using NIR 
spectroscopy for sorting PCTs at an industrial scale, the most significant challenges to ensure a reliable, high-quality 
feedstock allowing fibre -to-fibre recycling are precision in composition and colour recognition, automated 
categorisation and quality assessment of output material. Moreover, the heterogeneity of PCTs poses several 
challenges for textile disassembly and pre-processing due to the complex nature of textile structures and the diverse 
range of materials commonly used for performance and/or aesthetic reasons. Multi-material products represent 78% 
of the market (9% are multilayer, 69% are single layer with disruptors 16), requiring specialised sorting to identify, 
separate and recover individual components before recycling. While the second -hand market in the EU is growing, 
limitation in the current sorting technologies makes most second -hand clothes exported to developi ng nations, 
creating small value. This project addresses challenges in material composition, colour recognition, and multi - 
material textiles, increasing the availability of reliable textiles for the second-hand market and supporting its growth. 
While the recycling technologies need to scale up and further develop their processes to face the textile waste 
complexity, automated sorting using suitable robotics, sensors and AI technologies is required to meet their various 
requirements and specifications and thus facilitate the incorporation of textile waste into new yarns or products, 
increasing the circularity of textile products and limiting the use of natural resources. The lack of traceability of most 
textiles carries the risk of re -introducing unwanted textiles into the system, preventing a true circular value chain. 
Tracking and tracing technologies such as blockchain integrated with TTRFID tags can support addressing this issue. 
Therefore, structured and accessible data on material composition and substances of concern are important 
information for appropriate sorting and recycling. The practical application of tracking and tracing technologies is 
thus of utmost importance to sorters and recycler s. Also, to ensure the efficient implementation of innovative 
solutions, it is essential to analyse the TEF and LCA of upscaled processes in conjunction with developing viable 
BMs. SORT4CIRC overall methodology has been illustrated in Figure 2, and it will tackle the following challenges:

Garment type and wear condition determination: 
The current process for determining garment type and wear condition is fully manual and labor-intensive. Particularly 
for products entering recycling streams, revenues often do not cover sorting costs. Automating this process is essential 
to establishing a cost-effective circular economy (CE) model. The proposed AI-based technology supports both pre- 
sorting and fine sorting by automatically detecting product types and wear conditions (e.g., holes, pilling, and 
discoloration). Garments are spread on a conveyor belt, scanned from both sides with high -resolution imaging, and 
sorting software assigns them to appropriate categories. The items are then automatically directed into the correct 
bins via a switch system. 
To ensure technical robustness, the AI systems utilize machine learning models trained on diverse, high -quality 
datasets that account for the variability in garment types, wear conditions, and additional features such as style and 
fiber composition. High -resolution imaging is rigorously tested under industrial conditions to maintain consistent 
performance despite challenges like dust, vibrations, and lighting fluctuations. Quality assurance measures include 
ML pipelines for continuous monitoring, automated retraining based on new data, and lifecycle management for 
sustained reliability. Additional mechanisms such as periodic calibration, benchmarking against industry standards, 
and fallback strategies (e.g., manual sorting or reclassification) enhance robustness in large-scale operations. 
Retrieval of product data without data carrier: 
Product data from e -commerce platforms and, in the future, from Digital Product Passports (DPPs), can support 
sorting decisions. The connection between a product to be sorted and its data can be established via data carriers 
embedded in the clothing item. However, many clothing items currently lack such carriers, and it is unclear whether 
all DPP-enabled products will integrate easily readable carriers in the sorting process. For example, hidden QR codes 
may not be readable under high-speed sorting conditions. 
To overcome this challenge, existing AI algorithms will be enhanced to recognize individual products and infer their 
SKUs using image similarity techniques. These systems will leverage advanced computer vision models and 
continuously updated product databases to match garments with their digital profiles. To ensure reliability, the models 
will be subjected to stress testing for edge cases, such as heavily worn garments or partial visibility. The integration 
of this product data into the sorting process will significantly improve sorting quality and efficiency. 
Facilitating the sorting for reuse: 
Current buyers of wearable clothing are mainly second -hand shops in Europe and exporters to the Global South. 
However, as collection volumes increase in Europe, second -hand markets risk becoming saturated, potentially 
leading to wearable products being recycled prematurely. To address this, new channels for reuse must be unlocked. 
Suitable options include retailers that currently only purchase new goods, as well as efficient repair and upcycling 
services. 
MAIN SUB-CHALLENGE 1: EXPANSION OF CONVENTIONAL TEXTILE SORTING SYSTEMS: To increase the reliability 
of the identification of black textiles, multilayers and interfering material, it is critical to enrich the conventional 
textile sorting systems with appropriate new measurement technologies. If different materials, impurities or 
production methods of garm ents cannot be adequately detected using conventional measurement technology, such 
textiles will inevitably harm the purity of the end product. SORT4CIRC will develop textile identification using a vision 
system to recognise and classify a broader range of textiles, including several optical techniques offering new 
detection functions (e.g. low-content materials, black C-based textiles, PA6/PA6.6, cotton and linen differentiation, 
multilayers, etc.). A demonstrator equipped with AI-based algorithms capable of classifying into 15 categories at an 
industrial speed will be tested first out of line using hyperspectral imaging systems and other methods. 
For optical techniques, experimental approaches will be mainly based on NIR imaging or spectroscopy. Still, the 
characterisation of black textiles, particularly those with carbon-based pigments, is very difficult due to the extremely 
low reflectance of such materials. This issue affects all experimental techniques based on reflection (including NIR 
spectroscopy/imaging & Raman). The potential of other techniques to solve specific difficult analytical problems 
will be tested. For example, CEA will perform a spectral analysis ranging from visible to long wave infrared using a 
Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) to define the best bands to classify carbon black textiles. This will be followed by 
mounting an adapted MWIR multispectral imaging to classify black textiles. CEA and IOM will also deal with (i) 
the identification of certain compounds with a minor concentration in the textile, such as elastane (content < 3wt%), 
(ii) the detection and identification of chemical contaminates such as oils, greases, varnishes, rust, etc., but also 
moisture (wet textiles), (iii) the detection and identification of finishing agents (e.g. flame retardants) and coatings.

The textile DPP methodological implementation will cover all the TVC data from upstream to the end consumer.

tailored for textile applications that is both eco-friendly and recyclable with the help of SCATTERERID project. The 
RFID tags will store and transmit comprehensive data about each textile item, such as material composition, lifecycle 
information, and CO2 emissions. The chosen RFID technology will be designed to withstand typical textile processes, 
including washing and heat exposure. In that sense, there are 7 possible TRL 4 alternatives: a bi-layer of fleece/non- 
woven17, an embroidering polyamide fully silver-coated HC1218, 2 distinct PCB types (Rogers 3003 and Rogers 58)19,

substrate22 and a nickel-copper-coated ripstop23. There will be 3 main key points to check: (i) contaminant detection 
on the final product; even a small amount of TTRFID chip embedded in the fabric could alter the recycling process; 
(ii) thermal resistance, methanolysis processes require high temperatures (>150°C) and can affect and degrade chips, 
this needs to be checked; (iii) RFID chip components recycling, to be fully integrated in the recycling process, 
materials used for RFID manufacturing should be recyclable and do not affect the recycling process.

process. The findings will also serve as input to the DPP token economy model. 
To estimate the SORT4CIRC value chain environmental performance, a LCA will fully comply with ISO14040 and 
14044 standards. The boundary limits determine the applied methodology for the environmental assessment. Initially, 
an ex-ante, attributional, gate-to-gate approach (starting from the point of receiving the textile – and ending at the 
point that the textile is ready to leave the sorting facility) will be employed to estimate the direct impact of replacing 
the existing sorting process by the innovative SORT4CIRC process. Subsequently, an ex-ante, consequential cradle- 
to-cradle LCA study will be performed to estimate the sequential impact and track the benefits derived from the 
implementation of the SORT4CIRC process to the whole value chain of the textile recycling process. Thus, the 
boundary limits will be expanded, including all the upstream (collection and transportation) and downstream 
(distribution, recycling, etc.) processes. The downstream processes depe nd on the further processing the sorted 
textiles (upcycling/downcycling/burning). The LCI data of all foreground and background processes will be modelled 
using the ecoinvent database v3.10. To quantify the environmental impacts and be aligned with the European 
Directives, the EU recommends applying the Environmental Footprint v3.1 method. Uncertainty analysis will also 
be applied to examine the accuracy of the environmental i mpact assessment. The purpose is to quantify the output 
values’ fluctuation (quantification of the error margin), which derives from the volatility or lack of knowledge of the 
input data, by developing non -linear uncertainty models and estimating statistic al quantities of interest. Therefore, 
the 1st step will identify potential errors and pitfalls propagated to the output calculated values. 
The economic and environmental analysis will eventually enable fair technology benchmarking of the developing 
SORT4CIRC process against other state-of-the-art sorting processes, including the existing one deployed by TXHO. 
Data about relevant technologies will be gathered through a literature review, and the key comparators will be 
defined. This comparison will help us to d etermine the breakeven sorting cost, identify the competitive position of 
the SORT4CIRC sorting process within the market and define the areas of improvement. 
MAIN SUB-CHALLENGE 2: DESIGNING INNOVATIVE BMS SECURING VIABLE CIRCULARITY OF COLLECTED 
TEXTILES OVER LONG-TERM THROUGH ECONOMIC EFFICIENCY: The TVC involves many stakeholders, including 
producers of new clothes, consumers, collectors, sorters, and recyclers. SORT4CIRC introduces new technology - 
driven features into this value chain, enhancing the efficiency of circularity by improving the sorting process for 
collected textiles. In turn, this supplies the post-consumer TVC with higher-quality and greater quantities of materials 
suitable for reuse or recycling while also opening new business opportunities that require precise configurations and 
strategic decision-making. Addressing this business challenge effectively requires the development of innovative 
BMs that establish the framework for stakeholder interaction and coordination throughout the value chain, which are 
essential for business success and viability. A key challenge to address in the future is determining the quantity of 
sorted re-wearable clothing to be distributed in the market considering the market demand, establishing appropriate 
pricing, and identifying the most effective sales channels. Another critical issue is assessing the financial viability of 
selling second-hand clothing alongside new collections for companies adhering to the EPR policy and evaluating 
how this policy might impact profitability. Understanding and analysing consumer behaviour regarding new 
collections and second -hand clothing is crucial, which necessitates further investigation. To overcome these 
challenges and to develop robust BMs, SORT4CIRC will apply analytical techniques (e.g., game -theoretical model) 
considering critical factors (e.g., market demand, market competition, consumer behaviour, price, etc.), which 
enables us to discover an efficient setting for the circularity of the sorted textile. 
MAIN SUB-CHALLENGE 3: OPTIMISING THE DESIGN AND OPERATION OF THE AUTOMATED SORTING SYSTEM 
FOR ENHANCED EFFICIENCY: Capacity planning of the new sorting system is challenging and crucial to ensure the 
system can handle expected volumes without bottlenecks. This includes accurately determining the number of 
facilities and staff, designing a practical layout, ensuring smooth human -machine interaction, and optimising task 
assignment and line balancing. To overcome this challenge, SORT4CIRC will apply a systematic, stepwise, and 
recursive approach, where each relevant facility design problem, including equipment selection, staff requirements, 
facility layout planning, and line balancing, is first dealt with separately in a reasonable sequence, mainly through 
the optimisation modelling techniques (e.g., mathematical programming). Collecting and combining the results from 
solving these problems leads to an initial system design. In the second stage, a fine-grained simulation model of the 
initially designed sorting system will be developed using FlexSim © Simulation software to assess the system's 
operational efficiency. This model can mimic the behav iour of a real -life sorting system by capturing detailed 
characteristics and incorporating dynamic and stochastic elements. The developed simulation model enables us to

primarily verify whether the designed sorting system operates smoothly and efficiently and to reveal any existing 
bottlenecks. Thus, if the designed system does not meet the design requirements and key performance indicators 
(KPIs), we can adjust the model  parameters directly in the simulation model based on the experts' opinion or by 
tuning the model’s assumptions serving the simulation model. This recursive loop continues until we develop a 
system that fits requirements and satisfies the KPIs. The applic ation of the simulation model is polyvalent in this 
project and is not limited to bottleneck identification and system efficiency checks. This model is also employed in 
comparative studies to evaluate the existing sorting system against the automated, tech nology-integrated system 
SORT4CIRC aims to develop. The visual and dynamic aspects of the simulation model are also precious in making

collective impact on the global economics of the sorting process. Specifically, the profit can be even four times higher

recycling material, giving consumers access to high-quality recycled textiles and garments.

2.2.2 Intellectual property management  
The IP strategy is based on principles included in a Consortium Agreement (CA) and is “proactive”. It consists of 
identifying all relevant outputs during the tasks implementation and establishing, with the partners having generated 
the results, the most appropriate strategy to protect (IP, non -disclosure), to exploit (further research project, 
commercial agreements) or to disseminate (open access publications, international conferences, website) the results. 
For any protectable result that may arise during the project, the following steps are carried out: (i) the partner notifies 
the consortium about the technical contents to be protected & related ownership rights; (ii) in the case of jointly 
owned new IP, the procedures for IP protection, use, and licensing will comply with the rules set in the Grant 
Agreement and described in the CA; (iii) Filing the related IP protection application is followed directly by the 
relevant partner(s), according to the perimeter agreed with other partners, and the perimeter of innovation. We have 
already identified in Table 4 results that could generate IP, as well as internal know-how, knowledge, expertise and
