1. Excellence  
In line with the European Green Deal, the Processes4Planet partnership and the European initiative for more 
affordable, secure and sustainable energy (REPowerEU), the RIA proposal POWER2HYPE aims to establish a 
sustainable route for the hydrogen peroxide production.  Based on air and water as feedstocks, and renewable 
energy as the sole energy source, the POWER2HYPE route addresses an economically viable and green alternative 
to the classical energy -demanding anthraquinone oxidation (AO) process. Hydrogen peroxide will be produced by 
paired electrolysis at both the cathode and anode using an innovative custom -made 200% electrolyzer. Aiming the 
electrification and decentralization of the chemical industry , the POWER2HYPE concept proposes the TRL 6 
demonstration of electrolytic hydrogen peroxide production at any given concentration (from 20 to 99 %) at 
the site where it is needed . With this flexibility of concentration range, hydrogen peroxide could be used from 
chemical intermediates, sanitation, bleaching to niche markets as propulsion fuels.

Hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) is a widely used commodity chemical with a market evaluated at 3.6 billion € in 2020 and 
forecasted to grow to 4.7 billion € by 2026. 1 It has a strong oxidizing capacity, and releases only H 2O and O 2 as 
sideproducts. Industrial applications include wastewater treatment,2 bleaching,3 sanitation,4 chemical synthesis5 and 
aerospace fuels.6 Thus, H2O2 is listed among the 100 most essential chemicals globally .6 The H2O2 production 
capacity in Europe reached 1.2 million tons (calculated as 100 % H2O2) in 2020, i.e. 35 % of the global production.7 
Worldwide H2O2 production is expected to continue its growth trend, reaching 7 Mtons/y by 2030.8 POWER2HYPE 
partner Solvay, as leading global H2O2 producer and supplier, commands half of the current whole European 
production capacity alone.9   
The current industrial production of H 2O2 is predominantly (95%) based on the alkylanthraquinone autoxidation 
process (AO),10 which is considerably energy demanding (4.4 kWh/kg H 2O2,11 mostly coming from fossil fuels 
and amounting up to 6.2 109 kWh/year of energy consumption), leading to significant GHG emissions represent 2.4 
million tons CO2, 13 (i.e. 1.8%, to the total GHG of the chemical industry in Europe)12. Additionally, this process is 
highly centralized in large chemical units, limiting the supply of H2O2 to remote areas. Finally, the process involves 
sequential hydrogenation and oxidation steps of alkylanthraquinone (AQ) , which is dissolved in a mixture of 
organic solvents, followed by H2O2 extraction and distillation, with the subsequent generation of waste. Compared 
to the classical AO process, the new electrochemical route proposed by POWER2HYPE will: (i) achieve better 
overall energy efficiency (energy demand of 3 kWh/kg of H 2O2), (ii) use sustainable and abundant materials as 
catalysts and cell components; (iii) leave no subsequent generation of toxic waste /side products (vs. highly 
hazardous products in AO); (iv) use a low-energy downstream process (DSP) to produce hydrogen peroxide at any 
given concentration up to 99 wt.%; (v) improve the dynamics/agility of the process and large volume down-stream 
products (e.g. propylene oxide) in order to adapt to the intermittent availability of renewable electricity quickly; (iv) 
decentralize the hydrogen peroxide production and even in miniaturized and therefore cost-effective and 
lowerbudget production lines, taking in account the flexibility of electrochemical devices and its integration with 
widespread distribution of renewable electricity.

1.1.2 Project ambition & added value vs. state of the art (SoA)  
The anthraquinone oxidation (AO) process  
The AO process is a multistep process that requires significant energy input and a well -developed infrastructure. 
Moreover, it poses substantial risks associated with oxidation steps under pressure and the handling of concentrated 
hazardous chemicals like H2O2.13 This process can be divided into 4 steps, starting with the (i) hydrogenation of 
2alkylanthraquinone (AQ) with H 2 to anthrahydroquinone in a mixture of solvents using Pd -based catalysts. The 
resulting anthrahydroquinone undergoes (ii) oxidation by O2 to generate H2O2 and so is anthraquinone regenerated. 
This oxidation step is highly hazardous and requires a careful process design for risk mitigation.14 Subsequently, the 
(iii) extraction with solvents removes H 2O2 and the anthraquinone left in the or ganic solvent is recycled. This 
extraction step is crucial for the economic viability of the whole process, and unextracted peroxide can account for 
big losses in the AO process. Finally, (iv) crude H 2O2 is purified and distilled to reach the usual commerc ial grade 
concentration of 30-70 wt.% H2O2.  
Considering all the specific steps and the risks involved, besides considerable improvements and high process 
efficiency in large scales, the production of H 2O2 through this indirect AO process is rather  energy demanding 
with a consumption of 2.9 - 5.7 MJ/kg H2O2 (0.8 – 1.6 kWh/kg H2O2)15 plus 3.0 kWh/kg H2O2 associated with the 
generation of H 2.16 Moreover, highly concentrated H 2O2 solutions require special safety measures  for handling, 
transportation, and storage. In this regard, besides specific applications requiring high concentrated H 2O2, the main 
use of H 2O2 in bleaching and disinfectant requires 2 -8 wt.% H 2O2 solutions, with the consequence, that H 2O2 is 
diluted again after shipped at 50 -70%. Transportation in lower (safer) concentrations has the drawback of moving 
mostly water, thus negatively affecting the carbon footprint and economic viability of the final product. Therefore, 
the possibility of providing a direct a nd decentralized process for the H 2O2 synthesis would be safer, cheaper, and 
significantly more sustainable not only for the chemical industry but also for the society.

Electrochemical production of hydrogen peroxide  
The electrochemical production of H2O2 is based on water, air, and renewable electric energy as input. Thus, it is also 
representing an elegant green solution for onsite and on-demand H2O2 production, thereby avoiding risks associated 
with large-scale storage and transportation of highly concentrated H2O2. There are two electrochemical pathways for 
H2O2 production: the cathodic oxygen reduction reaction (ORR, Eq. 1) and the anodic water oxidation reaction 
(WOR, Eq. 2). Both pathways involve a two-electron transfer:20   
O2 + 2H+ + 2e- ⇌ H2O2  E0 = 0.67 V vs. RHE  (1)  
2H2O ⇌ H2O2 + 2H+ + 2e-  E0 = 1.76 V vs. RHE  (2)  
ORR and WOR reactions have already been explored as half -cells. The ambition of POWER2HYPE is to match 
both into one single electrochemical cell, thereby making optimal use of the electrons and obtaining H2O2 as main 
product at the anode and cathode.   
The cathodic pathway (Eq. 1) has already been known for almost 150 years 23 and reached a pre-commercial scale 
in the 1990s for the on -site production of alkaline H 2O2, utilizing carbon chips coated with a high surface area 
carbon/PTFE composite. 24 Major disadvantage was the alkaline environment in which H 2O2 decomposes more 
readily,21 and limits its application due to intensive DSP to obtain pure H 2O2. As for acidic electrolytes, noble 
metalbased (Pd-Hg, Au-Pd and Au -Pt-Ni) are the most active catalyst materials.  22,23 However, the scarcity of the 
platinumgroup metals and the environmen tal issues associated with Hg make the use of these type of catalysts 
questionable regarding sustainability. Carbon -based catalysts show relatively low activity under acidic conditions, 
however high activity and faradaic efficiency under alkaline condition s,22,26,27 which matches the cathodic 
environment in a dual membrane configuration. 37 For the improvement of the cathodic catalyst materials,  
POWER2HYPE aims for catalysts based on carbon with no noble metals. In POWER2HYPE, the interplay between 
the nature of the carbon, its surface chemistry and porosity, effect of a dopant and non -noble metal species on the 
performance of the carbon in H2O2 production will be investigated.  
The ORR involves a ternary interface reaction of gas (O2), liquid (an aqueous electrolyte) and solid (active catalysts). 
Such ternary interface reactions require the use of gas diffusion layers (GDL) . The majority of commercially 
available GDL materials are based on wet -laying technologies (carbon paper made from chopped fibers) since this 
technology features a sufficiently high productivity and the potential to achieve competitive costs.  There are few 
systematic investigations of GDLs in the cathodic production of H 2O2. The consortium partner SGL Fuel Cell 
Components GmbH is a leading manufacturer of carbon and graphite materials and will produce custom-made 
GDLs with tailored adjustments to maximize the three -phase boundary for maximum species conversion at the 
catalyst sites.  
The anodic pathway (WOR) has gained considerable interest in the last years. WOR efficiency, selectivity, and 
stability has remarkably improved, especially due to catalyst and process development.48 Several promising candidate 
materials for catalysts selective towards WOR have been identified,  including oxides such as BiVO 4, CaSnO3, and 
ZnO.24-25 Boron-doped crystalline diamond electrodes (BDD)  are nevertheless the most promising electrode 
material for highly efficient WOR. 26 BDD electrodes can suppress the most common side reactions in aqueous 
electrolytes: hydrogen and oxygen evolution in the cathode and anode, respectively. Figure 2 shows the BDD 
overpotential against hydrogen evolution reaction (HER, -1.2 V vs. SHE) and oxygen evolution reaction (OER, +2.5

Key approach of the POWER2HYPE process is to pair the anodic and cathodic H2O2 generation in one cell using 
non-critical and abundant materials only. This procedure adds substantial advantages compared to cells utilizing only 
one of the half -cells for H 2O2 generation, where the second electrolyte cannot be valorized and is often di scarded. 
The ambition of the POWER2HYPE proposal is to progress to larger -scale H 2O2 production targeting also 
applications requiring higher H 2O2 concentrations, which are to date not yet commercially addressed with 
electrochemical H2O2 production. Hence, POWER2HYPE focus specifically on applications where concentrations 
of 3% and above  are required. The process performance will be enhanced by reducing the specific energy usage, 
thereby offering an industrially relevant alternative for the AO process. To realize our ambition, POWER2HYPE 
will build on recent progress made by the project consortium.

1.2 Methodology  
1.2.1 Concepts and assumptions behind POWER2HYPE  
While the electrochemical chlorine production is a well-established technology in industry and hydrogen production 
is already available at pilot scale, electrochemical production routes for other chemicals are currently limited to low 
TRLs, many times with low efficiencies and low production volumes, and mostly restricted to niche applications. In 
POWER2HYPE the target is to develop an electrochemical technology to produce hydrogen peroxide, an 
important bulk chemical, essential for a wide range of applications. In combination with renewable electric 
energy sources, it aims to set another milestone towards the low-carbon electrification of the process industry.  
Electrochemical H2O2 production meets all requirements for a "green”, sustainable production process: the required 
educts are air (oxygen), water, and electricity. Both electrodes of the paired electrolyser generate the target product, 
and no by-products are generated besides low amounts of O2, H2O and H2. Neither the anode nor the cathode requires 
any critical or toxic raw materials. The electrolyser is designed for operation at high current densities of at least 400 
mA cm -2 to enable high production rates per electrode area. Nevertheless, the electrol ytic process with DSP (and 
downstream derived bulk chemicals) should be flexible to run under part-load or in intermittent operation (switchon 
and –off within few minutes and mainly determined by the periphery), e.g., when electricity from renewable resources 
should not be sufficiently available.  
  
  
Climate change targets and European dependence on fossil-based energies  
The main objective of the European Green Deal is to achieve carbon neutrality in the EU by 2050. As an important 
milestone towards this goal, the EU’s net greenhouse gas emissions should decrease by at least 55% by 2030. 
However, as energy prices have dramatically risen due to changes in the geopolitical landscape, the European  
Commission (EC) proposed on the 8 th of March 2022 an outline of a plan to make Europe independent from 
Russian fossil fuels well before 2030, starting with gas. This European initiative for more affordable, secure, and 
sustainable energy ( REPowerEU)42 seeks, among other measures, to rapidly adopt technologies allowing for 
replacing gas in power generation. Such joint effort can reduce the European demand for Russian gas by twothirds 
before the end of the year 2022. One of the pillars of the REPowerEU initiative is the fast reduction of fossil fuels 
utilization in the industry and power system by a) boosting energy efficiency , b) increasing the share of 
renewables in the energy mix and c) enhancing electrification of industrial processes.

Another important activity will be the development of a low-energy down-stream process (DSP) by SolvGe. The 
DSP consists of a device for concentrating the 3% and 10% H2O2 streams using the vapor pressure difference between 
hydrogen peroxide and water molecules. This is achieved by imparting a velocity differential between a gas and the 
liquid medium to promote a high mass transfer rate. Optimal mass transfer rates allow the process to occur at ambient 
conditions. The concentration reactor will be designed to ensure a high sweeping gas saturation rate, leading to a low 
energy consumption of the DSP. The properties of the sweeping gas can be varied to control the concentration rate 
and maximum output concentration, in a completely automated and flexible manner.  
TRSA, IRIS, SolvGe and Solvay will focus on the design, integration and testing of the electrolyzer and DSP system 
with the necessary auxiliary systems, fluid management, control and safety, electricity supply and management to 
pave the way for the demonst ration of POWER2HYPE pilot plant in Phase III. A digital twin of the demonstration 
will be designed by IRIS, and the process chain will operate virtually before the physical prototype, thereby finding 
important bottlenecks in the process. The H2O2 production will be integrated with the DSP to supply a single pilot 
unit that can be completely operated from one control panel.  
At the end of the second phase, the decision milestone 8 will define the status of the project . With this decision 
milestone it will be evaluated if the stacks can produce H2O2 in the targeted amounts (20 kg/d, 3 kWh/kg H2O2, 160 
% FE, 400 mA cm2) and the integration with DSP is accomplished.  
Phase III – M36-48, TRL 5-6  
The last phase of POWER2HYPE will concentrate all efforts into the demonstration of the reaction and process that 
turns water, air and renewable electricity into the production of H2O2. This activity is covered by Solvay and TRSA 
and represents the highlight of the POWER2HYPE project. The objective is the demonstration at TRL 5-6 of a 
viable and integrated electrochemical process in an industrial environment with the capacity of producing 20 kg/day 
(based on 100 wt.% H2O2). Although there is the potential to concentrate H2O2 up to 99 wt.%, the DSP will tune for 
a maximum of 70 wt% for storage-safety reasons. The whole process chain is to operate at efficiencies meeting and 
exceeding those of competing processes. Furthermore, in the European-wide roll-out of the technology, its connected 
economic, environmental and societal impacts will be assessed by EI, TRSA, IRIS and Solvay. IRIS will transfer the 
technical data into a virtual demonstration for extensive optimization modeling and predictive robust process 
integration and upscale. The time-resolved upscale approach provides the ability to follow the dynamics of fluctuating 
renewable power supply to be taken into account, so that a GHG -neutral process can also be ensured in operation. 
Technological learning curves considering component co st level will be designed based on literature data and 
POWER2HYPE results. This learning curve model will serve as input for an early TRL CAPEX, OPEX and 
economic viability assessment, including sensitivity analyses.

1.2.7 Open science strategy  
Open science practices (i.e. an approach based on open cooperative work and systematic sharing of knowledge and 
tools as early and widely as possible in the process) will be implem ented as an integral part of our proposed 
methodology and for increasing our chances of delivering our objectives, as follows:  
• Early and open sharing of research: publications in open access journals ensuring early sharing of publications 
(pre-prints) to satisfy open access requirements and enabling quick sharing of knowledge via deposit in 
repositories. Participation in open peer -review will also be encouraged to ensure transparency and support the 
shift towards Open Science.  
• Open access to research data  and other related research outputs  (e.g. to software, algorithms) will be 
promoted following the principle 'as open as possible, as closed as necessary' and our strategy is to ensure that 
Open Science and IPR Management and closely linked.  
• Research output management: all research outputs will be documented by the generators (researchers) of the 
outputs. Aspects as ownership, novelty, IPR protection, etc. will be established in a timely manner, and

The European Green Deal  
In line with the European Green Deal, POWER2HYPE shall be a positive force in the European chemical industry’s 
ambition to become climate neutral by 2050. Reaching this goal will only be possible with the help of climate-neutral 
and circular economy solutions developed by the industrial players themselves, in our case, the development and 
implementation of an electricity-based process to produce H2O2 that can benefit from a progressive decarbonization 
of the power sector.  
Moreover, the project will contribute to the EU Chemicals Strategy for Sustainability , which is the first step 
towards a zero-pollution ambition for a toxic-free environment announced in the European Green Deal. In line with 
the strategy’s aim to significantly increase the protection of human health and the environment from harmful 
chemicals, POWER2HYPE will develop a flexible technology that will produce H 2O2 on-site at nearly any 
concentration. This feature will remove the need to handle, transport and store highly concentrated H 2O2 solutions 
thus increasing substantially the safety of the process. No organic solvents are required in the POWER2HYPE 
production process and no critical waste streams are generated. A ll materials (including catalysts) are free of noble 
metals, thus avoiding upstream wastes, e.g. in mining processes.   
Moreover, the impact of a greener production of hydrogen peroxide on its end -markets will be substantial. As 
already introduced on Figur e 10, H 2O2 is utilized in several industries with a large carbon footprint that will be 
decreased thanks to POWER2HYPE. The environmental burden of conventionally produced H 2O2 that is currently 
propagated to the downstream processes where H2O2 is used, will be drastically reduced by shifting H2O2 production 
to POWER2HYPE technologies.

2.1.3. Barriers     
The following potential barriers for the industrial implementation of POWER2HYPE technology have been 
identified:  
• Availability of cheap carbon-neutral energy that represents a very substantial part of, or could even exceed, the 
low-carbon power capacities anticipated by the International Energy Agency (IEA) in 2050. This a general 
challenge for all Power -to-X technol ogies, also in the context of growing e -mobility and of other sectors 
transitioning to renewable energy base. This challenge can only be met through intensified scale -up of renewable 
electricity generation, particularly in regions with favorable solar and wind potentials. This scale-up must be a top 
priority of policies in the EU and in all member states.   
• Higher production costs related to using low-carbon technologies. This is also a general challenge for renewable 
alternative production pathways in comp arison with conventional fossil -based processes. Competitiveness will 
increase through rising prices of fossil feedstocks (such as currently induced by the Russian war in Ukraine) and 
common price volatility in fossil markets. Competitiveness will also inc rease through enhanced efficiency from 
technical learning curves, once the POWER2HYPE technology will be industrially implemented. Furthermore, 
regulatory frameworks, such as the EU ETS, provide an economic advantage for sustainable production pathways. 
Finally (but importantly), price premiums for “green” products are to a certain extent accepted by users who 
advertise the sustainability of their products.   
• Investment requirement. Large-scale roll-out of new production processes require substantial invest ments. The 
main barrier in this context is the risk and uncertainty related to such investments. Companies need to have the 
certainty that there is a stable market for new product, in particular in the light of a cost premium of newly 
introduced renewable products (compared to conventional fossil-based counterparts). Planning reliability must be 
provided through stable political and regulatory frameworks, clearly and reliably favoring truly sustainable 
production processes.   
• Manufacturing experience and prove of concept on a large industrial scale is missing . Key technologies for 
POWER2HYPE, such as the specific electrochemical cell, electrodes, membranes and the concentration unit, have 
not been developed and proven at scale. Even though electrochemical process and reactors in general have already 
been industrially implemented (e.g. in chlor alkali and water electrolysis), manufacturing of specific 
POWER2HYPE components has still to be established. To do so (up to pilot-scale) is one of the project’s objective. 
Beyond POWER2HYPE, more R&D efforts should be dedicated to the development of gas -diffusion electrodes 
(GDE) and related technologies (membranes, electrochemical cells, etc.), as such technologies are crucial for 
successful implementation of electroch emical conversion of gaseous educts. More dedicated R&D projects will 
help to readily provide the technologies when they are needed at relevant scale. An important gap to be filled also 
is to build a solid knowledge base on the investments cost to be expec ted when scaling the process further up. 
Techno-economic studies on the relation between CAPEX, OPEX, process performance, market and regulatory 
boundary conditions are crucial to better understand financial requirements and risks associated to industrial rollout 
of the POWER2HYPE technology and other electrochemical processes. For this reason, there is an entire work 
package dedicated to technology impact assessment (WP7) in POWER2HYPE. Such assessment studies should 
generally be part of all R&D projects on renewable technologies, processes and products.   
• Social acceptance of sustainable chemicals and awareness creation.  The social acceptance of the 
POWER2HYPE technology itself is not expected to represent a barrier, as it offers the possibility to replace  a 
largescale chemical production process that entails environmental risks and GHG emissions by a sustainable 
carbonneutral process generating now harmful waste streams. However, the POWER2HYPE approach requires 
substantial input of renewable electric ener gy. Consequently social acceptance of the scale -up of solar and wind 
power plants in conjunction with the POWER2HYPE approach are  addressed in a sociotechnical analysis to 
provide policy recommendations for a successful roll-out of the technology.

Focus on Europe  
In general, the market was negatively impacted by COVID -19 in 2020, however, the currently rising demand from 
the pulp and paper industry and increasing consumption of hydrogen peroxide as raw material for propylene oxide 
production (HPPO Process) are expected to drive the market growth. In 2018, the global market volume for hydrogen 
peroxide in the pulp and paper industry was 2,200.93 kiloton, and is estimated to reach about 2,820 kilotons by 2024, 
with a CAGR of about 5%, which will stimulate the demand for hydrogen peroxide in the coming years and make it 
one of the key markets for POWER2HYPE. More specifically, the European pulp and paper industry had a production 
capacity of 43.65 million ton in 2019, with a growth rate of 1.49% compared to the previous year, which, in turn, 
stimulates the market demand for hydrogen peroxide.  
In view of this  trend and in line with the POWER2HYPE implementation roadmap (Figure 11), exploitation and 
implementation activities will focus on these sectors and applications. Assuming that POWER2HYPE technology is 
applied to cover the newly generated capacity (5% CAG R) from the point in time when it has reached commercial 
scale (2030), the market share of this new technology can be projected to reach 40% of the total European 
capacity by 2040 (4.2 Mio tons/year).  
  
A global perspective  
Newer opportunities for hydrogen peroxide in wastewater treatment and the growth of the electronic industry in 
developing Asian countries are likely to create opportunities for the market in the coming years. Furthermore, the 
Asia-Pacific region is expecte d to dominate the market and is also likely to witness the highest CAGR during the 
forecast period.  
For instance, China is the largest pulp and paper producing nation in the world, producing about 125 million ton of 
cardboard and paper in 2019, with a growth rate of about 4% compared to the previous year, which, in turn, stimulates 
the regional market demand for hydrogen peroxide. The electronics industry in China is also the largest in the world. 
Due to these factors, the market for hydrogen peroxide in the region is expected to have a steady growth during the 
forecast period.  
By 2050, counting on having growth still in the European market (taking market share up to 60%), with new adopters, 
and assuming a conservative market penetration worldwide (taking over less than the new capacity created, 3% Associated 
with document Ref. Ares(2022)7691164 - 08/11/2022 
CAGR), we believe that POWER2HYPE technology could nearly displace the AO process, with over 80% of  
the capacity installed using the project’s technology.  
Competitors  
The hydrogen peroxide market is consolidated in nature, with the top five players occupying nearly 70% of the 
market. The majority of companies are focusing on expanding their production capacities, primarily in the AsiaPacific 
region. Some of the major players in the market include Solvay (key industrial partner in POWER2HYPE), Evonik 
Industries AG, Mitsubishi Gas Chemical Company INC, Arkema, and Kemira,, among others.

WP2 is dedicated to the development of advanced and industrially viable materials and cell compon ents for the 
electrochemical process and reactor systems. Specifically, materials-related research in WP2 is devoted to catalysts, 
gas diffusion layers/electrodes, ion-exchange membranes and BDD anodes.   
WP3 deals with the development and an efficient cou pling of anodic and cathodic reactions to achieve an optimal 
convergent paired electrolysis for H 2O2 production. WP4 aims at the design of an electrochemical reactor paired 
H2O2 production and at the development of the downstream processing for further H2O2 concentration.   
WP5 will focus on the development, building, commissioning and testing of a pilot plant to produce H2O2 using air, 
water and electricity. This pilot plant will be explored in WP6 to demonstrate the entire POWER2HYPE process for 
producing H2O2 under industrially relevant operational conditions, with a focus on technical feasibility, stability, 
load-flexibility, cost-efficiency and scaling factors, always in view of a future commercialisation. WP6 also includes 
tests to validate the applica tion of the produced H 2O2 in industrial processes (such as bleaching or epoxidation 
processes), in collaboration with associated industrial partners (H2O2 users).   
WP7 is dedicated to technology impact assessment, including prospective lifecycle assessmen t (LCA), 
technoeconomic analysis (TEA), examination of social acceptance and model-based (in silico) design of a scaled-up 
production system.

3.2  Capacity of participants and consortium as a whole   
The consortium of POWER2HYPE consists of 15 legal entities located in Germany, Belgium, the Netherlands, 
Austria, Italy, Spain, Portugal, & France. These partners cover the whole value chain of the H 2O2 production 
including key components development and production up to construction and operation o f H 2O2 plants. The 
complementarity of partners’ skills will ensure the upraise of POWER2HYPE technology from TRL3 up to TRL6 
within the project timeline. Key industrial partners will lead the further steps towards industrialization and 
commercialization beyond the end of the project.   
The consortium is composed of two Universities (Wageningen University, WU and Friedrich-Alexander-Universität 
Erlangen-Nürnberg, FAU) and three research institutions  (Fraunhofer Society, FHG; Wageningen Food & 
Biobased Research, WR; Energieinstitut an der Johannes Kepler Universität Linz, EI). FHG has vast experience 
coordinating European projects, communicating with EU project officers and an excellent record of accomplishment 
in anodic H2O2 production. WR has remarkable experience in ORR. Together, FHG and WR will pave the way for 
efficiently pair H 2O2 electrosynthesis. EI will communicate with all WPs to monitor and contribute to the 
sustainability and economic viability of the process. FAU and WU have outstanding experience  in carbon-based 
materials synthesis and characterization, thereby setting the ground for a sustainable electrocatalytic process. 
POWER2HYPE has ten companies on board, 6 industries, 3 SMEs and 1 startup. Among the companies are four 
entities of the indust rial Solvay group  (Solvay SA, SOLVAY; Rhodia Operations, RHDO; Solvay Specialty 
Polymers Italy SPA, SSPI; Rhodia Laboratoire du Futur, SRLF), bringing in expertise in both membrane 
development and H 2O2 production. The SME DiaCCon ( DIA) is a specialized developer of electrolyser and 
industrial-scale BDD electrodes. The company SGL Fuel Cell Components GmbH ( SGL) is one of the world’s 
leading manufacturers of carbon components as gas diffusion layers and bipolar plates. The SME  Iris Technology 
Solutions ( IRIS) and the industry Tecnicas Reunidas ( TRSA) support the consortium with strong skills in 
engineering and digitalization ( IRIS) as well as chemical plant engineering and piloting ( TRSA). The innovative 
start-up SolvGE (SLVG) is developing H2O2 concentrator units for downstream processing of raw and diluted H2O2 
streams. Finally, the SME GLOBAZ ( LOBA) provides extensive experience in dissemination and communication 
activities. More details are shown below in Table 17 on each partner’s key expertise and role within the project.
