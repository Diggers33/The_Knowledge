3.1 Work plan — Work packages, deliverables and milestones  
The project objectives have been the starting point for preparing a robust plan of the work to be implemented within 
this Innovation Action (Demo) project (Section 1.1), building upon past successful research. All actions have been 
analysed by the interdisciplinary consortium. It was agreed how best to distribute the workload among the experts in 
keeping with their relevant fields of knowledge, infrastructure, resources and in the context of brewery industry, 
bioplastics supply, packaging industry value chains and packaging waste management. A clear, comprehensive and 
realistic work plan  has been split into 9 WPs logically distributed along the value chain, with clear tasks, many 
interdependent, with very close interaction between the WP and task leaders (see Pert Diagram and Gantt Chart). 
Logistical matters like the time needed for order ing raw materials, shipping new bio -based materials/prototypes, 
installations, and possible scenarios of delays have been considered. To ensure the focus  towards the target 
applications all the technical WPs are led by industrial partners. 
WP1 deals with the production of new PHA based compounds and coatings for the specific packaging applications 
(food, beverage, homecare and cosmetics). Transfer of previous knowledge of the industrial partners to BioSupPack 
feedstock, derived PHB and PHA coatings will take place, considering a lab scale initial period for it followed by the 
biobased materials production at pilot scale.  Plasma technology  for feedstock´s pre -treatment and increasing 
fermentation yields will be implemented at pilot scale. In WP2, led by plastic converters partners, high-performing 
packaging prototypes will be produced at large scale using conventional technologies in the plastics industry, after 
an assessment of the processing ability at pilot plant of developed compounds and coatings in WP1 a nd with the

3.2.  Management structure and procedures. 
Organizational structure and the decision-making, including a list of milestones (table 3.2a) 
The Consortium of BioSupPack will be coordinated by partner AIMPLAS. The project management structure has 
been conceived to ensure the competent industrial, scientific and business management of the project and to enable 
efficient and effective integration  and communication along the managerial chain. The project success will be 
strongly based on the close interaction and partnership of SMEs & Large companies (65% of the partners in number) 
and the research and other organisations. 
This management structur e has been implemented successfully in similar EU Projects coordinated by Partner 
AIMPLAS (e.g. FP7; BRIGIT, BUGWORKERS and BISIGODOS, and H2020; JOSPEL, URBANREC, 
NEMOSINE, CARMOF, GRAMOFON, RECOTRANS, C -SERVEES, DAFIA and BIOnTOP), and as such, has 
been adopted by the BioSupPack 
Consortium with the latest 
improvements suggested by H2020.

and a confidentiality agreement will be signed with all its members.  
All boards have a majority representation of Industrial Partners as described below. An overview of the governance 
structure and decision-making process is also detailed below. The composition of the boards roles and the decision-
making mechanisms have been designed to ensure that all decisions are taken in accordance with the Industrial

runs as a “commercially” orientated project with a very clear business focus. Nev ertheless, there is the opportunity 
for all partners to express their opinion and canvas support through the GA who ratifies progress and decisions made 
by the management bodies.

the structure ensures that there is dedicated and qualified management and skills-sets mobilised at the different levels.

5. Ethics Issues
5.1 Ethics 
During the preparation of this proposal, in keeping with the required Ethics Appraisal Procedure, an Ethics Self-assessment was 
performed with the completion of the Ethical Issues Table, and this project does not involve Ethics issues except with the handling 
of personal data during the survey activities that will be held to gain insight on consumers and stakeholders’ perspective in WP3 
& 6.  
 
We will make sure that we keep with H2020 guidelines in terms of recruitment, informed consent, data storage, protection and 
destruction procedures (no persona l information will be collected during surveys, which will be anonymous, not involve minors 
nor any audio or video recording). 
Although the self -assessment identified no ethics issues arising from the actual workplan itself, it is worthy of comment the 
measures that we are taking during the project work plan to address and mitigate any concerns that could indeed appear in the 
future: 
• Regulatory and safety -related activities have been incorporated from the outset of the project with the intention of 
gathering data to fuel into a further safe and compliant development phase. In terms of product, safety aspects will deal 
with food safety regulations as most relevant to packaging but also with environmental toxicity to soil of biodegraded 
plastics. Furthermore, the use of a LCSA approach, which identifies environmental as well as health impacts, can help 
identify where the maximum impact co uld occur providing the opportunity for process or material modifications to 
minimise these impacts.  
• The appropriate health and safety procedures conforming to relevant local/national guidelines/legislation will be followed 
by staff involved in this project. In this sense, all partners follow recognised hygiene and safety rules, including chemical 
regulations such as REACH, which compliance will be studied in WP6 where it applies (polymers are exempt from but 
will be duly followed when dealing with additives, monomers and oligomers). 
• Finally measures related to responsible research & innovation, spread as relevant along the work plan, are overseen in 
WP8. Additionally, S -LCA will also be performed to encompass social & ethical aspects (like child work and g ender 
equity) (WP6) and ethical concerns of consumers regarding our bio -sourced materials or their EoL will be analysed in 
task 3.4 and task 6.3 (WP3 & 6). 
All in all, the developed materials are in fact expected to be safer for both the consumer (increased shelf-life, use of safe natural 
materials) and environment (less land filling, less depletion of fossil resources and less long term pollution by plastics) and more 
sustainable than their standard (synthetic) counterparts or even than some of th eir bio-based relatives- many of which are food 
competing, the ethics of which are questionable - thereby responding to growing consumers desires to make more responsible 
choices.  
Furthermore, in terms of end of life, as seen, currently only 32.5% of the p lastic packaging are currently recycled and a far too 
high proportion is landfilled (24.9 % on average) which, in the end, leads to direct and indirect pollution for example when plastics 
are blown away from their place of disposal.So, having access to sol utions with alternative EoL within a novel cycle business 
chain, -being enzymatic recycling of packaging waste as the best EoL, with the final recovery of carbon sources for 
feedstock fermentation, that we will promote while advocating for improved waste management of bioplastics that also 
generates increased value for our stakeholders- would represent significant socio-economic and environmental progress. 
Responsible plastics consumption is urgently needed and can no longer be done at the expense of the environment: plastics not 
only need to meet the techno -economical requirements of the target application, they also need to ensure sufficient 
sustainability criteria especially at end of life. Thus, the new packaging claimed under BioSupPack will be 
environmentally safe (sustainable and improved value from enzymatic recycling) and will contribute to Circular Economy 
(CE), respecting all the ethical principles.

2. Issues related to protection of personal data:  
 
Type: AFTER (post-grant requirements) 
 
Assessment: Survey activities on consumers and stakeholders perspective will be conducted in WP3 and WP6. Data will be 
generated by expert and industrial partners int erviews, brand owner and customer surveys and online consultations and 
pools. The involvement of consumers and stakeholders in consumer perception and marketing studies, including video 
observation, workshops, interviews, surveys etc. implies considerable data protection issues. In addition, in task 4.1, sensors 
will be placed on board trucks, which could infringe on the privacy of the truck drivers, and in task 4.3 QR and blockchain will 
be used for tracking products from manufacturing to consumer, which could imply data protection issues. The data protection 
is insufficiently addressed and general measures should be implemented. 
 
Measures: 
 
2.1. The host institution must confirm that it has appointed a Data Protection Officer (DPO) and the contact details of the 
DPO are made available to all data subjects involved in the research. For host institutions not required to appoint a DPO 
under the GDPR a detailed data protection policy for the project must be submitted as a deliverable. 
2.2. The beneficiary must explain how all of the data they intend to process is relevant and limited to the purposes of the 
research project (in accordance with the ‘data minimisation' principle). This must be submitted as a deliverable. 
2.3. A description of the technical and organisational measures that will be implemented to safeguard the rights and freedoms 
of the data subjects/research participants must be submitted as a deliverable. 
2.4. Description of the anonym isation/pseudonymisation techniques that will be implemented must  be submitted as a 
deliverable. 
 
These requirements are contractual obligations and will be implemented during the project (by the end of month 6 after project 
starts). These ethics requirements appear as ethics deliverables (D 10.1) and an extra work pack age (WP 10 - Ethics 
requirements) that has been automatically created by SyGMa.
