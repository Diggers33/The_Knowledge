1.3 Concept and methodology 
Concept 
Recover is a 4-year RIA project with 17 partners from 8 countries across Europe (Belgium, Italy, Spain, Germany, Portugal, 
Ireland, Luxembourg and UK). The consortium is very strong with plastic suppliers companies, inn ovative biotechnology and 
bioplastics companies and RTDs, as well as large end users. The proposal aims to develop biotech solutions for AWP treatment 
that will include plastic -degrading microorganisms, novel enzymes, insect larvae and earthworm which performance will be 
demonstrated in real plastic contaminated matrix both in ex-situ (composting, vermicomposting, reactor and insect rearing 
chambers) and in-situ (soil) experiments. In addition to compost and vermicompost a portfolio of added value products will be 
obtained including bioplastic formulations for application in agriculture ( mulching films, sticks, pots), food packaging (trays, rigid 
containers, films) and coatings based on chitin/chitosan extracted from insects rearing on AWP; biofertilizers from organic matter 
leftovers after AWP treatments and chitin extraction. A great attention will be dedicated to establishing simple and repro ducible 
conditions that can be replicated in the farms or municipalities as a profitable activity that can benefit the whole value chains from 
plastic producers to the end-users involved in the project.

of the plastic waste in Europe is currently recycled and the larger share is incinerated or even landfilled7 (high variability between 
countries). In the light of the EU’s policy object ive of achieving a resource efficient recycling society, it is hard to accept that in 
Europe we still landfill nearly 40-50% of plastic waste8. This all contributes to the depletion of limited resources and to the pollution 
of the environment. Besides thos e often non-optimal disposal routes, plastics, in particular packaging (bags, bottles, lids, etc.), 
account for 89-95% or roughly 8 Mtons of marine litter and 17 Mtons of plastic packaging is irreversibly dispersed on land every 
year worldwide9. Thus, the very slow rate of degradation of plastics leads to an accumulating pollution persisting for hundreds of 
years. Microbial biotechnology may contribute to this enormous challenge. The advances in our understanding of microbial 
functions from enzyme, to pathways, and entire metabolic networks now allow the engineering of complex metabolic functions in 
microbes10. With the ever-advancing tools from synthetic biology and the implementation of new strategies involving the use of 
microbial, insect and earthworm consortia, we can overcome the major challenges for plastic waste bioremediation and providing 
simultaneously a biotechnological plastic waste-based value chain for the synthesis of added value products, which will empower 
the recycling industry to a qualitatively new dimension. Proving biotech solutions for plastics EoL in our target sectors is of greatest 
relevance as they consume more than 40% of all plastic produced each year (packaging 40%, agriculture 4%) 11. As such, more 
research and development has to be invested and more cost-competitive solutions have to be found to allow their eventual large-
scale market-driven uptake.  
 
The RECOVER project will: 
 Develop a range of biotech tools with capabilities for degrading plastics wastes from agriculture and food packaging 
 Validate the use of the resulting biotech solutions for the bioremediation of soil and organic matrices contaminated with 
plastic and for the biotransformation of plastics into added value products 
 Progress in the use of the by products from the process (chitin, chitosan and organic leftovers) for the production of 
agricultural and packaging items and biofertilizers  
 Assess at each process step, the environmental impacts and economic feasibility in c omparison with existing 
benchmarks. 
 Provide recommendations for suitable business approaches for the new process and resulting added value products 
from an integrated bio-based value chain perspective

Overall approach and methodology 
Our approach and methodology involve two main components: 
1)    R&D and validation: Research activities with the main focus to deliver Biotech tools with AWP biodegrading/biotrasforming 
capabilities within a number of scenarios. Besides, the development and the validation work will focus not only on the 
applicability of the solutions for AWP treatment and their scalability, but also on their environmental, social and economic 
sustainability using e-LCA, s-LCA and LCCA methodologies among others. The safety and compliance with other applicable 
standards and regulations of the developed treatment and products will be studied. 
2) Innovation: This work will accompany and cross -cut the R&D wor k with the aim of preparing for post -project further 
development leading to the future replication of the project results. It consists of the following activities: 
a) guidelines and recommendations for future full -scale implementation of the proposed techn ologies via 
analysis of the new value chains, of the relevant ethical aspects as well as policy makers outreach. 
b) dissemination of the project results, including a series of activities to exchange knowledge between the stakeholders 
to validate their interest in the results of the project. 
c) exploitation and business planning to further increase the TRL of the developed processes and products beyond an 
expected final TRL5 to carry them over the notorious ‘valley of death’ towards commercial adoption. 
 
How sex and/or gender analysis is taken into account in the project’s content 
The RECOVER consortium takes a comprehensive approach towards gender aspects. Gender is relevant to the content of the 
project because of the significant variability between the cas e of food packaging wastes and agricultural films ones. Indeed, 
women are the main actors in the sorting of household wastes but also have a prevalent role in decision -making in the food 
industry whereas the presence of females in the agricultural sector i s traditionally limited , while t here is a strong presence of 
females along the value chain of the bioeconomy industries. As such, innovation will consider the gender dimension and cater for 
the needs and motivations of females where possible. The dissemina tion and communication activities will therefore also be 
tailored to both genders to maximise RECOVER technologies acceptance. In order to foster the future of the circular Bioeconomy 
in Europe we will act for the education and training of the next generat ion of scientists, technologists and others jobs needed in 
the EU. 
Besides in terms of gender dimension of the consortium, further discussed in section 3.3, the consortium is committed to foll ow 
the principles of gender mainstreaming in accordance with the  Treaty of Amsterdam (1997) and other EU policy directives. It is 
noteworthy that there is a strong presence of female researchers, technologists, and industrials in the consortium, a number of 
which are occupying key positions in the project. A recruitmen t policy for future researchers and developers for the project is in 
place and is designed to ensure a reasonable level of balance between the sexes and afford them with equivalent opportunities.
