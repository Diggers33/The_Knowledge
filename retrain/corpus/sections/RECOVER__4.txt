1.1.  The project summary
Project Number 1 887648 Project Acronym 2 RECOVER
One form per project
General information
Project title 3 Development of innovative biotic symbiosis for plastic biodegradation and synthesis to
solve their end of life challenges in the agriculture and food industries
Starting date 4 01/06/2020
Duration in months 5 48
Call (part) identifier 6 H2020-BBI-JTI-2019
Topic BBI-2019-SO2-R3
Apply microorganisms and/or enzymes to resolve end-of-life issues of plastics
Fixed EC Keywords Circular economy, Environmental biotechnology, bioremediation, biodegradation,
Microbiology, Enzymology
Free keywords
Agri-food waste plastics; microplastics; microorganisms; enzymes; insects; earthworms;
biodegradation modeling; chitin-based biopolymer; eBeam; compost; biofertilizer;
mulching film
Abstract 7
RECOVER is a disruptive RIA proposal built upon the actual needs to solve the contamination of agro-fields with
non-biodegradable agro-plastics as well as to improve municipal waste handling by decreasing drastically the
packaging fraction going to unsustainable waste management routes.
We intend to reach our ambitious objectives applying biotech solutions that will include symbiotic bioprocesses
between previously reported high powered microorganisms through microbiotic systems vectored by several insect
species. Indeed, one of the main innovations in our proposal is the combination of endogenous and exogenous
microorganisms and their inoculation on insects. This will allow achieving higher levels of biodegradation and direct
conversion (at the same time) of agri-food plastic wastes in insect by-products such as chitin, that can be converted
in chitosan (high value as bioplastics raw materials and other interesting industrial applications due to anti-microbial
activity) that will be used in active packaging, enhanced mulching films and biofertilizers applications.
RECOVER will help providing novel biotechnological solutions applying microorganisms, enzymes and insects to
degrade conventional plastic packaging and agricultural films waste streams but also result in new feedstocks for the
bio-based industries. The process will be optimized and performed either ex-situ in composting reactors (preferred
route for non-recyclable plastics from municipal solid waste or for agricultural films that can be collected easily) or in-
situ in the case of bioremediating soil pollution, by e.g. mulching films, further contributing to the long-term removal
of non-biodegradable polymers from the environment. In addition, RECOVER can solve the microplastics pollution at
the level of both industrial composting and soil.

1.3.3. WT3 Work package descriptions
Work package number 9 WP1 Lead beneficiary 10 12 - IDELUX
Work package title Agro-plastic waste (APW) mapping, characterization, collection strategy and pre-
treatment
Start month 1 End month 12
Objectives
● To quantify the currently non recovered Agri-food plastic waste (AWP) depending on regional distribution, AWP
nature, application.
● To characterize the different types of plastics used in agriculture and agri-food sector and the effect of ageing in AWP
● To select the proper AWP types for the project
● To diagnose the current collection and waste management approach and propose viable new ones in the context of
RECOVER value chains
● To study and validate feasible pre-treatments to enhance AWP degradation.
Description of work and role of partners
WP1 - Agro-plastic waste (APW) mapping, characterization, collection strategy and pre-treatment [Months:
1-12]
IDELUX, UAL, UNIPI, ASAJA, UMH, ASU, NATURPLAS, IRIS, FEMTO, ENTO, MANOR, ENCO, SAV
Task 1.1. AWP inventory and characterisation (M1-M6)
A wide range of plastics is commonly used in agriculture and agri-food sectors. First of all, the literature data will be
reviewed to establish a mapping of the agricultural films use vs. country. In terms of the food plastic packaging, the
rough composition of the currently non recycled mixed plastic packaging fractions among the relevant municipal solid
waste streams will be inventoried for the most representative EU countries.
A complete collection of samples (>30) will be developed by IDELUX, NATURPLAS, UNIPI, and ASAJA and
analyses will be carried out to characterise the main variables that are expected to affect the biodegradation
capacities (polymer type, polymerization grade, presence of plasticizers and other chemical additives, elemental
composition, functional groups on the surface, hydrophobicity/hydrophilicity, crystallinity, surface topography,
mechanical properties, molecular weight distribution and mass balance, etc.). Plastic samples will be classified
depending on plastic type (PE, PS), plastic use (mulching, green-housing, packaging) and ageing (raw & wasted samples,
AWP).
Leader: IDELUX, Partners involved: UNIPI, ASAJA, NATURPLAS, MANOR, SAV, ENTO
Task 1.2. Selection of AWP to be used into the project (M3-M12)
In this subtask, a selection of 4-6 types of most representative fossil plastics (including in each case virgin plastics and
AWP counterparts picked individually from MSW or agricultural films) will be made in order to provide normalized
samples to all the WPs. Based on the results of the previous task, the selection criteria will be centred to cover the most
significant plastic in agricultural and food packaging environments, the trends, the review in terms of state of the art for
the research on the use of micro/macro organisms for their degradation, etc.
Leader: UNIPI. Partners involved: NATURPLAS, SAV, ENCO, UAL, UMH, FEMTO
Task 1.3. Strategy for AWP collection and sorting (M3-M12)
The current practices in terms of end of life handling of the selected AWP will be reviewed under the lead of ASAJA
in case of agricultural plastics and of IDELUX in case of food packaging. A strategy for the targeted plastic waste
collection and delivery to the appropriate degradation plant will be proposed. The cases where a collection is not seen
viable and an in situ (soil, compost) degradation is preferable will be highlighted.
For the selected AWP types, it will be developed, by UNIPI, a logistic chain analysis, determining costs and involved
stakeholders for the phases of collection, transport, treatment and re-use. From this Base Scenario analysis a general
strategy for the reconstruction of a Project Scenario to be better delineated in WP6 will be designed.
In view of a possible automatic sorting of the AWP compatible with the RECOVER end of life management , the
previous samples will be supplied to IRIS for near infrared spectroscopy (NIR), hyper spectral imaging (HSI) in visible
(VIS), and short wave infra-red (SWIR) spectral ranges to test the accuracy of the distinction among different materials
thanks to digital image processing and chemometric analysis of the obtained spectra. Contaminated streams which could
affect the biodegradation, e.g. with pesticides, will also be analysed to determine the accuracy of their detection. The
Page 11 of 53
combination of the results obtained from several techniques may also be tested. The most suitable approach to implement
will be recommended for post project implementation. The outcome information will help correlating the composition
of the waste stream with the efficiency of the treatment.
Leader: IRIS. Partners involved: ASAJA, IDELUX, UNIPI, UAL, FEMTO
Task 1.4. Pre-treatment of AWP (M3-M12)
In this task, several abiotic pretreatment (fragmentation, micronization, thermal treatments, UV irradiation, oxidizing
agents, pH, irradiation by eBeam to initiate chain scission) will be done to enhance AWP biodegradation of selected
samples from Task 1.2. One approach in abiotic pre-treatment of AWP will be accomplished by eBeam treatment
(performed by ASU). Considering that when polymers are irradiated material degradation and cross-linking occur
simultaneously, and that application of lower doses results in enhanced cross-linking of the material whereas the use
of higher doses leads to material degradation AWP from Task 1.2 will be irradiated with a wide range of doses (50
– 200 kGy) to initiate chain scission and subsequent degradation. The effect of the pre-treatments will be quantified
using techniques from Task 1.1. An efficiency and cost-benefit pre-analyses will be done to select the pre-treatment
procedures to be applied on the collected samples used in the following WPs.
Leader: UMH. Partners involved: UNIPI, UAL, ASU, ENTO

Description of work and role of partners
WP2 - Selection of biotech tools for AWP biodegradation [Months: 1-24]
UAL, UNIPI, BRUNEL, UMH, ASA, NUTRINSECT, IRIS, OWS, IDELUX, ENTO, OWS RF
Task 2.1. Selection and building of microbial communities for AWP degradation (M1-20)
Microorganisms for the biodegradation of AWP (from WP1) will be selected among the strains available at UAL and
SynbiSTRAIN BRUNEL microbial collections. SynbiSTRAIN collection includes over 400 Pseudomonas strains (from
3 species), tens of composting Geobacillus strains, and Bacillus species; each of which are documented to contain
plastic degradative properties. These sequenced collections are built to maximally represent species evolutionary and
behavioural diversity. The potential candidates from UAL microbial collection will be also sequenced. Combinations
of strains will be selected and tested. Because the strains to be tested are or will be fully sequenced, it will be possible
to study population enrichment of mixed cultures to identify strains with greater capacity to use AWP as carbon source;
and those that are associated with greater degradative performance. These will be identified using pre- and post-culture
re-sequencing and comparisons of the starting and finishing sub-populations; in parallel cultures using the selected
plastic substrates will be performed. From this, strains will be selected for iterative rounds of selection and consortia
performance optimization according to their final use in WP4.
In parallel, the dominant microbiomes of the earthworms and insect larvae determined in Task 2.4 and strains isolated
and enriched from AWP-enriched soils will be characterized by sequencing. The species will be used to inform selection
of strains for consortia building with macro-organisms, and new strains from enrichment will be combined with
existing strain resources based upon comparisons of performance, and associated enzymes used to inform new enzyme
development (Task 2.2).
UAL will be in charge of community development and strain selection. BRUNEL will perform sequencing and genomic
analysis. UMH and UAL will provide earthworms/vermicompost and insect samples from Task 2.3, respectively. The
extent of AWP modification/mineralization will be analysed by means of techniques described in WP1 and WP4 by
IRIS. UNIPI will perform morphological (SEM), thermal (TGA, DSC), and chemical (FTIR, GPC) analysis on AWP.
Leader: UAL. Partners involved: BRUNEL, UMH, ASAJA, IDELUX, UNIPI
Task 2.2. Design and development of novel synthetic enzymes for AWP biodegradation (M1-M24)
The BRUNEL SynbiSTRAIN collection sequences, and public sequence resources for single strain and metagenomic
datasets will be screened for all members of enzyme families with known plastic-degradative properties (targets
led by selection process in WP4). From these a new strategy will be developed and implemented for selected /
directed evolution based upon synthetic DNA templates. These will be designed to simultaneously optimize the in-
vitro recombination of templates and represent the naturally occurring variation within enzymes that affect resulting
proteins. Using this strategy the coding space of the enzyme families will be explored in a focused fashion to combine
independently evolved enzyme optimizations from different lineages of the same species, and from independent lineages
that would have no capabilities to recombine and co-evolve enzymes in nature; through the use of metagenomic data it
will also include genes and alleles that are derived from non-cultivable strains and species.
The growth of strains (of E. coli) carrying novel enzymes will be determined on media containing indicators, plastic-
based carbon sources, and on plastic surfaces (as determined by the AWP substrates selected in WP1) to select and
enrich for novel enzymes and variants with high activity. These will variably be present in single colonies or mixtures.
The optimum enzymes will be identified by population enrichment on these substrates, followed by population re-
sequencing of plasmid-extracts. The most abundant enzyme versions will be identified within these populations (through
an in-house method of ‘in silico subtractive hybridization’ that first identifies the variants within a population, and then
determines their proportions in the population), this process will also be used to identify secondary adaptive mutation
occurring within the enzymes that contribute to their adaptation to the combination of previously separately occurring
Page 15 of 53
variation. Based upon the results of this one of two or both strategies will follow: 1. New synthetic templates will be
generated that preferentially contain variations that have been selected through selection and recombination to attempt
further enzyme improvement, including secondary internal adaptations; and 2. Final enzymes present in mixtures will
be specifically synthesized for pure enzyme production.
At a final stage, it is envisaged that a re-coded version of the enzymes optimized for production in commercial strains /
species (e.g. Bacillus will be generated). This or other process optimization steps will be selected based upon progress
and needs determined through the larger program of research. Additional stages of optimization for pH, temperature, or
other process / production requirements may also be pursued in WP3 depending upon the wider needs and progress of
the project. Partners UNIPI, OWS, IRIS will perform analysis on AWP before and after degradation.
Leader: BRUNEL. Partners involved: UAL, ASA, OWS, IRIS, UNIPI
Task 2.3. Preselection of earthworm and insect larvae AWP-degraders (M1-M20)
The potential polymer-degraders in composting earthworms (Eisenia andrei) and soil-dwelling earthworms (e.g.,

This WPs aims to obtain stable microbial/enzyme inoculums (PMC) and the worms and insects larvae fortified with the
microbial consortia selected in WP2. For this purpose microorganisms/enzymes will be prepared in suitable formulations

Task 3.1. Production of stable plastic-degrading microbial/enzyme consortia (PMC) (M12-M24)
Subtask 3.1.1. Production of microorganisms and enzymes
The microorganisms and enzymes selected in WP2 will be produced by ASA in bioreactor up to 25 L reactor in enough
quantity for the following tasks in WP3 and WP4. The culture medium and protocol will be replicated in 10 L scale.
The culture conditions will be adjusted according to the information provided from WP2 for each microorganism and
to the further optimization for scaling-up. After that, the physical-chemical parameters of the fermentation process will
be optimized. Furthermore, the carbon and nitrogen sources of the fermentation media will be optimized in order to
establish fed batch processes. Growth rate and maintenance of AWP degrading activity will be checked and culture time
will be selected according to production peaks.
Then downstream processes for the enzymes and microorganisms will be developed. The fermentation procedures
and purification processes will be scaled up to a fermentation volume of 25 L. Finally, the selected enzymes and
microorganisms will be characterized i.e. with regard to their AWP degrading capability.
Leader: ASA. Partners involved: UAL, BRUNEL
Subtask 3.1.2. Preparation of stable PMC formulations
PMC stability upon spray drying, lyophilizing and/or storage at different temperatures and times will be tested by taking
samples periodically after treatment and assayed for microorganism survival and maintenance of AWP biodegradation
capability. Treatment will be selected according to the easiest way to store, transport and manipulate PMC without
losing AWP biodegrading capabilities.
PMC formulations intended for adding to soil or compost will be prepared by adding microbial biomass or enzymes to
inorganic or organic carriers, or a combination of both. Inorganic matrix clays such calcium and sodium bentonite, and
organic matrix such as alginate, polyethylene (PEG) spent coffee, biotic-inert compost, biochar or extract of compost
will be tested.
PMC formulations for feeding earthworm/insects will be prepared by adding microbial biomass or enzymes to
pre-composted organic materials such as agroindustry residues for earthworms and food suitable for insect larvae.
Formulations will be re-designed and re-evaluated according to results obtained in Task 3.2.
Microorganisms and enzymes stability and maintenance of AWP degrading activity will be monitored by using the
techniques described in WP2. UNIPI will analyse the AWP after degradation.
This task will provide at least 2-4 PMC stable formulations for use in WP4.

Task 4.1. Development of biodegradation processes (M18-M30)
Subtask 4.1.1. Examination of operational conditions to optimize AWP degradation of the selected PMC
The effectiveness in the biodegradation capability of the selected PMC for complete mineralization of the AWP will
be examined at medium scale (4 litre reactors). Tests will be run (1) under the conditions of a limited competition and
biodiversity (with small amounts of organic matter and partial sterilization) and (2) in the presence of a full bio-diversity
and competition. The biodegradation will be monitored on the basis of ISO 14855 (compost) and ISO 17556 (soil) and
the growth conditions defined in WP3. The biodegradation will be determined by measuring the final conversion of
the organic carbon of plastics into CO2. In a first stage one well-defined type of plastic will be used to examine the
biodegradation potential of the selected PMC, followed by the treatment of mixed AWP. In order to ensure a fast as
possible result the plastic will be milled into powder. When positive results are obtained the test conditions (temperature,
moisture, aeration,…) will be adjusted to maximize the degradation rate and examine the operational boundaries.
Subtask 4.1.2. Examination of operational conditions to optimize AWP degradation of the selected FP-earthworms and
FP-insects
Based on the information of WP3 with regards to growth conditions of FP-earthworms and FP-insects and knowledge
obtained in subtask 4.1.1, the biodegradation capability for complete mineralization of AWP by these organisms will
be examined. The biodegradation will be monitored on the basis of ISO 14855 (compost) and ISO 17556 (soil) (CO2
measurements). In a first phase milled single stream plastic will be examined. Subsequently plastic films and mixtures
of AWP will be examined. The operational conditions will be optimized to obtain high degradation rate.
Leader: OWS RF. Partners involved: UMH, UAL
Task 4.2. Set up of pilot-scale ex-situ plastic degradation process (M24-M36)
Subtask 4.2.1. Ex-situ AWP degradation by composting and vermicomposting
In the strategy to manage biodegradation of plastic residues ex situ, a biodegradation reactor of at least 10 kg aimed at
using the PMC and/or FPBug-worms produced in the previous WP3 will be set up. The optimal conditions will be based
on the information of Task 4.1. The organic waste will be optimised and blended with defined proportions (ranging from
10 up to 50% (v/v) of pre-treated AWP. Temperature and moisture of the substrates will be kept in optimal conditions
for successful composting and further vermicomposting process. Once bio-oxidative phase of composting is finished
FP-earthworms will be added so the vermicomposting phase will start. Periodically, samples will be taken for assessing
the efficiency of the biodegradation process in the following tasks. Mass balance, mass flow, etc will be monitored.
Final compost/vermicompost will be checked for the presence of microplastics (Task 4.4) after screening out large size
plastics and an additional trial will be performed with the aim to remove those materials by means of treatment with
suitable PMC. Samples will be taken periodically and analysed as above.
Also with plastic contaminated waste of real-life waste streams of IDELUX (compost – digestate) will be treated by the
developed bioremediation process of specialised vermicomposting.
Leader: OWS RF. Partners involved: UMH, IDELUX
Subtask 4.2.2. Ex-situ AWP degradation by insects rearing

be taken for assessing the efficiency of the biodegradation process in the following task 4.4. Mass balance, mass flow,

etc. will be monitored as well as insect larvae performance and enzyme activity as in WP2. After the end of the insect-

Task 4.3. Set up of in-situ bioremediation of AWP in soil (M24-M36)
Based on the information of task 4.1 the dynamics of plastic-degrading activities in plastic-contaminated agricultural
soils using compost and vermicompost amended with PMC produced in WP3 as well as fortified soil-dwelling

Task 4.4. Monitoring of AWP degradation
Subtask 4.4.1 Chemical decomposition assessment
The biodegradation of AWP will be followed by its chemical decomposition: monitoring of markers residues (phthalate
esters such as di-n-butyl phthalate and di-(2-ethylhexyl) phthalate) derived from synthetic polymer biodegradation
in both earthworm tissue and final composted substrate (vermicast) and soil. The physical decomposition will be
determined by the median degradation time (DT50) of total raw plastic from initial load of residues by spectroscopic and
microscopic techniques. Finally, a weight-of-evidence assessment of in situ plastic biodegradation will be determined.
UNIPI will analyse plastic debris for morphology, chemical (FTIR) and thermal (TGA, DSC) properties.
Leader: UNIPI. Partners involved: OWS RF, IRIS, FEMTO.
Subtask 4.4.2. Inline monitoring disintegration and non-degraded plastic
Samples from task 4.1 will be supplied along the biodegradation tests and IRIS will use hyper spectral imaging (HSI)
in VIS, NIR and SWIR spectral ranges to test the ability to quantify the size of the residual plastic fragments and of
the distinction among different materials thanks to digital image processing. Different approaches for sample collecting
and analysis will be studied to maximise the representativity of the results as it is likely that the analysis is limited to a
few mm below the surface of the compost. The data, together with other analysis in the previous subtask, will support
the selection of the most efficient treatment.
Leader: IRIS Partners involved: OWS RF, UNIPI
Subtask 4.4.3. Predictive modeling as support for biodegradation process optimisation.
IRIS will set up a database for the partners to centralise all relevant information for the modeling task by M3. It will
be populated with the results from the previous tasks in terms of disintegration/biodegradation profiles both in and ex-
situ. Next, the available data will be pre-analysed to select the most discriminant variable subsets and to define new
factors using, for example, PCA (Principal Component Analysis) and Factorial Analysis. This will also rely on support
and expert know-how of the partners. Pre-processing of data before data modeling will give optimum input sets to the
machine learning algorithms. Then, the data will be modeled by benchmarking different machine learning algorithms
using fitness functions as objective output to optimize different aspects of the known parameters influencing the
biodegradation and disintegration such as composition (monitored in task 1.3), pretreatment, vermicompost composition
and external conditions. The characteristic curves will be represented in a “parametric form”, including key values
such as biodegradation time and biodegradation percentage achieved. The resulting predictive models will be used to
select the most appropriate process and micro/macro-organisms mix for degrading a target type of plastic and/or reach
a complete biodegradation in a given timing. As such, once the models are trained and validated with unitary testing,
a Monte Carlo method will be developed to calibrate the system input parameters in order to obtain a closest match to
a desired output (and/or fitness function). Once the calibration process is completed, a CBR historical case base and
system will be defined, bootstrapped with initial cases (eg. from WP2), and as larger scale cases are processed in tasks
4.1, 4.2 and 4.3, they will be incorporated, based on a similarity matching algorithm, which will also be developed.
Synthetic data can also be generated for the CBR to complement existing empirical results for data modeling needs,
especially in early phases of the project. After modeling the single steps, DST will be used to support the optimal process
in next task but also in the cascade process (defined in WP6).
Leader: IRIS, Partners involved: OWS RF, UNIPI, IDELUX, ENTO, NUTRINSECT
Task 4.5. Establishing protocol of action of the overall fortified system. Sequential study (M24-M36)

sequence, will be performed. It will comprise the three main ingredients developed in the project: fortified insects (FP-

Task 5.1. Development of upgrade biofertilizers (M24-M36)
Subtask 5.1.1. Development of vermicompost derived from plastic-organic waste mixtures
Using the outputs of WP4 activities, selected earthworms with the most efficient plastic degradative capacities will
be included into specific containers to produce vermicompost using at least 6 different mixtures of AWP with
other substrates approaches. The device will be portable and modular design including stock and pre-treatment of
plastic section and environmental control (humidity and mixing devices). The process will be optimized to produce
adequate sizing and format of bio-fertilizers according to new European Fertilizer Regulation. The quality of produced
biofertilizer will be tested and compared with commercial biofertilizers. UMH will test the fertilizing properties of the
developed materials in greenhouse conditions using different application dose and comparing with equivalent NPK
commercial fertilizers. The biopesticide and/or plant biostimulant activity of produced biofertilizers will be tested by
UAL.
Leader: UMH. Partners involved: UAL, ENTO, IDELUX.
Subtask 5.1.2. Development of biofertilizers from insect manure and chitin extraction residues
Manure from insect breeding chambers (WP4), leftovers coming out from chitin extraction from insects (Subtask 5.3.1)
will be identified by ENTO, UAL. Those considered potentially suitable for biofertiliser preparation will be analysed
to determine their main nutrient and physicochemical properties (i.e. analysis of the N, P, K, aminoacids, total Carbon,
total organic matter) by UMH. The formulation of the suitable mixtures will be investigated and post-treatment steps by
chemical and enzymatic hydrolysis, dewatering, drying, composting, pelletizing will be proposed by ENTO in function
of the final application.
UNIPI in cooperation with FEMTO will prepare biocomposites containing bio-fertilizer self-fertilizing agriculture items
(sticks, yarns, etc) based on soil biodegradable polymeric matrices as a binder, such as polyhydroxyalkanoates or
polybutylene succinate-co adipate to act as carrier for the bio-fertilizer.
The proposed biofertilizer development will be addressed attending to construct added-value products that would be
improved by the incorporation of PMC, fortified worms and insects/larvae that will introduce important added-values
such as biopesticide, biofertilization and biostimulation effects. The final composition of the organic fertilisers will be
compared with market available fertilisers. The biopesticide and/or biostimulant activity of produced biofertilizers will
be tested by UAL by a set of standardized procedures and compared with commercial biofertilizers. Added-value will
be also determined by UMH studying yields, but also parameters related to soil and plant quality, such as reduction
of chemical application of pesticides and fertilizers, antioxidant level, C vitamin in field experiments. SAV will also
validate the fertilisers from the project on plant growth in rural fields and in city parks in comparison with conventional
non-biobased fertilisers.
Leader: ENTO. Partners involved: UAL, UMH, ASAJA, UNIPI, NUTRINSECT, FEMTO, SAV.
Task 5.2. Extraction of chitin, and conversion to chitosan (M24-M48)
To circumvent the usage of alkaline solutions and acids for the production of chitosan from chitin, ASU in collaboration
with NUTRINSECT and UNPI will extract chitin from the insects/worms coming from Task 4.1 using a combination
of irradiation by electron beam (eBeam) treatment and chemical methods. Raw chitin material will be eBeam treated
to introduce degradation of chitin and break up the tight chitin network. Initially, a wide range of doses (20-200 kGy)
will be applied and the solubility of irradiated raw material in several (green) solvents will be tested at laboratory scale.
Page 27 of 53
Additionally, it will be tested if irradiation allows the reduction of alkaline solutions and acids during conventional
chemical extraction. Extracted chitin will be converted to chitosan by eBeam treatment. The extracted chitin and chitosan
will be provided for Subtask 5.3.2, Subtask 5.3.3 and Subtask 5.3.4. Based on the results, the chitin extraction method
will be further optimized regarding chitin and chitosan yields iteratively.
Leader: ASU Partners involved: UNIPI, ASAJA, NUTRINSECT, UMH.
Task 5.3 Processing of chitosan and chitin (M24-M48)
Subtask 5.3.1. Characterization and first trials of processing of chitin and chitosan
UNIPI will analyse the chitin and chitosan produced, by FTIR, NMR, and thermal analysis. Chitin and chitosan will
be compared with commercial grade in terms of processability as additives in biobased and biodegradable polymeric
blends and composites. The chitin and chitosan will be formulated with commercially available bio-based polymers
and additives to allow thermoplastic processing. These are for instance plasticisers, stabilizers and processing aids. The
different chitin and chitosan-based formulations with biodegradable polymers (polylactic acid, polyhydroxyalkanoates,
polybutylene succinate), will then be evaluated regarding their process ability by extrusion and injection moulding by
UNIPI in cooperation with FEMTO. Thereby an optimization of processing parameters (temperature, processing rate,
etc.) will also be carried out. This activity will be performed in first instance, at laboratory scale using a Haake minilab
extruder, with Haake mini jet at UNIPI to produce dog bone shaped test specimens. Specimen will be used to characterize
the materials for thermal and mechanical properties relevant to evaluate properties and performances of the grades of
chitin and chitosan as well as the different formulations obtained and thereof identify potential applications. Specimen
produced by UNIPI will be evaluated by ASU concerning optical properties and surface energy in terms of contact angle
measurements. Moreover, water vapor permeability & permeability against O2 will be monitored by ASU.
Based on the results of these characterisations, the formulations will be optimized in iterative loops. A maximum of five
formulations will be selected for pilot scale trials conducted at UNIPI on pilot scale extruder (twin screw extruder, pilot
injection moulding, calendering, films by blow moulding), and at FEMTO for industrial injection moulding. These tests
will be conducted in order to further optimize formulations and processing parameters of polymeric formulation with
chitin/chitosan, to verify lab results and to produce sufficient material for demonstrators for applications in agriculture
(packaging, sticks, pots), and food packaging (trays, rigid containers, films).
Leader: UNIPI. Partners involved: ASU, NATURPLAS, FEMTO
Subtask 5.3.2. Chitosan and chitin for coatings.
Solubility of chitin and chitosan will be tested in view of applications as coating on plastic or cellulosic substrates. Up to
3 suitable solvents will be selected by using the Hansen solubility parameter approach. The chitosan and chitin solutions
obtained will be further formulated by applying suitable bio-based additives such as plasticisers, antioxidants etc. These
formulations will then be evaluated by applying them on different cellulose and plastic substrates. These coated materials
will be characterized with respect to their mechanical and barrier properties as well as their antimicrobial activity by
UAL. For further modification of the mechanical and barrier properties and to enhance the antimicrobial activity, the
dried and wet coated materials produced by UNIPI on lab, scale will be irradiated via eBeam treatment by ASU to
enhance barrier, mechanical and anti-microbial performances.
Based on the results, both formulations as well as coating parameters will be optimized in iterative loops. Up to two
formulations for each substrate category will be selected and applied in pilot scale coating trials at ASU partner sites.
These tests will be conducted in order to further optimize formulations and processing parameters, to verify lab results
and to produce sufficient material for demonstrators (food packaging).
To evaluate the effect of chitosan coated films on perishable food, chitosan coated films will be applied as lids for food
trays. These food trays are then used to analyse the shelf life of chicken in comparison to commercially available trays
with support from IRIS.
OWS RF will perform biodegradability test on the developed formulations in different environments such as compost
(ISO 14855), soil (ISO 17556) and water (freshwater ISO 14851 and under marine conditions ASTM D6691) according
to international standards in order to define suitable end-of-life options and evaluate effect by accidental release in the
environment.
Leader: UNIPI. Partners involved: IRIS, FEMTO, ASAJA, ASU, NUTRINSECT, UAL, OWS RF, MANOR
Subtask 5.3.3 Utilization of chitin/chitosan production by-products for optimized mulching films
Within this subtask chitin/chitosan by-products will be characterized and used to improve mulch materials for optimized
conditions such as temperature and O2/CO2/H2O contents considering soil respiration. ASU in collaboration with
UNIPI will use by-products of WP4 and Subtask 5.3.1 as well as the extracted chitosan to improve existing mulching
polymer films for their light and heat transmission, absorption, reflection/emissivity (UV-VIS and IR-spectroscopy) and
for UV stability and selective gas permeability (permselectivity). The chosen chitin extraction residues will be applied
to existing mulching films by coating and/or by compounding to mulching film polymers closing the loop within the
RECOVER approach. Mechanical & thermal properties include density, puncture resistance, specific heat capacity and
permeability for gases including H2O, N2, O2, and CO2. The required mulch permeability will be determined in a
Page 28 of 53
numerical simulation module based on existing models and the intrinsic permeation coefficients determined within this
subtask. NATURPLAS will cooperate to films production, optical characterization and pre-testing to estimate films
suitability to be used in mulching, the results will be fed back in iterative loops for further optimization. SAV will make
the validation of the mulching films in real agricultural fields.
Leader: UNIPI, Partners involved: ASU, NATURPLAS, SAV

Task 6.1 Mass, energy balance, techno-economic studies. and development of RECOVER cascade strategy (M24-M48)
Previous energy balances allow to demonstrate that all the concepts proposed into the project are reasonable. However,
they are based on small scale experiences and some hypothesis on final yield products of the processes. Dynamic tool
will be set up in the reactors used in WP4 for the continuous monitoring of the sub-products obtained inter-phases
and energy balances of the different steps of the technological chains. It will allow identifying undesirable situations
conducting to loss of efficiency/sustainability. For each step a single tool will be developed, integrated in a multistep
system. ASAJA with the support of OWS RF will be responsible to create this dynamic tool and connect it to the
Data Center. This tool will provide simplified reports for better understand the performance of the different steps and
their contribution to the overall process. These data will also be useful for further techno-economic and Life Cycle
Analysis (LCA). ASAJA will be in charge of techno-economic analysis. IDELUX will provide data from benchmark
waste treatments for this and next task.
The most efficient integrated cascade concept will be discussed based on WP5WP4/5 results using subsequent treatment
processes of specialised larvae-worm-insect plastic degrader capacities to produce a synergistic enhanced degradation;
products of the piloting will be chitin/chitosan and vermicompost and a zero-waste scenario will be targeted. (theoretical
exercise supporting future upscale). Mass balance, mass flow optimization and engineering procedures will be used to
define the full scale strategy in both agricultural and municipal use scenarios.
In terms of the CO2 emitted during the performance of the biodegradation process, a concept of coupling of the reactor
with a microalgae culture for CO2 capture will be designed with ALGAENERGY (BIC member in our AB).

Task 6.2 Life cycle analysis
The environmental impact of the developed Biotech processes will be screened, including the extraction of the different
end-products, based on ISO 14040 and ISO 14044. Set of boundaries depends on the choice of products of previous
WP’s and will be defined during the project A detailed assessment of the different inputs and outputs will be performed in
close cooperation with the partners of WP4 and WP5. (as well as the data from task 6.1). Impact assessment calculations
will be performed using SimaPro LCA software in combination with e.g. the Ecoinvent and ELCD LCA databases.
Major impacts in terms of human health, resources depletion and environmental protection will be determined. This
way the ‘hot spots’ in the process will be determined and suggestions for improvement will be formulated. The results
will be compared to the current environmental impact of pollution with plastics. Moreover a base scenario “the no
implementation of proposed technologies” will be used, thus providing a simple and clear image of the advantages and
disadvantages of implementing the proposed technologies.
Page 31 of 53
Apart from the environmental impacts also the life cycle cost (LCC) and social life cycle aspects (sLCA) will be
examined, resulting in a total life cycle sustainability assessment dealing with the 3 main pillars: people, planet and
profit. The social risks will be calculated based on the Soca add-on to Ecoinvent in OpenLCA. The following social
impact categories will be evaluated in this study: child labour, fair salary, forced labour, and safety measures. The
economic costs will be calculated in terms of total production cost, total life cycle cost, and the main cost contributors.
Leader: OWS/OWS RF. Partners involved: ASAJA, NUTRINSECT, UAL, UNIPI, UMH.
Task 6.3 Risk assessment and social acceptance
As a key for the future uptake of the proposed AWP degrading process, the social sustainability will be investigated by
risk assessments of the new value chains developed in the RECOVER project. ASAJA will be in charge to determine
risks assessments by means of various danger categories identifications as i) Physical: noise, pressure, electrical hazard,
temperature, machinery, etc; Biological: insects, larvae, worms, bacteria, viruses, fungi, etc; Chemicals: liquids, gases,
fumes, dust, aerosols, heavy metals, organic contaminants, etc.; Ergonomic: posture, workstation, gestures, bad posture,
etc; Psychological: pressure, workload, etc.
To assess the potential dangers and their associated risks, a survey will be made among the partners involved in the
development of innovative biodegradation processes, especially for the technologies that will be developed at operative
scale in the demo sites from WP4. Risks will then be classified according to their severity and likelihood of occurrence
and preventive measures will be suggested. Complementarily, the social acceptance assessment will be done by ASAJA
across their networks. Social impact assessment builds on local knowledge and relies on a participatory process to
analyse the concerns of interested and affected parties. It includes study of public opinion, level of knowledge and/
or concerns about communities of microorganisms, insects, inoculation technique, chitin-chitosan conversion, added-
value new biomaterials, etc... Thus, opinions from society – i.e. the opinions of citizens, businessmen, representatives of
ecological and social organizations, public authorities and politicians – will be taken into consideration. In this regard,
at least 100 surveys (online, or during the project roadshow of events) will be carried out. Surveys will include closed
and open questions.

Task 7.2 Roadmap for the set up of RECOVER value chains and new cross-sector interconnections in the bio-based
economy (M1-M24)
This task seeks to boost new bio-economy value chains based on RECOVER results, by strategically provide impetus
to all the key actors along the value chain. The societal transition towards bioeconomy implies structural remodelling
of the value chain players as discussed in section 2.1 in relevance to the expected impacts in the call topic. Thus, a
stakeholder analysis (identifying and prioritizing who to mobilise and engage with) will be shaped approaching key
actors across all stages of the value chains. The analysis will be based on a 3-step process: i) Mapping Stakeholders, ii)
Recruiting Stakeholders (Acquisition) iii) Managing Stakeholders (Retention) considering their knowledge, interests,
positions, alliances, relationships, key roles and responsibilities. To connect in a collaborative dialogue the identified
players along the value chains appropriate measures will be adopted as 1) Workshops, roundtables, open days, action
groups to bring together key players in new cross-sectorial value chains and maximise their pull effect. 2) Online
consultations and pools, surveys and interviews in order to gather relevant information to feed into subsequent activities
of this WP. Thereafter, a roadmap to create new cross-sector interconnections in bio-based economy will be designed
to facilitate the establishment of new bonds among different sectors that may benefit from RECOVER innovation.
It will be built on top of the analysis of the following settings: i) Economic & Market: insight of biotech & plastics
value chains structure (quantity and quality), demand and supply, Users/Consumer perceptions and new potential
business opportunities/ Market drivers/barriers. ii) Socio-cultural: Public awareness, Environmental awareness, Human
health, Specialized knowledge/skills available; iii) Policy & Regulation: current political framework for plastics EoL,

agenda; iv) Finance: Mobilisation of capital, Favourable/ adverse environment, Policy incentives, Financial models and
instruments. In addition, needs and expectations of consumers - which shall contribute to speeding up the time-to-market
of the project solutions - will be investigated considering gender aspects.
