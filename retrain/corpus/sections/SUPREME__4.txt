1.1.  OBJECTIVES   
1.1.1.  CONTEXT  
Powder Metallurgy  (PM) includes a large variety of industrial processes embracing most metallic and alloy materials to 
manufacture parts by heating, pressure or printing operations. Compared to more traditional processes such as machining, forging 
or casting, it allows dra matic cost savings because it intrinsically limits the raw material consumption , which means the 
material losses are reduced to the minimum. PM typically uses 1 more than 97% of the starting raw material in the finished part 
(against 40-50% for machining, 75-80% for forging and 90% for casting). Besides, PM has the lowest energy requirement per 
kg of finished part (<30MJ) compared to the other manufacturing processes (>65MJ for machining, >45MJ for forging, >30MJ for 
casting)2.  
On top of that, PM enables to significantly reduce the number of process steps compared to other manufacturing methods and 
hence allows more modularity, which makes it a more integrated an d flexible approach, well suited to high volume components 
production requirements as well as highly complex parts.  
Under the concept “PM”, one must distinguish: 
 
- Powder Manufacturing, in which metals are produced in powder form by several possible ways; 
 
- Parts Manufacturing, in which powders are processed by different possible technologies to form a component. The 
European PM Industry employs approximately 40 000 people with production sites in 20 member’s states. The European 
Market of global PM has an a nnual turnover of over 8,7 Billion €, with annual worldwide metal powder production 
exceeding 1 million tonnes. The market is expected to reach a turnover of over 12 Billion € by end 2022 3. To achieve 
this growth that will ensure jobs and environmental sustainability, the PM industry has to face some challenges, such as 
(i) to decrease the processes total cost breakdown, notably by further optimizing raw material resources efficiency, as 
well as energy consumption for both continuous and batch processes; (ii) to increase the current production rates, and 
(iii) to expand PM processes to new application areas. In this context, SUPREME proposes to integrate a set of 
innovative and fast-growing PM processes that will be improved in parallel in order to combine advanced metallic material 
and efficient energy flows towards new optimized and versatile production routes of metal end-parts. 
 
The overall objective of SUPREME is to optimize ferrous and non-ferrous metal PM processes, currently individually available at 
TRL7 and beyond, by cross-sectorial improvements in material and energy efficiency throughout the supply chain, from raw 
materials (fluids, solids or gases , including minerals and water ) to finished products in a range of different end -applications 
(automotive, cutting tools, aeronautics, medical and tooling). The goal is then to demonstrate a new integrated and optimized 
approach of a set of PM production routes in real industrial settings, from TRL5 to TRL7 4, i.e. ready for adoption on the PM 
market. This will enable a significant reduction in the total cost breakdown as well as in the environmental footprint toward s a 
more sustainable Industry, and therefore to reinforce PM Industry competitiveness in Europe to maintain and develop jobs 
To achieve an ambitious impact, SUPREME will focus on a selection of innovative and fast-growing processes that will address 
a significant part of the market by 2020. Indeed, some classical PM processes, such as sintering, have already been intensively 
used for several decades, and the residual potential for material use  reduction and efficiency increase is li mited since strong 
efforts in research, development and innovation have already been done to reach a near optimum. However, for several emerging 
technologies entering the market, still representing a very low percentage of the production but definitely occ upying the best 
choices for the next PM generation technologies, there is a big challenge in reaching better yield while enhancing the energy 
efficiency to make this advanced manufacturing processes competitive with the traditional ones. SUPREME will ensure the 
adoption of these new technological improvement in real industrial settings, for the following processes:

1.1.2.  OBJECTIVES OF THE PR OJECT  
SUPREME project aims to achieve a holistic processes optimization for more  sustainability in Powder Metallurgy that will be 
quantified by a set of Key Performances Indicators (KPI), monitored by a quality and process control, and validated by  an eco-
innovation methodology. Depending on the type of PM process considered, the para meters available for process control and 
monitoring as well as the current practices in the sector to be compared to, the KPIs will be defined as follows  
 
Two main indicators to quantify material and energy efficiency improvements: 
→ Raw material resource s efficiency : this KPI will be defined either (i) absolutely as the amount of material 
consumption for a given process (typically in kg), or (ii) relatively, as the raw material yield losses compared to the current practice 
in the sector. Several raw material resources will be considered, accordingly to the process requirement and the most significant 
metrics that can be optimized: solids / fluids (including water) / gases.  
→ Energy efficiency: this KPI will be defined either (i) absolutely as the amount of energy consumption for a given 
process (typically in kWh), or (ii) relatively, as the rate of energy losses compared to the current practice in the sector. 
Two indicators derived from the above main KPIs to track impacts on productivity and CO2 emissions: 
→ Production rate: this KPI will be defined as productivity, typically in kg/hour. 
→ Carbon dioxide emissions: this KPI will be defined either (i) absolutely as the amount of carbon dioxide emitted for 
a given process (typically in kt), or (ii) relatively, as the rate relative reduction of carbon dioxide emissions compared to the current 
practice in the sector

EFFICIENT MINERAL CO NCENTRATION   
Metal sourcing, extraction and beneficiation are common issues for the whole metallurgy sector. At the European scale, primary 
metal production, alloy creation, downstream processing, integrated metal products and alloy recycling accounts for 46%  of all 
EU manufacturing value and 11% of the EU’s total gross domestic product (GDP 6). Although the project SUPREME wouldn’t 
solve all extraction problems of the metallurgic sector, it will concentrate on specific issues related to the metals and the ir alloys 
at stake in the project, mainly Fe, Ni, Cr, Cu, Sn. 
 
The basic objective of mineral processing is to separate the minerals into at least two products so that the valuable mineral(s) are 
present in the concentrate(s) and the invaluable particles (i.e.  gangue) in the tailing(s). Obviously, this type of process is never 
perfectly optimized: for example, the locked particles where the valuable mineral is attached to or is inside of an invaluable particle 
should not go to either class.  If these types of particles are taken into the concentrate, they will decrease the concentrate grade 
because of the invaluable particles that will be introduced to the concentrate flow.  On the other hand, valuable minerals will be 
lost if these particles are taken into the tailings and thus the recovery of the valuable minerals from the ore to the concentrate will 
suffer. Both cases will negatively affect the yield losses and the energy efficiency of the mineral process. 
Most of today’s mineral enrichment plants 
consist of a size reduction stage (i.e. grinding) 
and a mineral concentration stage (i.e. 
flotation). Since the grinding and the following 
separation stage are typically wet processes, 
they are followed with dewatering stages both 
for the concentrates and for the tailings. 
Flotation is a complex physic -chemical 
process where surface properties of the 
floated minerals are modified in a way that 
they will bec ome hydrophobic and attach to 
the surfaces of rising air bubbles. This takes 
place in a flotation cell, where the minerals are mixed with water as well as other chemicals and air is dispersed through a rotating 
axle to form an evenly rising bubble flux. Wh en the valuable mineral is floated and the invaluable gangue is left in the tailings, 
which is the more common case, then one is dealing with direct flotation. The opposite where invaluable material is floated, like 
ink in waste paper recycling, for exampl e, is called reverse flotation. Individual flotation cells are further connected to form a 
flotation bank (or stage). These are yet connected with each other to form a flotation circuit for a given mineral, say for Copper 
Bearing Chalcopyrite, for example. To improve performance, each circuit typically has return-flows back to the first flotation stages, 
thus generating circulating flows inside the circuit. Furthermore, a typical concentrator plant has more than one mineral to be 
concentrated so there are several cascaded circuits. When adding the constantly varying feed material, it is easy to see that this 
is a rather complicated process to run and to optimize. 
Water management: As mentioned earlier, mineral enrichment, processes are typically wet processes that include water-handling 
stages. Depending on the geographical location, water can be a very scarce and valuable resource. On the other hand, in some 
locations there might be severe problems with excess water due to rainfall into a large mining site. Furthermore, water quali ty 
typically has a big influence on the efficiency of the mineral extraction process. Regardless of the process related issues, to gain 
the “social license to operate” (SLO), local community and officials require compliance to environmental permit limits set fo r 
tailings waters. For these reasons, there is a growing need for comprehensive water management tools at mine sites7.

GAS ATOMISATION   
Atomisation is one of the most popular method to produce metal powder. The 
most important mechanisms are to form a thin sheet of liquid matter and to 
increase its velocity thanks to the atomisation media (gas or liquid) in order to 
disintegrate the liquid matter.  
Gas atomisation process (gas, i.e. typically N2 or Ar, is the atomisation media) 
is very common and has the advantage to produce mostly spherical particles 
with a size from 0 to 500 µm8. 
It is obvious that the particle size and shape requirement i s very linked to the 
powder manufacturing process, and SUPREME project is mainly focusing from 
1-5 to 150 µm. The Figure 7 is giving the typical size requirement for MIM, 
SLM, LMD and HIP processes. 
ASL has worked in the gas atomising field for many years. Since a non -
negligible part of the powder cost is linked to gas & energy (electricity in the 
furnace) consumption, ASL will concentrates its effort in SUPREME on 
improving the gas atomised powder shape (quality) and the gas atomiser 
efficiency (gas consumption, powder yield) . Experiments using different gas pres sures and nozzle types will target strong 
improvements in the powder yield (i.e. the particles having the right targeted dimension for the manufacturing process), and the 
use of higher atomising gas temperatures (N2) to reduce overall energy and gas consumption in the overall process.  
Moreover, emerging and fast growing market of metal additive manufacturing is representing an additional challenge: although 
powders needed in the metal additive manufacturing are currently mostly produced by gas atomisation process, it has certain 
limitations, like consuming large amounts of energy and gas, providing particles with less than perfect particle shape due to  
satellite of particles in the production process (limiting the powder flowability). Optimising the gas ato mising process to 
specifically target the metal AM powders in SUPREME will involve many technologies under development at ASL and will 
require ASL knowledge. ASL have identified 4 critical areas for powder suppliers into this market: 
- Powder quality in terms of shape, density, flowability 
- Powder quality in terms of purity/contamination 
- Powder yield and cost 
- Energy consumption of the powder production process. 
 
 
ASL is proposing in SUPREME to use the anti-satellite gas atomising process developed for standard powders and to optimise it 
for the size fractions needed for metal AM (20-60 µm and 60-120 µm). This will ensure that powder particles are as spherical and 
as satellite free as possible giving the powder packing densities and excellent flow properties. Modifications to the atomiser vessel 
and gas circulation system will be necessary to improve powder cross -contamination in the manufacturing process an d will 
address the powder purity/contamination issues seen with standard powders.

WATER ATOMISATION   
Water atomization is another way to produce particles. High pressure water is used as the atomization media, making the process 
cost effective (compared to gas atomization) and suitable for iron, stainless steels and low -alloy metal powders. However, the 
metallic powders produced by water atomization have generally irregular shapes and oxidize surfaces, which could be critical in 
some manufacturing process and for some materials.  
Powders are produced by melting in an induction furnace and then pouring 
from the induction furnace into a tundish or crucible that is positioned over the 
atomizing jets.  A metering nozzle directs the stream into the center of the 
atomizing water.  As the water jets hit the molten stream, the stream is 
disintegrated into powder an d the water/powder slurry is collected in a 
chamber.  The slurry is then pumped to a de -watering device (such as a 
vacuum filter). 
After the powder is de-watered (typically at this stage it contains between five 
and six percent moisture) it is dried in a rotary dryer which is heated by a 
combustion burner.  Once dried the powder is screened or classified to a final 
particle size depending on the product it is being used for.  The typical batch 
size for powders is batch is between one and five tons 9. SUPREME project 
will investigate how far the water atomized powders could be used in 
fast growing PM process like additive manufacturing , reducing the e nergy/raw material consumption compared to gas -
atomized processes. 
MECHANICAL ALLOYING BY BALL MILLING   
Mechanical alloying (MA) is a solid -state powder 
processing technique involving repeated welding, 
fracturing and rewelding of powder particles in a 
high-energy ball mill. MA has been shown to be 
capable of synthesizing a variety of equilibrium and 
non-equilibrium alloy phases starting from blended 
elemental or pre-alloyed powders. MBN developed 
and installed a high-energy ball mill (HEBM) in which 
kinetic energy is purely transferred during the impact 
to the powders and partly to the container, whereas 
ball/(powder)/ball interactions are minimized.  
This technique is able to generate numerous 
variants of composites materials and structures to 
promote different synthesis effects depending on 
materials and process conditions used (above). In 
particular, HEBM is well suited for the fabrication of 
metal-metal composite for sintering application (i.e. 
as metal binder for abrasive tools) and Ceramic -
metal composites for coatings (i.e. thermal spraying processes) 
with compositions not accessible by other routes, and – beyond this – it creates unique microstructure thanks to the improved 
compatibility between matrix and reinforcement hard phase.  For example, this technique gives access to composition based on 
FeCuSn materials which have a lower sintering conditions compared to Cobalt ( -20% in temperature) but with a  lower intrinsic 
cost of raw material.  
But powder as produced by high energy ball milling (HEBM) does not usually meet morphology targets for MIM processing, which 
requires a narrow Particle Size Distribution range and rounded particles. MBN have develope d methods to improve the 
morphological characteristics of the material by post processing via a technique called Jet Mill. The SUPREME project wants to 
develop further this processing chain by allowing the close integration of the different manufacturing steps to increase 
process efficiency of powder output in the MIM particle size distribution to approx. 100% and enhancing the homogeneity of th e 
material in terms of apparent density

SELECTIVE LASER MELT ING   
Selective Laser Melting is an additive manufacturing 
technique using 3D CAD models and laser energy to melt 
metallic powder. The part is built (printed) layer-by-layer, each 
layer being a powder bed with a 20-100µm thickness. 
Current commercial SLM machines have a building volume 
with a 250mm*250mm*250mm size and a building 
construction speed of 10 -30cm3/h. As highlighted by MTC 
recently10, additive manufacturing processes require powders 
with specific characteristics like size, spherical shape, 
flowability, purity, etc... Thus, atomization is currently one of 
the best way to produce these powders. However, because of 
the specific particle size fractions used in additive 
manufacturing and the moderate volumes up to now, powder 
atomizers are not yet specific, and yield is generally in the 
range of 45-60%. It is moreover not possible yet to reuse the smallest particles for further atomization. 25% of the cost of additive 
manufacturing parts is the raw material, and SUPREME will bring strong improvements by optimizing the processes further 
(machine fluid consumption, increase powder range usage, re-use of powder, etc.…).

into which powder is fed. The powder melts to form a deposit that is fusion-bonded to the substrate.

LASER METAL DEPOSITI ON  
Laser metal deposition (LMD) is a Direct Metal Deposition process in which 
a laser beam gives the energy. The powder filler material can be metallic or 
a combination of metallic and ceramic particles. By traversing both the 
nozzle and laser, a new material layer develops with precise accuracy and 
user-defined properties. The application of multi-layering techniques allows 
3D structures to be created. Both laser and nozzle can be traversed by a 
Cartesian gantry system or industrial robotic manipulator. 
LMD process yield is highly variable and is often defined by the application 
and its geometry. For surface claddings, the yield can be high (5kg/h – 
10kg/h), particularly if high laser powers (>> 5 kW) are used in conjunction 
with diffractive optics to enlarge the laser spot. However, the quality of the 
deposit (in terms of surface finish) is low, heat input is high (greater likelihood of thermal distortion) and the dilution ( intermixing 
between deposit and substrate) is also high. These process conditions are not suited for near net shape 3D structures. To achieve 
the latter, laser powers are generally much lower (<<2 kW) and as a result, productivity drops to 0.1-0.3 kg/h. At lower values of 
productivity (assuming correct choice of nozzle) the powder waste can be low (15 -25%). Like for PMD, SUPREME project will 
improve LMD productivity, with increasing the building speed, and reducing the powder waste during the whole process. 
Moreover, TWI has developed a world leading capability and proprietary software (ToolCLAD™ CAM software) to manufacture 
thin walled 3D geometries using LMD. The approach adopts a multiple axis deposition strategy which includes guided 
manipulation of the LMD nozzle in Cartesian space (xyz) relative to the surface of a continuously rotating and tilting build substrate.  
With precise synchronisation of the movements of rotation and tilt of the substrate with incremental movements of the coaxial  
nozzle, a continuous spiralling track weld can be deposited. This helical multi-layering technique allows a thin cross-section wall 
contour to form which is representative of the intended CAD geometry. By manipulating the substrate, and minimising the 
movement of the LMD nozzle, unsupported  overhanging features can be generated with a consistent high surface quality (in 
range 12-20 microns has been achieved with nickel alloys). The technique uses a coaxial LMD nozzle and has been used to 
manufacture pressure vessels and casings for gas turbi nes. However, productivity remains low (0.1 -0.3 kg/h), thus SUPREME 
project will look to develop procedures to manufacture similar geometries while using an LMD 3 beam nozzle: rather than 
introducing powder into the melt pool coaxially, the three -beam nozzle introduces powder in three discrete beams, increasing 
productivity. This allows for a much high powder flow volume but can compromise melt pool quality and consistency as the nozzle 
moves.

HOT ISOSTATIC PRESSI NG  
Hot Isostatic Pressing (HIP) is a solid-state process known for its material usage efficiency 
and its ability to manufacturing high value parts. Near -Net Shape Hot Isostatic Pressing 
(NNS-HIP) is an advanced HIP process which can manufacture complex shape (like for 
castings) with excellent material property (microstructure). It is being developed to 
manufacture near net-shape parts using pre -alloyed atomized powder, which is loaded in 
sacrificial canister up to its packing density and consolidated within a HIP vessel. The 
process has the potential to vastly improve the “ buy-to-fly” ratio of large aero components 
(going from 10:1 to 1.5:1) when compared to a conventional casting-forging and machining 
manufacturing route  
NNS-HIP can provide the required functionality of components in a manner which drastically 
cuts down the level of wasted material, reducing the environmental impact of the parts. For 
example, large aerospace components which are traditionally being made by casting -
forging coupled with extensive machining can be manufactured to near net shape using 
this advanced technology. Moreover, these components cannot be made by other net -
shape processes such as casting, as the properties resulting from these manufacturing methods do not allow them to withstand 
the high temperatures, stresses and lifetime requirements of the aero-engine components. 
There are many benefits of the HIP technology over the conventional manufacturing processes such as forging, casting and 
machining in many applications. In the SUPREME project, efficient near net shape HIP of complex pa rts will be 
demonstrated on Ni-based materials, with a strong objective to minimize the raw material consumption by 70% and to minimise 
machining operations and energy consumption up to 45% (which ultimately can give up to 50% cost saving).

Nowadays, diamond blades and wires are commonly used for sawing natural stone, concrete and ceramics. These hard metals 
represented 26% of the hard metal powder consumption in 2014, which was established to approximatively 80 ktons. The cutting 
section of the tool consists of synthetic diamond crystals embedded in a metallic matrix by various powder metallurgy (PM)

As a generic rule, the tougher and  more difficult to cut the workpiece, the finer and stronger the diamond type to be selected, 
while the matrix must wear at a rate corresponding to the rate of diamond breakdown. An incorrect choice of the matrix 
characteristics or diamond type, size and c oncentration yields a tool that wears away excessively or refuses to cut altogether.

1.3.4.  APPROACH AND METHODO LOGY  
SUPREME has gathered an ambitious consortium that will enable to cover the full supply chain of powder metallurgy from mineral 
extraction to end part manufacturing. Several production routes have been defined to demonstrate process optimization in terms 
of raw material resources reduction, energy efficiency, yield improvement, carbon dioxide decrease based on a set of advanced 
ferrous and non-ferrous metals. On the upstream of the supply chain, SUPREME will enable to achieve more efficient mineral

manufacturing for mining which will ensure a process demonstration in real operation settings. Then, SUPREME will address

ASL, GKN and MBN to upgrade the existing equipment into advanced and more sustainable facilities to make powders affordable 
and reliable to address part manufacturing processes. Metal parts for business cases demonstrations will be covered on a broad 
range of materials and processes: all production routes will be validated by end-users (DELLAS, MBA, MOO, CRF, and PRISMA). 
These developments will be based on raw material resources reduction and energy efficiency improvement for additive 
manufacturing and near-net shape processes and thanks to the support of research and innovation activities led by TECNALIA, 
CEA, TWI, PRODINTEC and IPC. Equipment manufacturers (RENISHAW, RHP) will also have a core role for optimizing the

Gas atomisation  
Gas atomization is a process to manufacture high quality 
metal powders. During the gas atomization process, the 
molten metal is atomized thanks to inert gas jets into fine 
metal droplets which cool down during their fall in the 
atomizing tower. Metal powders obtained by gas -atomization 
offer a generally spherical shape combine d with a high 
cleanliness and low oxidation level. Traditional gas atomising 
plants simply spray the molten droplets into a large vessel and 
collect the powder at the bottom of the vessel and the exhaust 
cyclone. These simple atomisers have the disadvantag e that 
fine particles get recirculated inside the atomising vessel 
creating thick dust clouds. These fine metal powder dust 
particles interfere with the droplet formation and result in 
particles that have multiple smaller particles stuck to the 
surface of larger particles. These particles generally known as 
“satellited” particles do affect the flow -ability of the atomised 
powders and leave particles looking far from perfectly 
spherical. Gas atomisers are generally very energy intensive 
process equipment and  most of the energy is used to 
Atomising Systems Ltd.  has developed technology that can 
address the shortcomings of the existing gas atomisation 
process in several ways. To reduce the amount of satellite of 
the powder particles in the atomising vessel ASL has 
developed an anti- satellite system that dramatically improves 
powder flow characteristics of the atomised powder. The 
improved flow properties should enable use of a wider particle 
size range in additive manufacturing (AM) type process 
applications (SLM, Blown powder processes etc.). Being able 
to use powders with a wider particle size range would increase 
the powder yield from the as -atomised powder and by that 
reduce production cost of the metal powder feedstock used for 
AM applications.

Water atomisation 
Water Atomization typically is not considered the optimum 
powder production route for Additive Manufacturing.  Because 
of the high cooling rates of the atomizing water the powder 
particles are irregular in shape which generally leads to poor 
packing density and poor powder flow. Some researchers 
have identified mixes of different particle sizes that improve 
the packing but generally these have a negative impact of the 
flow of the powder.   In addition to the shape of the powder, 
atomization by water, leads to a higher level of oxidation on 
the powder surface. This oxygen can lead to poor fusion and 
inclusions in parts made by SLM (selective laser 
melting).  Compared with gas atomization the oxygen content 
can be three to four times higher.   Despite these problems 
water atomization, from an energy perspective, is far more 
efficient than gas atomization in producing the fine powders 
required by Additive manufacturing.   The higher momentum 
imparted by the denser water (compared with gas) to the 
molten metal stream leads to a better yield of the 10 -45-
micron powder required for many SLM machines.  The energy 
required by water atomization is considerably less than gas 
atomizing syste ms since the water can be recycled more 
readily than gas. 
Methods of water atomization utilizing low water to metal 
ratio’s, along with low atomizing jet angles will be explored to 
improve both the sphericity and surface roughness of 
powder.  These methods will be implored utilizing high water 
pressures with short jet distances to improve the yield of the 
10-45-micron size fraction (that necessary for AM 
Machines).  In addition, secondary processing, such as jet 
milling, hammer milling and cold rolling will  be explored in an 
effort to improve particle shape in regards to particle packing 
and powder flow.  Alloy development will take place to develop 
alloys that meet minimum strength requirements while keeping 
oxygen content similar to that of gas atomization .  Surface 
powder treatments, such as annealing the powder in hydrogen 
and mixtures of hydrogen and nitrogen as well as vacuum will 
be employed to lower the oxidation on the surface of the 
powder.  Mechanical properties (including fatigue) of these 
alloys will be compared to standard water and gas atomized 
powders to quantify the changes made to the powder. 
The economics of the various process will be compared to gas 
atomization to quantify the benefits of each processing 
method. 
Ball milling process downstream integration between milling, classification/sieving and comminution 
Powder as produced by high energy ball milling (HEBM) does 
not usually meet morphology targets for MIM processing, 
which requires a Particle Size Distribution (PSD) below 38 
micron and particles round in shape. MBN have developed 
methods to improve the morphological characteristics of 
HEBM powders for MIM using a classification process called 
Jet Mill. The process works by agitating a cloud of powder in 
a chamber by jet gas stre ams (air or inert gas) before being 
passed to the classification chamber where the powder is 
sorted according to particle size. The interaction of the powder 
with the jet streams cause many high velocity inter -particle 
impact collisions that removes sharp edges, particularly for 
brittle materials, producing more spherical particles. The 
retention time during jet milling is very short (less than three 
minutes) because particles are continuously classified during 
the process and removed once they reach desire d size. The 
The powder classification system currently is not connected to 
the ball milling reactor chamber. In the Mechanomade® HEBM 
system of MBN the grinding means are accelerated to the 
impact working surfaces at very high velocities, generating 
high inertial and vibrational forces. A new powder downloading 
system will be developed based on pneumatic approach, 
which allows a fast discharge of the milling reactor thanks to a 
differential pressure generated in the reactor. The automated 
and continuous powder handling system will connect the 
milling plant with the classification device and will include a 
micronization system capabl e of disaggregating the coarser 
powder fraction (above 38 micron) for fully recovering all the 
powder and maximizing the yield. The powder will be then 
conveyed automatically in a proper mixer to homogenize the

Homogeneous and dense Metal Matrix Composites  
Metal Matrix Composites production by MIM is addressed 
in the project with cutting tools made of FeCuSn matrix and 
diamond particles. For such materials, the feedstock for 
molding is prepared by mixing a suitable ratio of powders 
and binders. 
Several issues are met at this step: inhomogeneous 
mixing due to a difficulty to break agglomerates of fine 
particles, low density after sintering if the finest particles 
surround the biggest ones which limits matter diffusion. 
To produce homogenous and dense MMC, the feedstock 
preparation has to consider various parameters such as the mixing 
speed and the temperature, the mixing time and the sequence. 
The binder and powder characterist ics will also affect 
homogeneity. Therefore, the ball milling process will supply 
suitable particles to guarantee a good distribution of particles in 
the feedstock without any agglomerates and a low particles size 
distribution which makes easier the densification by sintering. 
High engineering tolerance on complex parts 
The MIM process has already been investigated to 
develop micro -components with a high level of 
engineering tolerances. Previous work led with CEA 
showed, the ability of MIM process to produce medical 
tools, including a higher fork length and a thinner fork 
section of the functional part without finishing treatment 
after sintering. By this work, a standard deviation below 4 
µm at the 150 µm diameter branch level was measured. 
To avoid finishing operations on thicker components, such level of 
dimensional tolerance (10 µm) has to be reached. 
Improvements of feedstock homogeneity, mould surface, injection 
operation setting, debinding step by a better control of binder 
decomposition gas emission and suitable support for components 
during sintering will be investigated during the project. 
Environmental 
Most of part of MIM industry uses polyacetal (POM) binder 
system which provides good moldability and excellent 
shape retention. Binder removal is done in a gaseous acid 
environment, i.e. highly concentrated nitric acid at  a 
temperature of approximately 120°C, which is below the 
softening temperature of the binder. The acid acts as a 
catalyst in the decomposition of the polymer bind er. 
Reaction products are burnt in a natural gas flame at 
temperatures above 600°C. 
However, this requires specific debinding furnace and 
gaseous nitric acid (> 98,5 %), which makes the process 
more expensive than thermal debinding, that can be 
carried out in the same furnace of sintering process and 
which does not require gaseous acids. Likewise, the 
condensation of nitric acid must be strongly considered as 
a corrosion problem for the thermal treatment of the 
metallic product. Moreover, the amount of resi due 
generated in the catalytic debinding is higher than thermal 
debinding.  
With high nitric acid concentration, the medium is strongly 
oxidizing. The anhydrous acid vapour does not react with 
most of the commonly used metal powders, e.g. Fe, Ni, 
Mo, Si, but Ti, Co, Cu and their alloys undergo oxidation in 
this debinding atmosphere, even at low temperature. 
To overcome environmental and technical issues of catalyst 
debinding using polyacetal -based polymers mixtures, alternative 
chemistry of binding will be considered. By using water soluble 
binder (PEG), the incompatible alloys can be processed by MIM.  
 
Indeed, with PEG based binders the debinding is divided in 2 
steps: water debinding to remove water soluble binder and thermal 
debinding which is combined with sintering cycle. Such approach 
is economically relevant: 2 furnaces required catalyst debinding 
and sintering furnace).

Canister Manufacturing 
Despite the success of manufacturing these aerospace components, there are 
several issues which need to be overcome before this technology can prove itself 
as a viable option for routine production of medium to large sized components. 
These issues include availability of HIP unit sizes within EU (currently parts up to 
Ø1200mm by 1000mm high), metal powder cost through resource effectiveness, 
availability of sophisticated degassing system, tooling cost, canister/powder 
interaction and difficulty of processing some materials suc h as aluminium alloys 
and nickel base super alloys. 
Near-net shape HIP is a lean manufacturing route to produce high integrity parts 
which possess shape complexity of casting and properties of thermo -
mechanically forged material. Conventional manufacturing methods for materials 
with high alloy content have low process yields and typically utilize only 10 to 30% 
of the material purchased in the final product; the remained becomes scrap during 
machining27.  
Previous work has shown the feasibility of manufacturing NNS HIP aerospace 
components28. In this technique, a sacrificial canister is designed in such a way 
that once filled with metal powder and subsequently HIPed, 100% theoretical 
density is achieved. Depending on the powder packing density within the canister, 
there is volume shrinkage (~30%) during the HIP cycle. The sacrificial canister is 
usually removed by machining and/or pickling operation and a net shape  
component is produced. The geometry of the canister is designed through finite 
element modelling, taking into account the shrinkage during powder consolidation, 
canister material deformation etc. This is important to achieve the required 
geometry of the component after the removal of canister material. PM HIP material 
are usually endowed with a fine isotropic microstructure with mech anical 
properties superior to casting and similar to wrought material. 
The aim of SUPREME is to develop and 
validate a resource-effective NNS PM HIP 
manufacturing route for Inconel 626 
components for aerospace application. 
This work will establish the HIP processing 
window for Inconel 625 powder and 
explore new low cost canister 
manufacturing methods development. The 
effect of narrow and wide powder particle 
size ranges on microstructure and 
mechanical properties of the HIPed 
material will also be assessed. Thus, using 
optimised process parameters, small scale 
representative geometrical component will 
be produced and assessed to see if they 
meet the OEM requirements in terms of 
accuracy, integrity and mechanical 
properties.

Producing a process control and monitoring system common for different industries to improve their processes 
Common industrial processes do not often monitor in 
real-time the key parameters of their production and rely 
on post-production analysis or even do not have relevant 
indicators of key parameters. Moreover, the available 
process monitoring tools are limited to certain analysers 
and do not provide the user with the needed flexibility for 
controlling their process efficiently.  
By developing the SUPREME novel control and monitoring system 
PAT technologies will be applied whereby the industry brings the lab 
to the production process obtaining relevant data, in real time 
through dedicated sensors. These sensors can range from simple 
temperature probes to complex optical analysers which perform on-
line chemical analysis of metals or deposition layers. With this 
information and after applying deep -learning and process control 
techniques we will be able to provide the operator with the essential 
information to efficiently improve their process in terms of raw 
material usage, energy consumption, faulty production red uction, 
etc.

2.2.2.1.  MANAGEMENT OF RULES FOR INTELLECTUAL PROPERTY  
All partners will sign a consortium agreement. The principles of this agreement relating to Intellectual Property (IP) are summarised 
below. Each participant will list its pre -existing know-how as an annex of the consortium agreement, thereb y defining its pre -
existing non-patented IP relative to the other partners. During the project, the IP management principle is that each participant 
executing any task of the project work-plan owns the IP limited to the new information, new know-how, or deliverable generated 
by its own execution of this specific task. Each partner is then responsible of filing patents when necessary. The management of 
IP conflicts by the consortium is expected to be necessary in some critical cases. In order to avoid polemi cs and conflicts, all 
partners agree on the following practical rules: 
- The Project Coordinator (PC) will be bound by specific confidentiality clauses to be detailed in the Consortium 
Agreement; 
- If, for the progress of the project, a partner needs to transf er any not yet protected information to one or more other 
partner(s) and if the time needed for protecting the information would penalise the progress of the project, the transferring 
partner may register this information by addressing it to the Project Co ordinator together with the identification of the 
receiving partner; 
- The PC acknowledges the receipt of the registered information. In its acknowledgement, the PC appends a copy of the 
registered information with its signature on each page. The PC further informs the receiving partner(s) that it registered 
some information; 
- When receiving the acknowledgement from the PC, the transferring partner discloses the information to the other 
partner(s). By signing the consortium agreement, both transferring and receiving partner(s) recognise that the information 
registered by the PC will define the boundary of the information which has been transferred for the sake of executing the 
project; 
- Any unprotected information, which is not transferred by respecting the prev ious detailed steps, must be handled on a 
confidential basis by the receiving partner(s). However, by skipping the previous steps, the transferring partner implicitly 
recognises that the boundary of the transferred information is already protected by one of the following items: i) Patent(s); 
ii) The appropriate annex to the consortium agreement listing the pre -existing know -how; iii) Any previous legal 
agreement between the involved parties; and iv) A previous registration by PC. 
Every meeting of the Execut ive Board will collect, examine and ru le on any IP issues including: i) Identification o f patent 
opportunities, ii) Resolution of polemics and conflicts and iii) Patent situation within the consortium. 
2.2.2.2.  MANAGEMENT RULES FOR  PATENTS APPLICATIONS  
Each partner is responsible for filing patents where appropriate. If a patent may be constructed based on overlapping know-how 
of several partners, the basic principle is that the involved partners will equally share the ownership of the patent and the costs of 
applying for it. If one participant owning some part of the overlapping know-how is not willing to endorse its percentage of the cost 
of applying for such shared patent, the conditions of transferring its know-how will be negotiated on a case by case basis by the 
involved partners. In any case, only scientists who contributed to the innovation, which resulted in the patent, are included in the 
authors’ list of the patent. This is in accordance to the present praxis and legal requirements of patent applications, b oth in EU 
and in the USA. 
2.2.2.3.  MANAGEMENT RULES FOR  PUBLICATIONS  
The primary goal of this project is to enable the participating industrial and research centres to secure their respective IP relative

to the project is committed to the confidentiality until the publication of the results in a scientific journal or as the contribution to a

The SUPREME project is structured into 8 WPs, with a top down approach, covering the all value chain from raw material

composite, Maraging steel) and non-ferrous based materials (Hastelloy C22, Inconel 625), as these materials are widely used in

WP1 is focusing on efficient raw material production. The main target of WP1 is to increase the extraction and recovery efficiency, 
and at the same time lowering the fluids consumption (mainly water in this case). An optimized process control from IRIS will be 
applied to optimize and further control the main parameters of the raw material production processes. Outputs from WP1 will have

casting, forging and extrusion. 
WP2 is covering efficient powder production processes. The SUPREME project covers 3 types of processes for ferrous and non-
ferrous material production: ball milling (MBN), gas -atomisation (ASL) and water -atomization (GKN). MBN will be focusing on 
ferrous-based alloy (FeCuSn) used as metals bonds for the manufacturing of diamond tools.  Such material will allow replacing 
Cobalt binder, which will at the same time reduce cost by 30% and avoid the use of scarce elements. Furthermore, MBN will 
target the improvement of the yield of ball milling process by 30% (time & energy), thanks to optimised milling parameters (<38µm 
particle size). GKN and ASL will focus on a strong objective for atomization process: to reduce energy consumption by 20% (for 
gas heating, gas and water pressurizing), gas consumption by 30% (Argon, N2) and powder production yield by 10%. The work 
will mainly focus on developing and assessing the efficiency of atomization systems upgrades (nozzle type, re-circulation system, 
pre-heating) and also to optimized the size distribution towards different uses (MIM, SLM, LMD, Sintering, HIP…). 
WP3 and WP4 are at the heart of the project. Their objective is to increase the efficiency of 3D manufacturing processes (without 
mould: SLM, LMD, PMD) and near -net shape manufacturing processes (with mould: MIM, HIP, Sintering). WP3 will focu s on 
process improvement (increasing the powder bed thickness for SLM, reducing the gas consumption during process and purging 
for SLM and LMD, increasing the building speed of both processes) and also on powder usage improvement (re -using powders 
for multiple printing, replacing gas -atomized powder by water -atomized powder). WP4, will target the improvement of material 
and energy consumption in near-net shape processes. Although these processes are far more efficient in terms of material usage 
compared to conventional machining, strong improvements are still possible. The development on the MIM process will aim at 
developing accurate process control and minimizing rejected parts to near 0%. This process will be demonstrated for new 
materials such as FeCuSn composite and 17-4PH. Development on HIP will mainly concentrate on the improvement of the buy-
to-fly ratio compared to casting process, and on reducing rejected parts due to powder segregation issues. 
As part of the SUPREME project’s particular emphasis on result dem onstration, WP5, is focusing on 5 use -cases. Although all 
end-users are competing in the worldwide market on a cost/production volume base, a part of the cost saving brought by the 
SUPREME project can directly be translated to savings in raw material losses, consumables (die, assembly parts), part weight, 
and fluid consumption in the whole manufacturing process (from extraction to final parts). DELLAS, as cutting tools manufacturer, 
will demonstrate a >15% reduction in cost with MIM technology compared to current TRL9 technology relying on conventional 
sintering process, and will at the same 
time improve the health and saf ety 
working conditions. PRISMA , as 
aeronautic part supplier, will 
demonstrate a >30% saving in part cost 
with SLM technology compared to 
conventional machining. This will rely on 
both a reduction of raw material & 
energy use thanks to part optimization 
(lower weight) and a suppression of 
assembly steps. MBA, for medical 
sector, w ill also rely on SLM and MIM 
processes to reduce by 25% the raw 
material consumption compared to 
today production technology, while 
maintaining the same biomechanical 
equivalence. MOO, as mould supplier 
for plastic industry, will demonstrate a 
functional s aving equivalent to 10% 
improvement in plastic injection process, thanks to conformal cooling. Finally, CRF, as automotive supplier, will focus on medium 
production volume (10k-100k) to demonstrate with SLM process a 10% energy & raw material consumption reduction compared 
to conventional processes (machining and assembly). Finally , the WP6, leaded by IRIS, will cover a common need to previous 
WP, which is a process control, which will allow to optimize all processes toward certain key parameters. 
WP7 will cover dissemination, and WP8 will handle the management of the SUPREME project, which is gathering together more 
than 20 partners, with a strong participation of SME and industrial partners, towards a common objective: reduce raw material,
