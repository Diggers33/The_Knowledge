MULTIPLE will bring together snapshot mosaic filters, OLED -based sensors,  and state -of-the-art machine 
learning to deliver breakthrough and cost effective snapshot hyperspectral imaging and spectrometric solutions 
covering a broad spectral range and suited to actual industrial monitoring and control needs.  MULTIPLE 
multimodal monitoring systems will be  IoT native, exploiting open source cloud, big data, and deep learning  
technology. A fast orchestrated deployment of data-driven AI-based models will foster production optimization. 
The affordability of monolithic uncooled CMOS and CCD sensors sensitive in the visible (VIS) and part of the near 
infrared (NIR) range has fuelled the spread of machine vision and photonic sensors throughout machinery and 
production lines. They are necessary elements of any automated production line we can imagine, monitoring a wide 
variety of magnitudes and providing feedback for optimising manufacturing processes and product quality. Europe 
has played a clearly leading role in the field with key suppliers  of sensors (e.g. AMS), cameras (e.g. Basler, JAI, 
PhotonFocus), software (e.g. MVTec) and a vibrant network of SMEs and midcaps delivering turnkey solutions for 
process monitoring and control to virtually any manufacturing sector.  
While hybrid and cooled sensor and FPA technologies (e.g. InGaAs, InSb, MCT) have been traditionally regarded 
as expensive and bulky, the high demand and technical advances on InGaAs FPAs have cut down their cost making 
them attractive for the industrial vision market. Relevant camera suppliers in this segment like PhotonFocus already 
market InGaAs cameras with tag prices under the 10,000€ barrier, with the expectation of further cuts in the short -
medium term. Besides, new technologies like OLED sensors are set to make a breakthr ough in terms of cost and 
performance and have already shown a big potential in chemometric applications.  
The last years have seen a research boom in hyperspectral imaging (HSI) and spectroscopy measurement 
technologies, especially in a spectral range tha t spans the visible, short -wavelength infrared (SWIR), and mid -
wavelength infrared (MWIR). This part of the optical spectrum has proved as a privileged proxy to material 
properties, especially suited to non-destructive monitoring. It avoids the damage and risks of ionising radiation, and 
also the environmental noise of thermal radiation. Compared to widespread panchromatic or trichromatic systems, 
spectrally resolved technology has demonstrated far higher accuracy and specificity, key to address more and mo re 
scenarios of industrial monitoring and control, across many different sectors.  
This context explains the optimistic forecasts on the hyperspectral imaging systems market, projected to reach USD 
21.33 bn by 2023 from USD 8.14 bn in 2017, at a strong CAGR of 17.5%1. Hyperspectral imaging -i.e. sensing with 
a crowd of finer spectral bands - has been associated to bulky and expensive spectrometers only capable of push -
broom imaging, not suitable to take snapshots, while the term snapshot has been largely associated to a few spectral 
bands, with configurations other that Bayer-based or filter-based solutions both bulky and expensive. 
Mosaic pixel-level filters by IMEC have set a breakthrough in the field, enabling snapshot hyperspectral imaging in 
the visible range first, and in the SWIR range more recently. This technology -as a coating process - is suitable for 
volume production and does not alter the compactness of imagers. That is, it is suited to deliver low SWaPC (size, 
weight, power and cost) solutions.  
Moreover, the maturity of computational imaging for dual and multi -aperture optics -already empowering many 
smartphone cameras- brings a clear opportunity. Combined with the feasibility of cost effective snapshot HSI camera 
cores in the SWIR and VIS range, it sets the conditions to deliver novel snapshot HSI VIS/SWIR imagers with killing 
price and capabilities. The promise of bringing to the market broadband highly selective hyperspectral imaging  
covering what is likely the most important spectral range for process and product monitoring is only a collaboration 
away. A collaboration of few European organisations. 
The use of low cost high performance OLED sensors, together with advanced laser -based concepts, in the context 
of chemometric analysis offers clear opportunities to deliver novel on -line and in situ analysers in an even broader 
spectral range with a big impact on process and product quality. 
The development of models for measurement and classification is still prone to handcrafted engineering workflows 
(costly and slow). Desktop software libraries and small sets of data are the main and most usual resources used for 
modelling, building, and testing. When faced to high dimensional data (e.g. hyperspectral imaging) this burden is 
likely to discourage t he development effort. It is for a reason that most results reported on HSI have an academic 
source and few commercial solutions are available yet compared to the wide adoption of standard vision systems.  
The recent success of  data-driven approaches and deep neural networks -especially for image analysis - enables a 
faster development of models with better regression, classification, or segmentation accuracy. While the scarcity of 
suitable data for training has remained a barrier for adoption, new cloud-based architectures have demonstrated the 
capability to collect, store, and annotate big data, enabling the training of new models in a fraction of time.

MULTIPLE will deliver breakthrough and cost-effective snapshot HSI cameras  in the broad VIS/SWIR range,

1.1 Objectives 
The main goal of MULTIPLE is the de velopment of cost effective multimodal monitoring solutions with a 
breakthrough impact on production quality and efficiency. Key technology features pursued by MULTIPLE are a 
broader spectral range with snapshot hyperspectral capabilities, and an IoT native approach to the development and 
deployment of machine learning models (i.e. statistical and deep learning models) for overall monitoring and control 
of production lines. 
With this aim, MULTIPLE will pursue the following technical goals: 
1. The development o f cost -effective HSI SWIR (0.9 -1.7µm wavelength) camera cores ready for volume 
production, covering a missing offer highly demanded by the market.  
2. The development of dual aperture snapshot HSI imagers combining snapshot CMOS and InGaAs hyperspectral 
sensors based on mosaic filters, delivering breakthrough snapshot hyperspectral imaging (HSI) capabilities in 
the whole VIS/SWIR range (0.4-1.7µm wavelength). 
3. The development of novel spectrometric solutions for accurate highly specific chemometric analysis in an 
extended VIS/MWIR (0.4 -5µm wavelength) range. Innovative concepts in OLED sensors, laser -based 
spectroscopy, and signal processing will be combined in MULTIPLE to deliver new spectrometric solutions with 
breakthrough sensitivity and performance.
