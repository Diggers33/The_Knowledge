Project Number 1 101023685 Project Acronym 2 BioSupPack
One form per project
General information
Project title 3
Demonstrative process for the production and enzymatic recycling of environmentally
safe, superior and versatile PHA-based rigid packaging solutions by plasma integration
in the value chain.
Starting date 4 01/06/2021
Duration in months 5 42
Call (part) identifier 6 H2020-BBI-JTI-2020
Topic
BBI-2020-SO3-D4
Demonstrate superior bio-based packaging solutions with minimal environmental
damage
Fixed EC Keywords
Polymers and plastics, Bioprocessing technologies (industrial processes relying on
biological agents to drive the process) biocatalysis, fermentation, Bioproducts (products
that are manufactured using biological material as feedstock) biomaterials, bioplastics,
biofuels, bioderived bulk and fine chemicals, bio-derived novel materials
Free keywords
PHA/ PHB & high performance rigid packagin; plasma, PHA coatings, fatty acid
grafting; post-consumers waste sorting & selective enzymatic recycling (EoL); brew
spent grains (BSG) biomass feedstock.
Abstract 7
BioSupPBioSupPack goal is to deliver novel, cost-competitive and versatile bio-based packaging solutions based on
PHA, that demonstrate high-performance for the packaging of food, cosmetics, homecare and beverage products as
well as no environmental damage during & after their use, by means of:
1. Optimization of PHA based formulations based on significantly use of >85% w/w of renewable resources.
2. Upscale the different conversion processes as well as post-consumers waste sorting & recycling following advanced
industry 4.0 approach.
3. Broad the range of rigid packaging applications by tailoring biobased materials & packaging properties through the
optimization of the formulations in combination with plasma technology or grafted coatings.
4. Integrate plasma technology in 3 different points of the value chain(biomasss pre-treatment, packaging production
& packaging waste pre-treatment) increasing: i)PHB production yield, ii)PHB purity, iii) packaging performance(high
oxygen & water barrier) and iv effectiveness & yield of enzymatic recycling.
5. Demonstrate and increase recyclability through:i) setting up a real-time monitoring system for the selective
separation of developed packaging after its use; ii) mechanical recycling of industrial scraps and selective enzymatic
recycling of packaging waste as the best EoL, with the final recovery of carbon sources for feedstock fermentation.
Thus, the new packaging is environmentally safe(sustainable and improved value from enzymatic recycling) and
contributes to Circular Economy(CE).
6. Establish a new value chain including the development of logistics and management of both the brewery and
packaging waste.
Complying with the industry needs (cost/performance competitive vs. fossil-based non-biodegradable counterparts
and legislative compliance),as well as with consumers´ awareness, BioSupPack will have a great impact on EU
bioplastics & end users’ sectors, the biorefineries and biotechnology industries and on the society.

1.3.3. WT3 Work package descriptions
Work package number 9 WP1 Lead beneficiary 10 8 - SABIO
Work package title Production of biobased materials for specific packaging applications
Start month 1 End month 24
Objectives
• To define the biopolymers and possible additives technical requirements according to converters and end users.
• To establish the specifications for the packaging demonstrators, their manufacturing, and their target EoL.
• To establish plasma technology in the pre-treatment of brewery waste by optimization of technology parameters.
• To optimize the fermentation process of pre-treated brewery waste at laboratory scale.
• To produce new biobased materials (PHB) by fermentation process of pre-treated brewery waste.
• To select and modify commercial PHA grades for PHA based coatings for fibre-based rigid packaging applications
(service packaging).
• To produce PHB formulations for rigid packaging applications (sauces, cosmetics, homecare, beverage).
Description of work and role of partners
WP1 - Production of biobased materials for specific packaging applications [Months: 1-24]
SABIO, AIMPLAS, AUA, CTB, BUT, PLT, NFG, MICA, ILAB, GPI, ULK
WP1 aims at producing the new biobased materials to be used in the production of the packaging prototypes in WP2.
PHB will be produced by NFG from BSG provided by MICA by fermentation routes. AUA and BUT will contribute to
the preliminary optimization/adaptation of the bioprocesses according to the biomass characteristics at lab scale. Plasma
BSG pre-treatment will be carried out by PLT to remove the lignin fraction. Subsequent enzymatic hydrolysis using
appropriate enzyme consortia provided by ASA will target the hydrolysis of mainly cellulose and hemicellulose and
also lipids and proteins if feasible. The obtained material will be used for the design and the production of compounds,
suitable to produce rigid packaging at pilot scale, by SABIO at the end of the WP. In addition, PHA commercial grades
will be selected and tailored by CTB for their application as wet coatings on paper/cardboard substrates provided by
GPI at small pilot scale.
Task 1.1 Definition of target biobased packaging specifications. Leader: SABIO. Partners involved: ULK, NFG, MICA,
ILAB, AIMPLAS, CTB, GPI (M1-M4)
Materials´ specifications will be defined for each packaging application based on the materials currently used, the
processing technologies for their production and the specific performance required. UV and oxygen stability and basic
mechanical properties of the material for packaging production will be agreed with respect to the product shelf life.
Moreover, the size of the packaging and its packing arrangement for transport will be set. The participating packaging
manufacturers’ (ILAB, GPI) and end users’ (ULK, NFG, MICA) will collaborate, by providing their expertise in
the packaging sector. SABIO´s expertise in the development of tailor-made biobased materials according to the final
packaging applications will also contribute to the packaging requirements definition. Other specific market requirements
for the implementation of new materials will be defined (e.g. cost, regulatory/conformity and environmental aspects,
etc.).
Task 1.2 Pre-treatment of brewery waste by plasma technology. Preparative actions for fermentation processes. Leader:
PLT. Partners involved: AUA, NFG, ASA (M1-M20)
PLT and AUA will optimise BSG pre-treatment using the novel, scalable DBD plasma-bubble reactor designed by PLT
using atmospheric air as feed gas and water as solvent. The main target will be to remove lignin, while retaining intact
most of the cellulose and hemicellulose for subsequent enzymatic hydrolysis. Plasma parameters of voltage, frequency,
duty cycle (e.g. 5, 10, 15, 25, 45%) and the air relativity humidity will be optimised for process efficiency. Electrical
and optical diagnostics will be employed to characterise the plasma reactors which may also support future process
control approaches. Different BSG concentrations (e.g. 50, 100, 150, 200 g/L) in aqueous suspensions, mechanical
stirring, pre-treatment duration, and BSG particle size (e.g. 50–800 μm) will be evaluated. Different BSG particle sizes
will be produced using a hammer mill or a jet milling pilot equipment available at AUA. Depending on preliminary
data, a Design of Experiment approach may be employed to optimise the most influential plasma treatment parameters.
The air DBD bubble reactor will be tested to produce low levels of ROS and RNS using low power conditions and
Page 12 of 62
larger volume vessels. Quantification generated ROS and RNS generated as a function of the feed gas (air/N2/O2)
will be mapped and correlated to BSG pre-treatment efficiency. ROS, RNS and BSG compositional changes (e.g.
sugars, cellulose, hemicellulose, acid soluble lignin, total lignin, protein, lipids etc.) will be identified using off-line (e.g.
NREL analytical protocols, Total Kjeldahl Nitrogen) and on-line analytical methods (e.g. FTIR). Conventional BSG
pre-treatment methods (i.e. dilute acid, hydrothermal, microwave assisted pre-treatments) will be used for comparison
purposes.
The remaining pre-treated BSG solids will be subjected to enzymatic hydrolysis using appropriate enzymatic cocktails
provided by ASA containing mainly cellulase and hemicellulose as well as lipase and protease. At least four different
enzyme cocktails will be evaluated considering polysaccharide to sugar conversion yield, final sugar concentration,
initial enzyme activity required and hydrolysis duration (protein and lipid hydrolysis efficiency will be also considered)
in small scale by AUA. Different parameters affecting enzymatic hydrolysis (e.g. enzyme dosage, solid loading,
addition of surfactants, degree of pre-treated biomass) will be optimised targeting more than 90% BSG cellulose and
hemicellulose to sugar hydrolysis yield. Enzymatic hydrolysis efficiency will be evaluated on (1) untreated BSG, (2)
plasma pre-treated BSG, and (3) BSG pre-treated with conventional methods.
Task 1.3 Biobased materials production (lab scale). Leader: NFG. Partners involved: AUA, BUT, ASA (M3-M14)
The BSG-derived cultivation media prepared in Task 1.2 will be used for PHB production, at first at laboratory scale,
to optimize and set the crucial cultivation approaches, strategies and parameters to maximize productivity, final PHB
concentration and carbon source to PHB conversion yield. AUA and NFG will focus on bioprocess development using
the bacterial strain Burkholderia sacchari. BUT and NFG will employ extremophilic halophilic bacterium Halomonas
halophila CCM 3662 and thermophilic bacterium Schlegelella thermodepolymerans DSM 15344. Based on prior
experience, PHB production will be optimised in lab-scale bioreactors (up to 5 L). Batch and fed-batch cultures will be
carried out using either commercial sugars with simulated sugar composition as in the BSG hydrolysate or the crude BSG
hydrolysate obtained in Task 1.2. Fermentation optimisation will focus on initial C5/C6 sugar concentration, carbon-
to-nitrogen ratio (C/N), carbon-to-phosphorus ratio (C/P), inoculum preparation, BSG-derived media composition (e.g.
protein hydrolysate products and fatty acids from lipids), antifoam addition, feeding strategy, pH control strategy, kLa,
aeration and agitation. Concentrated BSG hydrolysate will be used as feeding media. The individual process will be
compared and evaluated in terms of PHA titers, productivity and yields and the most promising protocols will be further
tested.
NFG, AUA and BUT will optimize downstream separation and purification (DSP) using “green” solvents (e.g. 1,3-
dioxolane, ethyl lactate, ethyl acetate, diethyl oxalate, dimethyl carbonate) and recyclable SAS (e.g. ammonium laurate)
for efficient and sustainable PHB recovery without Mw reduction. PHB recovery using chloroform and conventional
surfactants (e.g. sodium dodecyl sulfate) will be employed as control protocols for PHB extraction and purification from
bacterial mass. DSP development will focus on process parameter optimization (e.g. initial bacterial mass concentration,
temperature, reaction duration) to maximise PHB purity and recovery yield while at the same time maintain high Mw
and achieve desirable PHB thermo-physical and mechanical properties. LCA and costing evaluation in WP6 will be
employed to identify the optimal DSP.
As AUA and BUT possess comparable equipment and experience, interlaboratory verification of developed cultivation
protocols will be performed to evaluate robustness and reproducibility of the process and identify potentially tricky
parameters and steps with respect to further scale-up of the process planned in Task 1.4. Up to 50 g of PHB samples
will be obtained by fermentation (up to 30 L) and used for polymer characterization for subsequent bioprocess scale-
up and packaging formulation tasks.
Task 1.4 Biobased materials production (pilot scale). Leader: NFG. Partners involved: AUA, AIMPLAS, PLT, SABIO,
BUT (M9-M24)
A series of scale-up air DBD plasma bubble reactors will be designed and built by PLT ranging from 1-50 L using the
optimised process developed in Task 1.2. The already scaled technology of PLT will be used for the reactor scaling
process. The scale-up of enzymatic hydrolysis of the pre-treated BSG will be performed by NFG using the optimal
enzyme cocktail and process conditions identified in Task 1.2. The hydrolysis will be conducted in 1500 L reactors and
followed by microfiltration and concentrating the hydrolysate to approximately 700 g/L.
The most promising fermentation strategy and DSP developed in lab scale in task 1.3 will be transferred into larger
scale by NFG. Firstly, fermentations will be tested and further optimized in bioreactors with scale of 75 L with the use
of up to approximately 5 kg of a feedstock. The process of PHA production will be further scaled up to pilot scale using
1500 L fermentor with filling of 1000 L. Up to 75 kg of feedstock is needed for pilot scale experiment. The optimal
DSP process identified in Task 1.3 together the currently used chloroform-based process will be employed by NFG to
isolate PHB suitable for task 1.5 with high purity and molecular weight of 500–600 kDa.
Page 13 of 62
AIMPLAS will collaborate in the characterization of preliminary PHA samples in terms of rheological properties, and
if considered, the preparation and characterization of preliminary compounds in the compounding lab equipment in
collaboration with SABIO. Afterwards, SABIO will produce preliminary compounds at pilot plant scale from small
PHA batches (3-5 kg) provided by NFG for their characterization. This will help to select the most suitable PHA material
to be produced by NFG in the 75 L reactor.
At a pilot scale we will not only evaluate process of PHA production from BSG, but we will also generate sufficient
amount of the material for subsequent tasks focused on production, processing (WP2) and validation of packaging
applications (WP3). Within the task, 150 Kg of PHA will be produced and transferred to other partners. In this task
we will take advantage of experience of NFG with development of PHA production process at industrial scale and of
established synergy cooperation between NFG and BUT. BUT along with AUA will provide analytical support to PHA
production in pilot scale (such as determination of biomass and PHA yields, determination of residual substrates in
cultivation media etc.) and, in addition, BUT and AUA will be able to scale-down the process to laboratory bioreactors
and solve potential problems whenever needed. In addition, the information obtained in the processing of commercial
PHAs in the production of the first packaging prototypes at pilot plant scale in Task 2.1, will be used as reference, to have
a basic knowledge about the amount of additives necessary for achieving desired properties set in T1.1. The described
connection between the partners involved in Task 1.4 and Task 2.1 will help to facilitate the scale-up of PHA production
in the best efficient way, producing a new material with the suitable properties for prototypes production in Task 2.3.
Valorization of additional BSG components: A) Isolation of phenolics will be performed by BUT prior to enzymatic
hydrolysis of BGS at pilot scale. Hence, apart from yielding potentially valuable product, removal of the phenolics will
be beneficial in terms of improvement of subsequent PHA production process since they are known to act as microbial
inhibitors reducing efficiency of PHA biosynthesis. Isolation of the phenolics will be carried out by commonly used
approach of solid-liquid extraction using suitable organic solvent (ethanol, methanol) etc. The process will be at first
evaluated at laboratory scale in terms of productivity (e.g. obtained grams of phenolics per kg of BSG) and purity of
the product for high value applications. Subsequently, isolated phenolic compounds will be tested for application as
antioxidant additives in PHAs or PHAs formulations by AIMPLAS; B) Isolation of proteins will be performed by AUA
and BUT either before or after plasma pre-treatment and enzymatic hydrolysis of BSG cellulose and hemicellulose.
The isolation procedure will consist of protein solubilisation using an alkaline solution followed by acidification of the
solution for protein precipitation at the isoelectric point and removal of inorganic salts and water by ultrafiltration. The
production of protein isolates high purity higher than 90% will be targeted. The obtained protein isolate will be analysed
in terms of purity (e.g. protein, fat, sugar, fiber etc.) and amino-acid composition and evaluated for suitability as animal
feed and/or food supplement. Moreover, it is important to understand the process conditions in which protein is not
denatured to maintain the best functionality. The BSG protein isolate will be also hydrolysed by commercial proteases
and obtained protein hydrolysate will be used as nitrogen source for PHA production.
Task 1.5 Production of biobased formulations for packaging applications. Leader: SABIO. Partners involved: NFG,
GPI, AIMPLAS, CTB (M11-M24)
SABIO will lead the activities of designing formulations of material for BioSupPack rigid packaging items, taking in
account the information collected in collaboration with ULK, NFG and MICA, regarding the properties and production
process of the packaging items. All additives and raw materials used shall be food contact approved. Iterations with
the processing assessment at pilot plant scale in task 2.1 will allow achieving the final compositions of PHA based
compounds and coatings.
Subtask. 1.5.1. Fine tuning of PHA compounds for bottles and secondary packaging.
In this subtask, SABIO will be engaged in the biobased material production (melt-mixing process) at pilot scale
level, followed by characterization and optimization process until suitable materials properties are achieved for target
applications (bottles for dressings, cosmetics, washing machine liquids and secondary packaging for beer bottles). The
optimization process to reach the most suitable compositions will take into account the processing assessment carried
out by AIMPLAS in task 2.1.
Subtask 1.5.2. Fine tuning of PHA coatings for fibre-based packaging.
The optimization of the coating PHA formulation will be carried out based on a patent pending plastisol technology
(solvent free) by CTB. During the first period of the project commercial PHA (such as PHBv) will be used whereas,
when available, PHB from the project will also be tested. The formulation will be optimized to reach the specific project
target defined in Task 1.1 to obtain minimal water vapour permeability and an increased durability. This can be achieved
by increasing the crystallinity with nucleating agents, adding hydrophobicity with different additives (fatty acids, waxes)
and by crosslinking the PHA. Both conventional food contact approved crosslinkers, such as polymeric carbodiimides
and partially biobased polyisocyanates as well as novel biobased crosslinkers based on starch aldehydes, citric acid or
itaconic anhydrides will be investigated. Also, a variety of plasticisers will be tested as they can heavily influence the
biodegradation, flexibility and durability of the coatings. Additives will also be added to the formulation to provide
Page 14 of 62
specific characteristics and adequate conditions for the drying process, as well as to improve the printing quality on
the polymer substrate. Suitable substrates provided by GPI will be pre-treated to improve the printability and perform
initial coating trials.

Task 2.1 Processing assessment of new biobased formulations at pilot plant scale. Leader: AIMPLAS. Partners involved:
SABIO, CTB, ILAB, GPI (M13-M22)
Before the production of packaging prototypes, the biobased materials from WP1 (compounds and coatings), will be
assessed from the processing point of view at pilot plant scale, according to the corresponding processing technologies
used in the plastic industry. Different iterations between task 1.5 and this task will allow obtain final formulations
in task 1.5 of compounds and coatings suitable for the plastics processing technologies involved in the packaging
production: extrusion blow moulding (EBM), injection moulding (IM), sheet extrusion and thermoforming, and wet
coating technologies (flexography, dip-coating, gravure-coating, knife coating, calendering, spray-coating). AIMPLAS
and CTB will oversee this assessment in collaboration with SABIO and GPI as biobased materials converters and
with ILAB and GPI as packaging producers. Processing parameters of the final compositions will also be optimized at
pilot plant scale, and other aspects like wall thickness and thickness distribution of bottles and secondary packaging,
application weights of coatings (grammage), etc, will be assessed.
Task 2.2 Improving of packaging performance. Leader: PLT. Partners involved: AUA, ASU, AIMPLAS, GPI (M15-
M28)
Subtask 2.2.1. Plasma treatment over rigid packaging
In this task, PLT’s unique reactors will be employed and optimised for the rapid treatment of the generated materials.
The reactive species generated in will be monitored by optical emission spectroscopy (OES) and optical absorption
spectroscopy (OAS) and employed for process optimisation. The effect of plasma will be tested on selected structures
and compositions of the different packaging formats for food and cosmetics products, and the resulting hydrophobicity
and barrier properties (oxygen and water vapour) will be characterised. The use of WCA, AFM, Raman, and FTIR
spectroscopy will be used to fully understand the changes in the polymer chemistry due to NTP interactions and
characterise the morphology most desired for more on-line analysis when testing at larger scales for quality assurance.
PLT, in collaboration with AUA, will optimize the plasma treatment required for each specific packaging applications.
AIMPLAS and CTB will measure the performance improvement achieved in the packaging. IRIS will apply its expertise
in monitoring systems for assessing the plasma effect on the surface of the packaging using Raman spectroscopy,
assessing the homogeneity and persistence of the effect on the packaging surface. The potential for material sterilisation
will also be assessed with the technology.
Subtask 2.2.2. Grafting modification over rigid fibre-based packaging.
Additionally to the PHA coating formulated in task 1.5, an optional well investigated nano-scale grafting modification
with bio-based fatty acids will be carried out by ASU on precoated samples on both sides of the fibre-based packaging in

order to improve the resistance to water/humidity of the external side and to improve the WVTR and humidity dependent
OTR of the precoated side. Bio-based formulations currently developed by ASU will be adjusted for that purpose. The
quality of the obtained samples will be validated by chemical (FTIR spectroscopy) and physical tests (contact angle).
WVTR and OTR will be measured. Cobb tests for liquid resistance and staining tests for grease resistance will be also
performed following the requirements defined in WP1.

development process in close cooperation with NFG and ULK, aiming to comply with every specification provided.

For each type of packaging, addressed to each specific sector, the development process will start by designing the

of the parts, will follow.
Machine and production environment will be set when all the necessary parts are finished, which will be followed by
processing the material and produce the prototypes. Every prototype will go through the packaging support engineering
activities to access all prototypes properties; namely, dimensional characterization, weight, top load, side load, section
weight, neck dimensions, drop test, environmental stress cracking, and any other that may seem fit. AIMPLAS will also
provide support for migration testing when applicable.
Two prototypes for cosmetic applications, one for food/sauces, one for homecare and one clip secondary packaging are
expected to be obtained, with different characteristics and volumes. Packaging demonstrators produced from new PHB
compounds will be provided at M30 in sufficient quantities for validation trials in WP3 and EoL analysis in WP4/5.
Subtask 2.3.2 Production of the fibre-based rigid packaging.
Coated cardboard, paper and pulp-structures defined for the project demonstrators will be produced at pilot scale by GPI
and characterized. Extruded PHA films and sheets will be provided by AIMPLAS to be used as lids for the different
packaging. Lids will be thermoformed at GPI in case of the cups. The focus will be given to the processability of the
novel materials, formability, adhesion to the substrates, sealability, microwavability, final laminate quality including
mechanical, barrier and optical properties. A benchmarking will be done versus the current cups, trays and bowls which
use PE as barrier coating. If needed, iterations will be made with the previous task.
Packaging demonstrators will be provided between M24 (commercial PHA-based coating) and M30 (new PHB-based
coating) in sufficient quantities to WP3 for validation trials and to WP4/5 for EoL analysis.

Task 3.1 Validation of food and homecare packaging. Leader: ULK. Partners involved: GPI, ILAB, AIMPLAS (M25-
M38)
ULK will lead the validation of food packaging following the work in WP1 and WP2 using the food packaging produced
in Task 2.3 based on the requirements defined in Task 1.1. Plastic containers/bottles (i.e. ketchup, mustard) and bowls/
trays/cups for ready to eat foods and beverage (e.g. coffee) will be tested by ULK and GPI’s customers respectively. The
shelf life of the packed products will be compared to the current packaging solutions by sensory, physical-chemical and
microbiological assessment. The packaging prototypes produced in Task 2.3 will be tested by ULK on real food systems
(sauce, i.e. ketchup, mustard) via case studies. Microbiological (e.g. total microbial count, yeasts and molds, lactic

made from the packaging prototypes obtained from Task 2.3 and stored under isothermal and non-isothermal (dynamic)
conditions in the range 0-20ºC. The effect of packaging, temperature and time on endogenous enzymes found in that
kind of products such as pectin methylesterase and polygalacturonase will be studied. Appropriate assays will be used
for determination of enzyme activities, using suitable equipment such as HPLC, spectrophotometer, microplate reader
and autotitrator. Rheological behaviour will be evaluated using an appropriate rheometer and colour changes will be
instrumentally measured by a chromameter.
Homecare packaging validation will include plastic bottle containers for fabric-wash liquids that are highly used and

to evaluate their on-line capability. Additional tests for bottle quality and strength will include a full set of physical

Task 3.4 Study of consumer perception and acceptance. Leader: ASU, Partners involved: NFG, ULK, MICA, ENCO
(M25-M38)
This task is led by ASU who will carry out the implementation and evaluation of the consumer surveys. This is based
on the experience of the ASU in all aspects which related to the topic of food, beverage and homecare packaging. With
support of a subcontract for the collection and analysis of consumer perception. These two are supported by NFG in the
field of cosmetic packaging, whose expertise in this aspect of packaging. To determine the perception and the confidence
of the consumers in the innovative and sustainable solution of BioSupPack, the consumers are specifically addressed.
This ensures the sustainable orientation of BioSupPack to the actual market needs. Thus, the time of introduction to
the market of the proposed solutions in form of the developed prototypes can be minimized and market success can be
optimized. An additional objective is to check whether the packaging specifications defined in WP1 and the prototypes
developed in WP2 meet the consumers' requirements.
To achieve these addressed objectives, a two-step investigation procedure is applied:
1. The selection criteria and information needs of consumers with regard to packaging material are determined in the
form of a qualitative analysis by a literature study. First, the criteria consumers associate with sustainable packaging will
be determined, being the focus on the four product groups (cosmetics, homecare, food and beverages), whereby criteria
such as credibility, transparency, individuality, relevance or novelty factor are to be taken into account. The results of
these qualitative analyses are then evaluated by a pre-validation. This is done through qualitative group discussions
with consumers who feel addressed by the keywords "packaging, transparency, sustainability", etc. The outcome is
expected to be a presentation of the criteria relevant to sustainable packaging and the developed prototypes. In addition,
observational methods (video-taping) will be used to improve the understanding of the consumer and his emotional
approach. The active interaction between material prototypes and application can also be better understood here.
2. A quantitative analysis, in which the opinions of the consumer segments are determined and quantified with the help
of the previously determined criteria. The focus is especially how they respond to sustainable packaging and developed
prototypes. Online surveys with personal interviews will be conducted in the individual countries. The results will be
quantitatively evaluated and checked for their significance.
These investigations, supplemented by other expert interviews, literature research and brand owner surveys will be used
as multipliers to extrapolate consumer perception and acceptance tests on a larger international scale.
NFG, as a producer of cosmetic formulations, for example shower peeling milk containing PHA particles, will test
the customers’ response to the new bio-based packaging on their products according to their own methodology. Three
approaches for validation will be utilized. The first is business-to-customer research which tests usability and visual
evaluation of the packaging. Customers are regular users of cosmetics products and provide valuable feedback in
person. The second is business-to-business research that consults the packaging with beauty professionals and cosmetics
producers. Business partners provide valuable feedback regarding professional usage in beauty salons, market potential
and possible setback in high volume filling and production. The third approach is small marketing research with a
sample size of 100 people. Marketing research consists of both qualitative and quantitative questions and evaluates the
packaging design and properties from a marketing perspective.
ULK will coordinate consumer testing by using current professional methodology of consumer research experts.
Samples of both current and trial packaging will be tested for functionality, appearance and home use. Additionally, an
approach of consumer perception for the environmental footprint of the novel packaging and value perception will be
included. The consumer group will be defined according to the ULK expertise on consumer sample selection for such
products, including typical every-day users through the large ULK consumer database.
MICA, in collaboration with Calidad Pascual, will perform product validations through: 1) physical tests; 2) a panel
of possible consumers and some market intelligence questionnaires. We could provide them with a beer pack with this
new packaging so that consumers can make an assessment with an online questionnaire.
The evaluation of consumer perception is complemented by the social life cycle assessment (S-LCA) carried out by
ASU in WP6. In order to profile the differences in perception and acceptance of bio-based packaging among all genders,
the gender of the respondents is taken into account in the analysis of the results.

SAV, AIMPLAS, MICA, GPI, IRIS
This WP aims at developing the protocols for the logistics for feedstock collection and packaging waste management,

Task 4.1 Logistics, procedures for feedstock collection. Leader: SAV. Partners involved: MICA (M1-M12)
The overall objective of this task is to improve the logistical steps of locally and regionally source of malt bagasse and
thus, ensure sustainability, security and reliability.
Subtask 4.1.1 Improved mobilization of malt bagasse.
SAV will design a shared online platform in which all the actors of the marc feedstock chain will be able to update “live”
and “in prevision” their marc generation or consumption quantitative requirements, hence ameliorating the management
of bagasse, whose quality will be analyzed by installed devices if necessary. SAV will speed up this process by gathering
all the sources that will enable a fair, trustworthy and effective market for this stock.
The platform will group all the “actors” as “nodes”, where each node will contain information from all stakeholders,
from marc producers to end-users, passing through logistic companies and many other entities. The platform will enable
also SMEs and large-sized companies to monetize their marc waste by providing alternatives to the traditional disposal
system (mainly cattle fodder), and hence entering the market as a strong competitor to satisfy potential customers’ needs.
The end-users will also have a choice in which bagasse to use according to pre-established specifications. Moreover,
both stakeholders (seller & buyer) will be able to set in advance the prizes willing to receive/pay, establishing a fair-trade
negotiation. The platform will have an international reach across all Europe and will optimize the routes, through the
implementation of shared logistics and crossed-information, resulting in a speeding up of the delivery services, usage
of trucks with adequate capacity, and ultimately, a more efficient and less polluting mobilization system.
The final benefits of this shared platform initiative will reflect in an economically wealthier and environmentally
healthier way of boosting the usage of industrial waste and adding value to the possible subproducts generated. Overall,
the linear economy traditionally seen in this segment will become a circular economy.
Subtask 4.1.2. Solutions for logistics operations
With the data and architecture of the cloud platform developed in point 5.1.1. The buyers will be able to purchase the
required quantity, based on a certain product/quality specs of feedstock needed for the industrial process. Sellers will

terms of vehicle type and distance to the bio-material collection and shipping point.

WP5 - Mechanical and Enzymatic recycling [Months: 3-40]
ASA, AIMPLAS, AUA, BUT, PLT, SABIO, NFG, GPI, SAV
The purpose of WP5 is to assess the recyclability of the novel biobased materials and packages with the purpose of
developing a whole sustainable value chain in terms of environmental and economic aspects. This assessment considers:
i) reintroduction of post-industrial scraps in the productive processes, by carrying out a mechanical recyclability study;
ii) demonstrate feasibility of a whole process (comprising packaging waste pre-treatment to reduce the Mw, enzyme
screening, improving and production, enzymatic PHB packaging waste degradation and PHA upcycling) that will allow
the upcycling of PHA plastic waste in an efficient, low carbon and environmentally friendly way. Plastic waste will be
pre-treated with plasma technology, biocatalysts will be improved using molecular techniques and PHA degradation
and upcycling will be optimized. This double focus in recyclability will allow optimize sustainability of the whole value
chain.
Task 5.1 Mechanical recycling of biobased post-industrial scrap. Leader: SABIO. Partners involved: GPI, AIMPLAS
(M25-M40)
SABIO will perform technical activities aimed at assessing the mechanical recyclability of biobased post-industrial
scraps which are composed of packaging production process waste and off-specs pieces. The process of mechanical
recycling of biobased materials produces recycled material in form of pellets suitable for the production of packaging.
The process of mechanical recyclability of post-industrial scraps is composed of two steps:
a) Size reduction process of scraps. This step makes use of a milling machine to convert mixed scraps into homogeneous
particulate suitable as feed for a continuous melt mixing process. A set of trials is performed in order to select the
most appropriate settings of the milling equipment (i.e screen mesh; Rpm of rotor) in order to avoid large particles and
minimize finus.
b) Production process of pellets of recycled materials. This step is performed by a continuous melt mixing plant.
A set of trials is carried out to produce samples containing different shares of post-industrial scrap. In particular 4
compositions are explored in order to cover all the possible mechanical recycling scenarios and industrial working
routines. Samples composed of respectively: 30% - 60% - 90% -100% of post-industrial scraps (w/w) are prepared. An
activity of optimization of processing setting is also performed with the purpose of enhancing the quality of biobased
recycled material. Physical and mechanical properties of biobased mechanically recycled material are measured.
In terms of recycling the cellulosic materials in the case of coated cardboard packaging, pilot scale repulping trials will
be performed at GPI before and after coating removal during PHA enzymatic recycling to devise the best approach in
terms of compromise between process effectiveness and quality of the recovered fibres. The fibres and newly obtained
fibre-based substrates will be characterised in terms of morphology and mechanical properties to define their range of
accessible applications.
Task 5.2 Pre-treatment of packaging waste by plasma technology for enzymatic recycling. Leader: PLT. Partners
involved: AUA (M17-M24)
Page 30 of 62
PLT and AUA will optimise the operation of the novel DBD non-thermal plasma pin design system (Figure 6) from lab
to pilot scale as pre-treatment of PHB-based packaging waste to enhance the subsequent enzymatic hydrolysis for 3HB
monomer recycling. PHA based packaging waste will be ground at small size shreds and subsequently pre-treated with
plasma technology to modify surface properties that will facilitate the subsequent enzymatic hydrolysis. The potential
primary degradation to reduce the Mw of PHA packaging waste via plasma pre-treatment will be also evaluated. As
the properties of PHA provide a high level of water resistance, there is room for improvement to enhance surface
interactions for increased breakdown efficiency. To do this, a simple route of hydrolysis is required. By utilising a
plasma discharge and introducing the polymer samples to the gas chemistry it creates, hydrolysis can be instigated. After
this has occurred, and the surface of the polymer has become more polar, the rate of acceptance of and interaction of
enzymatic material with the treated polymers is expected to be enhanced. Within the plasma gas chemistry, when using
ambient air, there is the generation of reactive oxygen species such as O, OH, and O3. To facilitate this, a homogenous
plasma discharge will be generated that can interact with the full surface of the polymers. By carrying out electrical
and optical measurements, the gas chemistry and electrical discharge type of the non-thermal plasma system utilised
will be characterised and repeated at an optimal capacity. The plasma treatment parameters that will be optimised are
relative humidity, treatment duration, discharge frequency, power, and the introduction of trace amounts of gases (e.g.
CO2). Product analysis can be used when comparing the formation of different reactive species to the changes in surface
morphology and properties to highlight the most likely routes of interaction and, therefore, the highest contributing
reactive species for enzymatic breakdown. The modified polymer (and potential lower Mw PHB chains) obtained during
plasma pre-treatment will be evaluated via enzymatic degradation (Task 5.4) and upcycling (Task 5.5). The thermo-
physical and mechanical properties of the pre-treated PHB will be identified, while emission spectroscopy (OES), optical
absorption spectroscopy (OAS), and Raman spectroscopy will be employed to identify as to what gas chemistry is most
desirable for PHB enzymatic degradation achieved in Task 5.4.
Task 5.3 Identification of highly efficient PHB depolymerase, cloning and heterologous production (lab and pilot scale).
Leader: ASA. Partners involved: AIMPLAS, AUA, BUT (M3-M28)
Subtask 5.3.1 PHB depolymerase development via functional genomics, metagenomics, enzyme engineering and
structural/functional characterisation (M3-M18).
AUA, AIMPLAS and BUT will develop highly efficient, robust and stable PHB depolymerase producing 3HB
monomers from used PHB packaging waste. Two strategies will be employed focusing initially on PHB depolymerase
selection from either (1) currently available bacterial strains or (2) new organisms identified from extreme marine
environmental sites and plastic-contaminated marine sites. Genetic, biochemical and in silico resources will be exploited
for the identification of a highly efficient PHB depolymerase. In the first strategy, the genomes of already sequenced
bacterial strains, available at NCBI database (e.g. Bacillus megaterium, Schlegelella thermodepolymerans), will be
computationally mined employing suitable algorithms for depolymerase-like open reading frames. The analysis will
reveal depolymerases of various classes. Bioinformatics analysis will be employed for the in-depth phylogenetic
analysis of the full family in an effort to initially cluster all known depolymerases in evolutionary linked sub-families
and to identify new targets as well as invariable or evolutionary conserved amino acids and structural features in
each one of them. The second strategy will focus on the analysis of existing metagenomics libraries created from
extreme marine environmental samples (e.g. geothermal springs, biofilms fouling plastic debris at the sediment-water
interface) aiming at selecting novel extremozymes with functional PHB depolymerase activity from thermophilic,
barophilic and halophilic microorganisms. Suitable degenerative primers will be designed and used in a PCR to amplify
bacterial depolymerase genes. Selected genes of the identified putative depolymerases, will be chosen, for cloning and
recombinant expression/purification.
AUA will implement protein engineering approaches and will conduct appropriate manipulations of genes aiming at
the design of more efficient and tailored depolymerase variants. The design of depolymerase variants will be created by
directed evolution/side-directed mutagenesis series and screening. Molecular modelling and computational methods will
be used to design side-directed depolymerase variants. Family DNA shuffling will be used to generate sequence diversity
by recombination, combining useful mutations from individual genes. Libraries of chimeric depolymerase genes will be
generated by random fragmentation of a pool of related genes (generated in the first approach), followed by reassembly
of the fragments in a self-priming polymerase reaction. High-throughput screening of the engineered depolymerases
and selection of high-efficiency variants will be conducted in plates using minimal salt media supplemented with PHB.
The ultimate goal of the engineering work is to provide at least three depolymerase variants with high specificity in
3HB monomer production and PHB hydrolysis rates (100% for crude PHB samples and higher than 50% for used PHB-
based packages (as will be identified in Task 5.4).
Subtask 5.3.2 Cloning of improved plasmids in suitable host strains (M10-M20)
The plasmids including the genes for PHB depolymerizing enzymes developed by AUA in 5.3.1 will be cloned
in efficient E. coli and Pichia pastoris strains by ASA. AUA will provide to ASA the recombinant plasmids, and
Page 31 of 62
recombinant E. coli including both E. coli Top ten and expression strains, such as BL21 (DE3). The clones exhibiting the
highest enzyme expression will be selected and investigated for their enzyme production capability. For this purpose, an
optimization of the expression conditions (medium, pH, temperature, induction strategy) will be carried out. The most
effective recombinant strains will be selected for further experiments.
Subtask 5.3.3 Downstream processing (M15-M24)
For the PHB depolymerizing enzymes selected under 5.3.2 purification processes in laboratory scale will be developed.
The downstream processing for intracellular enzymes expressed in E. coli comprise the steps separation of biomass, cell
disruption, extraction of enzyme, precipitation and affinity chromatography (for enzymes with his-tag). For enzymes
produced in Pichia pastoris-systems the culture supernatant will be concentrated by ultrafiltration and purified by
adequate chromatography methods.
Subtask 5.3.4 Scale-up of the production processes into technical scale (M15-M28)
Fermentation and downstream processes developed under 5.3.2 and 5.3.3 will be scaled up into technical scale. The work
includes the adaption of the technical process parameters as agitator speed, aeration strategy, oxygen control as well as
the verification of the fermentation strategy (continuous, fed batch, repeated batch, etc). Finally, enzyme preparations
will be produced in 900 L scale by ASA. The produced enzymes will be used at demonstration scale of depolymerisation
of PHB based packaging waste in Task 5.4.
Task 5.4 Enzymatic degradation of PHA based packaging waste. Leader: SAV. Partners involved: ASA ,BUT (M21-
M40)
The hydrolysis of plasma pre-treated PHB-based used packages into 3HB monomers will be initially optimised by
AUA, AIMPLAS and BUT in lab-scale using enzyme consortia containing at least 3 purified PHB depolymerase
produced in Task 5.3 and either commercial lipase (e.g. Lipopan® 50 BG) or ASA provided lipase. The hydrolysis of
untreated PHA-based used packages and native PHB extracted from bacterial mass in WP1 will be used as controls
for comparison purposes. Reaction conditions, such as temperature, pH, initial enzyme activities, initial package waste
concentration, initial particle size of package waste and enzymatic cofactor concentration, will be optimised at lab scale
using a Design of Experiments approach to maximize the 3HB monomer recovery from plasma pre-treated PHA-based
packaging waste. The degradation process will be scaled up by SAV using a 1.500 L chemical reactor until reaching a
treatment capacity of 200 kg of packaging waste. ASA will produce at large scale (50L reactor) and provide the required
enzymes using the optimal enzyme cocktail and reaction conditions identified at laboratory scale. PHB degradation will
be followed by characterizing the PHB waste and identifying and quantifying monomer/oligomer production through
mass spectroscopy and gas chromatography, respectively. The crude 3HB-rich hydrolysate will be evaluated as feeding
medium from PHB production via fermentation in Task 5.5. If the crude hydrolysate is inhibitory to PHB production
via fermentation then the crude 3HB-rich hydrolysate will be clarified by sequential treatment with activated carbon,
acidification to convert 3HB salts in the acid form, strong cation exchange resin treatment for cation removal, weak
anion exchange resin for anionic impurities removal and evaporation for 3HB concentration.
Task 5.5 PHAs upcycling from enzymatic recycling. Leader: NFG. Partners involved: AUA, BUT, AIMPLAS (M25-
M40)
To perfectly close the material cycle, crude or pre-treated 3HB-rich monomer hydrolysates obtained in previous task 5.4
will be utilized as carbon sources in bacterial fermentations for PHB production. Actually, two potential strategies will
be tested. At first, the crude 3HB-rich hydrolysates will be used directly as carbon source supplemented with appropriate
inorganic nitrogen and phosphorus sources and salts for PHB production employing the same bacterial producers and
fermentation strategies as described in Tasks 1.3 and 1.4. BUT and AUA will evaluate at lab-scale fed-batch fermentation
(e.g. initial 3HB concentration, feeding strategy) the effect of crude 3HB-rich hydrolysates in bacterial growth and PHB
accumulation. In the case that crude 3HB-rich hydrolysates are inhibitory to bacterial growth, then clarified 3HA-rich
solutions will be produced in task 5.4. Second potential strategy is to use crude 3HB hydrolysates or clarified 3HB-rich
solutions as feeding media for intracellular PHB accumulation after the utilisation of BSG hydrolysates in the bacterial
growth stage. BSG hydrolysates are rich in C5/C6 sugars as well as nitrogen sources and minerals which support
bacterial growth. Thus, using BSG hydrolysates for bacterial growth could be followed by using crude or clarified 3HB-
rich hydrolysates, lacking any nitrogen or mineral sources, for PHB accumulation. LCA and costing studies in WP6
will be employed to identify process sustainability using both strategies. NFG will be responsible for process scale-
up at pilot scale (300 L bioreactor) of the most promising of the two approaches. All the information gathered will be
mutually exchanged withing the WPs 1 (task 1.4) and Task 5.5, and all achieved results will be compared: the yield of
the fermentation, overall effectiveness of the process and final properties of PHB.

Task 6.1 Environmental analysis. Leader: ASU. Partners involved: AUA, All (M9-M42)
The aim of this task is to critically evaluate the potential environmental impact of the newly developed fibre-based
materials and packaging. In accordance with the regulations of ISO 14040 and ISO 14044, LCA analysis is performed
in the following steps: definition of scope and goal, development of relevant inventory inputs and outputs, assessment
of their potential impacts and interpretation of results within the context of proposed new materials and products.
The environmental impact categories to be analyzed include: global warming potential, human toxicity, ecotoxicity,
resources use and depletion, energy demand among others. The LCA will be performed using the specialized Software
GaBi. At the level of the developed prototypes, the intention is to demonstrate the positive environmental impact of the
food packaging materials developed by BioSupPack, compared to their counterparts on the market.
For a complete and reliable evaluation of the environmental influences and the resulting sustainability of the new
developed materials, it is essential to include the performance indicators (such as barrier) of the developed materials
with regard to their suitability for use in food packaging. For a qualified statement on sustainability, the classical LCA
analysis is therefore supplemented by the consideration of the specifications of the packaging defined in Task 1.1.
The influences of the material properties of the newly developed materials, which are relevant for the design of the
packaging, e.g. the water vapour or oxygen barrier, must be taken into account with regard to their influences on the shelf
life of the food. In this way, the influences of a changed shelf life of the food on the overall environmental influences of
the packaged food including its packaging can be recorded and actually used in comparative analyses to conventional
petrochemical based-polymer packaging. Therefore, ASU will contribute its expertise in food packaging design and
shelf life modelling to fulfil this task.
Furthermore, the results of WPs 4 and 5 will also be considered in the LCA. Because recyclability as well as waste
management are also essential components for the environmental concerns of the newly developed materials and
prototypes. While the potential secondary use must also be considered to correctly, reflect the negative environmental
impacts that arise.
In addition, the project partners will be supported by an interim report on the materials developed in Task 1.3 and 1.4.
The aim is to provide the project partners with preliminary information on the environmental characteristics of the
possible options as soon as possible in the project. This serves to provide a suitable basis for the selection of the most
promising options for upscaling in the following tasks, in relation to the aspects of the LCA results.
Task 6.2 Economic analysis. Leader: ASU. Partners involved: All (M9-M42)
This evaluation will quantify and analyse the capital and operating costs and cost variations resulting from the application
of the proposed technologies for each of the phases of the material life cycle and supply chain of the packaging products.
Page 36 of 62
This will be achieved by conducting a life cycle cost analysis (LCC). The LCC will have a similar structure and will be
carried out in parallel with the LCA (in terms of functional unit and approach).
Cost determination and cost-benefit analysis are the two basic steps of this analysis. Once the process chain for the
production of the new materials and the corresponding packaging has been defined, cost models can be created on this
basis to estimate the product costs as a function of various parameters, such as batch volumes, processes used, type
of materials, supplies and other inputs, etc. The new materials and products can then be compared with alternative
materials and products from an economic point of view, using the results of the analysis. In order to determine the total
cost reductions along the value chain of packaging products which can be achieved with BioSupPack, we will conclude
in which conditions and for processing which materials the proposed production lines are economically feasible to reach
to the market.
Task 6.3 Social analysis. Leader: ASU; Partners involved: All (M9-M42)
The social and socio-economic benefits resulting from the implementation of the new packaging products will be
addressed in this task. For this purpose, the Social Life Cycle Assessment (S-LCA) method is used. This innovative
method focuses on the social impacts and at the same time integrates the traditional LCA method steps as well. The
S-LCA comprises the following steps:
• Definition of the objective and scope of the assessment
• Inventory of factors that can lead to an impact; a life-cycle inventory will be established for indicators (e.g. number
of jobs created) linked to impact categories (e.g. local employment), which relate to five main stakeholders (e.g. [i]
workers, [ii] consumers, [iii] local community, [iv] society, [v] actors in the value chain)
• Impact assessment based on the selection and calculation of appropriate impact indicators
• Interpretation of the results
The proposed S-LCA methodology includes a detailed inventory method tailored to real data, which is collected
directly in the field of the application thanks to tailor-made surveys, that allow modelling the life cycle of the proposed
technologies. The environmental LCA and LCC is supported by S-LCA based on generic and site-specific data, which
can be quantitative, semi-quantitative or qualitative. It covers categories such as health and safety, job creation, working
conditions, well-being, etc.
The desired positive results are valuable inputs to stimulate preferences for these new materials and packaging
technologies. Furthermore, they will also be used as part of the baseline information aimed to design an adequate strategy
to increase job opportunities, stakeholders and consumer’s acceptance.

the EU28, regulatory compliance will be regularly checked throughout the project life.
EUBP with support of AIMPLAS will gather, assemble and share the knowledge base and expertise of consortium
partners, regarding regulatory compliance acquired over the project execution and supply evaluated data. This will
ensure the development of the most suitable material concepts. Supported via AB meetings, the activities in T6.4 will be
discussed with all affiliated partners and AB members (such as industry partners and brand owners), which will ensure
a continuous outlook on possible risks and compliance issues. Therefore, BIOSUPPACK will be able to plan mitigation
and also prepare possible audits, address internal or general regulatory compliances as well as especially identify non-
compliant issues. By this, regular AB support will add to BIOSUPPACK’s internal regulatory and safety assessment,
reporting all compliance activities to the consortium and also ensure the continuous monitoring of regulatory and
standardization impacts on BIOSUPPACK’s material concepts along the entire value chain. The regulatory assessment
will be amended by a complete safety evaluation, that will be performed for each BioSupPack packaging application.

To successfully connect and cooperate and acquire positive support 
from relevant stakeholders and the public at large, a strategic plan 
will be implemented in Task 7.1., on top of the preliminary outline 
presented in Fig ure 15. The overall communication campaign, 
which covers the whole project duration, will be led by the 
Innovation, Communication & Dissemination Manager, Mr.  
Christian Schulz  (EUBP), who holds an established extensive 
network and communication capacity to ensure that benefits and 
impacts of the project are genuinely conveyed to key stakeholders 
and the general public (in particular through brand owners retailers 
and fast food chains (for the case of service packaging). Our aim is to i) generate understanding of what BioSupPack 
seeks to achieve, ii) stimulate interest and support, and iii) motivate the collaboration of actors along the value chain 
as well as of the general public in order to boost the transition to the bio -based economy in EU. The BioSupPack 
communication campaign leverages one-way, bilateral, and multilateral strategic channels. Following a preliminary 
stakeholder analysis (which will be developed in Task 7.2), the target audiences of BioSupPack communication 
activities comprise; monomers & biopolymer suppliers, agro-food processors, plastic packaging manufacturers and 
converters, personal car e and food manufacturing industry and retailers, other packaging end users, consumers & 
consumer organizations, agricultural associations, public authorities, standardisation & safety 
agencies/organisations, scientific communities, and recycling & industrial composting companies. Particular efforts 
will be made to engage with and raise awareness of the general public. It is important to address consumers’ concerns 
by informing the public about our strong dependence on fossil -based conventional plastics to w hich renewably 
sourced, bio-based, high-performance, and cost competitive bioplastics can be the more sustainable alternative. The 
awareness raising actions carried out will aim to generate acceptance and open the path to building trust towards 
innovative biopackaging solutions, through optimal communication and transparency using appropriate language 
enabling EU consumers to make informed purchasing and consumption decisions. A set of key messages will be 
developed specifically to popularise complex subjec ts and to bring the issue closer to people’s lives by making it 
more understandable. The campaign will be rolled out through various channels and tools (figured in Fig.-14), with 
the following approach: 1. ‘Push’ communication (graphic identity, newsletter s, press releases, promotional 
materials, gadgets, etc.) and 2. ‘Pull’ communication and interaction (website, social media, liaison with other 
initiatives and projects, joint communication, etc). The fact that our demonstrators are broadly focused on consumers 
applications like standard and service packaging (which in turn was a prominent reason for their selection) will allow 
maximising the awareness of the impact of the bioeconomy, and indeed BBI programmes, on their everyday life.
