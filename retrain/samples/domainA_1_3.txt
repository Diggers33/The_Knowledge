### 3.1.1 Methodology and approach

The use of NIR spectroscopy for real-time food quality control in smart manufacturing lines is a highly promising and innovative approach. The methodology we propose is based on the acquisition of high-quality spectra from food products or raw materials, which are then analyzed using chemometric algorithms to extract relevant information about the chemical composition, physical properties, and safety of the products. The approach is designed to be non-destructive, rapid, and non-invasive, enabling continuous monitoring of food quality throughout the manufacturing process.

The methodology can be divided into the following steps:

1. **Spectral acquisition:** The first step is to acquire spectra from the food products or raw materials using a portable or inline NIR spectrometer. The spectrometer is designed to cover the relevant spectral range (e.g., 800-2500 nm) and to have sufficient spectral resolution to capture the relevant features of the spectra. The spectral acquisition is performed under controlled conditions, such as temperature and humidity, to ensure the reproducibility and accuracy of the measurements.
2. **Data preprocessing:** The acquired spectra are preprocessed to remove noise, baseline drift, and other artifacts that may affect the quality of the spectra. The preprocessing techniques include smoothing, normalization, and baseline correction, among others. The preprocessing is performed using dedicated software tools, such as MATLAB or R, and is optimized for the specific application and type of spectra.
3. **Chemometric modeling:** The preprocessed spectra are used to develop chemometric models that relate the spectral features to the quality parameters of interest, such as moisture content, fat content, protein content, or microbial load. The chemometric models are developed using multivariate regression techniques, such as partial least squares regression (PLSR) or principal component regression (PCR), and are validated using independent data sets. The models are optimized for the specific application and type of spectra, and are designed to provide accurate and reliable predictions of the quality parameters.
4. **Model deployment:** The validated chemometric models are deployed in the smart manufacturing line, where the spectra are acquired in real-time using the NIR spectrometer. The spectra are preprocessed and analyzed using the chemometric models to provide real-time predictions of the quality parameters. The predictions are used to trigger alarms or to adjust the process parameters, such as temperature, pressure, or flow rate, to ensure that the quality parameters remain within the desired range.
5. **Model updating:** The chemometric models are periodically updated using new spectra and quality data acquired during the manufacturing process. The model updating is performed using adaptive algorithms, such as recursive PLS or moving window PLS, to ensure that the models remain accurate and reliable over time.

The methodology we propose is highly flexible and can be adapted to a wide range of food products and quality parameters. The approach is designed to be user-friendly and to provide actionable information to the operators and quality control personnel in the manufacturing line. The use of NIR spectroscopy for real-time food quality control in smart manufacturing lines has the potential to improve the efficiency, safety, and sustainability of the food manufacturing process, while ensuring the quality and safety of the final products.
