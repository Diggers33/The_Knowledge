Analyse short- and long-term food security drivers and characterize their key variables and interrelations along the food supply chain  Drawing and further extending the results of relevant studies (e.g. SWD(2023)49) and the assessment reports by IPCC, IPBES and IRP, SecureFood will structure the analysis of food security drivers along six main dimensions: climate and environment, political landscape and strategy, market economy, technical and technological opportunities, demographic trends (including migration) and consumer behaviour (including attitude towards food waste). The work will be guided by the four main pillars of food security, i.e., food availability, access, utilization and stability, and it will address all the elements and activities alongside the food supply chain, from production to processing, distribution, retailing and consumption. SecureFood will identify key variables for the drivers of food security according to their short and long-term impact, either positive or negative, at regional, national, European and global level, while it will also specify their interlinkages, interrelations and trade-offs. The outcomes of the analysis will also guide the identification of the most appropriate data, data sources, inventories and hubs that address those drivers and variables, and that will be accessed and consumed by the SecureFood system. Introduce a novel, agency-driven Resilience Governance Framework  and detail a holistic Food Systems Resilience Management Framework as part thereof. O5. Digital twin and simulation modules towards enhancing food systems resilience SecureFood will use advanced modelling and digitalization techniques to develop and implement a smart replica of the physical food supply chain, i.e. its digital twin, which will mirror all functions and processes of the food supply chain into a virtual space. The SecureFood digital twin platform will not only simulate and execute what-if scenarios and stress tests (without risking making direct applications in real food supply chains) but will also enable a real-time synchronization between the physical supply chain and its digital version, enabling the timely response to potential disruptions. 1.1.2 Ambition  SecureFood aspires to achieve beyond state-of-the-art innovation in four major dimensions (I.1- I.4). These are:   I.1 Evolving the scientific knowledge base by introducing a novel exploratory approach to risk identification and scenario building The introduction of resilience in food system security is relatively new and still the subject of significant research effort. Risk identification is a critical topic for bringing resilience thinking into food security practices. With the deep uncertainties inherent to most, if not all, drivers of change, the use of exploratory approaches to risk identification and management appears as the only viable option. The state of the art in scenario discovery and evaluation (for risk identification) involves a two-phase approach, using Principal Component Analysis (PCA) to analyse the structure

the solution space and the Patient Rule Induction Method (PRIM) to discover the classes’ boundaries10. SecureFood will progress beyond the current state of the art, developing a novel evaluation approach combining PCA and Independent Component Analysis (ICA). Thus, additional information about the scenario space’s structure will be gained and more metrics for their evaluation will be produced. This novel approach for evaluating simulation generated scenarios will provide an improved interpretability assessment, significantly improving the risk identification process.  I.2 Safeguarding food supply and security at different scales through a holistic Food Systems Resilience Management Framework  Framing food systems resilience: Until now most practices and discussions on food systems resilience are mainly focused on their ability to resist and absorb stressors and shocks (i.e., maintenance of the status quo), as well as on their capacity to recover swiftly upon disruptive events (i.e., bounce back to the status quo)11, ensuring the supply of sustainable and quality food and access to all. The Food Systems Resilience Management Framework will adopt all these principles of resilience and will provide a systematic approach for advancing food systems’ ability to anticipate, withstand, respond and recover upon crises. However, under SecureFood, it will go beyond the state of the art by further broadening the lens of resilience by introducing the concept of reorientation as an integral component of food systems’ resilience-building efforts, that can safeguard food security in the long-term. Reorientation is related to the ability of food system actors to change expectations and accept alternative system outcomes before or after a disruption (bounce forward) (e.g. shift to less meat consumption and more seasonal produce). Integrating the reorientation concept, the Food Systems Resilience Management Framework will have on its cornerstone the need for reshaping and redesigning the food systems trajectory through target adaptations that have the potential to introduce structural changes, transform food systems and foster food and nutrition security, economic and social well-being and environmental sustainability.  The trade-offs between all those results as well as between the food actors will be thoroughly analysed.  Assessing and measuring resilience: According to the Food and Agriculture Organization of the UN (FAO), despite the growing interest, conceptualizations that would allow food systems resilience to be measured and assessed remain underdeveloped12. To this end, FAO has published a conceptual framework and analytical model for measuring food systems resilience at country level, highlighting though that all those characteristics that make food system resilience attractive, also make it difficult to model and measure5. Although many approaches have been developed and applied, either they do not adopt a holistic and systemic approach to address the complexity of relationships among multiple variables or, they do not consider the multiple components of resilience, failing to reflect the different perceptions of food actors on resilience matters4. SecureFood will go beyond the state of the art to address these challenges acknowledging that the development of a mechanism for measuring resilience mandates the blending of multi-, inter- and transdisciplinary approaches as well as the engagement of food systems stakeholders in framing multidimensional, and commonly agreed, resilience metrics and indices. The SecureFood resilience assessment ecosystem (methodologies, models and tools) will be a co-creation action, that will pull the multidisciplinary expertise of the Consortium partners and will bring together food system stakeholders so that their different perspectives on resilience are translated into key metrics and indices, and their tacit knowledge and expertise shapes problem’s modelling and resolution. The entire cross-fertilization approach will allow the SecureFood resilience assessment ecosystem to provide risk, vulnerabilities and resilience estimates that are underpinned by the different priorities and views of stakeholders. I.4 Enabling the application of innovative scientific knowledge to realistic case studies and scenarios through the use of advanced digital technologies  The state-of-the-art of digitalisation of the food supply chain is constantly evolving as new technologies emerge and are adopted by the industry. Some of the key areas of digitalisation that are currently being used in the food supply chain include (a) Internet of Things (IoT) and sensors to monitor various aspects of the supply chain, for real-time tracking and optimization, (b) Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Machine Learning (ML) to consume large amounts of data for predictive analytics and optimized decision-making, (c) robotics and automation to increase efficiency and reduce labour costs. While digitalisation offers many potential benefits to the food supply chain, there are also several challenges that need to be addressed to fully realize these benefits. These include (i) the lack of effective data management and sharing, (ii) the increased cost, as the adoption of digital technologies can require significant upfront investment in hardware, software, and training, which can be a challenge for smaller organizations and those with limited resources; (iii) the increased complexity in integrating different systems and technologies, particularly  legacy systems;  and (iv) the difficulties in low-latency fusing of information and forecasting future events. To this end, SecureFood aims to improve food systems’ resilience by leveraging digital tools to facilitate knowledge-building, streamline processes, and support decision-making. The project involves four key digital tools: the data observatory, the early warning system, the digital twin-driven smart supply chain, and the Agricultural Policy Simulator (AgriPoliS). SecureFood will overcome the constraints of cost-effective solutions and the silos that have been created by existing digital platforms and legacy systems, through the development and horizontal deployment of the data observatory. This observatory will be a web-based dashboard that will collect different datasets from diverse platforms and tools (available observatories and data platforms, including market variables, weather data, socio-economic data, among others), through a set of open and easily adoptable APIs, and will provide data analytics and visualization capabilities. The early warning system, based on complex event processing technologies, will consume data from various sources and will process them, identifying and analysing cause-and-effect relations between different events to provide situational awareness with low latency. The digital twin-driven smart supply chain will be developed and used to produce the digital twin realizations of specific food supply chains corresponding to different use cases. These will function both as simulation tools but also as enablers for the transition to a smart supply chain. Thus, the digital twins will complement the actual food supply chain providing connectivity, visibility, agility, integration, and intelligence, and thus enhancing the overall system’s resilience. Scientific Knowledge Scientific knowledge is a major outcome of the project and the foundation on which both the processes and the digital tools will be based. It involves several tasks that contribute to SecureFood’s objectives: Resilience modelling for food systems: This will be done by analysing first, the drivers that may affect food systems at different levels. Starting from a recent analysis published by the EC20, that identified several drivers, both short- and long-term, further analysis will be performed to better understand how these drivers affect food security, how they interact with each other, whether additional drivers exist and whether the food security pillars (availability, access, utilization, and stability) are covered. The next step will be to identify the corresponding risks, vulnerabilities and interdependencies based on the drivers and define food system resilience dimensions by linking resilience concepts originating from other disciplines to these drivers. The presence of deep uncertainties renders the identification and managing of potential risks especially challenging. Thus, a novel, exploratory approach will be developed for scenario building and discovery21 utilizing a proven five-step approach that will be augmented with independent component analysis (ICA) as a quantitative decision support tool. The scenarios will be used for a foresight analysis, that will analyse and evaluate potential interventions/measures for risk mitigation and resilience building based on a standard methodological approach. The interdependencies will be analysed using a multi-stage approach methodology: a) apply an analytic hierarchy process (AHP) for weighting the criteria to be considered, and b) a hybrid simulation (agent-based and discrete event modelling) for assessment of the interdependencies. A food systems resilience management methodology will then be developed based on properly selected indicators that cover all identified food resilience dimensions, while measures/actions for resilience building will be defined, providing all the ingredients for a comprehensive resilience management approach. The use of mixed-integer linear programming techniques will support decision-making process for selecting (and implementing) the measures/actions that minimize the identified risks for food security. Collaborative Processes The processes defined by SecureFood will be formalized into two frameworks. They will function as applicability and sustainability mechanisms for organizing and adopting the project’s results beyond its lifecycle, providing a systematic and effective way to apply the developed scientific knowledge and enhance food system resilience at different levels.  • Resilience Governance Framework: The agency-driven Resilience Governance Framework will organize the collaboration of the involved stakeholders, public and private, providing the means for the efficient adoption of the projects results and their application beyond its lifecycle. It will be based on ideas and concepts that have been used in different domains for collaborative governance, adapting them to the resilience governance of food systems. The same multi-actor approach that will be followed for the project’s implementation will form the core of the governance framework as well, ensuring a systematic way of applying the required processes efficiently and effectively. The processes of assessment, evaluation, harmonization, management of change, communication etc., will be based on concepts like group mental models and sensemaking optimization, while the framework’s structure will be based on similar frameworks, like IRGC’s risk governance framework, adjusted and tuned for food system resilience. Being recently recognized as a very important dimension in food security, agency will be the main design focus and differentiating point between the SecureFood governance framework and similar frameworks in other domains. This means that the constituent processes of the framework will be designed and optimized targeting organisations and actors to prepare for and invest in their own adaptive capability to capture, manage and exploit information, knowledge and technologies into their daily processes so as to strengthen further their preparedness level towards undesirable events. SecureFood’s ecosystem provides a portfolio that serves this purpose e.g., the risk-resilience management suite , simulation models, digital tools, information exchange platform.   • Food Systems Resilience Management framework (FSRM): The Food Systems Resilience Management (FSRM) Framework will streamline all the resilience related processes, e.g., assessment, building, management, producing a systematic approach for building technical, organizational, and operational resilience in the food ecosystem. Based on the knowledge built within the project by the collaboration between security and food  system experts and  actors, that will bring resilience concepts to food system security, it will address all potential hazards and threats that could compromise food security and provide recommendations on the appropriate preparedness, prevention, response, and mitigation measures. The framework will have a two-fold application: a) detail the requirements of concrete national plans on food systems resilience, b) provide guidance on the development of resilience plans at entity, food-actor level. The framework’s principles will be integrated into the FSRM tool, which will allow users to qualitatively assess various resilience-related indicators and extract indices that reflect the maturity of their resilience management procedures. • Digital Tools The digital tools will function as enablers and facilitators on various aspects of food system security. SecureFood will also contribute to standardization procedures within the EU and worldwide.
