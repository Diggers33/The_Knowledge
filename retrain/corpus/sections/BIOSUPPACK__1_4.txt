State of the art and progresses in the field of the use of biomass for PHA biopolymers production 
Various crude cellulose and hemicellulose and bacterial strains have been used for PHB production39,. Among the 
bacterial strains used , Burkholderia sacchari emerges as the most interesting one. Wheat straw hydrolysates have 
been used in 2 L fed -batch bioreactor cultures of B. sacchari to produce 105 g/L PHB (72% intracellular content) 
with 0.22 g/g yield and 1.6 g/L/h productivity 40. Hardwood hydrolysate has been employed in fed -batch bioreactor 
cultures by Paraburkholderia sacchari for the production of 34.5 g/L PHB (58% intracellular content) at 0.15 g/g 
yield and 0.72 g/L/h productivity. 41 Apart from this mesophilic bacterium, utilization of extremophiles agrees with 
concept of Next Generation Industrial Biotechnology which was recently formulated by prof. Chen42. Its use brings 
many benefits associated with robustness of cultivation process against contamination by common mesophilic 
microflora. So, the process can be operated at reduced or minimized sterility precautions with substantially reduced 
energy demands which improves its economic feasibility and LCA impact. Halophilic Halomonas halophila CCM 
366243 and thermophilic Schlegelella thermodepolymerans DSM 1534444 bacterium have been identified for PHA 
production of lignocellulose-based resources.  
Sustainable PHB recovery technologies focus on the utilization of environmentally benign solvents for PHB 
dissolution directly from wet bacterial mass followed by PHB precipitation. Ideal solvents should meet requirements 
as favorable toxicity and safety profile 45, low boiling point for easy recycling, water miscibility to ensure efficient 
PHB precipitation, efficient PHB dissolution at relatively low temperature, high PHB recovery yield and purity from 
wet bacterial mass, and no PHB Mw and property degradation. Water should be used for PHB precipitation from the 
dissolved PHB. Besides, sustainable PHB recovery could be also achieved by selective dissolution of non -PHB 
bacterial mass using switchable anionic surfactants (SAS) that switch from water-insoluble neutral forms into anionic 
water-soluble compounds through pH shift that is achieved by simple addition or removal of CO 2. Ammonium 
dodecanoate (laurate) is a relatively low -cost SAS that can provide high PHB recovery (99%) and purity  (90%), 
while 98% recycling of the surfactant is achieved by simple pH reduction by CO2 addition46.  
Beyond the state of the art, BioSupPack will develop efficient fermentations for PHB production using B. 
sacchari and thermophilic bacterial strains using BSG hydrolysates pretreated in a DBD air plasma reactor. DSP 
will be achieved using green solvents or SAS to ensure efficient PHB recovery with high purity and yield and low 
Mw and property degradation.

State of the art and progresses in the field of nanoscale surface modification by chemical grafting for improved 
hydrophobicity and to optimize barrier properties 
PHA have excellent oxygen barrier properties but for sensitive to moisture absorption or release applications the 
water vapour barrier needs to be further improved. With this purpose, polar substrates are generally combined with 
hydrophobic layers, such as PE layers, by wet lamination (with adhesives), extrusion lamination (without adhesives), 
wet and extrusion coating. For the "wet" processes, water -based options are preferred for environmental reasons. 
Such solutions and dispersions of waxes, starches or poly (vinyl alcohol) (PVOH) are available. In addition, physical 
and chemical vapor deposition (plasma coating) on a planarization layer is possible. It can consist of bio-ORMOCER, 
an organic-inorganic hybrid polymer, which is only partially based on renewable resources and offers no intrinsic 
barrier without inorganic coatings. However, the i norganic coatings can be scratch -sensitive and thus be protected 
by an additional top layer. 
For an innovative approach it is also necessary to improve the water vapour barrier properties of bio-based films and 
coatings. This is possible by green chemical grafting with fatty acids (FA) from renewable resources75,. The chemical 
grafting will be applied on polymer-based films and coatings by the so-called transfer method which was developed 
for the surface grafting of PVOH-based films76. The main attractions of this solvent free grafting process are its low 
cost, its use of existing industrial bio -based reagents and its potential to be applied on a printing machine at high 
speed and high efficiency. It is also a clean chemistry proce ss that does not give rise to by -products or to polluting 
effluents. In addition, the FA green grafting is expected to provide easy emptying properties due to significantly 
reduced surface energy of the grafted surfaces. These rebalance properties are comparable to emerging non bio-based 
market available solutions such as LIQUIGLIDE™ developed by the Massachusetts Institute of Technology, Toyal 
Lotus® developed by Toyo Aluminium K.K. or AsahiGuard E-Series developed AGC Chemicals.

BioSupPack will provide additional improvements of the water vapour of precoated board by the grafting of 
nanolayer of bio-based fatty acid on  top of the precoated internal side  and on the external side of the cardboard 
substrates. This technology, already applied on different coatings at ASU, allows a high repellence of liquids (e.g.

Enzymatic recycling of biopolymers 
Enzymatic depolymerization could evolve into a sustainable route for bioplastics recycling where conventional 
recycling schemes cause significant loss in quality and value 81. PHB depolymerases and lipases are the most 
extensively studied enzymes82. They can decompose PHB to produce 3HB monomers, dimers, oligomers or longer 
Mw chains depending on the depolymerase producing strain83. However, the native intracellular PHB depolymerase 
produced by Bacillus megaterium ATCC 11561 produces only 3HA monomers84. Schlegelella thermodepolymerans, 
apart from PHA production, is capable of extracellular hydrolysis of a wide range of PHAs 85. Commercial lipases, 
such as Lipopan® 50 BG, have also been used for enzymatic depolymerization of PHA into oligomers.86 Most studies 
focus on the isolation and characterization of enzymes depolymerising different biopolymers, but limited studies 
have focused on enzymatic bioprocess development for biopolymer recycling. 87 Bioprocess development for 
enzymatic depolymerization of PHB-based materials has not been reported yet. However, the population of aliphatic 
polyester-degrading microorganisms has been reported to decrease in the order of PHB>PBS>PLA. Few specific 
PLA degrading enzymes have been described compared to PHB depolymerases, indicating that hydrolysis is highly 
favored in case of PHB enzymatic degradation , so a higher conversion efficiency can be expected for PHB. The 
monomers or oligomers produced can be used to repolymerize, obtaining a low Mw PHB (Mw ≈ 2000)88. 
Geothermal springs and biofilms on marine plastic debris provide access to microbial communities with wide 
diversity in extremophilic microorganisms89,that may serve as potential sources of PHB depolymerases90, which are 
expected to exhibit higher efficiency in plastic/bioplastic degradation.  
AUA has expertise in high -throughput functional microbial and viral metagenomic technologies for complex 
microbial community analysis and mining new enzymes as well as the developme nt of recombinant expression 
systems (e.g. E. coli) in various R&D projects many of which are commercially oriented (e.g. funded by Prince 
Sattam bin Abdulaziz University in Saudi Arabia, H2020-MSCA-RISE-2017, Bayer CropScience AG in Germany, 
Sea Spread Lt d in Hong Kong etc.) involving the use of modern techniques including protein chemistry, kinetics 
analysis, biophysics and omics technologies (genomics, transcriptomics, proteomics, bioinformatics).

Innovation potential 
Feedstock recovery for PHAs production and other potential applications 
BSG accounts for about 31% of the original malt weight, representing around 15-
20 kg of BSG per 100 L of beer produced 93. Its composition94,95 (Table 5) varies 
with barley variety, harvest time, and malting and mashing conditions. Being 
commonly used as animal feed, it has been used as crude renewable feedstock to 
produce various fermentation products.  However, despite its alread y described 
potential (low cost, large availability throughout the year and rich composition in 
polysaccharides and protein)96, about the 20% is still landfilled. BSG valorization 
into biopolymers like PHA could represent a potential new business for both the 
biorefinery and biotechnology industries derived from BioSupPack project. 
BSG represent s approximately 85% of the total by -products of the brewery 
industry. Considering the annual beer production per EU country and the production of ca 15-20 kg BSG per 100 L 
of beer, then ca 10.8 BSG MTon (wet basis) was produced in 2016 (Eurostat). Considering 50% cellulose and 
hemicellulose BSG content and 0.25 g/g conservative practical sugar to PHB conversion yield, as well as the fact 
that 20% of the by-product is landfilled, then ca. 270.000 Ton PHB could be theoretically produced in the EU. 
Other interesting BSG components have other potential markets that can be exploited , like phenolics and protein 
isolates. The predominant phenolic acids in BSG are ferulic acid and  p-coumaric acid, with sinapic, caffeic and 
syringic acids being present in lower quantities . Phenolics can be used  as antioxidant additives  in bioplastics and 
proteins in the food sector by the use of derived proteins as animal feed or food supplements.
