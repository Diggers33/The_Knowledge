1.1.  The project summary
Project Number 1 837761 Project Acronym 2 BIOnTop

General information
Project title 3 Novel packaging films and textiles with tailored end of life and performance based on
bio-based copolymers and coatings
Starting date 4 01/06/2019

Topic
BBI.2018.SO3.R10
Develop bio-based packaging products that are biodegradable/ compostable and/or
recyclable
Fixed EC Keywords
Food packaging, Coating and films, Bioremediation, biodegradation, Waste
management, Waste recycling, Materials engineering (biomaterials, metals, ceramics,

Free keywords
Bioplastic, PLA, copolymers, coatings, barrier, biodegradation, home composting,
recycling, waste management, end of life, packaging, textile, food, personal care, eco-
design, circular bioeconomy
Abstract 7
Only 31% of plastic is currently recycled and plastic packaging still have a deficient end of life. Thus, improvements
are needed to provide cost effective solutions with high bio-based contents and suitable performances for demanding
packaging applications, with a consumption of 19M ton/year, while still achieving compostability in mild conditions.
Using sustainably sourced comonomers, additives and fillers to formulate novel PLA copolymers and compounds,
the BIOnTOP project will deliver recyclable-by-design cost competitive packaging solutions that can be mechanically
recycled, industrially/home composted or even suitable for anaerobic digestion.
Moreover, the barrier properties of delivered bio-packaging trays, films and derived packaging, will be enhanced using
removable protein-based coatings and a novel fatty acid grafting technology to decrease permeability and compete
with fossil packaging.
In the field of textile packaging , most used coatings are not bio-based and of different nature from the coated fibres,
making material or organic recycling extremely difficult. New PLA coatings or fatty grafting will allow reprocessing
without significant loss of properties.
BIOnTOP packaging, based on >85% renewable resources, will be compatible with a broad range of packaging
applications’ requirements but also multiple end of Life options. Our materials will be biodegradable in home
composting conditions but also recyclable for multiple use secondary packaging.
Based on new circular bioeconomy value chains, BIOnTOP will generate growth for EU bioplastics and end users’
industries in the food and personal care sectors with potential in many fields: BIOnTOP production is estimated to
reach close to 9.6 Mton per year by 2030, overall leading to €40 M turnover and 170 new jobs. All in all, reducing
the environmental footprint of plastics, our new bio-based packaging will have a significant positive social and
environmental impact.

Description of work (where appropriate, broken down into tasks), lead partner and role of participants.
Task 1.1 Update review of the state-of-the-art
An up-to-date review of scientific literature (peer reviewed journals and magazines), patents and company brochures
will be carried out to ensure that all relevant research in the area is identified and to make certain any developments
since proposal preparation stage are picked up. This task will complement and update the State-of-the-Art presented
in Section 2.1.3 and will map any competitive commercial technologies. This will also include a market survey and
benchmarking of existing solutions and in development state. This task, which will be led by ASU, will be performed
by each partner in their field and the results obtained will feed into review paper(s) that will be published in open access
scientific journals of high reputation.
Leader: ASU. Partners) involved: all.
Task 1.2 Survey and selection of materials for the new developments
Among the predefined biopolymer (PLA) of interest for the project, as well as selected diacids (LA, MA) for
copolymerisation and coating formulations, a survey of commercial grades (from EUBP members as well as possibly
other manufacturers) that are available in a suitable form/scale for our development will be performed. In the same
way, the selected additives that can be extracted from biomass (FA; as plasticizers citrates produced by citric acid,
oligomers of LA, cardanol derivates; organic fillers from wheat, etc.), will be included. The selection will be based
on functional and mechanical properties, colour, odour, biodegradability or other EoL scenarios, processability, safety
legislation (especially for food contact applications) & REACH compliance issues and price range. This will allow
identifying further functionalization, additivation, etc. needed. Selected grades will be ordered to start formulation work
at lab scale in WP2 and 3.
Leader: EUBP. Partners involved: AIMPLAS, ASU, INSTM, CTB, TOTAL-CORBION, PLANET, BioMi, SILON,
ARCHA.
Task 1.3 Specifications of target bio-based packaging and textiles

requirements and types for packing F&V, dairy food, tea, personal care products and for multisector bags) based on the

CMSA and EMSUR) as well as end users’ expertise (WEAREBIO, ENTREPINARES, UBESOL) will work together.
Other specific market requirements for the implementation of new materials will be defined (e.g. cost, regulatory /
conformity and environmental aspects, etc.).

Work package number 9 WP2 Lead beneficiary 10 2 - INSTM
Work package title Development of copolymers and compounds with tailored biodegradability
Start month 1 End month 24
Objectives
• To produce copolymers of lactic acid/lactide (LA/LD) and biomass derived acids (malic and succinic) tailoring
copolymerization reaction conditions in order to achieve copolymers with different comonomers ratios and molecular
weights providing polymers with low crystallinity favouring in this way their biodegradability rate.
• To achieve biodegradable copolymers with appropriate mechanical and thermal properties required for selected
applications (packaging) and upscale their productions.
• To use selected natural additives, fillers, processing aids and plasticizers to develop compounds with tailored
biodegradability profile and enhanced barrier properties.
• To characterise the novel copolymers & compounds developed and their processability to films and fibres.
Description of work and role of partners
WP2 - Development of copolymers and compounds with tailored biodegradability [Months: 1-24]
INSTM, AIMPLAS, CTB, Total-Corbion, PLANET, BioMi, EMSUR, WEAREBIO, OWS, ARCHA, INNOTECH
Two different LA copolymers will be developed (starting trials at TRL3 scale) in order to increase the biodegradation
rate of current market available PLA and maintaining its final properties. AIMPLAS will oversee the copolymerization
reactions and INSTM will lead the compounding. TOTAL-CORBION will select and provide LD and PLA whereas
REVERDIA (AB) will provide succinic acid (SA) and OENO will provide malic acid (MA). Based on the
characterization of the different obtained copolymers, a selection of the best ones from the point of view of properties,
processing and initial biodegradation results from WP4, will be done. The chosen copolymers will be upscaled via
its best copolymerization route in order to achieve a TRL5. Further optimization of biodegradability will be achieved
thanks to the preparation of compounds and biocomposites, using specific additives. The neat and filled compounds will
be tested in film and textile application. The suitable compounds will be upscaled. Resulting materials will be provided
to OWS in WP4 for biodegradation tests that will guide further developments. In link with WP7, ARCHA will provide
assessment of raw materials selected in terms of REACH compliance.
Task 2.1 Preparation of Lactic Acid based copolymers
Subtask 2.1.1 Synthesis of succinic acid (SA) and lactic acid (LA)/lactide (LD) copolymers. Two different approaches
will be used also to obtain succinic copolymers. First, LA, SA and a diol [such as 1,4-butanediol (BDO), ethylene glycol
or propylene glycol] will be reacted through a direct polycondensation with different monomer feed ratios. In that way
controlled molecular weights and different lengths of the multiblock copolymers will be obtained. This reaction can be
done in bulk state with catalysts such as titanium (IV) butoxide under vacuum at high temperature (100-180ºC). An
additional chain extension step will be considered to further increase the molecular weight if required.
The second one will imply the batch synthesis of a polyester prepolymer of SA and a diol molecule (e.g. BDO) (normally
prepared also in two steps: first step under nitrogen atm. at temperatures of 160-215ºC and a second step at vacuum
with a metallic catalyst at 180-230ºC) followed by a ring opening polymerization (ROP) with LD through reactive
extrusion (REX) or batch copolymerization. In this context, AIMPLAS has developed similar procedures under the
framework of Biorefine 2G project (FP7-KBBE). Batch reactions will be optimized in terms of catalyst, stirring systems,
temperature, vacuum and reaction time. For REX reactions different configurations of the extruder, catalysts (as stannous
octanoate, tin alkoxides, aluminum isopropoxide, calcium reagents as well as organic catalysts), temperatures profiles
and residence times will be applied in a well-designed twin-screw co-rotating extruder. The first trials will be done in
a HAAKE™ MiniLab extruder and once the best conditions in terms of overall efficiency have been identified, the
synthesis will be scaled up in a pilot plant twin screw extruder (subtask 2.3.1). These modifications will allow to obtain
different monomers distributions, lengths, molecular weights, crystallinities and thermal, mechanical, rheological and
biodegradabilty properties.
Subtask 2.1.2 Synthesis of malic acid and lactic acid/lactide copolymers. Due to the presence of a hydroxyl group in
the MA molecule, the obtention of a polyester is not necessary in this case and a direct polycondensation can be applied
directly in a glass or steel reactor under reduced pressure at high temperature (130-180ºC). Alternatively, the production
of a pre-polymer of LA over which the MA will be added. Finally, a third approach will be based on the ROP with
LD through a batch copolymerization. In this case the protection of MA as a benzyl malolactonate can be required. All
the reactions will be optimized in terms of catalyst, stirring systems, temperature, vacuum and reaction time to reach

copolymers with different ratios MA/LA-LD and molecular weight values that will produce polymers more amorphous
than the PLA itself.
In both subtasks, prepolymers will be collected along the synthesis to have the right molecular weight for application
in PLA based coatings for textiles (ca 10.000 g/mol) in task 3.3. Likewise, in the 2 subtasks, the best conditions will
be replicated to obtain 2-5 kg of the new copolymers to be characterized and provided for initial biodegradability tests

Task 2.2 Characterization of copolymers

(DSC) will be used. For prepolymers, the hydroxyl content will be measured to calculate the LD amount required in
the following reaction.
Subtask 2.2.2 Characterization of the developed copolymers. Copolymers will be characterized employing several
techniques. Identification of the copolymers obtained will be done by FTIR and nuclear magnetic resonance

melting temperature of the developed copolymers, thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) will be employed to evaluate their
thermal stability, gel permeation chromatography (GPC) to determine their molecular weight (Mw) and polydispersity
index and finally rheological tests will be very useful to measure their behaviour in melt state that will provide relevant
information related to processability of these copolymers. AIMPLAS will perform these tests with the support of
INSTM. Based on the characterization of the different copolymers and their processability, the most suitable ones will
be selected for each target application.

Description of work and role of partners
WP3 - Development of multifunctional coating solutions with tailored properties [Months: 5-28]
ASU, AIMPLAS, INSTM, CTB, Total-Corbion, EMSUR, SILON, Sioen , IRIS, ARCHA, INNOTECH
Description of work
Our barrier coatings will be multifunctional used for barrier, easy emptying and sensory purposes. In terms of barrier
properties, our coatings will provide barrier to i) oxygen & UV (whey), ii) aroma (polysaccharides like alginates), iii)
water vapour (FA), iv) grease & liquids (FA for repellence or plastic or PLA coating for textiles). As we work with
either closed (plastic films) or woven and non woven textiles substrates, the substances, application and thickness used
to get such effects have to be tailored. In link with WP7, ARCHA will provide assessment of raw materials selected
in terms of REACH compliance.
Task 3.1 Development, processing and testing of a new water barrier and repellence coating
In order to obtain sufficient water vapour barrier and repellence properties (repellence towards water for textile and food
products for easy emptying properties) bio based fatty acid anhydrides and commercially available fatty acid chlorides
as benchmarks will be selected and tested for solvent free grafting. The grafting will take place on whey protein-based
barrier coating (from Task 3.2) as well as on PLA films and textiles. TOTAL-CORBION will supply suitable reference
PLA substrates for the coating. These FA will be selected and applied to the substrates using a transmitter paper enriched
with FA reagent or via a gravure printing process initially at TRL3. The most suitable processing will be selected and
performed at TRL4. The grafting process itself takes place in a convective oven. Lab coating will be used as initial
application to evaluate and select the most promising processing reagents (different molecular weights of FA) and
processing parameters (temperature and time). The knowledge gained from these experiments will be used to support
the upscale of the grafting process in WP5 using a standard printing equipment for the grafting process.
Leader: ASU, Partners involved: ARCHA
Task 3.2 Processing and testing of the barrier and repellence coatings for films & trays
Whey protein-based formulations developed in Wheylayer² and Thermowhey projects will be applied on commercially
available polymer substrates using a semi-automatic lacquering device. If necessary, minor adjustments, e.g. regarding
plasticiser, additives or fillers, will be tested to ensure optimal wetting and grafting properties on PLA and BIOnTOP
films when available. Process modelling and simulation of respectively the patented in process protein denaturation
and the drying stage will be used as supporting tool for improved performance and up-scalability, with support from
IRIS who owns a share of the related patent (see table 13). If adhesion is not sufficient, standard substrate pre-
treatment (Corona) will be performed. These coatings will be grafted with FA using the reagents and process parameters
developed in task 3.1. The coated and grafted materials obtained will be characterised regarding mechanical and
barrier properties (checking BIF above 10 is reached for both VWTR at ASU and OTR at IRIS in comparison with
different substrates previously coated with whey without FA) as well as regarding their thermoformability at EMSUR/
INNOTECH. Repellence properties will be characterised based on contact angles and surface energy with different
liquid food simulants. Based on these results the coating formulations as well as coating parameters will be optimized
in several iterative loops in order to reach the envisaged performance. The data will be used as supporting information
for upscaling stage in WP5.
Leader: ASU, Partners involved: EMSUR/INNOTECH, IRIS, AIMPLAS, ARCHA
Task 3.3 Production and characterisation of PLA and FA coatings for textiles
Besides the direct grafting of the PLA fibers with FA from task 3.1, also the functionalization of PLA textiles by
coating with PLA will be developed. We will mainly focus on bio-based solutions for hydrophobic properties with
Page 21 of 62
excellent adhesion, sufficient flexibility and cleaning durability to match the needs of the textile food wrap and carrier
bag demonstrators (see WP6). As starting materials, the plasticized compounds or prepolymers coming from WP2 will
be used. TOTAL-CORBION will also select and appropriate grades of PLA (final polymers and prepolymers). During
the coating tests, we will also evaluate the plasticizers selected in task 2.4.1.
Small scale coating trials are foreseen, and 4 different coating processes will be tested: direct, transfer, hotmelt coatings
and FA grafting. For each, the processing parameters such as coating thickness, curing conditions, viscosity as a function
of temperature, etc. will be optimized:
• For transfer coating (CTB), a film is laminated onto the PLA textile substrate. For the food wrap, we will develop
(formulation and application method) a low Tg grade PLA adhesive coating that will act as a tie coating for the wet
lamination of PLA films to PLA textiles. The right crosslinkers (food approved) and plasticizers will be selected to
improve the mechanical properties of the coating.
• For the direct coating (CTB), PLA plastisol coatings will be developed (comparable to PVC plastisol coating) and
applied on PLA textile. A plasticizer suitable for PLA will be selected and mixed in high amounts with low Tg grade
PLA granules. Further, we will select suitable crosslinkers (e.g. food approved carbodiimides) in order to increase the
durability of the coatings to make them compatible with the intended use of the product. The crosslinker needs to cure
at low temperature because high temperatures cause the PLA coating to penetrate through the fabric. This can be used
for PLA coated shopping bags.
• For hotmelt coating (CTB), PLA in molten state is coated at elevated temperatures onto a textile. A compatible
plasticizer, which is not degrading at high temperatures, needs to be selected to improve the flexibility of the PLA
hotmelt coating and to lower the application temperature (applying PLA hotmelt at too high temperature on PLA fabric
will cause deformation or melting of the PLA fabric). This type of solution will be used for PLA textile food wrap.
• For textile grafting by FA (ASU), the esterification process will take place directly on the textile by using a solvent
free grafting approach. Therefore, bio-based FA anhydrides and FA chlorides as benchmark will be applied via manual
gravure coating directly on the textile and placed in a convective drying. Process optimization of the gaseous phase
transport and reaction phenomena will be performed in order to create textiles with super hydrophobic and repellence
properties.
The multiple coating approaches followed will maximize the chances for success and lead to a broader vision on the
applicability of PLA coatings for packaging and textiles, even beyond the specific BIOnTOP applications. For example,
the hotmelt coating method can be used for heat sealing processes.
The coated textiles will be fully characterized, which includes measuring abrasion (Martindale, EN ISO 12947-2),
hydrophobicity [via water repellence (AATCC Test Method 22-2005) and a water/alcohol solution resistance test for
the aqueous liquid repellence (AATCC Test Method 193-2005)], delamination (ISO 2411), air permeability (EN9237),
flexibility (EN ISO 7854-C), dimensional stability (EN ISO 5077) and tensile strength & modulus (ISO 13934-1).
Results will be compared with coatings available in ARCHA portfolio and other fluorinated coatings widely used in
textile waterproofing.

Task 4.1 Life cycle sustainability assessment
The sustainability of the developed products is evaluated by performing a life cycle sustainability assessment (LCSA).
The evaluation considers all three “pillars of sustainability” by combining the evaluation of environmental impacts
(planet), social issues (people), and economic costs (profit) by means of environmental life cycle assessment (e-LCA),
social life cycle assessment (s-LCA), and life cycle cost assessment (LCCA).
The LCSA will be used to evaluate the developed packaging and bags for food applications among others throughout
their life cycle phases: from production, to use and end-of-life. The end-of-life models will take into account the results
of the biodegradation tests performed in subsequent tasks. The LCSA is performed in four interacting steps, as defined
in LCA standard ISO 14040: (1) goal and scope definition, (2) life cycle inventory, (3) impact assessment, and (4)
interpretation.
During the goal and scope definition phase, the system boundaries and the functional units in which the results will be
expressed are determined. These play a very important role for the outcome of the study, so the appropriateness and
feasibility of different options will be discussed and evaluated in cooperation with and based on information provided
by the other WPs.
During the life cycle inventory phase, the in- and outputs of the studied processes are mapped. Where possible, these
are based on measured data provided by the other WPs. When appropriate measurements are unavailable, the data will
be completed using engineering calculations, literature data, and expert opinions.
During the impact assessment phase, the gathered data are combined with a number of impact assessment methods to
calculate environmental, social and economic impacts or costs and their main causes. The environmental impacts will
be calculated using the ILCD and ReCiPe impact assessment methods and the Ecoinvent database as implemented in
SimaPro or OpenLCA. These are used to determine midpoint impacts such as climate change and ozone depletion, and
endpoint impacts such as damage to human health and resource depletion. The social risks will be calculated based on
the Soca add-on to Ecoinvent in OpenLCA. The following social impact categories will be evaluated in this study: child
labour, fair salary, forced labour, and safety measures. The economic costs will be calculated in terms of total production
cost, total life cycle cost, and the main cost contributors.
During the interpretation phase, the results of the impact assessment are evaluated. The evaluation discusses the total
impacts and the main impact causes on which improvements during and after the project could focus, as well as the
competitiveness of the developed solutions relative to current market solutions. The reference scenarios which are used
for the benchmarking comparison will be discussed with the partners from the other WPs in order to assure that useful
and appropriate comparisons can be made. The interim results will support the selection of the most sustainable materials
from WP2-3 to be used in the production of demonstrators in WP5-6. The final LCCA results will be used as input for
the study of the post-project route to market.

Task 4.2 Material recyclability and reprocessing
Subtask 4.2.1. Material sorting. The goal is to prove the ability to sort the novel materials developed from other standard
plastic wastes so they can then be handled through their respectively adequate organic or mechanical recycling stream
(PLA, copolymers, compounds, etc.), eg. in case of mixed recyclable wastes or of inaccurate initial organic fraction
separation at the consumer level. Samples will be supplied along the finetuning of the materials in WP2-3 and IRIS
will use near infrared spectroscopy (NIR), hyper spectral imaging (HSI) and high-definition colour cameras to test the
accuracy of the distinction among different materials thanks to digital image processing and chemometric analysis of
the obtained spectra initially established for each system independently. For meeting the requirements of all materials
under study, the combination of results from different sources is expected to be required (e.g. hyperspectral cameras
respectively in the visible and NIR ranges). The most suitable approach to implement will be recommended for post
project implementation in recyclers’ sorting line. Once final materials are available from the validation trials in each
sector in WP5-6, their correct identification will be verified.
Subtask 4.2.2. Multilayer recycling. For multilayer materials, procedures developed in previous projects will be applied.
INSTM will optimise the treatment in laboratory tests allowing to separate the layers while keeping with best potential
for future industry transfer in terms of best cost-time-efficiency compromise. The separation of the layers was previously
achieved by washing out the barrier coatings with tepid water and/or water at room temperature with enzymatic
detergents. Alternatively, hot water, which is used currently for washing processes at ROMEI recycling plant, or water
containing salts could be used. The layers will be separated by density separation and they will be also evaluated
concerning re-processing. Tests will be made to compare the properties with virgin polymers and define which treatment
optimally preserve mechanical properties. Scaling up options and process/products potentialities will be assessed by
ROMEI on the basis of the achieved results and also specific tests carried out in its laboratory based on known standard
for plastic recyclates and market requirements.
Subtask 4.2.3. Material reprocessing. This subtask will apply to the monomaterials or to materials with a “negligible”
coating fraction. ROMEI, INSTM, PLANET and SILON will evaluate the ability to reprocess compounded pellets and
coated films from WP2-3 and demonstrators from WP5-6 (to simulate -post-consumer scrap) or production scrap from
the project. Materials will be cleaned, milled and re-processed via extrusion to pellets. The pellets will be characterised
and the need to blend them in different amount with similar virgin material will be tested to limit loss of properties
if needed. Tests will validate if FA grafting, PLA coating on textile, or eventually whey coating on thick film can be
tolerated by the reprocessing step. The accessible properties of the recycled materials will be proposed.

Task 4.3 Biodegradation under industrial and home composting and anaerobic conditions
The compoundsdeveloped in WP2 and WP3, as well as, where relevant, semi-finished materials like monofilaments, will
be evaluated for biodegradation under industrial and home composting conditions and anaerobic conditions according
to international standards (compost: ISO 14855, anaerobic: ISO 15985). One of the innovative outcomes of the project
is to develop a PLA material that is also biodegradable in mild conditions. As biodegradation testing takes time, the
different developed compounds will be evaluated early in the project to degrade in compost at ambient temperature
(28°C). The maximum allowed test duration to obtain complete biodegradation is 1 year for home composting. The
most promising compounds with regard to biodegradation will be evaluated for degradation under marine conditions to
evaluate the impact by accidental litter but also in soil to understand the potential for further applications like agriculture
films (applications relevant to EUBP and to our AB).
The results will be directly communicated to partners of WP2/WP3 in order to evaluate if further modifications of
the materials are needed to ensure sufficient biodegradation rates. Selected formulations meeting the criteria for home
composting where applicable for target demonstrators will be recommended for scaling up of the demonstrator solutions
in WP5 and 6.

Task 4.4 Predictive biodegradation modeling as support for eco-design of the new materials applications
Subtask 4.4.1 Setup and data pre-processing, pre-analysis. IRIS will set up a data base for the partners to centralise all
relevant information for the modelling task by M3. It will be initially populated with historical data, then complemented
by results from the previous task in terms of biodegradation profiles both in industrial and home composting conditions.
Next the available data will be pre-analysed to select the most discriminant variable subsets and to define new factors
using, for example, PCA (Principal Component Analysis) and Factorial Analysis. This will also rely on support and
expert know-how of the partners. Pre-processing of data before data modelling will give optimum input sets to the
machine learning algorithms.
Subtask 4.4.2 Data modeling and calibration. Next, the data will be modelled by benchmarking different machine
learning algorithms (such as SVR and RI, see biodegradation/disintegration modelling part in section 1.4) using at
least two fitness functions as objective output to optimize different aspects of the biodegradation, accounting for the
Page 26 of 62
effects of the known parameters influencing the biodegradation and disintegration such as composition and macroscopic
structural aspects. The characteristic curves will be represented in a “parametric form”, including key values such as
biodegradation time and biodegradation percentage achieved. As a first approximation (although not limited to), two
model challenges will be considered:
1) Biodegradation: testing is done on powders in order to try to link the degree of biodegradation with molecular
characteristics of the coatings and copolymers e.g. ratio of the different comonomers, additives, molecular weight,
crystallinity, structure.
2) Disintegration: depends on e.g. thickness, density, form (weaving or other conversion method) and temperature (e.g.
it will depend if we target home or industrial composting). The modelling will follow an incremental approach whereby
in parallel with WP2-3, data on results from powder, pellets and sheets will be initially used (not accounting for some
structure aspects of the final items), and new data can be incorporated as it becomes available. The resulting predictive
models will be used, from M30, as a design support tool to select the right formulations for each project demonstrator
as input for WP5-6 taking into account product specifications for the desired application and EoL scenario. Then, the
models will be upgraded to be used on full items. Once the models are trained and validated with unitary testing, a
Monte Carlo method will be developed to calibrate the system input parameters in order to obtain a closest match to
a desired output (and/or fitness function).
Subtask 4.4.3 CBR implementation, integration testing resulting in a predictive eco-design tool. Once the calibration
process is completed, a CBR historical case base and system will be defined, bootstrapped with initial cases, and as real
cases are processed, they will be incorporated, based on a similarity matching algorithm, which will also be developed.
Synthetic data can also be generated for the CBR to complement existing empirical results for data modelling needs,
especially in early phases of the project. Finally, the software (app) and algorithm interface will be prepared and an
easy to use executable will be delivered.

Task 4.5 Organic recycling of developed packaging by industrial and home composting and anaerobic digestion
The packaging demonstrators from WP5 and 6 will be evaluated for suitability for organic recycling by industrial
composting in line with EN 13432 Requirements for packaging recoverable through composting and biodegradation -
Test scheme and evaluation criteria for the final acceptance of packaging (2000) and by home composting in line with
NF T 51-800 Plastics – Specifications for plastic suitable for home composting (2015) (no standard on European level
is yet available), which is similar to the certification systems OK compost HOME of TÜV AUSTRIA Belgium and
DIN-Geprüft home compostable. This does not only involve biodegradation testing, but also material characteristics
(mainly heavy metals), disintegration and effect on plant toxicity. Disintegration, which is the physically falling apart
of a material, is strongly influenced by thickness and density and will be carefully examined and therefore the partners
will be requested to provide a range of samples with variable attributes. Results will be communicated to WP5 and 6
partners. Finally, the packaging (optionally including spoiled F&V, tea, etc) will also be evaluated in real life home
composting conditions. Furthermore, the new concept of organic recycling by treatment in industrial biogasification
plants will be evaluated. Therefore, not only the biodegradation, but also the disintegration under anaerobic conditions
will be evaluated with the option of subsequent aerobic composting to represent industrial biogasification plants with
additional composting step.

Task 5.2 Development of packaging demonstrator monolayer films and trays
Subtask 5.2.1. Processing of thin monolayer films and trays from biopolymers. from task 5.1, the compounds will be
used for the flat film extrusion of monolayer at PLANET to be further used in thermoformed trays based on preliminary

Task 5.3 Multilayer Films and trays production upscaling
Subtask 5.3.1 Coating, Thermoforming. The goal is to produce different multilayers in keeping with the multilayer

Task 5.4 Packaging validation for mono & multilayer solutions
Subtask 5.4.1 Monolayer solutions validation. Trays and films produced in the task 5.2 will be used to pack different
fresh produce including berries at WEAREBIO. In each case, a benchmark representing the currently used packaging
material will be used for comparison. The shelf life will be tested (via physical-chemical, microbiological and
organoleptic studies). At several storage periods, analyses of different parameters (appearance, colour, odour, pH,
moisture content, lipid-oxidation, etc.) will be carried out. Recommendations for further improvements in tasks 5.2 in
case shelf life is not reaching our targets will be provided in terms of the process, structure/formulation. Samples still
containing F&V will be provided to WP4 too to mimic the case of rotten products disposed with the packaging.
Subtask 5.4.2 Multilayer solutions validation for food. To validate a given film, trays (and optionally pouches)
packaging of cheese, different assessments to ensure safety, functionality and organoleptic properties will be done by
ENTREPINARES. Microbiological indicators of health and safety of the packed product will be checked (molds /
yeasts / Mesophilic Aerobics / Total Coliforms / E. coli / Staphylococcus Aureus / Listeria Monocytogenes) as well as the
permeability of the material through the study of the evolution of the gas levels in the packaging for MAP and vacuum
loss in the case of vacuum packaged. The airtightness of the packaging material will be measure through immersion
tests under vacuum. Sensory analysis of the packaged product will be done to rule out changes in the organoleptic
parameters of the product due to migration of material (with panel of tasters to check that there are no differences
between conventional packaging and that of the new material). Physical and chemical parameters variation of food
throughout its life will be checked to validate the preservation of the nutritional properties of the product (% total dry
extract, % fatty matter, % protein, % salt, pH). It will also be visually checked (discolorations, deterioration of the
surface, exudates, mold growth, stickiness of cheese slices and release properties).
Furthermore, the functionality of the package will be checked (analysis of packaging atmosphere proportion,
hermeticity, shock resistance, peelability and resealing between lower and upper film, etc.). These analyses will be
done at the beginning of packaging and on a monthly basis throughout the shelf life of the product between 2 and 6
months, depending on the type of cheese packed, to check stability and / or evolution. In addition, hydrophobicity and
easy emptying (repellence) behaviour will be tested by practical packaging realease tests with selected dairy products.
Measuring the residual product content on the film will provide a valid information on improved repellence and easy
emptying behaviour of the FA-grafted solutions.
From above tests, if needed, iterations will be made with packaging manufacturers to improve the available packaging
for the trials until partners can validate that BIOnTOP biobased barrier solutions allows similar shelf life as non-biobased
counterparts.

Task 5.5 Food packages health & safety assessment
The present activity aims at performing a health & safety risk assessment on new developed products. ARCHA
will assess the chemical safety and food regulatory compliance of selected representative final packaging materials
developed within the project considering the regulatory requirements for food contact materials set out by the EC
Regulation No 1935/2004 and, more specifically, the Plastics Regulation (EU) No 10/2011. The mass transfer properties
of the selected materials will be assessed using conventional and realistic methods for migration evaluation. This will
be achieved by studying the migration of a range of representative molecules already present in the basic polymer(s)
but also of substances not been added for a technical reason during the production process, “non-intentionally added
substances” (NIAS), such as impurities, reaction and degradation and decomposition products. Migration tests will be
performed according to EC 10/2011 and establishing analytical methods based on gas- and liquid chromatography (GC
and HPLC) coupled with suitable detection systems, i.e. mainly mass spectrometry.

Task 5.6 Films from secondary raw materials
The post-industrial and cleaned post-consumer wastes from task 5.2 as well as those from task 5.3 after optional coating
removal step from WP4 will be provided to Bio-Mi. These SRM will be tested alone but also mixed with prime materials,
if needed, in order to achieve high mechanical properties of recycled films. Tests will be made on several blow extrusion
lines at pilot scale to produce films (thicker than in task 5.2) and further multiple use shopping bags according to 94/62/
EC directive on packaging and packaging waste. Developed films will have different ratio of recycled matrix starting
with 10% of recycled content where we aim to increase this ratio close to 100%. After blown extrusion process, we will
start with welding/sealing process to obtain final packaging e.g. shopping bags. A comparison with the virgin materials
will be made in terms of properties to validate if the reuse of the films in similar applications could be technically
feasible, although it is currently hindered by food contact legislations (unless working in a close collection system or
after the certification of the overall recycling chain by challenge test which would not make sense at TRL5). To that end,
also migration tests will be made on these materials in task 5.5. Characterisation of the bags’ mechanical properties will
be done by tensile and propagation tear resistance tests. At the end of process, we will obtain secondary packaging which
are bio-based, have high recycled content, are recyclable via mechanical recycling but also via organic recycling which
is more than acceptable alternative for the currently PE bags which are fossil based and don’t have clear EoL scenario.
We favoured bag above 50 microns as demonstrator as they have easier waste management and collection, are more
resistant (reusable) and can accept higher percentage of recycled content (vs. thinner films made from virgin polymer).
Based on the properties obtained further recommendations will be made in terms of other possible applications.

Description of work
This WP will entail the production of nets and textile packaging demonstrators at pilot scale and their validation in
industrial environments at packaging converters in the consortium. The work will start with an initial eco-design phase
(in 6.1 and 6.2 and 3 for compounds and textiles respectively) that will deepen the initial packaging structures definition
in WP1, while accounting for WP4 LCA and biodegradation model results to date, with support from OWS RF and
IRIS respectively. The most promising materials developed in WP2 and 3 will be evaluated for the processing into
nets and textile filaments first at pilot scale and after then by industry for the potential implementation in a range of
optionally coated textile products. Products will be characterized and compared with current standards (both fossil-
derived and bio-based).
Task 6.1 Compounding upscaling for biobased nets and textile packaging
Based on the optimal process conditions used at lab scale in WP2 and the information acquired from the characterization
results and biodegradation tests performed in WP4, the most suitable compounds will be selected for each textile
application (processability required by different methods). PLANET and SILON will produce in the range of 50 kg
of pellets in a pilot twin screw extruder of 4 most promising formula (respectively for nets and textiles). Compound
production will iterate on the basis of the feedback from industrial production of the demonstrators optimising the
formula if needed.
Leader: SILON; Partners involved: INSTM, PLANET
Task 6.2 Development of packaging demonstrator nets
Subtask 6.2.1. Processing of nets from biopolymers. CMSA with the support of AIMPLAS will produce nets for fruits
packaging by a modified Extrusion Melt Spinning (EMS) at industrial level. This process has a very specific die for
obtaining the extruded non-oriented nets in tubular form. In the project, oriented net will be produced although their
combination with plastic sheets will be taken into account as a contingency plan (the flexible bag would be produced with
the material developed for films from WP5) for less exigent applications. The process to obtain oriented nets includes
a new step after the net formation and cooling consisting on a new heating and stretching step by means of drawing
rollers to improve their mechanical properties. Additionally, small modifications will be considered in the tooling of the
spun-process machinery to maximize the stretching of the filaments and the weldability between filaments and between
nets and sheets.
Based on the work done during ECOBIONET project where PLA was used, each processing parameter (screw
temperature, screw speed, annular die rotation speed, die temperature, stretching ratio, etc.) will be analysed in order
to know their role in the quality of the net and process output with the BIOnTOP materials. The best parameters will
be optimized to produce enough extruded tubular nets to evaluate the robustness of the process and the average output,
and to do a complete characterization of the nets as described in tasks 6.1.2.
Subtask 6.2.2 Characterization of the nets obtained. According to the experience of CMSA customers and the
recommendations of standard CEN TC 261 ("Packaging") and ISO 2247, different tests will be performed to simulate the
mechanical and thermal stress that the net suffers from packaging in automatic machines until the product is consumed.
AIMPLAS & CMSA will evaluate the functionality of the new biodegradable nets, comparing them to the most
commonly used nets at present and the obtained during ECOBIONET project. The following aspects will be evaluated: i)
deformation of the nets resulting from packaging process and ii) deterioration of the nets due to environmental conditions
Page 37 of 62
(humidity, temperature, etc.). A small series of nets will be delivered for biodegradation tests in WP4 and task 6.4 for
food storage validation.
Lead: CMSA; Partners involved: AIMPLAS, WEAREBIO, IRIS.
Task 6.3 Development of textile fibres, yarns and fabrics for packaging
Subtask 6.3.1 Melt spinning processing for textile packaging from biopolymers. The compounds from task 6.1 selected
based on preliminary feasibility trials to obtain filaments in WP2 will be thoroughly evaluated for their processing into
textile. Depending on the further processing different melt spinning processes will be investigated. Starting conditions
and dimensions (dtex/diameter of the filaments) will be set-up at CTB in collaboration with the industrial end-users
SIOEN and SILON.
• Monofilament / tape extrusion (needing a minimum of 5 kg for one trial day): A monofilament is produced using
water-bath quenching meaning that hardly any melt drawing is performed. The quenched filament is heated above Tg
and drawn to the maximum extent (draw ratios between 6 and 10).
• Staple fibre extrusion (needing a minimum amount of 10 kg for one trial day): The process to obtain staple fibres
consists of extrusion, drawing, crimping and cutting. This will be done in 1 step (called short spinning, typically at
lower speeds) and in 2 steps (called long spinning, typically at higher speeds and for finer diameters). Staple fibres will
be predominantly produced using recycled material fractions.
Fine-tuning of the extrusion parameters will be done in order to optimize the filament production as well as the
final mechanical properties. The success of the extrusion process itself will be determined via filament homogeneity,
production speed, maximum drawing rate, filament breakages and observation of any anomalies. The processing
conditions that should be evaluated include: extruder and die temperature, throughput, drawing rollers speed and
temperature, die geometry, shear rate, melt and cold draw ratio, spinfinish application, etc.
Subtask 6.3.2 Full characterisation of produced filaments. The produced filaments will be fully characterized in order to
evaluate their further processing and guide potential feedback loops. Characterisation tests include mechanical analysis
(tensile strength, modulus, elongation at break) – under controlled conditions, determination of the materials density
(correlated to molecular structure and composition), shrinkage of filaments triggered by heat, and resulting shift in
mechanical properties, DSC (crystalline structure, melting temperature).
Furthermore, durability is an important factor for screening new formulations as indication that a certain life time
of the products can be achieved. Therefore, the tensile strength will be determined after ageing by UV and Xenon
light and by combined testing of increased heat and humidity in a climate chamber. In cooperation with WP4, the
biodegradability will also be evaluated at filament level to select filaments with best processing conditions and properties
for the validation into textile products.
Subtask 6.3.3 Processing of novel materials into fabrics. Based on the results of the previous sub-tasks, 1 or 2 novel PLA-
based formulations will be selected for validation by further processing into fabrics (semi-finished products). This will
require to produce filaments on a larger scale. For the processing into fabrics, we will focus on weaving (as it is relevant
for teabags and wraps) and on nonwoven (as it is relevant for the carrier bag). For the weaving and nonwoven processes,
the processing conditions will have to be established to deal with the novel materials and the resulting characteristics.
A key parameter will be the processing speed and the evaluation of the friction behaviour of the yarns.
Results of WP3 will be used for adding a functional coating to the fabrics. This will allow to obtain a hydrophobic PLA
coating by using the coating techniques that were developed in WP3.
The woven and nonwoven fabrics will be fully characterized and compared with similar current (coated) fabrics (non-
bio-based, non-biodegradable). This involves the mechanical characterisation of the fabric and the physico-chemical
analysis of the fabrics, with and without coating. Also the biodegradability (in interaction with WP4) will be investigated.
The functional performance will be dealt with in a sub-task of 6.5-. Recommendations for post project scale up and
broader deployment will be provided taking into account techno-economic feasibility. Based on the obtained properties
and the needs of the consortium and AB partners, potential other applications, besides the ones validated in task 6.4
will be suggested (e.g. meat absorption pads, meat nets, coffee pads, industrial packaging etc). The results will also be
compared to those achievable by PLA by TOTAL-CORBION.
Leader: CTB; Partners involved: CTB (leading 1st 2 subtasks), SILON, ARCHA, OWS RF, IRIS, TOTAL-CORBION
Task 6.4. Food packaging validation for nets and textile solutions
Subtask 6.4.1 F&V nets validation. The nets produced at CMSA will be tested in WEAREBIO´s facilities, to evaluate
their processability and welding in the automatic filling production line, and their behaviour during transport and storage.
Selected products to be packed in the nets are potatoes or tomatoes depending of harvest season. Afterwards, the ageing
of these nets will be evaluated using different laboratory technologies in AIMPLAS facilities. Although the shelf life is
not expected to be influenced in that format, tracking will be made along the storage. Sensory analysis will be performed.
If needed, recommendations for further improvements in tasks 6.2 will be provided in terms of the process, structure.
Samples still containing F&V will be provided to WP4 too to mimic the case of rotten products disposed with the
packaging.
Leader: WEAREBIO. Partners involved: ASU, AIMPLAS
Page 38 of 62
Subtask 6.4.2 Tea bag and food wrap validation. Monofilaments with a fineness of 30 dtex will be produced based
on the results obtained at pilot scale in task 6.3. These monofilaments will be woven by SIOEN into fine meshed
fabrics typically used for tea bags which will be filled in collaboration with a customer of SIOEN. Their performance
(mechanical properties, shelf life, etc) will be tested and compared to the currently available solutions (non bio-based
and bio-based).
An important characteristic of the tea bag is the preservation of the flavours and fragrances of the tea. Current available
solutions work with finely meshed materials to realise this. Within BIOnTOP, we want to do a first screening of the
addition of a water-soluble coating layer, which will keep the flavours and fragrances during shelf life but release them
as soon as the tea bag is immersed in hot water. In this way, the aroma barrier properties are enhanced and the shelf life
of the tea is extended. To this end, the coating of a water-soluble coating such as commercially available polysaccharides
such as alginates based coatings on top of the woven fabric will be evaluated (or lamination with an edible film thereof).
Aroma barrier will be measured indirectly by gas permeation measurement methods. Selected samples will be tested
with sensory panellists at ASU.
Lead: SIOEN, Partners involved: SIOEN, ASU
Task 6.5 Food packages health & safety assessment for nets and textile solutions
ARCHA will perform migration tests on the textile packaging as described in task 5.6.
Leader: ARCHA. Partners involved: those who processed/used materials to provide info & samples
Task 6.6 Textiles from secondary raw materials
The post-industrial and cleaned post-consumer wastes from the project will be provided to SILON (who will have pre-
evaluated with ROMEI and INSTM the possibility to reprocess the recycled PLA with good properties in WP4). CTB
and SILON will test the recycled pellets for staple fibre extrusion as this a common way to re-use recycled polymers
such as PET. The recycled BIOnTOP materials will serve as an alternative for the recycled polyester staple fibres, which
SILON standardly uses, with the advantage of not only being made from recycled materials but also being bio-based
and compostable. Work will start at the pilot extrusion line of CTB evaluating the processability to multifilaments of
the recycled materials and the resulting mechanical properties (mainly tenacity and strain). If required for processing,
virgin biobased polymers will be added. These staple fibres will be processed to nonwovens.
A hydrophobic coating (PLA or FA-based) will be applied by lamination on the PLA based nonwoven for final use as
carrier bags. Characterisation will mainly include mechanical strength, ageing behaviour and hydrophobic properties.
The recyclability of the coated bags will be tested too for a similar application. Based on the properties obtained further
recommendations will be made in terms of other possible applications.
Leader: SILON. Partners involved: SIOEN, STB, ROMEI, INSTM

This task, acting as an umbrella for the IPR, exploitation and dissemination activities encompassed in this WP, will

Task 7.2 Study of consumer perception
Consumers will be targeted to analyse their perception to drive our development to a future successful packaging and
to finally assess their confidence in BIOnTOP innovations. This will ensure the sustained alignment of BIOnTOP to
market real needs to help decreasing time-to-market of the proposed solutions. A survey (questionnaire) will be designed
and submitted to consumers to get insight into how they perceive the consumption of food packed with our new bio-
based solutions with optimal conservation properties but also safer for the environment. This will complement societal
assessment (s-LCA) performed in WP4. Consumers will be surveyed on their habits towards shelf life information,
packaging format they use, emerging needs, material type and EoL labelling to comprehend their attention to end-of-
life options for packaging, as well as their reaction regarding the source of our new bio-based materials, the ethical
concerns they may have regarding material’s origin from food by-products or their potential leak in the environment
at EoL. The questionnaire will be disseminated among different channels: association offices available nationally both
from MC and our AB as well as its network; website and social networks; newsletters; Consumer’s magazine; partner
associations of European Consumers Union, ECU. This last, in particular, will ensure a strong coverage at European
level, providing a direct insight on consumers attitudes on the issue and their requirements. The project partners will also
contribute to the survey in their respective value chains. While setting the questionnaire, performing this study as well
as during the processing of data, gender aspects will be also considered, as environmental/safety/cost-efficient factors
on packaging selection vary depending on the gender of the consumers. Furthermore, inputs from sensory analysis
performed in WP5 and 6 will be given for consumer perception study. Results from the questionnaire will be discussed in
three focus groups, in which associations, companies and institutions will participate. Findings will be used to steer the
project towards consumers’ requirements/influence. The survey structure will be also taken as baseline for developing
an information guide for the consumers that will be used for the dissemination of results.
Leader: MC. Partners involved: Advisory board, OWS(terminated)/OWS RF, ENCO, EUBP

This task seeks to boost new bioeconomy value chains based on BIOnTOP results, by strategically providing impetus to

Task 7.4 Knowledge and IPR management
This task intends to capture and effectively manage the IP used in BIOnTOP project and generated by it. Measures
and rules for knowledge management and protection will be set up to ensure an effective dissemination and a fair
commercial/non-commercial exploitation of the project results. The IPR management will be carried out all along the
project schedule to derive the greatest commercial value out of the intangible assets (IA) and intellectual capital (IC).
Patent watch will be performed periodically to confirm the novelty of the proposed R&I work and guarantee that the
consortium partners are free to operate. The background information and knowledge held by the partners will be secured
according to the IPR strategy (IPR ownership, Access rights, Non-Disclosure Agreement /Material Transfer Agreement
if any 3rd party need to test the novel materials we aim at protecting, etc.). The results generated will be regularly
screened in terms of novelty, patentability and protection to define the most appropriate IPR scheme to secure the project
outputs. After assessing the commercial potential, all protectable results will be protected (patent, copyright, trademark,
etc.) by the owner or joint owners. Business information or valuable know-how that cannot be protected by patents or
other forms of registered protection will be protected via contractual mechanisms (NDA/MTA) or trade secret. A policy
on publication of results will be implemented where a clearance procedure will be followed by all parties, to mitigate
against the risk of publicly disclosing information before patent protection is in place and avoid that presentations/
publications may hinder/jeopardise patent protection. The IPR Manager will work closely with the knowledge owners
to assist them in fulfilment of the IPR rules, as well as negotiating any additional agreements that might be necessary
in line with developments as they unfold (Joint Ownership Agreement(s), etc.). The IPR Manager will also support the
inventors and knowledge owners in acquiring further insight on to how to deal with their new inventions.
Leader: ENCO. All partners will be involved.
